## History of Performance Management {#slide-5}

- WW1- Formal appraisal with Army rating/ranking system based on merit
- 1920s- Forced rank used
- 1940s- Behavioral methods used
- 1960s- Self-evaluation
- 1970s- Focus on merit-based pay 
- 1980s- Forced ranking utilized in GE
- 1990s- 360-degree feedback and appraisal developed
- 2000s- Flatter organizations
- 2010s- Several larger orgs. move away for formal annual appraisal for more continuous options (Adobe, Deloitte, Microsoft)

5

## Features of Performance Management {#slide-8}

- Continuous communication and feedback ( Pulakos , 2009)
- Multi-source input (supervisor, peers, self, customers)
- Explicit alignment of performance standards with strategy
- Fairness & transparency critical (distributive, procedural, interpersonal, informational justice) ( Aguinis , 2012)
- Managing performance through established measures
- Considering past, current, and future performance

## Benefits ( Pulakos , 2009) {#slide-14}

- Aligns individual behavior with strategy
- Enhances communication & trust
- Increases motivation, skill development, retention
- Provides defensible basis for HR decisions
- Culture of continuous improvement

## Challenges in Performance Management {#slide-15}

- Bias in appraisals
- Lack of managerial involvement or skill
- Employee resistance
- Inadequate Feedback
- Disconnect from Org. strategy

## Role of Managers ( Pulakos , 2009) {#slide-16}

-  Translating strategy into individual goals
-  Providing continuous coaching and feedback
-  Building trust and fairness perceptions
-  Overcoming reluctance to deliver critical feedback
-  Acting as the "link" between HR systems and employee experience

## Evidence-Based Performance Management {#slide-17}

Integrating scientific evidence and data to enhance PM practices

Key Elements:

- Use of validated assessment tools
- Continuous feedback loops
- Alignment with strategic business objectives

Benefits:

- Increase objectivity, reduce bias, capture true job performance, and improve decision making
