## Combining Multisource Ratings {#slide-9}

-  Ratings from different sources may need different weights depending on context.
-  Supervisors often weighted higher for administrative decisions; peers and subordinates for developmental feedback.
-  Composite scores improve reliability and overall validity.
-  Consider contextual weighting (e.g., team-based roles → more weight on peer ratings).

## Peer Feedback {#slide-10}

- Evaluation provided by colleagues at the same level to give insight into daily interactions, teamwork, shared duties, and collaboration

## 360-Degree Feedback Drawbacks {#slide-12}

![](ppt/media/image4.jpeg "Picture 9")

- Conflicting roles in being a peer and judge
- Peer appraisal can impact group dynamics 
- Difficult to integrate and combine multiple sources of feedback 
- Different goals from different sources
- Inexperienced raters may introduce unreliable ratings
- Requires training to work effectively 
- Costly in resources and time 
- People over-estimate themselves

## Impact of Feedback Training {#slide-22}

- Studies show that trained raters provide more accurate, reliable, and actionable feedback (Smither et al., 2005)
- Proper and effective feedback training  helps ensure that 360-degree feedback systems lead to improved performance and employee development ( Morgeson  et al., 2005).

## Smither et al., 2005 {#slide-27}

Meta-analysis on multi-source feedback

- Validity of multisource feedback

<!-- -->

- Multisource feedback ratings are related to other measures of performance 
- Raters from different perspectives conceptualize performance in similar ways but have varied ratings

27

## Smither et al., 2005 {#slide-28}

-  Across studies, the overall effect size of performance improvement following feedback was small:

<!-- -->

- Direct reports (subordinates): Corrected mean effect size of .15
- Peers: Corrected mean effect size of .05
- Supervisors: Corrected mean effect size of .15
- Self-ratings: No significant improvement, with a corrected mean effect size of -.04.

28

## Conway &  Huffcutt , 1997 {#slide-29}

- Psychometric properties of ratings

<!-- -->

- Reliability is low in manager and non-managerial roles
- Improved in non-manager roles
- Subordinates had the lowest mean reliability, peers in the middle, then managers the highest 
- Composite measures improved reliability
- Focus on numeric ratings

## DeNisi  & Kluger, 2000 {#slide-30}

- 360-degree improvement

- How in increase feedback effectiveness

- Focus on the performance and not on the person
