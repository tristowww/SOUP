Researchers have suggested that rater motives and the organizational context should be
considered as sources of performance appraisal inaccuracies. A review of the performance
appraisal literature revealed three primary non-performance factors that managers consider
when rating employee performance: (a) Potential negative consequences of ratings, (b)
organizational norms, and (c) the opportunity to advance self-interests. Using a policycapturing methodology, the current study investigated if these three non-performance factors,
as well as individual rater differences (e.g., conscientiousness, agreeableness, and performance appraisal experience), influence performance ratings. A sample of 303 experienced
managers rated the performance of a fictitious employee, featured in a series of hypothetical
scenarios, in which the above information was manipulated. Using hierarchical linear
modeling, the results revealed that each of the three non-performance related considerations
accounted for variance incremental to objective employee performance. Managers’ performance appraisal experience also predicted ratings, such that more experience was associated
with lower ratings. These results provide support for the view that non-performance factors can
be a substantive component of performance ratings. Copyright # 2009 John Wiley & Sons,

Introduction
The performance appraisal literature is dominated with research that has investigated rater cognitions
and the psychometric properties of appraisal formats (Bretz, Milkovich, & Read, 1992). However, for
decades researchers have argued that rater motives and the organizational context should be considered
as sources of performance appraisal inaccuracies. In recent years, several models of performance
appraisals have highlighted the important role that rater motivations and the organizational context can
have on appraisal ratings (e.g., Harris, 1994; Kane, 1994; Levy & Williams, 2004). The current study
examined the extent to which several specific motivational and contextual factors influence
supervisors’ ratings of their subordinates by using a policy-capturing methodology.

Most appraisal research has viewed rating inaccuracies as unintentional or unconscious mistakes
(e.g., Cardy & Dobbins, 1986; Schmitt & Lappin, 1980; Sinclair, 1988). Research on rater cognitions
and the psychometric properties of appraisal formats is based on the implicit assumption that raters are
trying to rate accurately and that rating inaccuracies are a product of raters not having the skills,
information, or tools necessary to rate accurately. Contrary to these perspectives, a number of researchers
have suggested that rating errors may be the product of strategic decisions made by raters (e.g., Cleveland
& Murphy, 1992; Kane, 1994; Murphy, Cleveland, Skattebo, & Kinney, 2004). Longenecker, Sims, and
Gioia (1987) interviewed executives and discovered that raters often knowingly give employees inaccurate
performance appraisal ratings after deliberate consideration of the consequences of ratings. Cleveland and
Murphy (1992) correspondingly suggested that the act of rendering a performance rating is a motivated
behavior and that managers rate in accordance with specific goals (e.g., a desire to motivate subordinates,
concern with maintaining civil working relationships, etc.).
The notion that supervisors are not entirely objective when rating their employees’ performance is
not surprising when considered in the context of the consequences of rating decisions. Formal
performance appraisal systems are used in roughly 90 per cent of organizations (Bernthal, Sumlin,
Davis, & Rogers, 1997) for administrative decisions, such as promotions and terminations, as well as
for employee development (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). As a result, performance appraisals often
directly affect employee development, career trajectories, and the allocation of money and resources.
Due to the heavy personal and occupational significance of appraisal ratings, it is understandable how
raters might have a difficult time maintaining their objectivity and neutrality when rating their
employees. For example, assigning an employee a poor performance rating, albeit accurate, might demotivate the employee, cause the employee to resent the manager, and/or hurt chances for a future
promotion. From a manager’s perspective, these perceived negative consequences might outweigh any
positive effects associated with providing an accurate rating. Accordingly, the manager might choose to
inflate the employee’s performance rating in hopes of avoiding one or all of these consequences.
A considerable amount of supposition and discussion exists as to what contextual factors raters consider
when rating and what factors motivate a rater to knowingly rate inaccurately (e.g., Larson, 1984; Villanova
& Bernardin, 1989). However, research has generally been sparse, and when it has been conducted, it has
investigated individual factors in isolation (e.g., Fisher, 1979; Klimoski & Inks, 1990). This is not
surprising given the difficulty researchers face in examining the complexity of the organizational context.
Using a policy-capturing methodology, in the current study, we simultaneously investigate the influence
of several contextual variables on performance ratings. Policy-capturing is a scenario-based research
methodology that allows researchers to determine how individuals utilize various pieces of information to
arrive at judgments (Cooksey, 1996). In the current context, the judgments rendered by participants are
performance ratings and the information presented in the scenarios describes the appraisal context. By
systematically manipulating the information presented to participants, we can examine the extent to which
various contextual factors are considered by raters when rendering a performance rating. As a result,
policy-capturing affords us the unique opportunity to determine (a) the unique effect that specific nonperformance factors have on performance ratings, while controlling for other factors, (b) whether raters
consider several of these factors when they render performance ratings, and (c) whether individuals differ
systematically in the type of information they consider when rating employee performance.

process by identifying and highlighting the unmistakable human element that is involved in the process.
Other researchers have presented similar ideas that outline the complexity and the potential partisan
nature of appraisal ratings. Whisler (1958) suggested that the utility of performance appraisals as an
accurate measure of employee performance is limited due to the hesitancy of raters to be completely
honest for fear of negative repercussions. Harris (1994) argued that in order for raters to rate accurately
they need to be motivated to do so. Predictors of rater motivations in Harris’ framework include such
things as situational factors (e.g., being accountable to a supervisor), negative consequences (e.g.,
damage to subordinate–supervisor relationship), and rewards (e.g., likelihood of pay increases or
promotions). Similarly, Kane (1994) discussed what he referred to as volitional rating behavior, or
‘‘willful’’ acts of distortion that occur at the time the rating is rendered and not when performance
information is observed, encoded, or recalled.
These ideas, although not new, are somewhat groundbreaking when considered in the context of
much of the existing performance appraisal research. This is because most of the existing performance
appraisal research has been conducted from the perspective that it is the intention of individuals to rate
performance accurately (e.g., Cardy & Dobbins, 1986; Schmitt & Lappin, 1980; Sinclair, 1988). In
contrast, what the researchers above have suggested is that raters can, and sometimes do, knowingly
alter their employees’ performance evaluations.
A multitude of reasons have been purported for why raters would manipulate performance ratings,
some of which have been corroborated by empirical evidence (c.f., Cleveland & Murphy, 1992;
Murphy et al., 2004). However, a comprehensive review of the literature revealed three main reasons
for performance rating distortion: (a) Avoidance of negative consequences (e.g., Bass, 1956; Bernardin
& Beatty, 1984; Curtis, Harvey, & Ravden, 2005; Harris, 1994; Ilgen & Knowlton, 1980; Klimoski &
Inks, 1990; Longenecker et al., 1987; Napier & Latham, 1986; Stockford & Bissell, 1949; Tziner &
Murphy, 1999; Tziner, Murphy, & Cleveland, 2005), (b) compliance with organizational norms (e.g.,
Bernardin & Beatty, 1984; Decotiis & Petit, 1978; Dipboye, 1985; Glickman, 1955; Harris, 1994;
Larson, 1984; Longenecker et al., 1987; Mohrman & Lawler, 1983; Tziner et al., 2005), and (c) pursuit
of self-interest (e.g., Bass, 1956; Harris, 1994; Ilgen, Mitchell, & Fredrickson, 1981; Villanova &
Bernardin, 1989). Based on our review, these motives emerged as the most commonly discussed.
In the next section, we review the literature related to these three motives and outline hypotheses for
the current study based on these motives. Although these motives are comprehensive in terms of
accounting for a substantial portion of the performance appraisal literature, they do not include every
reason that has been suggested for rating distortion. Various reasons have been posited that do not fit
under these three categories. For instance, some authors have highlighted that raters can intentionally
deflate ratings to send a message to an employee not performing at his or her potential, or to create a
paper trail for dismissal (e.g., Longenecker et al., 1987). However, for the current study, we restricted
our focus to the three motives that recurred with the most frequency in our literature review.

1988; Ilgen & Knowlton, 1980; Klimoski & Inks, 1990; Stockford & Bissel, 1949). In these empirical
investigations, researchers typically compare the ratings of participants who are told they have to give
feedback to participants who are told they do not have to give feedback. The raters who expect they
have to give feedback or who actually have to give feedback are found to record more lenient
performance ratings.
Similarly, rater discomfort with performance appraisals has been found to be positively associated
with rating leniency. Villanova et al. (1993) developed a performance appraisal discomfort scale and
found a positive correlation between raters’ discomfort levels and the performance ratings they
provided. The authors argued that these results are consistent with the job compatibility framework,
which purports that discomfort is produced when an employee’s preferences are in conflict with the
requirements of his or her job. These feelings of discomfort are thought to decrease involvement and
lead to withdrawal behaviors in the employee. In the case of performance appraisals, raters are thought
to withdraw from the task of providing accurate performance ratings by providing their employees with
uniformly high ratings.
The above research and theory suggests that raters may alter performance ratings as a kind of
preventive behavior. Research regarding reactions to feedback consistently indicates that negative
feedback is associated with negative reactions. For example, Brett and Atwater (2001) found that
recipients of negative feedback reacted to this feedback with anger and discouragement. In addition,
compared to positive feedback, negative feedback was not regarded as useful or as accurate in nature.
By inflating ratings, raters can neutralize or avoid potentially uncomfortable or adverse situations.
Because negative consequences are usually the result of low ratings or negative ratings (i.e., being
confronted about a low rating and having to communicate negative feedback), raters are typically
expected to inflate ratings to neutralize or avoid negative consequences. As a result we expect that
H1: Information regarding the likelihood of being confronted by the ratee will predict performance
ratings above and beyond what is accounted for by objective performance, such that a high
likelihood of confrontation will result in higher ratings compared to a low likelihood of
confrontation.

Organizational norms
Organizational norms represent a collective understanding of what is suitable behavior within an
organization (Schein, 1992). Several authors have purported that organizational norms have the ability
to alter performance ratings by informing raters of what constitutes acceptable rating behavior in their
organization. Mohrman and Lawler (1983) argued that organizational contexts have a moderating
effect on performance appraisal systems by informing raters how they should interpret the performance
appraisal process. For instance, they suggest that in a competitive organization, raters would not view
the appraisal as developmental even if the appraisal system was touted as such. This is because the
norms of the organization dictate that this would not be a reasonable expectation. Similarly, Glickman
(1955) suggested that in competitive settings, rating inflation could beget rating inflation. For example,
if some managers inflate their employees’ ratings, they are giving their employees a competitive
advantage. Therefore, in order for other managers to give their employees a fair chance, other managers
would have to do the same.

Other researchers have similarly stated that there is a relationship between organizational
norms for rating accuracy and rating errors. Harris (1994) argued that situational variables,
such as organizational norms, influence raters’ motivations to rate accurately. He suggested that if
there are norms for raters to provide lenient performance ratings, this will encourage raters to be
lenient. Similarly, Longenecker et al. (1987) found that raters were more likely to distort
performance ratings if they perceived that top management did not take the appraisal process
seriously. In interviews, managers stated that if others do not take the performance appraisal process
seriously, then they would be likely to adopt a similar mindset and not strive to produce accurate
ratings.
A similar concept, referred to as trust in the performance appraisal process, was presented and
discussed by Bernardin and Beatty (1984). The authors defined trust in the appraisal process as raters’
perceptions about the extent to which other raters in their organization rate accurately. They found that
the less trust raters had in the performance appraisal process (i.e., the more they perceived that other
raters did not rate accurately), the more lenient were the ratings that they provided.
These findings, in combination with theory and speculation, provide some guidance for predicting
the relative importance of organizational norms for rating accuracy in determining performance
ratings. Although organizational norms are organization-level phenomena, within the current paper we
are interested in individual level perceptions of these norms. In line with the aforementioned ideas and
empirical findings, we predict that raters will be influenced by their perceptions of organizational
norms when conducting performance evaluations to the extent that they will rate in accordance with
organizational norms for rating leniency.
H2: Information regarding organizational norms about rating inflation will predict performance
ratings above and beyond objective performance information, such that a norm for rating inflation
will result in higher performance ratings compared to when there is no norm for inflation.

Self-interest
Several authors in the performance appraisal literature have speculated that raters will distort
performance ratings in order to make themselves look more competent or to procure desirable
resources, such as bonuses (e.g., Bass, 1956; Villanova & Bernardin, 1989). Although this reason for
rating inaccurately has received little empirical attention, some theory exists and a few studies have
investigated the role rater self-interest plays in performance appraisal inaccuracies. Most typically it
has been suggested that raters will inflate their subordinates’ ratings to procure resources or to ingratiate
themselves to their superiors. For instance, Ilgen et al. (1981) found that when raters believed their
rewards were contingent on the performance of the person they were rating, raters gave more positive
ratings. Similarly, several researchers have speculated that raters use their employees’ performance
ratings to self-enhance. Villanova and Bernardin (1989) suggested that raters can inflate the ratings of
subordinates to enhance the image of the workgroup, to take credit for group performance, and to
increase employees’ perceptions of their organizational prestige. Similarly, Harris (1994) purported
that raters’ motivations will be partly determined by their desire to manage impressions. Raters are
likely to inflate ratings to the extent that they believe higher performing subordinates will make them
look more competent or help them obtain more money or resources.
These ideas, along with the existing research on rater self-interest, suggest that raters can use the
rating context as a platform to forward their own interests and that this can be an important factor in
determining rating inaccuracies. Consequently, we predict that

Personality
In addition to contextual variables, researchers have also suggested that rater dispositions can influence
how they evaluate employee performance. From this perspective, Kane, Bernardin, Villanova, and
Peyrefitte (1995) argued that aspects of rating inaccuracy can be driven by stable rater tendencies and
that personality traits may be effective in explaining these tendencies. Two traits that have been
examined in relation to performance ratings are conscientiousness and agreeableness (e.g., Bernardin,
Cooke, & Villanova, 2000; Tziner, Murhpy, & Cleveland, 2002; Yun, Donahue, Dudley, & McFarland,
2005), two dimensions of the Five Factor Model (FFM) of personality (McCrae & John, 1992).
Bernardin et al. (2000) investigated the relationship between the FFM and rating elevation. They
reasoned that conscientiousness and agreeableness should be most relevant to rating elevation.
Individuals high in conscientiousness are hard working, thorough individuals with high standards.
Thus, they predicted, and found, that raters high in conscientiousness were less prone to rating elevation. In
contrast, those high in agreeableness are sympathetic, cooperative, and desire social approval. Thus,
they predicted, and found, that raters high in agreeableness tend to produce elevated ratings.
Bernardin et al. (2000) did not hypothesize any relationships between rating elevation and the
remaining three personality traits, openness to experience, extraversion and neuroticism. However,
they did measure these characteristics and found no significant relationships between these traits and
the magnitude of ratings. This is not surprising when one considers that there are no components of the
performance appraisal process that appear particularly relevant to these characteristics. Researchers in
personality suggest that specific dimensions of personality are only expected to predict task
performance when the task contains elements that are relevant to the characteristics (Tett & Burnett,
2003). Since we could find no theoretical rationale or empirical evidence for the effect of rater openness
to experience, neuroticism, or extraversion on performance ratings, we did not include these
dimensions in the current study.1

The personality dimension of conscientiousness has been associated with a number of work-related
outcomes. Most notable is its positive relationship with overall job performance (Barrick & Mount,
1991). Furthermore, the positive association between conscientiousness and job performance has been
found to hold across a range of job types and positions (Barrick, Mount, & Judge, 2001). These findings
are not surprising given that conscientious individuals are purported to be diligent, performanceoriented, responsible, and thorough (McCrae & John, 1992), all of which are qualities that translate
well into organizational life. Additionally, conscientiousness is positively associated with both goalsetting behavior and commitment to goals that have been set (Barrick, Mount, & Strauss, 1993;
Gellatly, 1996).

to be negatively related to rating elevation (Bernardin et al., 2000). In a similar vein, Watson, Clark, and

Agreeableness
Agreeableness, while not as robust a predictor of job performance as conscientiousness, is significantly
related to job performance and interpersonal skills. Mount, Barrick, and Stewart (1998) found that
agreeableness was the most significant predictor of performance in jobs that involved interactions with
others and working in teams. These results coincide with the conception of individuals who score high
on agreeableness as being generous, kind, sympathetic, and trusting (McCrae & John, 1992).
The interpersonally focused qualities of agreeable individuals have been found to translate to the
performance appraisal context. Kane et al. (1995) suggested that individuals high in agreeableness may
produce more lenient ratings in particular situations, such as when raters anticipate future interaction
with the ratees or when the raters are solely responsible for the ratings. Both of these conditions are
implied in the scenarios utilized in the present study. Similarly, Yun et al. (2005) found that when
participants expected to have a face-to-face feedback meeting, highly agreeable raters rendered higher
performance ratings than those lower on agreeableness. Finally, as mentioned earlier, Bernardin et al.
(2000) found that raters high in agreeableness produced more elevated ratings than those low in
agreeableness. It makes sense that a rater who is generous, kind, and sympathetic is likely to be lenient
in his/her evaluations of employees, which will produce higher ratings than raters who are less
generous, kind, and sympathetic. Thus, for the current study it is hypothesized that
H5: Across scenarios, higher levels of agreeableness will be associated with higher performance ratings.

Appraisal experience
Although research has found that ratees’ work experience is associated with the performance ratings
they receive (e.g., Harris, Smith, & Champagne, 1995), to our knowledge, no study has directly
examined the effect of rater appraisal experience on performance ratings. However, rater experience
seems like a potentially important individual difference variable to examine when considering the
effects of non-performance variables on performance ratings. Related to the notion of experience is
expertise. A large body of literature has examined the performance differences between experts and
novices on complex tasks (e.g., Chase & Ericsson, 1981; Chase & Simon, 1973; Chi, Feltovich, &
Glaser, 1981). Across a variety of task domains, research has shown that individuals with more
expertise have a larger knowledge base in their area of expertise compared to novices. More
importantly, this knowledge base is organized into meaningful chunks or schemas, such that larger
units of information are constructed from meaningful relations among smaller units (Chase & Simon,
1973). This means that experts are better able to map new stimuli from a relevant domain onto existing
knowledge structures in that same domain.

experienced raters will have richer knowledge structures regarding performance appraisal. Thus, when

The current study
The current study utilized a policy-capturing methodology to determine the extent to which participants
consider factors other than objective performance when conducting a performance evaluation. In
policy-capturing, participants are presented with a series of written scenarios, in which independent
variables, referred to as cues, are systematically manipulated. Participants are asked to use the
information presented to them to formulate a rating judgment. For the current study, participants were
asked to conduct a performance evaluation after being presented with information about a fictitious
organization and a fictitious employee. Four variables were presented in the scenarios. Objective
performance of the fictitious employee acted as a control variable to see if the contextual considerations
predicted above and beyond objective performance. The three independent variables consisted of the
(a) likelihood that performance ratings would result in negative consequences for the rater, (b)
organizational norms of the fictitious organization, and (c) opportunity for participants to gain favor as
a result of the performance ratings they provide. Data were collected at two levels of analysis: Within
and between-person. Each participant responded to multiple scenarios, creating the within-person
level, and also completed individual difference measures, which comprised the between-person level.
The current study contributes to the extant literature on performance appraisals in several ways. First, by
presenting participants with information designed to represent several contextual considerations, the
current study will be able to determine the extent to which performance ratings are influenced by each of
these considerations. Second, because the information was presented to participants simultaneously, the
current study will reveal if raters balance several different considerations when rating performance.
Lastly, the current study will further our understanding of how individual difference variables influence
how raters evaluate employees. A policy-capturing methodology provides a novel way to study if, and
how, several contextual factors influence raters’ rating decisions.

Participants
The sample included 303 university alumni who had experience administering employee performance
appraisals. Of the participants, 114 were female, 189 were male, and one chose not report his or her
gender. The average age of participants was 41.65 years (SD ¼ 9.99).
Participants came from a wide range of industries (e.g., IT, education, healthcare, manufacturing,
etc.). On average, participants had 17.89 years of full-time work experience (SD ¼ 9.72), had been
conducting performance appraisals for 8.95 years (SD ¼ 7.5) and averaged 6.7 appraisals a year
(SD ¼ 6.15).

Procedure
Participants self-selected to complete the study after being sent an email from the alumni office that
included a short description of the study, a link to the study, and the requirements to participate. To
participate, individuals had to conduct performance appraisals as part of their current job or had to have
conducted performance appraisals as a requirement of a previous job. For their participation,
individuals were entered into a draw to win one of two $50 gift certificates to their choice of two
retailers. An information letter that contained further information about the study and a link to the
informed consent form and the survey was sent to all participants via email. The email stated that the
purpose of the study was to gain a better understanding of the decision making processes that managers
undergo when they rate their employees’ performance. Participants were asked to complete an online
questionnaire that contained a series of scenarios and to answer some opinion questions about
themselves. Participants were told that the scenarios contained information about a fictitious employee
and that they would be asked to rate the performance of this employee.
Once informed consent was obtained, the study began and scenarios were presented via computer,
one scenario at a time, with participants providing their performance rating for each scenario.
Following the scenarios, the individual difference measures were presented.

influential in the presence of clear objective performance information. To avoid unnecessary variance
in the scenarios, the name and gender of the employee featured in the scenarios was held constant
across all scenarios. The name ‘‘James’’ was chosen to represent the employee in the scenarios because

Care was taken to ensure that all of the cues in each scenario were of approximately equal length.
This was done to minimize the likelihood that cue length would contribute to systematic error variance

took roughly the same amount of time to read, which prevented shorter cues from getting priority over
longer cues because they are easier and faster to read. Also, having cues of equal length prevented
participants from drawing any specious conclusions about the importance of certain cues based on their
length (e.g., longer cues contain more information and, therefore, are more important).
Objective performance cue
The objective performance cue was created to provide participants with explicit information about how
well James had performed. By including information about James’ in-role performance, there was the
opportunity to assess whether and the extent to which contextual information influences participants’
performance ratings above and beyond that of actual employee performance. James’ performance was
manipulated by overtly stating that James was either a below average, average, or above average
performer. For example, a high level of performance was indicated by stating that ‘‘Over the past year,
James has been an above average performer.’’ This cue was designed to have three levels with roughly
equal intervals, and to be as explicit as possible. Consequently, an absolute statement about
performance such as ‘‘James is a good performer’’ was not provided; only a relative statement about
performance was used. This was done in order to minimize the extent to which the cue could be
interpreted (i.e., some participants could have different conceptions of what a ‘‘good’’ or ‘‘very good’’
performer is) and to ensure that the cue was evenly distributed. This cue was analyzed as a continuous
variable and was coded on a scale from 1 to 3 in the dataset (1 ¼ below average performer, 2 ¼ average
performer, and 3 ¼ above average performer).
Avoidance cue
The avoidance cue was designed to set up a situation whereby participants might have to face a specific
negative consequence as a result of the performance ratings they provide. The negative consequence
that was selected was having to handle an employee who was disgruntled as a result of his performance
rating. The likelihood of confrontation was manipulated by stating either that ‘‘There is a strong
chance that James will confront you about a low performance rating’’ or ‘‘It is highly unlikely that
James will confront you about a low performance rating.’’ The highly unlikely statement was designed
to be the zero level for the cue. Consequently, this cue was conceptualized and analyzed as a categorical
variable with two levels and was dummy coded: 1 ¼ Presence of negative consequences (i.e., you will
be confronted), 0 ¼ absence of negative consequences (i.e., you will not be confronted).
Organizational norms cue
An organizational norm cue was created to provide participants with information about the
organizational context relating to the performance appraisal process. The cue was designed to
specifically target norms that were relevant to the performance appraisal process. The extent to which
other managers in James’ organization inflated their employees’ performance ratings was the targeted
norm. This was manipulated by stating whether or not other managers in the organization were known
to inflate their employees’ ratings. A norm for inflation was indicated by stating that ‘‘Other managers
in your organization are known to inflate their employees’ performance ratings.’’ This cue was
conceptualized as a categorical variable with three levels. It was dummy coded such that the no
information level was used as a reference group: ‘‘Known to inflate ratings’’ was compared with ‘‘no
information’’ and ‘‘known to be accurate when rating’’ was compared with ‘‘no information’’.

Self-interest cue
The self-interest cue was designed to manipulate the possibility that participants could use the
performance ratings they gave to make themselves look better. This possibility was manipulated by
either stating that participants’ performance evaluations were dependent on James’ ratings or not. For
example, the cue level that was used to indicate that participants’ evaluations were dependent on
James’ performance stated ‘‘A determinant of your performance evaluation is how well your
employees perform on their evaluations.’’ This cue was designed to set up a circumstance whereby
participants would have the opportunity to get better performance ratings if James had a higher
performance rating. The other level of the cue stated that ‘‘How well your employees perform on their
evaluations is not a determinant of your performance evaluation.’’ This level was designed to be the
zero level for self-interest. Under this circumstance, participants did not have the opportunity to
augment their own situation by providing James with certain performance ratings. This cue was
conceptualized and analyzed as a categorical cue with two levels. In the analysis, it was dummy coded
such that the referent group was when participants’ performance evaluations were not dependent on
how well James performed (evaluations are dependent on James’ performance ¼ 1, evaluations are
independent of James’ performance ¼ 0).

Cue presentation
All participants were presented with the same 36 scenarios, which were created by combining the
different cue levels with each other (i.e., three levels of the performance cue, three levels of the
avoidance cue, two levels of the organizational norms cue, and two levels of the self-interest cue).
Additionally, three scenarios were randomly selected from the total set and duplicated for reliability
analysis, resulting in a total of 39 scenarios. To create the questionnaire, the order of the 39 scenarios, as
well as the presentation of the cues within each scenario, was randomized. This resulted in one
questionnaire format that was presented to all participants.

Dependent variable
After reading the cues, participants were asked to rate the overall job performance of James. This was
collected using a single seven-point Likert scale item. The item ranged from 1 ¼ poor to 7 ¼ excellent.

Individual difference variables
Conscientiousness
Participants’ conscientiousness was measured using the Big Five Inventory (BFI; John, Donahue, &

thorough job.’’ The scale consisted of nine items, all of which had a five-point Likert scale ranging from
1 ¼ very uncharacteristic of myself to 5 ¼ very characteristic of myself (a ¼ .82).
Agreeableness
Participants’ agreeableness was measured using the BFI (John et al., 1991). A sample item from the
agreeableness scale is ‘‘I see myself as someone who has a forgiving nature.’’ The scale consisted of
nine items, all of which used a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 ¼ very uncharacteristic of myself
to 5 ¼ very characteristic of myself (a ¼ .77).

Analyses
We analyzed the data using hierarchical linear modeling 6.0 (HLM; Raudenbush, Bryk, Cheong, &
Congdon, 2004). HLM was chosen to analyze the data because it is a data analytic technique that can
effectively account for both within and between person variance. As such, the data were analyzed at two
levels: Within-person (Level 1) and between-person (Level 2). For the Level 1 analysis, performance
ratings were regressed onto the four cues for each participant. Subsequently, the coefficient estimates
for each of the cues and the intercepts were pooled together to formulate population estimates of the
intercept and slopes for each of the cues. Prior to the analysis, the Level 1 predictors were centered by
subtracting each individual’s mean. This was done to remove the effect of between-person variance on
the Level 1 estimates, ensuring that only within-person variance is accounted for at Level 1 (Hofmann,
Griffin, & Gavin, 2000).
For the Level 2 analysis, the intercept and the slope coefficients that were estimated in the Level 1
model were regressed onto the individual difference variables. This analysis enabled us to determine if
between-person variance in the intercept and slope coefficients could be predicted by individual
difference variables. All Level 2 predictors (between-person predictors) were grand mean centered to
aid in the interpretation of the coefficients.

Discussion
A review of the literature on non-cognitive sources of performance appraisal error suggests that raters
purposefully distort performance ratings for a number of reasons. The goal of the current study was to
isolate specific determinants of rating distortion and examine their unique and combined impact on
performance ratings. The results revealed that participants’ ratings decisions were determined by
several contextual factors in addition to objective performance. Specifically, each non-performance cue
included in the current study contributed to performance ratings above and beyond what was accounted
for by objective performance.

Contextual cues
Overall, participants were consistent and reliable in how they used the cues to formulate their rating
decisions. We found that the avoidance, organizational norms, and self-interest cues each predicted
participants’ performance ratings above and beyond the objective performance cue. The within-person
(Level 1) analysis indicated that, as predicted, performance ratings were consistent with organizational
norms (i.e., when the organizational norms were to provide high ratings, participants provided higher
ratings and when they were for accurate ratings, participants provided lower ratings) and that when
rating inflation was in the self-interest of participants, performance ratings tended to be higher.
However, the results for the avoidance cue indicate that the direction of the relationship was opposite to
what was hypothesized. Participants were expected to provide higher performance ratings when they
were informed it would be likely that James would confront them about a low rating. However, the
results indicated that participants provided lower performance ratings when they thought they would be
confronted.
The pattern of results associated with the avoidance cue may be due to a liking mechanism. That is, it
is plausible that participants were more inclined to dislike James when he was described as likely to
confront them. This disliking could have resulted in the tendency to provide a confrontational James
with lower ratings. Supporting this notion, there is ample evidence in the performance appraisal
literature to suggest that raters’ liking of ratees can influence performance ratings. For instance, Tsui
and Barry (1986) found that raters’ liking for ratees was positively associated with performance ratings,
such that the more a rater liked the ratee, the higher the performance rating. This positive association
was found to be consistent across peer, supervisor, and subordinate ratings. Similarly, Decotiis and Petit
(1978) have suggested that rater affect towards ratees can influence the level of ratings, whereby
positive relations lead to more lenient ratings.

Individual differences
Personality
Neither conscientiousness nor agreeableness was found to predict performance ratings. These findings
are contrary to previous research, which suggests that rating behaviors are, to a certain degree,
the product of relatively stable and reliable dispositional traits (e.g., Kane et al., 1995). The absence of
personality effects in the current study may be the result of the strong situation faced by participants.
Personality theorists have long suggested that the effects of personality on behavior can be moderated
by the strength of the situation (e.g., Mischel, 1977; Stagner, 1977; Weiss & Adler, 1984). Specifically,
Mischel (1977) proposed that situations that lack ambiguity, labeled strong situations, can reduce the

Appraisal experience
Our analyses revealed that individuals with more years of performance appraisal experience provided
lower performance ratings than those with less performance appraisal experience. This finding aligns
well with the research on expertise which has shown that experts possess richer domain-specific
knowledge structures and process information at a deeper level compared to novices (Chase &
Ericsson, 1981; Chi et al., 1981). Thus, experienced raters may have been less affected by information
providing pressure to rate more leniently. Of course, our study did not directly test the notion that raters
with more performance appraisal experience had richer knowledge structures. Future research might
more directly apply the research on expertise to more comprehensively assess the effects of appraisal
experience. While rater experience is not a popular variable in the performance appraisal literature,
further investigation into the effect of performance appraisal experience on performance ratings, and
performance appraisal behaviors in general, may be fruitful.
The finding that performance appraisal experience alters ratings, in combination with the discovery
that the non-performance cues predicted ratings, suggests that rating behaviors, in particular, rating
distortion, are likely the product of a complex interaction between the organizational context, the
performance appraisal context, and raters’ individual differences. Future research should study the
associations across these levels to gain a more holistic and accurate understanding of what contributes
to rating inaccuracies. For instance, researchers interested in studying contextual and dispositional
variables should not limit the scope of their investigations to these specific levels only, but rather they
should seek to integrate variables from other domains, such as rater cognitions, into their investigations.

Strengths and limitations
An experimental policy-capturing methodology was chosen as the most appropriate for the current
study. Policy-capturing was chosen because it allowed for the performance appraisal context to be
studied as a decision making process whereby several independent variables could be examined
simultaneously. This was the first study, to our knowledge, to examine several purported contextual
influences of performance ratings simultaneously. The policy-capturing methodology allowed for
within and between-person analysis with sound internal validity and reasonable external validity
(Cooksey, 1996). Our analysis of within and between-effects made our study cross-level in nature.
However, future work could examine organizational norms as a higher-level variable instead of a
within-person variable as we treated it. Collecting data from multiple raters who are employed in
several organizations could accomplish this. In such a design, the organization variable could be
included as a between-person variable to examine if raters within the same organization have similar
rating tendencies and if there is a variability in rating styles across organizations.

unambiguous manner. In reality, this type of contextual information is likely to be ambiguous and,
consequently, may not affect rating decisions in the same manner they did in the current study.
Moreover, due to practical design limitations, we could not include every consideration that a rater is
faced with during a performance evaluation. The restricted quantity of information we could present to
raters contributed to the simulated nature of the scenarios. In addition, an inherent limitation of
experimental research is that there are no actual consequences of decisions as there would be in real
life. This limitation may have influenced the results of our study. For example, the finding that
participants assigned a lower rating when given a cue that their subordinate would likely confront them
with a low rating was both counter to our hypothesis and counter to what one might expect in an actual
appraisal. A potential explanation for our finding is that, due to the contrived nature of our study,
participants reported that they would punish a confrontational employee by providing a lower rating
because that is what they would like to do in this situation, rather than what they would actually do.
Despite the acknowledged limitation of external validity, a number of studies have demonstrated
evidence for the external validity of policy-capturing studies (e.g., Brown, 1972; Kirwan, Chaput de
Saintonge, Joyce, & Currey, 1983). We took several steps to increase the external validity of the current
study. For instance, all participants were real-world performance evaluators who conducted
performance appraisals as part of their job, all the cues were carefully chosen from the performance
appraisal literature to be representative of the performance appraisal context, and all cues were
designed to be realistic in their levels (Aiman-Smith et al., 2002).
In order to recruit a sample of participants with performance appraisal experience, we used a
convenience sample (i.e., university alumni). Conventional thinking would suggest that this would
limit our ability to generalize our results. However, given recent arguments presented by Highhouse
and Gillespie (2008), we do not feel that this is the case for our study. Specifically, it is unlikely that our
sample is dissimilar to the larger population of individuals who conduct performance appraisals as
there are no distinct characteristics of our sample that we would expect to interact with our variables of
interest. Moreover, participants in our sample all conducted performance appraisals as part of their
current and/or past positions.

Contributions and future directions
The current study contributes to the performance appraisal literature by demonstrating the extent
to which raters consider several specific contextual factors when rating employee performance. The
results demonstrate that even when an employee’s performance is presented in a clear and objective manner, raters still consider other factors and incorporate their considerations into the performance
ratings they provide. Although the notion that raters are not entirely objective when rating employees’
performance is not a new one, there have been no empirical investigations which have simultaneously
studied the extent to which several contextual factors can influence the accuracy of performance
appraisals. Much of the research that has been conducted has focused on unintentional cognitive lapses
or biases (e.g., Cardy & Dobbins, 1986; Schmitt & Lappin, 1980; Sinclair, 1988). However, as the
current study demonstrates, a number of other factors that are not directly linked to employee
performance or raters’ cognitions can significantly influence the level of performance ratings.
By examining the performance appraisal process as a decision making process rather than a purely
evaluative exercise, the current study effectively presented an alternative to the conventional
framework through which employee performance appraisals are typically studied. In doing so, we
hoped to expand the scope of variables that are typically considered influential in determining
performance ratings. We examined several contextual and dispositional variables simultaneously, and

to consider variables other than accuracy as the goal of raters. The larger performance appraisal
literature needs to expand its conceptualization of performance appraisals and accept that accuracy
may not always be the goal of raters. Recently, Murphy et al. (2004) found that different raters pursue
different goals when rating performance and that these goals are stable across time. Studies such as
these will help researchers conceptualize the rating process as more than an evaluative exercise. By
doing so, it is likely that we will get a more representative and, therefore, more accurate understanding
of what happens during the rating process.
An increased understanding of the performance appraisal process will enable practitioners to
improve the practice of performance appraisals. For example, performance appraisal interventions
could be created that attend to raters’ consideration of non-performance factors. Current performance
appraisal interventions, such as frame-of-reference training (e.g., Sulsky & Day, 1992) tend to be
designed to attend to cognitive errors. To our knowledge, there are no interventions that attend to
intentional distortions. Such interventions could be designed to address the unique influence that
specific contextual variables have on rating errors. Addressing the unique influence that different
pressures can have (i.e., normative pressures vs. self-interest) will help ensure that training programs
are in line with the complexity and nuance of the environment that raters face.
In order to create such interventions, researchers must first go back to the rating influence,
empirically establish the strength and direction of these influences, and then design interventions based
on the empirical findings. At the same time, organizations and raters may be resistant to these kinds of
interventions, as certain rating distortions may be perceived as advantageous. For instance, rating with
a goal other than accuracy can serve to maintain group harmony, increase employee morale, and/or
increase the likelihood of personal benefit. As the current study demonstrates, there are a number of
reasons for rating distortion and some of these reasons go beyond the immediate rating context (e.g.,
organizational norms and previous appraisal experience). Consequently, conceptualizing performance
appraisals as an issue broader than the act of rendering a performance rating would be a positive step
because it would help present raters’ behaviors as part of an integrated organizational process involving
complex human interactions.
