ABSTRACT

KEYWORDS

Employee perceptions of the fairness of performance
evaluations are critical to the success of any appraisal system.
Research on performance management includes extensive
studies on how bias emerges in the appraisal process.
Despite this, there is no empirical evidence linking formal
discrimination complaint filings – a key measure of bias - and
performance appraisals. To close this gap, we conduct an
empirical analysis using information on appraisal systems and
discrimination complaints from the US federal government.
Our findings suggest that agencies with better-designed
appraisal systems will experience higher appraisal-related
discrimination complaints, contrary to expectations. In
particular, an expansion of training and voice opportunities
are associated with an increase in appraisal discrimination
complaints, while increased differentiation between
performers is not consistently associated with complaints. For
managers struggling with performance management, it may
be better to spend time on improving performance-focused
interpersonal communication rather than redesigning
appraisal systems.

Performance appraisal;
discrimination; forced
distribution rating systems

Employee performance appraisal systems serve as a key tool for holding individual
employees accountable in the public, private, and nonprofit sectors. Appraisals
often serve multiple purposes: to provide documentation and justification for
future personnel actions, to motivate employees, to identify areas where professional development is needed, and to communicate organizational priorities, to
name just a few (Daley, 2004; Levy & Williams, 2004; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995).
These multiple purposes often clash, leading to interpersonal conflict.
While the private sector is more actively questioning the utility of employee
performance appraisal systems, most government personnel systems require some

sort of annual performance rating. Civil service reforms undertaken by the US
states over the last two decades are largely driven by concerns that government
needs stronger tools for holding employees accountable (Battaglio & Condrey,
2009; Kellough & Selden, 2003). Many of these reforms focus on making it easier
to fire public employees and strengthening the consequences for strong or weak
performance. There has also been increasing attention on performance appraisal in
the US federal government. Over the last fifteen years, the U.S. Office of Personnel
Management (OPM) developed standards for assessing the strength of performance appraisal systems (2006). More recently, OPM announced the Performance
Management Plus initiative to focus more attention on the relationship between
managers and employees, emphasizing on-going feedback and coaching (US
Office of Personnel Management 2016).
Scholars do not fully agree on how to measure the effectiveness of performance
appraisal systems. Potential options include the accuracy of ratings and a host
of behavioral, attitudinal, and cognitive reactions to ratings, including fairness
perceptions (Levy & Williams, 2004; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). Among the
potential measures available, discrimination complaints alleging biased treatment
in the performance appraisal process can serve as one of many behavioral signals
of fairness perceptions. Discrimination complaints in the appraisal process can
suggest any myriad of problems, including inaccurate or biased ratings, inconsistent application of performance standards, and/or interpersonal conflict between
the manager and the employee. Based on this, the research presented in this
article asks the following question: what is the relationship between performance
appraisal structure and the rate at which employees claim discrimination in the
appraisal process?
To understand the link between appraisal structure and appraisal discrimination complaints, this analysis combines two sets of data from US federal agencies.
Information on agency appraisal structure comes from scores assigned by OPM
to agency appraisal systems that were graded under the Performance Assessment
and Accountability Tool (PAAT) framework. The PAAT grades are combined with
data on agency discrimination complaints, which are reported publically on an
annual basis under the requirements of the Notification and Federal Employee
Antidiscrimination and Retaliation Act of 2002 (No FEAR Act).
The article begins with a review of the key themes in the research linking performance appraisal and bias. We next explore the literature on specific aspects of
performance appraisal system design that might be linked to perceptions of bias.
Following the presentation of hypotheses, our data are described in more detail.
The study will then turn to our empirical analysis. We conclude by reflecting on
the implications of our findings for current research, and outline steps for future
studies.

Literature review
A claim of discrimination in the appraisal process is a purposive behavior resulting
from a negative perception of the appraisal, and signals an employee believes her
appraisal was negatively impacted because of her gender, race, age, disability status,
etc. According to the psychology literature, this perception of harm might arise
from a rating viewed as inaccurate and/or biased. Both may lead an employee to
claim discrimination, and scholarship provides detailed studies on the different
manifestations of each.
The perceived accuracy of an appraisal system is based on the evaluation process used by managers to recall and document performance information (Landy,
Barnes, & Murphy, 1978; Landy, Barnes-Farrell, & Cleveland, 1980). This process
includes the supervisor’s ‘frequency of evaluation, identification of goals, and the
knowledge of their employees’ performance and job duties’ (Landy et al., 1980, p.
355). To improve accuracy in documenting performance scholars have identified
multiple strategies managers can use, including writing notes about performance
on a regular basis and storing them in an organized way so they can easily be
found for the writing of the appraisal (Feldman, 1981).
Frequent evaluations and interactions with employees will also ensure that both
the supervisor and employee are aware of each other’s goals, which can affect the
appraisal score as well (Wang, Wong, & Kwong, 2010).
A second key reason an employee may file a discrimination claim relating to
her performance appraisal is because she believes the rating is inaccurate due to
biased treatment by the supervisor. There is a long history of research on the ways
in which rater bias distorts ratings based on the employee’s race/ethnicity, gender, age, and other factors (Cox & Nkomo, 1986; Heilman & Eagly, 2008; Leslie,
King, Bradley, & Hebl, 2008). The stereotype-fit model suggests bias enters into
the appraisal rating, or other personnel decisions, when raters attribute stereotypes to an individual ratee (Dipboye, 1985). For example, if a rater attributes an
executive to being a white male, then she will most likely promote a white male
to that position, even though an ethnic minority is more qualified. Raters evaluate ratees to the extent that their perceptions of the ratee fit the job stereotype,
resulting in discrimination (Dipboye, 1985). Landy (2008) moves Dipboye’s (1985)
stereotype-fit model further and notes many of these stereotypes are negative,
particularly when applied to women and minorities. Furthermore, the subjective
nature of many personnel decisions, including appraisals, enables bias to emerge.
Additionally, organizations oftentimes lack safeguards that prevent biased thinking (Landy, 2008).
There are a number of things organizations can do to design their appraisal
systems to increase fairness perceptions by reducing opportunities for inaccuracy or bias. These are discussed in detail below, followed by hypotheses for this
study. For example, setting clear expectations for performance allows employees
to know what behaviors are most valued and how they should work to achieve

them. Involving employees throughout the appraisal process can serve to increase
the level of trust that employee have in the final ratings. Training managers and
employees in the requirements of the appraisal process, and educating both on the
norms and expectations underlying the system, can help to reduce conflict and
ensure standards are applied consistently. Finally, recognizing differences in levels
of performance among employees acknowledges the success of strong performers.
However, when taken to the extreme, differentiations between performers may
reflect bias rather than actual differences in contributions.
Setting clear expectations

Clear performance standards are linked to greater perceptions of appraisal system
fairness (Dickinson, 1993; Erdogan, Kraimer, & Liden, 2001; Folger, Konovsky, &
Cropanzano, 1992). Clarity is enhanced when employees can see how their work
contributes to the accomplishment of organizational goals. Repeated studies in
motivation theory consistently show that clear goals exert greater motivational
effects than vague ‘do your best’ goals (Latham, 2012). Setting clear goals in the
public sector can be particularly challenging, but also particularly important,
because goal ambiguity has been tied to decreased organizational productivity
(Chun & Rainey, 2005; Pandy & Wright, 2006; Tubre & Collins, 2000).
One way agencies can set clearer performance goals is by creating a ‘line of
sight,’ explaining to employees how their performance is related to program performance. Similar to setting clear goals, alignment of employee effort to organizational performance helps employees to prioritize work and seek out relevant
knowledge needed to achieve the goals (Hitt, Bierman, Shimizu, & Kochhar, 2001;
Locke & Latham, 2002). Goal alignment occurs when the objectives and purposes of individuals are aligned with the objectives and purposes of the organization (Ayers, 2013; Rubin, 2015). As alignment between employee appraisal
and organizational goals increases, employees indicate higher satisfaction with
appraisal systems (Decramer, Smolders, & van der Straeten, 2013). This leads to
the following hypothesis:
H1: As the clarity of performance expectations increases, the rate of appraisal discrimination complaints will decrease.

Employee participation

Employee participation in appraisal has long been acknowledged as important:
‘Employee participation in appraisal is one of the most widely researched factors … Research suggests that allowing employees to participate in the appraisal
process is associated with positive employee reactions to the appraisal system’
(Cawley, Keeping, & Levy, 1998, p. 616). Participation is useful because it gives
employees a better understanding of what aspects of performance will be rated,

the standards expected, and increases buy-in to both the content and the rating
(Silverman & Wexley, 1984).
Importantly, participation can occur at multiple points in the appraisal process.
Depending on the study, participation has been operationalized as participation in
system design, goal setting, self-assessment and/or providing examples of performance to the rater, participation in feedback conversations, or exerting influence
over the final rating (Cawley et al., 1998; Greenberg, 1986; Roberts, 2003; Roberts
& Reed, 1996). Managers who involve employees in setting goals and expectations
find that employees understand expectations better, are more confident that they
can achieve those expectations, and perform at a higher level (Roberts & Reed,
1996). When considered jointly, employee participation in setting goals, discussing actual performance, and presenting work examples to managers to inform
appraisal ratings are all related to procedural justice perceptions of the appraisal
process (Greenberg, 1986; Roberts & Reed, 1996). Combined, the research suggests the following hypothesis:
H2: As opportunities for participation in the appraisal process increase, the rate of
appraisal discrimination complaints will decrease.

Training

Training on performance appraisal systems should be one piece of developing
managerial competencies. Appraisal training provided to raters has received more
attention than appraisal training provided to employees. In particular, appraisal
training provided to raters generally focuses on improving rating accuracy. A
recent meta-analysis finds that rater training improves appraisal accuracy more
so than changes to the appraisal instrument, and the most frequently studied
rater training technique is frame-of-reference training (Roch, Woehr, Mishra, &
Kieszczynska, 2012). Frame-of-reference training uses examples, practice, and
feedback to help raters develop shared understandings of performance standards
(Arvey & Murphy, 1998; Roch et al., 2012). The training ensures managers will
rate similar levels of performance consistently across individual ratees, which has
been shown to be a key component of appraisal procedural justice perceptions
(Greenberg, 1986). In addition to improving accuracy, training managers in performance appraisal increases satisfaction with the appraisal system, resolution
of workplace conflict, and decreases rating distortions (Taylor, Tracy, Renard,
Harrison, & Carroll, 1995).
The few studies that examine appraisal training provided to employees describe
training specifically as contributing to perceptions of the fairness of the appraisal.
These studies argue that training employees helps them to understand how the
system works, understand performance standards, and understand what performance information may be relevant to provide to raters to improve accuracy of
the rating (Folger et al., 1992). Knowledge of performance appraisal criteria does
increase fairness perceptions of the appraisal, including overall appraisal system

fairness, satisfaction with the appraisal system, and satisfaction with the rating
(Erdogan et al., 2001; Taylor et al., 1995). Combined, the research on appraisal
training for managers and employees suggests:
H3: As training on the performance appraisal systems made available to employees
and managers increases, the rate of appraisal discrimination complaints will decrease.

Differentiation between performers

If an organization intends to use its appraisal systems to inform administrative
decisions such as pay raises, promotions, or discipline, it is important that the
assigned ratings allow managers to differentiate between levels of performance.
Unfortunately, decades of empirical evidence indicate that when ratings are used
to inform administrative decisions with significant positive or negative consequences, raters tend to inflate ratings, or issue inaccurate ratings more generally
(Jawahar & Williams, 1997; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). To address this, organizations may choose to implement appraisal system policies to encourage differentiation in both the ratings assigned and the consequences of the ratings. Such
policies are grounded in expectancy and equity theories. Equity theory suggests
individuals will view appraisal decisions as more fair when they receive more
positive consequences for high performance (Blume, Baldwin, & Rubin, 2009;
Latham, 2012). Similarly, expectancy theory suggests that individuals compare
their own performance to their peers. When an individual perceives she is performing at a higher level, but sees her lower-performing peer receive a similar
rating and/or consequence, she will view the appraisal system as less fair (Blume
et al., 2009; Latham, 2012).
Organizations may care about the distribution of their performance ratings
because they may perceive the ratings do not accurately reflect performance. To
address this, organizations may implement a relative rating system (as opposed
to an absolute rating system), and/or a forced distribution rating system (FDRS).
A relative rating system asks managers to rate employees against each other,
often placing them into ordered lists, while an absolute rating systems evaluates
employee performance against pre-determined elements and standards (Roch,
Sternburgh, & Caputo, 2007). Similarly, FDRS requires raters to evaluate employees by sorting them into predetermined performance categories (Dominick, 2009).
Scholars are currently debating the positive and negative impact of these rating
approaches (as summarized in Dominick, 2009; Moon, Scullen, & Latham, 2016).
In the short term, forced rankings may improve employee motivation and task
performance, and may attract high performing applicants to join the organization because they see that high performance is recognized (Blume et al., 2009).
Conversely, relative rankings under an FDRS scheme may result in lower employee
procedural justice perceptions, as compared to absolute rating against pre-defined
standards (Roch et al., 2007). Other criticisms suggest raters view forced rankings

as more difficult to use and less fair (Schleicher, Bull, & Green, 2008), and result
in ratings that are less accurate (Park, 2014).
In addition to pushing managers to change the distribution of ratings, agencies may choose to link ratings to positive or negative management decisions.
To recognize strong performers, organizations typically consider pay-for-performance schemes. Unfortunately pay-for-performance schemes in government
rarely deliver the expected increase in motivation and performance. In the US
federal government pay-for-performance did not increase public manager motivation and did not improve performance in multiple studies (Perry, Engbers, &
Jun, 2009). More recent efforts in US state governments have led to perceptions
of favoritism and failed promises (Battaglio & Condrey, 2009; Kellough & Nigro,
2002). Beyond the public sector experience, the use of ratings for administrative decisions may result in biased ratings against a particular group. One study
found ratings assigned to women were biased only when those ratings were used
to inform merit pay decisions (Dobbins, Cardy, & Truxillo, 1988). Since differentiation between performers may lead to favoritism and/or discrimination, we
propose the following hypothesis:
H4: When differentiation between levels of performance increases, the rate of appraisal
discrimination complaints will increase.

Data and methods
To analyze the relationship between performance appraisal structure and discrimination complaints resulting from the appraisal process, two agency-level data sets
are merged. For the independent variables, the data on federal agency performance
appraisal systems was provided by OPM to one of the authors in 2011.1 For the
dependent variable, information on agency discrimination complaints are calculated from agency No FEAR Act reports. Under this law, agencies are required
to publically report on an annual basis the number of discrimination complaints
they received in the fiscal year, including the basis for the discrimination (was the
unfair treatment due to the complainant’s gender, race, age, etc.) and the manner
in which the individual believes they were harmed (was the individual denied a
promotion, were they inappropriately disciplined, was their appraisal unfair, etc.).2
Agencies report aggregate complaint rates. We do not know which individual
employees filed which complaints, and we cannot determine what portion of
appraisal-related discrimination complaints allege bias in the form of race-, gender-, age-based, etc. discrimination. As a result, the unit of analysis is the agency.
Independent variables

OPM evaluates agency performance appraisal systems using ten criteria identified in the PAAT. These criteria include: alignment, results focus, credible measures, distinctions in levels of performance, consequences, employee involvement,

8

feedback and dialogue, and training. OPM includes two additional criteria related
to its responsibilities to audit agency personnel management systems, including
organizational assessment and guidance, and oversight and accountability. Based
on OPM’s definition and measurement of each criterion, the variables used in this
analysis were selected based on their fit with the theory presented above.
Each criterion includes multiple questions/components, and OPM assigns specific points to each component of each criterion. Combined, OPM assigns ten
points to each of the ten criteria, such that an agency with an outstanding performance appraisal system could earn a high score of 100 points. OPM designates as
system that scores 80 points or higher, out of a possible 100 points, as effective.
To document its efforts on each of the ten criteria, agencies answer multiple
questions and provide extensive documentation to OPM. OPM assigns scores for
each criteria based on their assessment of the evidence provided by the agencies
against the detailed criteria. The data-set includes the points assigned to each
component of each criterion. For example, the criterion of alignment includes
four components, each scored separately, with the maximum potential point total
equaling ten points. Additionally, multiple OPM raters evaluated agency PAAT
submissions to derive the final score for the assessment of the system. The data-set
does not include any notations on inter-rater reliability.
As noted in Rubin (2015), the PAAT data have many limitations. First, use of
the PAAT evaluation system is entirely voluntary and is initiated by the agency
itself. Many federal agency appraisal systems have not been evaluated via the
PAAT and are thus not included in the data-set, while others have been evaluated
under the PAAT framework multiple times. For the analysis conducted here, we
use the most recent PAAT score for those agencies that were evaluated multiple
times. However, given the age of the data it is possible that the scores used in this
analysis do not represent the current systems in use. Importantly, the voluntary
nature of the PAAT assessment means that we cannot assume that participation
in the PAAT review is a completely random process. Second the data-set only
includes the scores assigned by OPM for the individual criterion; the data-set does
not include the extensive documentation agencies provided to OPM.
Independent variables were derived from the PAAT criteria based on their fit
with theory, and informed by confirmatory factor analysis. The variable clarity
was formed by calculating the sum of the total scores from the PAAT criteria
on (a) alignment, (b) results-focus, and (c) credible measurement. Alignment
considers the degree to which employee performance plans create a line of sight
between individual goals and organizational goals, while the criterion results-focus
considers whether employees are actually held accountable for their contributions to organizational goal achievements (OPM, 2006). The criterion on credible
measurement considers the degree to which the appraisal program ensures that
performance measures for employees are specific, measurable, and objective. We
combined these into one variable, called clarity, because employees in agencies that

are successful in all three areas should have a better understanding of expectations
for their individual performance.
The empirical models operationalize agency efforts to differentiate between
levels of performance with multiple measures. As a global measure of differentiation, the variable distinctions calculates the sum from the OPM criteria (a)
distinctions among levels of performance and (b) consequences based on performance. Agencies are asked to report the distribution of performance ratings for the
most recent appraisal period, with more points given to agencies with a broader
distribution. To document the consequences for performance appraisal ratings,
agencies report the use of both positive and negative consequences resulting from
ratings, including cash awards and time off, or the placement of employees into
performance improvement periods. For a more fine-grained analysis, we also
consider three different forms of performance distinctions separately. Specifically,
in an alternative model we drop the global distinctions variable and instead include
three variables: (a) ratings which uses the OPM score assigned for the distribution
of performance ratings, with a broader distribution receiving higher points; (b)
rewards which uses the OPM score assessing the degree to which agencies provide
a variety of positive awards for high performance; and (c) consequences for poor
performers, which includes the OPM PAAT score for the formal consequences
imposed for poor performance, with more negative consequences earning an
agency more points. Interestingly, in the overall PAAT scoring scheme, agencies
earn more points for providing positive recognition than they do for imposing
negative consequences.
The variables voice and training were derived using one criterion from the
PAAT for each variable. Voice is calculated as the sum of the points assigned by
OPM for the criterion employee involvement, which considers the degree to which
employees participated in the design of the appraisal program overall, and the
level of employee involvement in setting their individual performance standards
(OPM, 2006). The variable training reflects the sum assigned by OPM for its criterion training. This criteria asks agencies to report whether the appraisal system
requires both employees and managers be trained on the appraisal process, and
whether they actually received training in the two years prior to PAAT completion.
Confirmatory factor analysis with varimax rotation assessed the combination
of PAAT criteria for the variables clarity and distinctions and supported the creation of the two variables. The two-variable structure explains 78% and 31% of
the variance respectively. Additionally, the two variable structure obtained an
acceptable fit (χ2(10) = 247.78 p < .01). The variable clarity produced a Cronbach’s
alpha scale reliability coefficient of .92. The variable consequences produced a
Cronbach’s alpha scale reliability coefficient of .73. Both variables have a high
degree of consistency within the model. We initially considered combining the
OPM criteria of (a) feedback and dialogue and (b) employee involvement into
a composite voice variable, but the factor structure did not support such a combination. As a result, voice is assessed using the single OPM criterion employee

Dependent variables

For the dependent variables, information on agency discrimination complaints is
calculated from agency No FEAR Act reports. Since 2003, agencies are required to
report annually on the bases and issues associated with discrimination complaints.
These reports include information on the number of discrimination complaints
alleging biased treatment in the performance appraisal process, in addition to the
total complaints filed during the year, and whether employees claimed they were
discriminated against based on their gender, race, etc. (for more information see
Rubin & Kellough, 2012).
The dependent variable, developed from the No FEAR Act data, is a ratio
calculated by dividing the number of appraisal discrimination complaints by the
number of employees in that agency. The number of employees was gathered from
OPM Fedscope data, using the September employee counts. We use a ratio instead
of the number of appraisal discrimination complaints because 50 complaints in
a small agency like the National Science Foundation means something very different than in a large agency like the Veterans Health Administration. With both
the complaint and appraisal system data coming from agency reports, our level
of analysis is the federal agency; there are 79 agencies in the sample.
In addition to the ratio of complaints to employees we develop two alternatives
dependent variables for assessing the impact of appraisal system design on discrimination complaints related to performance appraisals. The ratio is recoded as
a dummy variable with an agency receiving a score of 0 if it experienced no discrimination complaints tied to appraisal, or scored as 1 if it received any appraisal-related discrimination complaints. A third dependent variable is developed
where an agency receives a score of zero if it experienced no complaints; a score
of 1 if its complaint rate was greater than zero but equal to or less than the median
complaint rate; or a score of 2 if it experienced a complaint rate greater than
the median. Based on these independent variables, we analyze our hypothesized
relationships using (1) Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression using the ratio
dependent variable, (2) a logit model with the dummy dependent variable, and
(3) a multinomial logit model with the three-category dependent variable. The
logit and multinomial logit modes are interpreted using predicted probabilities.
It is critical to describe how we manage time in our analysis. Agencies completed the PAAT in different years and at different intervals. As noted above, this
analysis only includes the most recent PAAT scores. In the models, PAAT scores,
developed in year t, were linked with discrimination complaints in year t + 1. As
a result the dates for the dependent variables differ across agencies, but follow a
similar structure. For example, the 2008 PAAT score from the Federal Highway
Administration is paired with discrimination data from 2009; the 2009 Patent and

As a result, this allows us to avoid problems associated with same source bias and
interpret any relationships as: a structural component of the appraisal system in

Analysis
The analysis begins with a review of the descriptive statistics for our key variables (see Table 1). Agencies included in the sample average approximately 15,000
employees. As expected, there is a very small portion of federal employees filing
discrimination complaints tied to the appraisal process. Twenty-one agencies
reported no discrimination complaints tied to appraisals. This is not surprising;
this simply means that employees filing complaints indicated the unfair treatment
happened in other parts of the personnel process. The descriptive statistics in
Table 1, specifically the standard errors and the differences between the mean and
median value of the dependent variable, indicates this variable is highly skewed,
with a limited number of extremely high values. We chose to not drop these
observations from the sample so that we would not be ‘selecting on the dependent
variable’ and thus biasing our results. Instead, to manage this skew in the dependent variable, we use robust standard errors in all models. In the OLS model we
transform all variables by calculating the natural log.
The total PAAT score, assigned by OPM, could have a maximum potential
value of 100 points, where OPM defines a successful appraisal system as one that
achieves a score of 80 points or higher. In this sample, the average PAAT score
is 64 points. Less than half of the sample (33 agencies) earn a PAAT score of 80
points or higher. Within the PAAT scores, we see interesting variations between
the different aspects of appraisal system design. On average, agency systems are
strongest on providing voice opportunities, i.e. involving employees throughout
the appraisal process. Not surprisingly, agencies struggle most in making distinctions between employees. Agency efforts to make distinctions earn an average of
12 out of 20 points.

Discussion and conclusion
Existing scholarship suggests that well-designed performance appraisal systems
will increase employee perceptions of fairness. If we accept that appraisal-related
discrimination complaints are indicators of fairness perceptions, the results in
this study suggest that organizations with well-designed appraisal systems will
experience more discrimination complaints, contrary to existing literature. Across
all three models, increases in training are associated with higher complaint rates,
which contradicts existing research and the proposed hypotheses. Furthermore,
the multinomial logit and logit model results generally suggest that stronger
appraisal systems experience higher appraisal-related discrimination complaints.
Voice, the most frequently tested attribute of appraisal systems, was not significant in the OLS model, but in both the multinomial logit and logit models higher
voice opportunities was associated with grater complaint rates, contrary to existing research. Clarity, which assesses the degree to which employees understand
performance expectations, was not significant in the OLS model, but in both
the multinomial logit and logit models low clarity was associated with higher
probability of experiencing complaints, which is consistent with the hypothesis.
The effects of making distinctions in levels of performance on discrimination complaints was inconsistent across the models. Awards exhibited different
effects on complaints between the OLS (negative), multinomial logit (positive),
and logit (negative) models. Agencies with broader distributions in ratings were
more likely to experience complaints, generally, and higher complaint in the logit
and multinomial logit models, respectively, which is consistent with the hypotheses. Finally a low rate of consequences for poor performers is associated with
greater probabilities of high complaints and experiencing any complaints, which
is consistent with the hypothesis.
Psychology scholars have shifted research on appraisals to focus more on the
interpersonal and contextual settings in which performance appraisal is implemented. These studies suggest that the relationships between raters and ratees are
significantly important for perceptions of the appraisal process, but this shift in the
research does not suggest that the structure of the appraisal system is completely
disconnected from these perceptions. While we did not expect large relationships,
the weakness of the associations was unexpected.
As far as we have been able to determine in our review of existing scholarship,
there is no standard expectation for how much the appraisal system design should
explain effectiveness of the system, versus psychological factors. The results may
be due to measurement problems and the small sample. Seventy-nine observations
is more than adequate for OLS regression models, but is low for multinomial
logit and logit models. Additionally, bias in sample selection may influence the

results. It is difficult to determine how many performance appraisal programs
exist in the US federal government, so it is currently not possible to predict why
one agency would submit a completed PAAT. Despite these problems, we should
be cautious in dismissing these results too quickly. The sample includes agencies
with a variety of missions, occupations, and a broad distribution in employee
numbers. Two-fifths of the sample agencies met or exceed OPM’s standard for a
sophisticated appraisal system.
Moving forward, the results present a number of interesting challenges for
scholars interested in understanding how performance appraisal systems impact
agency culture, especially the culture of fairness in an agency. First, scholars have
identified multiple measures for assessing the effectiveness of appraisal systems.
One of these many measures are perceptions of fairness of the system, and the
filing of discrimination complaints is a narrow behavioral outcome of those fairness perceptions. We might learn more by going into an agency with multiple
appraisal systems and evaluating a broader collection of effectiveness measures.
It should not be difficult to find a US federal agency with multiple appraisal systems; appraisal systems are included in collective bargaining agreements, so an
agency with multiple bargaining units is likely to have interesting variations in
system design. Second, if we are to believe the results that structure is minimally
related to outcomes, it would be interesting to assess appraisal effectiveness in
two agencies that appear to have the same appraisal system design. If the system
is the same, but outcomes are different, we can infer that implementation, context, and interpersonal relationships matter more than design. Finally, as agencies
implement new appraisal systems, it could be instructive to know whether a new
design improves motivation, fairness, citizenship behaviors, commitment, etc.
Under the PAAT scheme, OPM is sending signals that US federal agencies
should make larger differentiations when rating employees, and that ratings
should have more frequent and stronger consequences for employees. These efforts
are leading to significant frustrations in agencies that have explicitly adopted
pay-for-performance systems which require differentiation in ratings. For example, the US Congress has exerted significant pressure on the Senior Executive
Service to ensure that very few senior managers are assigned a rating of excellence.
Widespread dissatisfaction with the appraisal and pay-for-performance system,
and early signals that the ratings distribution was discriminatory, played a large
part in the repeal of the US Department of Defense civilian personnel reforms.
Finally, attempts to implement a pay-for-performance scheme in the US Securities
and Exchange Commission and the US Consumer Financial Protection Bureau
also failed due to systematic discrimination against subgroups of employees. As
agencies respond to pressure from OPM, Congress, and others to revise appraisal
systems, the results here suggest we might benefit from asking the question: should
we focus on reforming the design of the appraisal systems, or should we focus on
how the systems are implemented instead? The results here suggest we might be
better off focusing on the latter and not the former.
