Evidence That Task Performance Should Be Distinguished

From Contextual Performance
Stephan J. Motowidlo and James R. Van Scotter

This study tests the merit of the distinction made by W. C. Borman and S. J. Motowidlo (1993)
between task performance and contextual performance. Supervisors rated 421 U.S. Air Force me-
chanics on their task performance, contextual performance, and overall performance. Data on
length of air force experience, ability, training performance, and personality were also available for
many of these mechanics. Results showed that both task performance and contextual performance
contribute independently to overall performance. Experience is more highly correlated with task
performance than with contextual performance, and personality variables are more highly corre-

lated with contextual performance than with task performance. These results support the dis

ion.

between task performance and contextual performance and confirm that performance, at least as

judged by supervisors, is multidimensional.

Despite a long history of concern about criterion issues (Aus-
tin & Villanova, 1992), the construct of job performance has
not yet been thoroughly mapped (Campbell, 1990). Sophisti-
cated job analysis techniques are available to identify important
tasks and behaviors, but their focus is primarily empirical and
typically specific to particular jobs or job families. As a result,
they have not produced a conceptually satisfying set of basic
underlying dimensions that can be used to describe the perfor-
mance requirements of jobs in general.

More theoretical attempts to dissect the performance domain
are beginning to show promise. They adopt quite different con-
ceptual orientations, different from traditional job analytic ap-
proaches and from each other, but they converge on the distinc-
tion between task performance and contextual performance
(Borman & Motowidlo, 1993). In our study, we tested the merit
of that distinction.

One of the most basic theoretical schemes for partitioning
job performance is a three-way division between (a) joining and
staying in the organization, (b) dependably meeting or exceed-
ing standards of performance prescribed by organizational
roles, and (c) innovatively and spontaneously going beyond pre-
scribed roles to perform such actions as cooperating with other
members, protecting the organization from harm, offering sug-
gestions for improvement, undertaking self-development, and
representing the organization favorably to outsiders (Katz &
Kahn, 1978). The second and third patterns of behavior are es-
pecially interesting because they contrast organizationally es-
sential activities that are prescribed by role requirements with
other activities that are also essential but discretionary and not

Organizational citizenship (Organ, 1988), prosocial organi-
zational behavior (Brief & Motowidlo, 1986), and organiza-
tional spontaneity (George & Brief, 1992) are closely related to
patterns of prescribed and discretionary organizational behav-
iors described by Katz and Kahn (1978). They differ from each
other in some important respects, but they all highlight behav-
iors that involve cooperation and helping others in the organi-
zation. They also share the assumption that affective vari-
ables—such as job satisfaction, mood, and stress—can have im-
portant causal effects on organizational cooperation and
helping (Bateman & Organ, 1983; George, 1991; Motowidlo,
1984; Motowidlo, Packard, & Manning, 1986; Organ & Konov-
sky, 1989). Parallel to results of the study by Orr et al. (1989),
MacKenzie, Podsakoff, and Fetter (1991) provided empirical
support for the importance of organizational citizenship behav-
ior. Their study showed that managers are apt to take at least
some forms of organizational citizenship behavior into account,
in addition to objective productivity, when asked to evaluate the
overall performance of insurance sales agents.

Proceeding from a different theoretical perspective, Camp-
bell (1990) developed another system for partitioning the per-
formance domain. Largely on the basis of work done to define
criteria for Project A—a long-term study of selection and clas-
sification practices for entry-level jobs in the U.S. Army—he
developed a performance model that divided the domain into
job-specific task proficiency, non-job-specific task proficiency,
written and oral communication, demonstrating effort, main-
taining personal discipline, facilitating team and peer perfor-
mance, supervision and leadership, and management and ad-
ministration. These factors are designed to sufficiently describe
the latent performance structure of all jobs in the Dictionary of
Occupational Titles (Campbell, McCloy, Oppler, & Sager,

1992). Some factors, however, are not relevant for some Jobs.
Therefore, although sufficient, they are not all necessary for de-
scribing the performance structures of all jobs.

Campbell’s (1990) model brings out an important distinction
between performance behaviors that contribute to organiza-
tional effectiveness because they involve task proficiency and
performance behaviors that contribute to organizational
effectiveness in other ways. The task proficiency factors in
Campbell's model are more heavily saturated with prescribed
role behavior, and the other factors are more heavily saturated
with elements of organizational citizenship behavior, prosocial
behavior, and organizational spontaneity.

Thus, three related themes circulate through these efforts to
describe generically important aspects of job performance. One
is the distinction between prescribed and discretionary role be-
havior. Another is the affectively flavored set of cooperation and
helpfulness behaviors in organizational citizenship behavior,
prosocial behavior, and organizational spontaneity, The third is
the distinction between performance behaviors related to task
proficiency and performance behaviors not related to task pro-
ficiency. The three themes merge in the distinction between task
performance and contextual performance that was made by
Borman and Motowidlo (1993).

Task performance includes two classes of behavior, One con-
sists of activities that directly transform raw materials into the
goods and services that the organization produces. It includes
such activities as selling merchandise in a retail store, operating
a production machine in a manufacturing plant, teaching in a
school, performing surgery in a hospital, and cashing checks in
a bank. The other class consists of activities that service and
maintain the technical core by replenishing its supply of raw
materials; distributing its finished products; and providing im-
portant planning, coordination, supervising, and staff functions
that enable it to function effectively and efficiently. Thus, task
performance behaviors bear a direct relation to the organiza-
tion’s technical core, either by executing its technical processes
or by maintaining and servicing its technical requirements.

In contrast, contextual performance behaviors do not sup-
port the technical core itself as much as they support the
broader organizational, social, and psychological environment
in which the technical core must function. Drawing on theoret-
ical and empirical work in organizational citizenship behavior
(Organ, 1988), prosocial organizational behavior (Brief & Mo-
towidlo, 1986), models of soldier effectiveness (Borman, Mo-
towidlo, & Hanser, 1983; Campbell, 1990), and models of man-
agement effectiveness (Borman & Brush, 1993), Borman and
Motowidlo (1993) identified five categories of contextual per-
formance. The categories are (a) volunteering to carry out task
activities that are not formally part of the job; (b) persisting with
extra enthusiasm when necessary to complete own task activi-
ties successfully; (c) helping and cooperating with others; (d) fol-
lowing organizational rules and procedures even when it is per-
sonally inconvenient; and (e) endorsing, supporting, and de-
fending organizational objectives.

The three themes mentioned in connection with earlier dis-
cussions about important aspects of performance behavior in-
tersect with the concepts of task and contextual performance.
Task performance is role prescribed (Katz & Kahn, 1978), but
contextual performance is more typically discretionary.

Contextual performance captures many of the helping and co-
operating elements of organizational citizenship behavior (Or-
gan, 1988), prosocial organizational behavior (Brief & Moto-
widlo, 1986), and organizational spontaneity (George & Brief,
1992), but task performance does not. Task performance in-
cludes primarily the two task dimensions in Campbell’s (1990)
performance model, along with elements of written and oral
communication, supervision and leadership, and management
and administration, and contextual performance includes
many of the elements of the other dimensions in Campbell's
model.

Thus, the concepts of task and contextual performance draw
conceptually from these other ways of slicing up the perfor-
mance domain. They are importantly different from other per-
formance models, however, because they explicitly separate be-
haviors that represent proficiency in performing specific work
tasks from other behaviors that are organizationally more valu-
able because of their interpersonal and broader motivational
implications. Another difference from other performance
models that emphasize such constructs as extrarole behavior,
prosocial behavior, organizational citizenship, and organiza-
tional spontaneity is that the concepts of task and contextual
performance were developed specifically for use in selection re-
search. Accordingly, they represent categories of behavior
meant to serve as performance criteria, and they are likely to be
predicted by different individual-differences variables.

Although the constructs of task performance and contextual
performance might be useful to help structure the performance
domain, the distinction between them has not yet been directly
and empirically supported. They deserve to be distinguished
only if both contribute independently to an individual’s overall
worth to the organization.

The argument for distinguishing between task and contextual
performance also gains force if they are correlated with differ-
ent personal characteristics. Borman and Motowidlo (1993)
suggested that the major source of variation in task perfor-
mance is the proficiency with which a person can carry out task
activities. This means that individual differences in knowledge,
skills, and abilities should covary more with task performance
than with contextual performance. Experience and training
performance should also be more strongly correlated with task
performance for the same reasons. Experience is substantially
correlated with both job knowledge and work sample perfor-
mance, which carry effects of ability (Schmidt, Hunter, & Out-
erbridge, 1986), and training performance is also correlated
with ability (Dickinson & Teachout, 1991; Earles & Ree, 1992).
However, behaviors such as volunteering, persisting, cooperat-
ing, and so forth are not likely to be strongly related to individ-
ual differences in knowledge, skills, and abilities. Instead, such
behaviors are probably better predicted by individual differ-
ences in interpersonal skills and motivation. Thus, personality
variables should be better predictors of contextual performance
than of task performance.

The purpose of this study was to test the usefulness of the
distinction between task performance and contextual perfor-
mance by examining their relations with overall contribution to
the organization and individual differences in ability, personal-
ity, experience, and training performance. To support the dis-
tinction, our results should show that task performance and

Method

The sample of research participants included 715 air force mechanics
in Grades E-2 (Airman) to E-5 (Staff Sergeant). Most (698) were main-
tenance technicians responsible for maintaining and configuring ai
craft, ordnance, and armament systems. A few (17) were vehicle main-
tenance mechanics.

Operational commitments limited the extent to which many of the
mechanics were able to participate in the study. Of 715 in the total sam-
ple, 392 completed at least part of a self-report questionnaire containing
questions about demographic variables. This group included 372 men
and 20 women: 317 Whites, 43 Blacks, 24 Hispanics, and 8 others who
could not be included in any of the three preceding categories. Their
mean age was 24.77 years (SD = 4.34 years). The experience variable
was a grouped measure that was coded | for 140 persons with 1-2 years
of experience in the air force, 2 for 61 persons with 3-4 years of experi-
ence, 3 for 113 persons with 5-8 years of experience, 4 for 58 persons
with 9-12 years of experience, 5 for 16 persons with 13-16 years of ex-
perience, and 6 for 4 persons with 17-20 years of experience.

The questionnaires also contained a version of the Assessment of
Background and Life Experiences, which was developed as part of Proj-
ect A for the army (Peterson et al., 1990). The version used in this study
contained 133 questions scored for social desirability, nonrandom re-
sponse, physical condition, and six personality variables: work orienta-
tion, dominance, dependability, adjustment, cooperativeness, and in-
ternal control. Internal consistency reliability estimates (alphas) for the
personality variables ranged from .82 to .89. Their intercorrelations
ranged from .23 to .61, with a mean of .41. Personality scores for 10
cases with nonrandom response scores of six or less, out of a maximum
possible score of eight, were set to missing data.

Scores on the Armed Forces Qualification Test (AFQT), which are
derived from verbal and quantitative subtests of the Armed Services
Vocational Aptitude Battery (ASVAB; Dickinson & Teachout, 1991;
Earles & Ree, 1992), served as ability measures; they were obtained
from administrative records. Only AFQT scores from ASVAB Forms 8
and above were used. They were available for 250 participants in our
sample. AFQT scores are restricted in our sample because they were
used for selection into these air force jobs. The standard deviation of
scores in our sample was 14.89; however, the standard deviation in the
applicant population was approximately 28.26. Because of this restric-
tion in range, we thought that these AFQT scores might not provide a
strong test of our hypothesis, but we proceeded with them because no
other measures of ability were available.

Grades given for training programs completed shortly after enlist-
ment in the air force were also obtained from administrative records for
270 participants in our sample. These grades served in analyses re-
ported here as measures of training performance.

We identified three persons to rate each mechanic. Raters were cho-
sen according to these criteria: (a) they observed the mechanic’s work
performance daily for at least 90 days; (b) they were authorized to direct
‘the mechanic's work performance and could give instructions, inspect
work in progress, or coach and advise; and (c) they were expert in the
technical aspects of the work. They held jobs with such titles as Super-
intendent, Assistant Superintendent, Flight Chief, Deputy Flight Chief,
Work Leader, and Section Chief, and they all had at least some form of
supervisory authority over the mechanics (although only one of them
was the formally designated supervisor). (For convenience, we refer to
all three raters as supervisors in the remainder of this article.)

For each mechanic, we assigned one supervisor to rate task perfor-

mance, a second to rate contextual performance, and a third to rate
overall performance. Overall performance was rated by the most senior
supervisor available. Thus, as many as three supervisors rated each par-
ticipant, and each one rated the participant on a different variable. No
supervisor rated a participant on more than one variable, and no super-
visor rated some participants on one variable and other participants on
different variables. Of the total sample of 715 mechanics, 667
were rated by at least one supervisor and 421 were rated by all three
supervisors.

‘Task performance was measured with 14 items adapted from critical
incident dimensions developed for vehicle mechanics as part of the U.S.
Army's Project A (Campbell, 1987), supplemented with activities
adapted from reports of task analyses of relevant air force jobs:

In comparison to others in this AFSC [Air Force Specialty Code],
how effective is this person in (a) inspecting, testing, and detecting
problems with equipment; (b) trouble-shooting; (c) performing
routine maintenance; (d) repairing; (e) using tools and test equip-
ment; (f) using technical documentation; (g) operating equipment;
(h) planning and organizing work; (i) performing administrative
duties; (j) working safely; (k) cleaning shop facilities; (1) invento-
tying tools; (m) cleaning and lubricating equipment components;
[and] (n) overall technical performance?

Supervisors rated participants on each item on a S-point scale ranging
from | = much below average to 5 = much above average. The mean of
the 14 items formed the task performance score (a = .95). To preserve
sample size, we computed this mean for all cases with ratings on at least
10 items.

Contextual performance was measured with 16 items developed for
this study to tap dimensions of contextual performance identified by
Borman and Motowidlo (1993):

While performing his or her job, how likely is it that this person
would (a) comply with instructions even when supervisors are not
present; (b) cooperate with others in the team; (c) persist in over-
coming obstacles to complete a task; (d) display proper military
appearance and bearing; (e) volunteer for additional duty; (f) fol-
low proper procedures and avoid unauthorized shortcuts; (g) look
for a challenging assignment; (h) offer to help others accomplish
their work; (i) pay close attention to important details; (j) defend
the supervisor’s decisions; (k) render proper military courtesy; (1)
support and encourage a coworker with a problem; (m) take the
initiative to solve a work problem; (n) exercise personal discipli
and self-control; (0) tackle a difficult work assignment enthusiasti-
cally; and (p) voluntarily do more than the job requires to help
others or contribute to unit effectiveness?

Supervisors rated each participant on a 5-point scale ranging from [ =
not at all likely to 5 = extremely likely. The mean of the 16 items formed.
the contextual performance score (a = .95) for all cases with ratings on.
at least 11 items.

Overall performance was measured with three 7-point scales, each
with a different set of anchors at the high, moderate, and low ranges.
One was anchored with high (6-7) = exceeds standards for job perfor-
mance, moderate (3-5) = meets standards for job performance, and low
(1-2) = does not meet standards for job performance. Another was an-
chored with high (6-7) = performs at a high level compared with others
of the same rank, moderate (3-5) = performs at an average level com-
pared with others of the same rank, and low (1-2) = performs at a low
level compared with others of the same rank. The third was anchored
with high (6-7) = contributes more to unit effectiveness than most mem-
bers of the work unit, moderate (3-5) = makes an average contribution
to unit effectiveness, and low (1-2) = contributes less to unit effectiveness
than most members of the work unit. The mean of the three scales
formed the overall performance score (a = .96) for all cases with ratings
on at least two scales.

Results

Table | shows correlations between task, contextual, and
overall performance in the sample of 421 mechanics for whom
complete performance data were available. The multiple corre-
lation between overall performance as the dependent variable
and task and contextual performance was .54 (p < .01), Hierar-
chical regressions were also computed, with task performance
added to the regression equation before contextual perfor-
mance and with contextual performance added to the regres-
sion equation before task performance. Results revealed that
task performance explains 13% (p < .01) of the variance in over-
all performance beyond that explained by contextual perfor-
mance and that contextual performance explains 11% (p < .01)
of the variance beyond that explained by task performance.

These results supported the hypothesis that task and
contextual performance contribute independently to overall
performance, but they were based on raw correlations that had
not been corrected for unreliability. It is not clear that internal
consistency reliability estimates computed for the performance
measures are appropriate for use in corrections for attenuation,
partly because they are likely to be inflated by halo effects and
partly because they do not include interrater sources of error.
Interrater reliability could not be estimated empirically from
data in this study, but other authors have offered estimates of
interrater reliability for performance ratings by a single super-
visor ranging from about .43 (Hunter & Hirsh, 1987) to about
-80 (National Academy of Sciences, 1989).

Because we had no way to determine which point estimate of
reliability would be most appropriate, we computed analyses in
which we corrected systematically for several different estimates
of reliability. In these analyses, we assumed that the reliability
of measures of task performance, contextual performance, and
overall performance might be either .40, .60, or .80. We com-
puted corrected correlations and performed multiple regression
analyses for all possible combinations of these three reliability
estimates for the three performance variables.

Our results show that the finding that task and contextual
performance both contribute independently to overall perfor-
mance holds for various estimates of reliability from .40 to .80.
Even if the most likely estimates fall in the lower portion of this
range—say, from .40 to .60—our results still show (a) that task
performance explains from 17% to 44% of the variance in over-
all performance beyond the variance explained by contextual
performance and (b) that contextual performance explains
from 12% to 34% of the variance in overall performance beyond
the variance explained by task performance.

Like many other studies of job performance and its predic-
tors, ours relied on supervisory judgments for measures of per-
formance. Consequently, our conclusions about relations be-
tween personal characteristics, task performance, contextual
performance, and overall performance apply to supervisors’
evaluations of performance. We presume that supervisors’ eval-
uations are reasonably good measures of objective perfor-
mance, but we cannot rule out the possibility that perceptual
and evaluative processes might account for our results in ways
unrelated to relations between objective patterns of behavior
that supervisors were asked to evaluate.

The most straightforward implication of our study is that per-
formance, as judged by supervisors, is not unidimensional. At
least two dimensions, task performance and contextual perfor-
mance, are worth distinguishing. They involve different pat-
terns of behavior, they have different antecedents, and they con-
tribute independently to supervisors’ judgments about an indi-
vidual’s overall worth to the organization.
