## Common Unconscious Biases {#slide-7}

- Halo Effect:  one positive trait overshadows other traits, skills, or behaviors
- Horn Effect:  allow one\'s judgement of a person to be influenced by an unfavorable impression 
- Recency Effect:  recent behavior carries too much weight in evaluating performance
- Primacy Bias:  tendency to overweight first impressions in evaluating performance
- Proximity Bias:  tendency to assess information easier with people whom we have more frequent contact.

<!-- -->

- 67% of managers felt their remote employees were more easily replaceable than on-site workers (SHRM, 2022)

<!-- -->

- Stereotyping:  generalizing about employee's performance based on their group membership
- Similarity/Affinity Bias:  preference for someone that is similar to oneself
- Alienation Bias:  giving unfavorable ratings for someone that the rater has nothing in common with
- Comparative Bias:  the rater makes comparison amongst ratees to provide ratings, instead of reviewing ratees as individuals

## Conscious Biases {#slide-8}

- Intentional and overt biases that an evaluator may knowingly apply or be aware of when assessing employee performance.

<!-- -->

- Tend to lead to unequal treatment among employees in ratings, evaluations, promotions, and development opportunities ( Aguinis , 2012)

<!-- -->

## Common Conscious Biases {#slide-9}

- Leniency/Severity Tendency:  rating consistently too lenient or too strict 
- Central Tendency:  rater avoid making extreme judgements of employees
- Confirmation Bias:  seeking out evidence to confirm a pre-existing belief about an employee
- Personal Biases:  when a manager feels a specific way about an employees based on a feature they like or dislike and it influences their performance rating
- Favoritism or Nepotism:  giving preference to certain employees over others based on personal relationships, friendships, or familial ties rather than merit.
- Gender and Racial Bias:  Conscious biases where evaluators favor or rate individuals based on gender, race, or ethnicity rather than performance.

## Impact of Bias on Fairness {#slide-11}

- Biases in performance management can lead to negative fairness perceptions amongst employees ( Aguinis , 2012)

Distributive Justice-  fairness of outcomes related to decisions

Procedural Justice-  fairness of process by which ratings are assigned and a decision is made

Interpersonal Justice-  interpersonal treatment in which evaluations are done

Informational Justice - transparency in decisions and communication

## Newman et al. 2020 {#slide-24}

Algorithmic Decision-Making in HR

-  Promise of Algorithms

<!-- -->

- Increased consistency and bias reduction through data-driven decisions.

<!-- -->

-  Perceived Fairness Issues

<!-- -->

- Employees may view algorithmic decisions as reductionistic---over-reliant on quantifiable metrics and lacking contextual nuance​
- Reductionism  -- algorithms perceived as overly reliant on quantifiable measures ignoring qualitative nuance and context 
- In experiments with 798 participants and a large-scale organizational study (N = 1,654), algorithmic decisions were consistently rated as less fair than human decisions, even when decision outcomes were identical.
- Example: Resistance to algorithmic hiring decisions at Google, despite increased efficiency​
