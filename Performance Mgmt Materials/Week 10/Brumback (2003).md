Even when people are carefully selected and
provided with on-time training, there will still
be differences among them in their
performance. Those differences are not a
liability, as any championship team with a star
player or two will likely testify.
The clicheÂ, ``there's no `I' in `team''', is just
that, a clicheÂ. Any team-based organization
needs to learn to blend the ``me'' and the
``we'' into teams that help it to reach and
sustain tall performance year after year.
Obviously, even a championship team with a
few stars also must count on consistent
contributions from its role players.
The purpose of this article is to tell about a
model the author has designed that allows
members of empowered teams to blend their
individual talents to manage their own
performance for the good of the organization.
The article begins with an overview of the
principles underlying the model. The article
ends with an overview of how the model
works.

Abstract
In a team-based organization, the performance not only
of the teams as a whole but also of their individual
members needs to be managed and managed very well if
tall performance (consistently competent, ethical, and
motivated behavior that always produces the best results)
is to be reached and sustained. In this article, four
principles are first overviewed that underlie a new model
of performance management designed by the author. The
principles are those of tall performance, accountability,
responsible empowerment, and performance
management. An overview of the new model is then
given. Among the model's salient features are the way it
preserves the ``we'' without sacrificing the ``me'', a
non-traditional way to do performance appraisals, and
accountability not only for results but also for behaviors,
including ethical conduct.

Accountability
Tall performance is not possible if people are
not held accountable for their performance. If
it were possible without accountability, there
would never be any need for follow-up such as
rewards and penalties.
But it is not enough to be held accountable
just for results. Pressure from the
conventional bottom line causes much
wrongdoing in business (Estes, 1996). To
counter that pressure, the ``bottom line of
behaviors'' needs to be added as the other part
of accountability, so that the manner in which
results are pursued can be taken into
consideration. Consider, for instance,
ill-gotten success. Given behavioral
accountability, such success would never be
tolerated, let alone rewarded. Or consider a
very positive pursuit felled by unforeseen
circumstances. Given behavioral
accountability, such a pursuit would never be
punished and might even be celebrated so as
not to discourage worthy risks.
Responsible empowerment
There are eight compelling reasons why
people should be empowered at work.
Succinctly put, the reasons are these:
(1) all species are genetically programmed to
manage their own lives;
(2) human beings have a psychological need
to develop their potential;
(3) empowering others is the morally right
thing to do;
(4) not being empowered can be unhealthy;
(5) the language of commanding and
controlling (e.g. ``subordinate'') is
subjugating;
(6) as in physics where every action produces
an equal reaction, labor-management
relations is a history of management
actions and the reactions of those
managed;
(7) from a philosophical standpoint, the
autonomy of will is an intrinsic human
condition; and
(8) empowerment is good for performance;
empowered people simply outperform
commanded and controlled people.
But there is a caveat that accompanies
empowerment. It must be responsible.
Empowering irresponsibly and being

empowered without then acting responsibly
would be anarchy. Responsible empowerment
can be ensured through providing a context of
organizational purpose, a vision and culture
that touts tall performance, careful hiring,
targeted training, guarded trust, and, of
course, good performance management and
the accountability it requires.
Performance management
Performance management is inevitable.
Imagine how random and chaotic would be
unmanaged performance. The issue, then, is
not whether to manage performance, but who
should do the managing and how. The answer
to the first question is that empowered people
should be their own performance managers.
As for how it is done, the process has four
phases if done properly. First, expectations
for both behaviors and results are set. People
can not be held accountable if nothing is
expected of them. Second, performance is
carefully tended along the way. Third,
performance is appraised. Fourth, the
appraised performance is sanctioned. Each
phase depends on the earlier ones, and the
last phase finalizes accountability.

The new model
The author designed for various government
agencies models of performance management
that were successfully implemented. The
model to be overviewed here, however, is
unlike the others and has yet to be
implemented, although a partial version of it
was successfully pilot-tested in several US
agencies. There are four unique features to
the model:
(1) It is designed for use by responsibly and
fully empowered teams (which can not
co-exist in a layered bureaucracy).
(2) Besides accountability for results and the
other two dimensions of behavior, people
are held accountable for their ethical
conduct (this form of accountability was
prohibited in the earlier models because
of the peculiarities of US Civil Service
law). Mainstreaming accountability for
ethical conduct into the entire process of
performance management is far superior
to relying on traditional means like
separate codes of ethics, which are usually
perfunctory and forgotten.

(3) The way performance appraisal is done

(4) The whole process preserves the ``we''

Let us walk briskly through an illustration of
the new model. For the sake of simplicity, let
us assume we have a business unit with
relatively few layers of management. The
unit is organized into cross-functional,
self-managed teams. Each team chooses
among its members a leader and usually a
performance consultant who serve in those
extra roles for however long the team decides.
Team leaders sit on a steering council along
with the executive group. The council sets
general direction, charters each team at its
inception, allocates company resources
among the teams, and helps to ensure that
their activities are coordinated and aligned
with the company's vision, values, and
strategic goals. There is also a performance
review board (PRB) made up of team
representatives who have certain
responsibilities relating to the appraising and
sanctioning of performance.
Setting performance expectations
This first phase is the most critical because
everything else flows from it. Two kinds of
performance expectations are set,
prescriptions and proscriptions. The exact
results intended are prescribed in the form of
written objectives, as in MBO. Very much
unlike MBO, however, all short behavior is
proscribed (usually neither tall nor short
behavior is considered in MBO). The reason
for this twist is that there is often more than
one right way to pursue the same results, but
there is never a wrong way that is tolerable.
New prescriptions must be set at the
beginning of each performance period,
but proscriptions need be created only
once unless competencies (and thus
in-competencies) change. A task force of team
representatives creates the proscriptions by
developing generic examples of incompetent,
unmotivated, and unethical behavior.
All teams, through their representatives, are
to suggest at the first of two council meetings
ideas for what the business unit's new annual
goals should be and what initiatives the teams
might take to help accomplish those goals
(initiatives that receive concurrence by the
steering council will then be transformed by
the teams into ``we'' objectives).

Each team meets to prepare for the first
meeting by raising and answering such
questions as:
.
Do we think our current vision and
strategic plan are still viable?
.
Is there any carry-over work to be done?
.
What products need to be improved?
.
What new products are needed?
.
Given our answers to these questions,
what do we think should be the unit's new
goals, and what should be our initiatives
in support of them that fit into our areas
of accountability?
Team representatives take the answers to the
first council meeting, where the council
considers all teams' inputs and then
tentatively chooses new annual goals for the
unit and the teams' supportive initiatives.
The teams then meet separately again, this
time to draft their ``we'' objectives along with
an action plan. All teams' draft objectives and
action plans are next reviewed and discussed
at a second meeting of the steering council.
Assuming teams had communicated among
themselves beforehand about possible
redundancies and conflicts among objectives
and possible dependencies on each other, no
``we'' objectives should have to be changed by
the council (for example, a team could secure
a tentative agreement from another team to
exchange two members whose expertise
would supplement the receiving teams). And
basically because of that, neither would any
changes have to be made in the annual goals.
Now it is time to move from the ``we'' to the
``me'' objectives. They differ in several
important respects. Because their
accomplishment contributes to the unit's
overall goals, team objectives are broader than
are individual objectives. Because they are
broader and because there are fewer teams
than individuals in the organization, team
objectives will be outnumbered by individual
objectives. Finally, more team objectives will
target end results such as products to be
delivered or services to be provided, whereas
more individual objectives will target
intermediate or enabling results such as
process improvement outcomes or
components of the product or service that
contribute to the end result.
After confirming their respective roles and
responsibilities, team members draft their
``me'' objectives and action plans. The team

Tending performance
Once performance gets underway on behalf of
the new annual goals, it needs to be tended.
That means monitoring it and its situations
and rescuing any performance that starts to
fall.
Under normal circumstances,
self-monitoring of behaviors should be
sufficient (formal monitoring by others is
required in some cases, such as that of new
members). All team members are responsible
for keeping a lookout for any unexpected
situational changes, for tracking progress
toward the ``we'' objectives, and for helping
each other as warranted in tracking the ``me''
objectives.
Performance is falling whenever any
troublesome short behavior occurs and/or
there is a risk that one or more objectives will
not be met. In such cases, the team may need
to rely on their performance consultants who
have been chosen and trained to take on the
extra, helper roles of mentors, coaches, and
resource guides.
Appraising performance
Performance appraisal is traditionally done
through ratings, which usually turn out to be
dishonest because it is so easy to fudge them
up or down the rating scale. Organizations
thus tend to go from one popular fad to
another searching for a way to get honest
ratings, but the schemes invariably fail, which
is why performance appraisal is a subject
people love to hate. Giving up on
performance appraisal, as some critics
advocate doing, is not an acceptable option
because to do so prevents accountability. The
solution adopted for the new model,
therefore, is to use not ratings, but questions
requiring yes/no answers. A ``yes'' is much
harder to fudge when the true answer is ``no''.
The model also requires both ``we'' and
``me'' appraisals. Some critics argue against
the use of individual appraisals in team-based
organizations and, concomitantly, against
giving individual rewards. But adopting this
argument does not allow for individual
accountability and glosses over the fact that
since each member has individual objectives,
each member's behavior and results can be
appraised. Furthermore, by not connecting

rewards to individual performance, any stars,
or most valuable performers (MVPs) will
eventually leave, and any slackers will get a
free ride.
This third phase begins with each team
member doing a ``me'' appraisal, that is,
separately appraising his or her own
performance by answering a series of
questions like these:
(1) How many objectives?
(2) All met?
(3) Which one(s), if any, were clearly
exceeded, and what were the extra
gains?
(4) Which, if any, objectives more closely
tied to which business unit goals?
(5) Significant contribution to any
objective(s) listed in Q4?
(6) If yes, briefly describe contribution.
(7) Any unexpected and very tough
obstacles encountered?
(8) If yes, which objective(s) involved and,
briefly, what were they?
(9) Anything (else) special about your
performance?
(10) If yes, briefly describe.
(11) Your behavior consistently positive (see
examples of short behavior)?
(12) If not, briefly describe what happened if
not already documented.
(13) Two people who can review your
behaviors?
(14) Two people whose behavior you know
well?
(15) Nominate anyone as an MVP?
(16) If yes, who and briefly explain why.
(17) Any performance skills you need/want
developed?
(18) If yes, identify and briefly explain what
and why.
(19) Data insufficient on results/any answers
difficult to verify?
(20) If yes, identify and briefly explain what
and why.
Certain of these questions are needed to
determine if any team members should get
larger performance rewards. Take two
members, for instance, where the behavior of
both had been consistently positive (Q11),
and both had met all their objectives (Q2). If
that were the only information available, then
the two individuals should share equally in
any reward. But if one member has more
favorable answers to certain other questions
(Q1, Q3-10) and perhaps was also nominated

There is another reason for including
questions 9-10. They are intended to appraise
and account for performance when it is
impossible or unnecessary to plan ahead for
all possibilities. For instance, there may arise
during the performance year unexpected
opportunities that must be seized at the
moment with no need or time to write out
objectives or perhaps even to know what to
expect. Or it may not be possible or necessary
to prescribe all results in advance for the work
of a team member with an extra role such as a
performance consultant.
The reason for question 13 is that two
knowledgeable persons, one on the same team
and someone outside the team, need to be
identified so that they can also answer
questions 11-12 about the person's behavior.
Appraisal of it is obviously more subjective
than appraisal of results and is also a more
sensitive matter, especially when there has
been a pattern of short behavior. The two
reviews, therefore, provide an independent
perspective.
Questions 19-20 are intended to flag
appraisals for closer review in cases where
data are insufficient and the appraisals may
thus be more difficult to verify.
As long as they elicit the same kind of
information, the appraisal questions actually
used do not have to be identical to the ones
suggested, but they do need to be the same for
all individuals. If questions 11-12 about
behavior seem too direct and possibly
self-incriminating, for instance, less direct
questions could be substituted, like these two:
(11) Feedback/rescue efforts from others
about behavior?
(12) If yes, briefly describe what happened.
After doing the ``me'' appraisal, each
individual gets two reviews of his/her
behavior. If the reviewers concur with the
person's answers to questions 11-12, they
certify the answers. If one or both reviewers
do not concur with the answers, they meet
with the appraised person, ask for and review
any documentation (e.g. of any formal
monitoring or rescue efforts), and try to reach
a consensus on the answers. If none is
reached, that part of the appraisal is
forwarded to the appraised person's team
without certification, along with a

documented basis for not having certified the
answers.
Teams then review the ``me'' appraisals.
This ought to be fairly pro forma for the
results part of performance as long as
objective and corroborating data are available.
The same should be true in reviewing the
answers to the questions about behavior if the
answers had already been certified.
Where the team needs to concentrate its
review obviously is on less substantiated
appraisals, disputed appraisals, and any
nominations for MVP. In arriving at decisions
on these three types of cases when they occur,
the team should get substantiating
information if it is available or can be deduced
(e.g. from team results). The team may also
need to get more input from the two reviewers
as well as from the appraised person in cases
of uncertified appraisals of behavior. In cases
where there is still uncertainty over the final
appraisal, the team may give the affected
members the benefit of the doubt or forward
the appraisals on to the PRB for a final
determination. However, these options
should not be taken until after the ``we''
appraisals are done because it is possible they
could change some of the ``me'' appraisals.
The team will also need to respond
constructively to any members who indicated
in their answers to questions 17-18 that they
want to acquire more performance skills.
Various options are available if personal
development would benefit the person and
would not disrupt the team's plans. A mentor
or coach could be picked to help the person,
roles could be exchanged within the team, an
exchange with another team might be made if
feasible, etc.
The next step is for the team to do the ``we''
appraisal; that is, they appraise their own
team performance. Someone outside the team
who knows the team's performance relatively
well is invited to attend this second meeting to
reinforce objectivity. The team does the ``we''
appraisal by answering questions similar to
the ones listed for the ``me'' appraisals.
In the final step, teams send their ``we''
appraisals, any unresolved ``me'' appraisals,
and all accompanying documentation (e.g.
data on results) to the PRB. The PRB then
reviews all appraisals and all related
documentation. It may ask a team to present
its case or a team member to present his or
her case and may also consult with any other
knowledgeable teams or sources if there is

While 100 per cent honesty in performance
appraisals is unrealistic, the procedure
described should yield more honest appraisals
compared to ratings. There are several
reasons why. First, making big, bald-faced lies
in response to yes-no questions requires
considerable audacity and is thus less likely to
be made. Second, people know appraisals will
be reviewed. Third, many of the questions ask
for readily verifiable answers. For instance,
whether an objective has been met can be
verified by comparing the documented results
obtained to the criteria that were stated in the
written objective. And on the more sensitive
and less objective questions relating to
behavior, there should be an evidentiary trail
from any formal monitoring required and
from any further interventions when short
behavior has been a problem. Fourth, a
person's performance is often visible to other
team members. It would likely take a
conspiracy of two or more people to conceal a
falsehood (granted, it is certainly not
impossible for that to happen). And finally,
because falsifying appraisals is an unethical
act, the falsifier faces possible punishment if
the act is discovered.
Sanctioning appraised performance
Only a sketch can be given here of this last
phase in which appraised performance is
rewarded, penalized, or neither. To ensure
fairness, all decisions made must adhere to
guidelines created by the PRB. Two examples
of guidelines for rewards could be that any
MVPs and MVTs will be eligible for
meaningfully larger bonuses, and any cases of
``negative success'' (e.g. ill-gotten gains) will
not be eligible at all. Two examples of
guidelines for penalties could be that any
team may surrender to the PRB some or all
responsibility for administering penalties, and
a due process for appealing decisions will be
developed and made available.

Once all appraisals are finalized, the PRB
decides whether none, some, or all of the
teams deserve rewards, such as bonus shares,
and whether any differences among the ``we''
appraisals justify allocating differing share
amounts to the teams. The PRB comes to this
decision after putting the ``we'' appraisals into
qualitatively different categories (e.g. one
would be for any teams to be given larger
shares because the answers to the ``we''
appraisal questions indicate even taller
performance).
Once a team has received its bonus share,
monies from it are allocated by the team
among its members according to whatever
rationale it chooses that is consistent with the
guidelines. For example, the team could
emulate the PRB's procedure and put its
members' ``me'' appraisals into comparable
categories and then decide on bonus
allocations among and within the eligible
categories. The team could also decide to set
aside some of the money for use by the whole
team.
As for penalties, while they should be
decided on a case-by-case basis in order to
consider any unique circumstances, some
uniformity and consistency are needed across
cases to minimize arbitrariness. A general
guide that gives examples of punishable
behaviors and suitable penalties is thus
developed by the PRB. Teams are to follow
the guide whenever it is needed.
Punishing its own members is a difficult
task, but there are good reasons for having the
team do it. If a team is to be fully empowered,
it should not shirk any of its responsibilities.
Second, the team knows its own performance,
tall and short, better than anybody else.
Third, while difficult, teams have the guide to
follow, and they can seek advice from the
PRB. Nevertheless, any team should, if it
chooses, be allowed to give the task to the
PRB. Any team that does so, however, should
lose certain eligibilities, such as, for instance,
bonuses or the maximum on any pay-for-skills
salary plan since the team is forfeiting one of
its roles.
