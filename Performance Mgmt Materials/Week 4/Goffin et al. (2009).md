Social comparison theory (Festinger,1954) implies that it may be more efficacious for job performance raters to compare an employee to other employees
rather than to use typical “absolute” rating standards. We assessed whether
the incorporation of social comparisons into performance appraisals, using
the relative percentile method (RPM), would predict criterion variance beyond that predicted by more traditional absolute ratings of performance. A
sample (N=170) of managers involved in an assessment center was used,
and the center provided criteria by which the relative criterion-related validity
of social-comparative versus noncomparative (absolute) appraisals could be
assessed. Overall, in consonance with a preponderance of earlier research,
social-comparative (RPM) performance appraisals showed incremental criterion-related validity over traditional absolute performance appraisal methods. © 2009 Wiley Periodicals, Inc.

Previous research has investigated the potential benefits of the RPM in performance
appraisal in two main ways. First, in an experiment, Wagner and Goffin (1997) demonstrated that the social-comparative rating
process underlying the RPM resulted in
greater accuracy than a traditional absolute
rating format. Second, Goffin et al.’s field
study (1996) found that the RPM possessed
higher criterion-related validity than a conventional absolute rating format. Outside
the domain of performance appraisal, McCarthy and Goffin (2001) found the RPM to
be advantageous in the context of letters of
reference; Lewis, Goffin, and Olson (2007)
found it to lead to improved accuracy in
personality ratings; and Olson, Goffin,
and Haynes (2007) found it improved
the criterion-related validity of self-rated
attitudes.
Although suggestive of possible advantages associated with the social-comparative
format of the RPM, the latter three studies
were not conducted specifically within the
domain of performance appraisal. Moreover, Wagner and Goffin’s (1997) performance appraisal experiment was limited
with respect to generalizability because it
was conducted in a laboratory setting using
undergraduates as raters. Although Goffin
et al.’s (1996) performance rating study was
conducted in the field, that work has two
main limitations. First, the sample size
(N=88) was relatively small. Second, the criteria by which the criterion-related validity
of performance appraisals was judged (e.g.,
various cognitive ability, personality, and
vocational interest measures) were not as
proximal to the domain of job performance
as might be desirable.

The research reported here further
investigated the criterion-related validity
of a social-comparative performance appraisal process (RPM) relative to two performance appraisal processes that did not
use social comparisons: behavioral observation scales (BOS) and graphic rating
scales (GRS). Our work improves on that of
Goffin et al. (1996) in three important
ways, thereby allowing a more credible
evaluation of the use of social comparisons
in performance appraisal.
First, this work benefited from a more adequate sample size (N=170). Second, it afforded more plausible criterion measures.
Specifically, the participants were managers
who were evaluated in an assessment center
(AC) as part of an organization’s selection
process. The AC was developed on the basis of
a job analysis and involved the sampling of
job-relevant behavior in standardized
conditions. The use of AC variables as criteria
by which to assess the criterion-related validity of performance appraisals is an innovation
that is entirely consistent with the general
consensus that the AC provides a valid work
sample measure of managerial job performance (e.g., Atkins & Wood, 2002; Schultz &
Schultz, 1998; Smith, 1991). Our use of the
terms predictor and criteria is more fully explained in the following section. Third, this
work benefited from its separate consideration of a composite and a global example of
the social-comparative strategy (RPM dimensional ratings and an overall RPM rating, respectively), as well as a composite and a global
example of the noncomparative strategy (BOS
and GRS, respectively).

1. Does a social-comparative composite job

variance beyond that predicted by a noncomparative composite job performance

Predictor and Criterion
Terminological Issues
Performance ratings are typically conceived
of as criteria. However, this study evaluated
the validity of performance ratings. From this
perspective, performance ratings were conceived of as predictors (or “tests”) that could
themselves be meaningfully validated against
criteria that are relevant to work behavior.
The criteria we used were AC scores. Our predictor/criterion designation is consistent with
the views of many experts (e.g., Austin, Villanova, Kane, & Bernardin, 1991; James,
1973; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995) who have
emphasized the need to evaluate the validity
of performance ratings. Furthermore, although predictors are typically administered
prior to criteria, this temporal ordering is not
integral to the psychometric concepts of
“predictor” and “criterion.” Thus, in this article, prediction is not meant to imply that
predictors had temporal precedence over
criteria.

Method
Participants and Procedure
The participants were AC candidates (N=170)
competing for various managerial positions
in a large forestry products organization.
After their AC evaluations, the participants
were hired or promoted into managerial positions. The managerial positions belonged to
two broad groups: nontechnical (N=89) and
professional-technical (N=81). Overall, there
were many similarities in the fundamental
roles fulfilled by managers in the nontechnical versus the professional-technical groups;
however, there was a tendency for the nontechnical managers to have more supervisory
responsibilities. There were 150 males and
20 females, and the mean age was 38 years
(SD = 7, range = 23–60). One year following
their participation in the AC, participants’
supervisors rated their job performance using
the performance rating approaches described
here.

AC Scores
The AC provided criterion data that were
used to assess the relative validity of the social-comparative and noncomparative job
performance measures being evaluated. Before formulating the criterion scores, it was
relevant to determine whether the AC variables tended to cluster in terms of the AC
dimensions or the AC exercises. A visual inspection and factor analysis of the AC scores

Job Performance Measures
Performance ratings were made by candidates’ supervisors approximately a year after
the candidates’ AC participation. The raters
were informed that ratings were for research
purposes only. Raters were not permitted to
see ratees’ AC scores.
Job Performance Dimensions
The following eight job performance dimensions, derived from a job analysis, were
common across the nontechnical and professional-technical groups: work practices and
procedures, planning and problem solving,
group cooperation and cross-functional management, promoting safety, communication,
personnel development, customer and supplier relations, and personal work style.
Social-Comparative Job Performance
Ratings
Social comparisons were captured in the performance appraisal process through the use
of the RPM (Goffin et al., 1996; Jelley & Goffin, 2001; Wagner & Goffin, 1997). As shown
in Exhibit 1, the RPM promotes comparisons
of ratees to one another and to all employees
in similar positions in the company. The
RPM involves a parallel rating process whereby
the rater considers all of his or her ratees
simultaneously on each performance dimension. Also, the RPM uses normative (percentile) anchors, which refer to all employees in
similar positions in the company.
Social comparative ratings were made for
the eight job performance dimensions described earlier. Factor analyses supported the
aggregation of the eight dimensional scores
into a single composite. As is typically the
case in performance ratings (see the metaanalysis in Viswesvaran, Schmidt, & Ones,
2005), a principal axis factor analysis

Social-Comparative Rating Process
The superior criterion-related validity of the

composite rating of performance was used.
The social-comparative method was also
superior to the noncomparative method for
predicting both average AC exercise score
and OAR. Arguably, the incremental predictive validity results (R2 and R in Tables III

practical significance of our findings for HR
managers. There was no evidence that the
conventional noncomparative performance
ratings could predict criterion variance beyond that predicted by the social-comparative ratings. On the other hand, both the
social-comparative global and the socialcomparative composite job performance

rating scenarios, a composite (multidimensional) performance rating would be
preferable. Moreover, it was in the case of

Social Comparison
Theory and Performance Appraisal
The integration of social comparison theory
within the context of performance appraisal
is a unique contribution of the study presented in this article. As explained earlier,
the original conceptualization of social
comparison theory suggested that people
compare themselves to others when there is
no objective means of evaluation. More recently, Kruglanski and Mayseless (1990) defined social comparisons more broadly as
“comparative judgments of social stimuli
on particular content dimensions” (p. 196).
This broader definition suggests that not
only do people compare themselves to relevant others, but they also make comparisons among others, as in the case of the
performance appraisal of a group of subordinates. Thus, in addition to its practical
importance, the application of social comparison theory to performance appraisal
also makes a contribution to the social cognition literature by demonstrating that social-comparative processes may improve the
veracity of the evaluations one makes of
others.
Numerous social cognition researchers
have suggested that social comparisons are a
constant and inevitable part of our everyday
lives (e.g., Festinger, 1954; Kruglanski &
Mayseless, 1990; Olson et al., 2000; Suls &
Wheeler, 2000). People engage in social comparisons when there is no objective standard to

measure against and when they experience
uncertainty—conditions consistent with a performance appraisal context. Social comparisons occur quickly and automatically, followed
by more careful consideration (Gilbert, Giesler,
& Morris, 1995). In two experimental paradigms, Gilbert et al. (1995) showed that social
comparisons are so automatic that people
make them even when they are provided with
only nondiagnostic (irrelevant) information.
After making an automatic comparison, the
subjects in their study who had sufficient cognitive resources (attention) were able to rapidly
and deliberately correct the effects of the nondiagnostic comparison. The social comparison
process therefore appears to consist of two
steps: an automatic stage, which is beyond a
person’s control, and a more deliberate stage,
in which the person considers the relevance of
the comparison information. We believe that
performance rating formats such as the RPM
that promote social comparisons are likely to
be advantageous because they capitalize on a
natural, automatic judgment process and stimulate deliberate reprocessing of the relevance
and accuracy of those social judgments.
The RPM that was used here (see Exhibit
1) promoted the use of social comparison
by requiring a rater to consider all of his or
her ratees simultaneously when making performance judgments. We believe that this
parallel processing of performance information, in conjunction with the RPM’s percentile response scale, is beneficial in that other
ratees serve as useful benchmarks through
which raters can more accurately calibrate
their ratings. In contrast, conventional rating formats such as the BOS and GRS rely
on serial processing, wherein raters are expected to consider each ratee individually.
Serial processing of performance judgments
about ratees may fail to take advantage of
natural social-comparative processes, thus
explaining the generally lower validity of
noncomparative ratings in studies where
comparative and noncomparative formats
have been evaluated in relation to each
other (Bommer et al., 1995; Goffin et al.,
1996; Heneman, 1986; Lewis et al., 2007;
McCarthy & Goffin, 2001; Olson et al.,
2007; Wagner & Goffin, 1997).

Arguably, any type of judgmental performance rating requires that raters use some type
of comparison standard or referent. A comparison standard represents the benchmark
against which a rater compares the ratees’ performance. There is evidence that raters prefer
benchmarks that are high in availability and
relevancy (Schrader & Steiner, 1996). The approach taken in the RPM, in which other ratees
serve as benchmarks, is likely to heighten both
availability and relevancy. Moreover, the RPM
refers to “all employees in similar positions” to
facilitate meaningful percentile scaling of responses. We expected that supervisors would
have a reasonable degree of familiarity with
this referent group because of the continual
and pervasive tendency to compare individuals (e.g., Festinger, 1954; Kruglanski & Mayseless, 1990). Furthermore, research in the area
of utility analysis has found that supervisors
have no difficulty providing estimates of the
value to an organization of employees who fall
at the 15th, 50th, and 85th percentiles using
referent groups such as the RPMs, and that
these estimates tend to be accurate (Bobko,
Karren, & Parkington, 1983; Schmidt, Hunter,
McKenzie, & Muldrow, 1979). Arguably, the
referent group inferences required in utility
estimation are more difficult to make than
those required by the RPM because supervisors
must not only have a sense of what the range
of performance is, but also what a given level
of performance is worth.

Conclusion
In summary, this study compared the criterion-related validity of a social-comparative performance appraisal process (RPM)

with that of two absolute performance
appraisal processes (BOS and GRS).
We found that the use of a performance
rating format that encourages social
comparisons resulted in higher levels of
criterion-related validity. The superior
criterion-related validity of the social-comparative format was evident regardless of
whether a global or a composite rating of
performance was used. We used a sample
of managers from a forestry products organization, but it would be useful to determine whether the results set out here
generalize to other organizations and other
occupational groups. The similarity of
our overall findings with those of Goffin et
al. (1996), Heneman (1986), Nathan and
Alexander (1988), and Wagner and Goffin
(1997) suggests that social-comparative
processes may well be worthy of greater
consideration in performance appraisals.
Further work is required to investigate
the specific mechanisms through which
social comparisons may affect job performance ratings.
