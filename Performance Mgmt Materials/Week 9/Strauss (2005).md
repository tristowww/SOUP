Abstract
Purpose – To examine the relationship between self-esteem and job performance using others’
perceptions of self-esteem and to examine agreement in ratings of self-esteem across sources.
Design/methodology/approach – A sample of 143 sales representatives, 113 supervisors, 420
peers, 435 customers, and 510 family and friends completed Rosenberg’s measure of self-esteem and a
measure of acquaintanceship. Peers and supervisors rated the subjects’ job performance. Correlations
and hierarchical regression were used to explore the relationships.
Findings – Customer, peer, and supervisor perceptions of subjects’ self-esteem related significantly
to peer and supervisor performance ratings, whereas self and family/friends perceptions did not. There
was limited support for the acquaintanceship effect (greater agreement across sources when
familiarity is greater), while context affected agreement (same context sources had greater agreement).
Practical implications – The study highlights the importance of looking at an employee from a
variety of perspectives. Also, training employees to develop self-enhancing behaviors may enhance
their outcomes. Finally, training raters that their perceptions of co-workers’ self-esteem may influence
evaluations of performance could reduce unconscious errors.
Originality/value – If this had been a traditional study measuring self-esteem’s impact on
performance ratings, no significant relationships could have been reported since individuals’
perceptions of their own self-esteem were not valid predictors of performance ratings. It may be the
individual’s public self-esteem (e.g. impression management skills) that influences performance
ratings. In particular, workplace sources perceived high self-esteem as being important to job
performance. The validity of self-esteem may be understated through reliance on the self-report
method alone.
Keywords Performance appraisal, Self esteem, Performance measures

organizations. Research in performance appraisal has changed from an emphasis on
psychometric issues to examining cognitive processing of raters and the social and
contextual variables which impact performance evaluation (e.g. Judge and Ferris, 1993;
Wexley and Klimoski, 1984). For example, research has considered the impact of rater
and ratee demographic characteristics (e.g. Liden et al., 1996; Mount et al., 1997;
Pulakos et al., 1996), the effects of similarity (perceived and actual;, e.g. Strauss et al.,

Paralleling the rise in interest in performance appraisals is an increased interest in
the relationship between self-concept traits of personality and workplace outcomes
(e.g. Barrick and Mount, 1991; Gardner and Pierce, 1998; Judge et al., 1998a; Salgado,
1997). Judge et al. (1997) consider self-esteem to be the most fundamental self-concept
trait because it represents the overall value that one places on oneself as a person. Few
can deny the popularity of self-esteem. According to Leary and Baumeister (2000, p. 1),
“self-esteem ranks among the most extensively studied constructs in behavioral
science”. A quick search using self-esteem as the subject on PsycINFO (an online
version of Psychological Abstracts) resulted in over 12,900 articles dating back to 1965.
An internet search yielded over one million hits on self-esteem. Many of these articles
and internet sites discuss raising self-esteem, assuming that higher self-esteem leads to
better outcomes (e.g. higher job performance).
A recent article by Baumeister et al. (2003) questions Western society’s focus on
self-esteem, noting that attempts to raise self-esteem have not necessarily led to better
outcomes. However, because the underlying belief in Western societies is that high
self-esteem is salient to positive outcomes (true or not), it is an important construct to
study in the workplace because perceptions of others’ self-esteem may influence
individuals’ outcomes. The current study’s primary purpose, then, is to evaluate the
relationship between self-esteem and job performance using others’ perceptions of
individuals’ self-esteem. This is the first study to evaluate 360-degree perspectives of
self-esteem in a field setting. Self-esteem is expected to manifest itself in self-enhancing
self-presentation behaviors or impression management behaviors that influence
observers’ perceptions of a person’s self-esteem and this external, perceived self-esteem
is expected to relate significantly with performance ratings by supervisors and peers.
Additionally, it is expected the agreement between self and others’ perceptions of
self-esteem will be greater when the rater knows the subject better. Finally, agreement
between other source perceptions is expected to be greater when subjects are observed
in the same context (e.g. the workplace) versus different contexts (e.g. workplace
versus home).
Self-esteem theory and research
Studies have investigated self-esteem at many levels – global, intermediate (e.g.
organizational-based), and task specific (see Gardner and Pierce, 1998). Additionally,
self-esteem has been characterized as both a trait variable (long-term, affectively laden
self-evaluation) and a state variable (short-term, situational, affectively laden
self-evaluation; see Leary and Baumeister, 2000). However, most organizational
research focuses on trait, global self-esteem, often defined as an overall evaluation of
personal worth that people make and maintain with regard to themselves (see Judge
et al., 1997; Rosenberg, 1965).
Theoretical approaches to self-esteem vary from suggesting that high levels of
self-esteem should relate to positive outcomes and vice versa (e.g. Korman, 1970) to
arguing that low self-esteem people are more adaptive in their behavior based on
feedback or situations than high self-esteem individuals (Brockner, 1988). Korman’s
(1970) self-consistency theory hypothesizes that an individual’s self-esteem is a
determinant of the outcome he/she will seek to attain. According to Korman, all else
equal, individuals are motivated to perform on a task or job in a manner consistent
with their self-esteem. Similarly, Festinger’s (1957) cognitive dissonance theory argues

Baumeister (2000) argue that the self-esteem motive is to avoid loss of self-esteem and
people act to maintain their current level of self-esteem. Alternatively, Brockner’s
(1988) behavioral plasticity theory suggests those with low self-esteem are more
responsive to external cues than those with high self-esteem. Brockner’s hypothesis is
that self-esteem moderates the relationship between situational factors and individual
behavior. Although studies have found support for this hypothesis (see LePine and
Van Dyne, 1998), there is some evidence that people with moderate levels of self-esteem
are more responsive to external cues than either high or low self-esteem individuals
(see Leary and Baumeister, 2000).
While there is mixed evidence supporting a direct relationship between self-esteem
and job performance (see Baumeister et al., 2003; Brockner, 1988; and Tharanou, 1979
for reviews), recent evidence has found support for this relationship (e.g. Carson et al.,
1997; Gardner and Pierce, 1998; Renn and Prien, 1995). Erez and Judge (2001) found a
direct positive relationship between self-esteem and job performance (r ¼ 0:26,
p . 0:01). Bono and Judge (2003) summarized current research on core self-evaluations
reporting a factor loading of 0.91 for self-esteem and a correlation of 0.23 between core
self-evaluations and job performance – providing indirect empirical evidence for the
importance of self-esteem. Further, in a recent meta-analysis, Judge and Bono (2001)
found corrected correlations of 0.24 between self-esteem and job satisfaction and 0.26
between self-esteem and job performance. However, a large amount of variability in the
individual correlations for the self-esteem – job performance relationship was also
found – the 80 percent credibility interval included zero (2 0.05 to 0.57).
Self-reports of self-esteem
There has been concern in the literature about self-ratings in general. In Atwater and
Yammarino’s (1997) review of self-other ratings of performance, they report that
research has shown self-ratings and self-assessments to be problematic (e.g. unreliable,
invalid, inflated, biased, and generally suspect and inaccurate when compared to
others’ ratings). Several researchers have addressed self-report measures of self-esteem
specifically. Greenwald and Banaji (1995, p. 10) argue that:
Studies done in the last few decades have established that a majority of almost any group of
research subjects reports favorable judgments when asked to provide self-evaluations,
including the self-evaluative responses to items on inventory measures of self-esteem.

Brockner (1988) states that self-reports of self-esteem may not be valid due to
individuals’ unwillingness or inability to self-report accurately. Mruk (1995) notes that
self-reports of self-esteem may be less valid due to a defensive mechanism that may be
operating – individuals may feel compelled to report higher self-esteem than they truly
feel due to an unconscious protection mechanism or conscious effort to manage the
impressions of others. Similarly, Locke et al. (1996) note that there is a strong need for
positive self-esteem and this need may cause individuals to use defensive maneuvers
such as relying on defensive values (e.g. being smart, attractive, wealthy), defensive
mechanisms (e.g. repression, evasion), or defensive actions (e.g. avoiding threatening
situations). Thus, one of the reasons for inconsistent results on the direct relationship

between self-esteem and job performance may be the self-report method of assessing
self-esteem.
Baumeister et al. (2003) argue that self-reports of self-esteem (the value one places on
oneself) are not necessarily accurate assessments of self-worth nor do they need to be.
According to these authors, self-ratings are simply perceptions of reality. They cite
research that individuals rating themselves high on self-esteem have been found to
overestimate their own intelligence and attractiveness. Similarly, Shrauger (1972, see
Baumeister et al., 1989) found no relationship between self-scores of self-esteem and
actual performance on a task, but a strong relationship between individuals’
self-ratings of performance and their self-report self-esteem. Tharanou (1979) argued
that most researchers do not consider the defensive presentation of self-reports and
suggested that other raters or other measures of self-esteem be used.
Others’ perceptions of self-esteem
Given self-esteem is one’s feeling of overall worth about him/herself (Judge et al., 1997;
Rosenberg, 1965), it is conceptually difficult to understand what is being measured by
others’ perceptions of a person’s self-esteem. As Baumeister et al. (2003) note,
individuals’ beliefs (e.g. about their own self-esteem) shape actions and actions shape
both individuals’ social realities and the realities of the people around these
individuals. Leary and Baumeister (2000) argue the self-esteem motive pertains to both
the public and private self and the two are intertwined. According to Baumeister et al.
(1989), different levels of self-esteem are associated with different patterns of
self-presentation, which are manifested in observable behaviors. For example, research
suggests high self-esteem individuals are more likely to persist but also know when to
quit (Baumeister et al., 2003). Additionally, high self-esteem individuals are more likely
to draw attention to themselves, seek to stand out, present themselves in an ambitious,
aggressive, self-aggrandizing style, claim desirable traits, take risks, accept
responsibility in advance, and seek out comparisons with other people whereas low
self-esteem individuals seek protection and display more cautious, conservative
behavior (Baumeister et al., 1989). Baumeister et al. (1989) argue a greater use of
self-enhancing self-presentational strategies exists among high versus low self-esteem
individuals.
Self-enhancement is one ingratiation tactic used to manage impressions of others
(see Kumar and Beyerlein, 1991). Impression management is defined as:
. . . any behavior that alters or maintains a person’s image in the eyes of another and that has
as its purpose the attainment of some valued goal (Villanova and Bernardin, 1989, p. 299).

According to Leary and Kowalski (1990), impression management is a function of five
factors:
(1) self-concept (the way a person views him/herself);
(2) desired identity image (how a person would like to view him/herself);
(3) role constraints (expectations associated with social roles);
(4) targets’ values (preferences of significant others); and
(5) current or potential social image (how a person is currently regarded or would
like to be regarded by others).

The first two factors (self-concept and desired identity image) relate conceptually to the
self-perception of self-esteem. The other three factors relate to manipulation of others’
reactions or perceptions of the self (role constraints or expectations, targets’ values, and
social image). Baumeister et al. (2003, p. 5) note:
. . . the concept of defensive self-esteem has recently been refined by the distinction between
deceptions of others (i.e. impression management) and deception of the self (see Paulhus,
2002, for a review).

Thus, others’ perceptions of an individual’s self-esteem may be the result of the target
individual’s self-enhancing self-presentation behaviors (i.e. impression management).
Further, impression management theory suggests that individual’s self-presentation
behaviors could differ across social situations (a function of role constraints –
expectations associated with social roles).
According to Hogan (1991), when considering personnel decisions, the appropriate
measure may be one’s social reputation. He notes that observers, in order to make
others’ behavior interpretable, sort behavior into categories to classify people. If others’
perceptions of individuals’ self-esteem are used to categorize people for human
resource decisions (consciously or unconsciously), it is important to understand the
relationship between others’ perspectives and job outcomes and how, or if, multi-source
perspectives of self-esteem converge. As Baumeister et al. (2003, p. 3) note:
. . . over the past few decades, the need for high self-esteem has risen from an individual to a
societal concern . . . the idea that low self-esteem lies at the root of individual and thus societal
problems and dysfunctions, has obvious implications for interventions on both the individual
and the societal level.

While self-esteem is about a feeling one has for oneself, it has become such an
important self-concept construct in Western society that not only are individuals
concerned with self-esteem for ourselves, we make assumptions about the self-esteem
of others which may impact outcomes for those others. Thus, the salience of the
construct warrants study from others’ perspectives – whether what is measured is
public self-esteem, self-enhancement, impression management, influence tactics, or
actual self-esteem is less important than how the perception of others’ self-esteem
impacts potential outcomes.
The hypotheses
Judge et al. (1998b) included a rating of subjects’ self-esteem rated by significant others.
They found moderate correlations between the self and significant other measures in
three samples (0.36-0.51). Both self- and other-reported self-esteem measures were
found to correlate with job satisfaction (0.16-0.66 for self and 0.29-0.35 for other) and
life satisfaction (0.46-0.52 for self and 0.36-0.44 for other). Similarly, Judge et al. (2000)
found that significant others’ ratings of subjects’ self-esteem were related to job
satisfaction and actual and perceived job characteristics (correlations ranging from
0.22 to 0.26, p , 0:01). However, the study found that self-reports were more highly
correlated with job satisfaction and perceived job characteristics – which could be
partially explained by common method variance. A study by Mount et al. (1994)
assessed the relationship between personality dimensions (as rated by self and
observers) and performance ratings. They found others’ perspectives of personality
dimensions accounted for significant variance in performance ratings beyond

self-ratings alone. Recent research has also found that self-enhancement is positively
related to a more favorable impact on others (Taylor et al., 2003). Furthermore,
impression management has been found to have a significant, indirect impact on
performance ratings (Wayne and Liden, 1995). Kristof-Brown et al. (2002) found greater
use of self-promotion impression management tactics led to interviewer perceptions of
person-job fit. Similarly, McFarland et al. (2002) found influence tactics (including
ingratiation) were positively correlated with interview ratings of job candidates.
The current study tests two hypotheses. The primary purpose is to test the
relationship between others’ perceptions (friends and family members, peers,
supervisors, and customers) of an individual’s self-esteem and job performance. This
builds on work by Judge et al. (1998b, 2000) by including multiple perspectives of an
individual’s self-esteem and two perspectives of performance (peer and supervisor
ratings). Based on the above-cited research on impression management,
self-enhancement, and self-esteem, it is expected that others’ perceptions of an
individual’s self-esteem will relate significantly with job performance. Furthermore,
similar to Mount et al. (1994), others’ perceptions of a target’s self-esteem should
account for significant variance in performance ratings beyond self-perceptions alone
(i.e. our public self-esteem adds to prediction over and above our private self-esteem).
H1a. Other’s perceptions of a subject’s self-esteem will be positively related to
performance ratings.
H1b. Other’s perceptions of a subject’s self-esteem will account for significant
variance in performance ratings beyond self-ratings alone.
A second purpose of this study is to assess when agreement among multiple source
perspectives of self-esteem is more likely to exist. If others’ perceptions of self-esteem
(in lieu of self-report measures) relate strongly to job performance, we need to
understand how and if other source perspectives agree with each other and with
self-ratings (similar to 360-degree ratings in performance appraisal). While others’
perceptions of individuals’ self-esteem are expected to differ across sources and
individual raters, several researchers have considered conditions that may lead to
greater agreement across sources (e.g. Funder and Colvin, 1988; Watson et al., 2000).
One of the more researched moderators of self-other agreement in ratings is
acquaintanceship (the acquaintanceship effect; see Colvin and Funder, 1991). In
general, agreement across judges of personality has been found to improve with
familiarity (e.g. Funder et al. 1995; Paulhus and Bruce, 1992). Trait visibility is also
considered to effect agreement across sources. Although findings are mixed (Watson
et al., 2000; Watson and Clark, 1991), acquaintanceship is likely to have an important
influence on low-visibility dimensions (Albright et al., 1997; Watson et al., 2000). Given
self-esteem may be less visible than other personality-related constructs (e.g.
extraversion), agreement between self-other perceptions of self-esteem should be
greater when the self and other know each other better.
A second condition considered important for convergence (agreement) between
independent other-source perspectives is the situation, or context, in which the
behaviors are observed. Observers tend to agree more when they observe individuals
in the same settings (the “overlap” hypothesis; see Funder et al. (1995)). Agreement
results from observers seeing similar, limited sets of behaviors on which they base
their judgments. Funder et al. (1995) found support for increased levels of agreement in

H2a. Agreement between self- and others’ perceptions of self-esteem will be greater
as others know the person better – specifically, family and friends’
perceptions of subjects’ self-esteem will agree more with subjects’ self-ratings

H2b. Agreement between self- and same source other perspectives of self-esteem
will be greater as others know the person better – for example, perceptions by
customers who know the subject better will align more closely with the
subjects’ self-esteem ratings than perceptions by customers who know the
subject less well.
H2c. Agreement between independent other source perspectives of self-esteem will
be greater for those others who see subjects in the same context – specifically,
peers’, supervisors’, and customers’ perceptions of subjects’ self-esteem will
align more closely with each other than with family and friends perceptions.
Methods
Subjects were 143 sales representatives from a large appliance-manufacturing
organization in the midwest of the USA. The majority of subjects were male (88
percent), averaging 35 years of age with approximately three-five years with the
organization and one-two years in the job. Participants were told that the data
collection was for developmental feedback and research purposes only and they were
clear the data would not be used for administrative purposes. In addition to completing
self-ratings of self-esteem and a brief demographic form, subjects asked five peers, five
customers, and five family members and/or friends to complete measures rating the
subjects’ self-esteem and familiarity with the subject. Supervisors were asked by the
researchers to complete similar forms for research purposes. Both peers and
supervisors were asked to complete a performance rating. Thus, in addition to 143
subjects, there were 113 supervisors, 420 peers, 435 customers, and 510 family/friends
who completed surveys.
Measures
Self-esteem. Self-esteem was measured using Rosenberg’s (1965) ten-item global
self-esteem scale for subjects when rating their own self-esteem (e.g. “I feel I am a

person of worth, at least equal in worth to others.”). All sources rated the subjects’
self-esteem on a five-point Likert scale ranging from “Strongly agree” to “Strongly
disagree”. Items were changed slightly when others were rating their perceptions of the
target individual’s self-esteem (e.g. “This person appears to feel he/she is a person of
worth, at least equal in worth to others.”). Scores on the self-esteem measure were
obtained by averaging the ratings. Coefficient alphas for source ratings were: self, 0.78,
supervisor, 0.87, peers, 0.82, customers, 0.81, and family and friends, 0.85.
Acquaintanceship. Raters’ acquaintanceship with the ratee was measured with two,
single-item measures (“How well do you believe you know this person at work?” and
“How well do you believe you know this person outside of work.”). For customers,
supervisors, and peers, the items were averaged. For friends/family, only the second
item was included in the measure. All sources rated acquaintanceship on a five-point
Likert scale ranging from “Extremely well” to “Not at all”. Coefficient alpha’s were:
supervisor, 0.68, peers, 0.75, and customers, 0.67.
Job performance ratings. Based on an analysis of the sales job, a nine-dimensional
measure of job performance was developed for use in a larger study. Each dimension
was defined by a one-sentence description, followed by three or four interpretive
examples illustrating important facets of that dimension. The dimensions were
initiative (“willing to seek out solutions to problems and learn more of the various
functions involved –, e.g. works extra hard when requested and takes on additional
responsibilities readily”), customer communications (“demonstrates a professional sales
approach with customers – , e.g. communicates effectively with customers”),
interpersonal skills (“practices basic communication skills, maintains good
interpersonal relations with customers, managers, and other [company name]
personnel”), commitment to the job and job attitude (“demonstrates a consistent,
dependable work effort, and maintains a positive attitudes toward fellow employees and
managers”), job knowledge (“understands work responsibilities, scope of job tasks, and
routines to be performed”), quality of work (“completes work thoroughly, accurately,
and according to specifications”), quantity of work (“maintains steady, acceptable level
of work output”), account management (“maintains existing accounts and identifies new
leads”), and overall performance (“a summary evaluation of overall performance against
work expectations –, e.g. consider extent to which objectives were achieved, assess
employee contributions towards objectives, recognize difficulty of objectives”).
The subjects’ supervisors and peers rated the sales representatives’ performance on
a five-point Likert scale ranging from consistently exceeds job requirements to
somewhat below job requirements. Again, raters were informed that the ratings were
being collected for research purposes only. Overall performance was the mean of the
ratings across all nine dimensions. The coefficient alphas were 0.90 for supervisors and
0.92 for peers.
Analysis. Analyses are reported for averaged self-esteem ratings within rating
sources and average peer performance ratings. The average intra-class correlations
(ICCs) for each source (see Shrout and Fleiss, 1979) were as follows: peers’ ratings of
self-esteem, 0.27 (F ¼ 1:38, p ¼ 0:10); customers’ ratings of self-esteem, 0.28 (F ¼ 1:39,
p ¼ 0:10); family and friends’ ratings of self-esteem, 0.76 (F ¼ 4:23, p , 0:001); and
peers’ ratings of performance, 0.40 (F ¼ 1:67, p , 0:05). Hierarchical regression
analyses were run to examine the incremental validity of observer perceptions over
self-perceptions of self-esteem.

self-esteem and supervisor and peer performance ratings are shown in Table I. The
first hypothesis (H1a) tested the strength of the relationships between others’
perceptions of a subject’s self-esteem and performance ratings. First, the correlations

self-esteem and performance ratings. However, customer, peer, and supervisor
perceptions of self-esteem related significantly to both peer and supervisor
performance ratings. Not surprisingly, when ratings of self-esteem and performance
were from the same source, the correlations were higher (r ¼ 0:43 for supervisors and

Discussion
If this had been a traditional study measuring self-esteem’s impact on performance
ratings, no significant relationships could have been reported since individuals’
perceptions of their own self-esteem were not valid predictors of performance ratings
by either supervisors or peers. However, examining self-esteem from multiple
perspectives suggests that it may not be the self-perceptions of self-esteem (i.e. one’s
self-report of their own self-worth) that influence performance ratings, but the
individual’s public self-esteem (e.g. impression management skills, public self-esteem,
or self-enhancing self-presentation behaviors; see Baumeister et al., 1989).
The primary purpose of this paper was to examine the relationship between others’
perceptions of individuals’ self-esteem and job performance. It is important to note that
the findings associated with supervisor ratings of performance are more critical given
they more typically result in tangible outcomes (e.g. promotions) for employees.
However, peers are often included in hiring interviews and based on empirical research
(e.g. Kristof-Brown et al., 2002; McFarland et al., 2002), self-enhancing behaviors impact
interviewers’ decisions. Work-related observers’ (supervisors, peers, and customers)
perceptions of an individual’s self-esteem significantly related to both peer and
supervisor ratings of job performance whereas the non-work-related observer (family
and friends) perceptions did not. Both zero-order correlations (Table I) and regression
analyses (Table II) underscore the impact that workplace public self-esteem has on
performance ratings by supervisors. For peer performance ratings, the strength of the
relationships are greater for customers’ and peers’ perceptions of subjects’ self-esteem
than for supervisor ratings of performance (see Table II). When all variables are in the
regression model, supervisor perceptions of individuals’ self-esteem were no longer
significantly related to peer performance ratings. In sum, however, the general trend
suggests that workplace others’ perceptions of self-esteem may be predictive of job
performance.
The differing relationships between others’ perspectives of self-esteem and
performance ratings by peers and supervisors can be partially explained by the real
differences in the source performance ratings (e.g. Harris and Schaubroeck, 1988). For
example, Facteau and Bartholomew (2001) found the lack of convergence across
performance rating sources is due to substantive differences across rater groups. While
the correlation between peers’ and supervisors’ ratings of performance was significant
(r ¼ 0:51, p , 0:001) in this study, the unexplained variance could be partially due to
substantive differences in what raters see as important in performance.
To further understand the nature of the relationships found, specific performance
dimensions for both peer and supervisor ratings of performance were explored. In the
past decade, there has been growing interest in the job performance criterion domain
(Borman and Motowidlo, 1993, 1997; Johnson, 2001; Motowidlo et al., 1997). Borman
and Motowidlo (1993) expanded the job performance criterion domain to include
contextual performance in addition to task performance. Contextual performance
includes behaviors that support the broader, organizational, social, and psychological
environment whereas task performance includes behaviors that support the technical
core. Self-report personality measures have been found to relate to contextual
performance to a greater extent than task performance (e.g. Borman and Motowidlo,
1997; LePine and Van Dyne, 2001; Mohammed et al., 2002). However, in the current
study, there was little evidence to suggest that others’ perceptions of individuals’

Interestingly, subjects’ self-report self-esteem was lower compared to observers
from all other perspectives. Thus, subjects did not appear to have a defensive
mechanism operating (Locke et al., 1996; Mruk, 1995) in which they felt compelled to
report unduly high self-esteem. This may have been due to subjects’ recognition that
the information was being collected for research purposes only. However, the fact that
friends and family rated subjects’ self-esteem higher than any other source (and
significantly higher than self-reports of self-esteem –, e.g. t ¼ 4:374, p , 0:001) may
have been due, at least in part, to wanting to make the subject look good, knowing that
the survey was being conducted at the workplace regardless of its purpose.
Another purpose of the current study was to examine agreement in ratings of
self-esteem across rating sources. Specifically, the acquaintanceship effect and the
overlap hypothesis (see Funder et al., 1995) were tested. H2a considered self-other
agreement, predicting that for sources likely to know the ratee better, the agreement
between self- and others’ perceptions of a subject’s self-esteem would be greater than
for sources likely to know the ratee less well (the acquaintanceship effect). Family and
friends were expected to know the subject better than customers, peers, or supervisors
and thus agreement between self- and others’ self-esteem ratings for this source should
be higher. This hypothesis was not supported. However, past research has found
strangers were as likely as friends to rate a subject’s personality similarly to the
subject’s self-rating (e.g. Albright et al., 1997).
H2b predicted that acquaintanceship would moderate the agreement between selfand same-source other perceptions of self-esteem. Thus, it was expected that for a
single “other” source (e.g. peers), those more acquainted with the ratee should agree
more with self-ratings of self-esteem than those less acquainted the ratee. This held
only for customers’ ratings of self-esteem (see Figure 1). Those customers who knew
the subject better had more similar ratings to subjects’ self-ratings of self-esteem than
those who knew the subject less well. Interestingly, those who knew the subjects less
well were more likely to rate subjects’ self-esteem high no matter what the self-rating
was. Range restriction in scores on self-esteem and acquaintanceship across sources
may have limited the possibility of finding other significant interactions. In sum, there
was limited support found for the acquaintanceship effect when using self-esteem, a
less visible trait, as the construct of interest.
H2c (the overlap hypothesis) considered the importance of context (workplace
versus home) in agreement between independent other-source perspectives of the
subjects’ self-esteem. Not surprisingly, peers’ and supervisors’ perceptions converged
significantly. Similar to results found by Funder et al. (1995), customer and peer ratings
(similar context) converged significantly but to a lesser extent than peer and supervisor
ratings (same context). The significant agreement between peers’ and family and
friends’ ratings of self-esteem could be due to the research design – subjects may have
used peers as friends to rate them and there may be overlap between the two rating

sources. The importance of context in this study is highlighted by the fact that only
workplace sources were valid predictors of performance ratings. As noted in Mount
et al. (1994, p. 278), “observer ratings may be better predictors than self-ratings because
there is greater point-to-point correspondence between the predictor and the criterion”.
Limitations and future research
The current study has certain limitations. First, what others’ perceive as a person’s
self-esteem (the public self-esteem) could be perceptions reflective of impression
management, influence tactics, or self-enhancing behaviors that have nothing to do with
an individual’s actual self-esteem. Further, other-perceived self-esteem could be spillover
(i.e. the halo effect) from other perceptions about that individual (e.g. perceptions of
competence). However, the argument is not that what others’ perceive as an individual’s
self-esteem is an accurate measure of that construct (albeit, it is possible that it might be
given Leary and Baumeister (2000) argument that public self-esteem is intertwined with
private self-esteem). The validity coefficients reported in the study demonstrate that
workplace others’ perceptions of a target’s self-esteem predict both peer and supervisor
ratings of job performance whereas self-reported self-esteem does not.
Furthermore, based on the research design of this study, directional inferences
related to the influence of others’ perceptions of a subject’s self-esteem are not possible
to determine. It is not possible to know if higher levels of self-esteem caused higher
levels of performance ratings or if subjects who received higher performance ratings
were then perceived as having higher self-esteem. As Tharanou (1979) argued, other
measures of self-esteem may need to be developed. Additionally, longitudinal studies
should be conducted to assess causality.
Another concern in the study is that the average intra-class correlations (ICC) for
sources were marginally significant for peers and customers (see methods section). As
noted in Mount et al. (1994), although averaging all possible ratings results in higher
predictor reliability, significant findings may or may not be attributable to the effects
of different perspectives. Furthermore, the apparent range restriction in scores of
self-esteem across sources may in part be due to the use of sales’ people as subjects.
Sales’ jobs require the ability to manage presentation to a greater degree than many
other job types. Future research should include subjects in a variety of job settings.
Finally, the lack of an objective measure of performance is a limitation. Obtaining
subjective performance ratings by two different sources, peers and supervisors, was an
attempt to mitigate this limitation. Future studies should incorporate both objective
and subjective performance measures to adequately assess these relationships.
Practical considerations
The results demonstrate that others’ perceptions (specifically, workplace sources) of a
target’s self-esteem relate to performance appraisals by supervisors and peers. Raters
should be trained to recognize that their own perceptions of others may be influenced
by other public behaviors and that these judgments may affect their ratings of
performance (not unlike other common unconscious rating errors – e.g. halo, similarity,
liking). Additionally, organizations may want to consider using others’ perceptions for
developmental feedback. Given trait self-esteem is considered a relatively stable
self-concept (Gardner and Pierce, 1998; Leary and Baumeister, 2000), it is unlikely that
one’s internal sense of worth could be altered via training. However, individuals’ public

As with all training, it is important to be specific about the types of behaviors that
would enhance outcomes. Given self-enhancing behaviors have been found to lead to
favorable outcomes (Taylor et al., 2003) and impression management has been found to
lead to higher performance ratings (Wayne and Liden, 1995), developing specific
self-enhancing behaviors could enhance others’ perceptions and consequently,
employees’ outcomes. Research has shown that job candidates using
internal-controllable attributions (e.g. confessing past performance was your own
fault) versus internal or external uncontrollable attributions (e.g. sickness, blaming the
system) received more favorable ratings (Silvester et al., 2002). Similarly,
Kristof-Brown et al. (2002) found self-promotion impression management tactics
such as describing past experiences in a positive manner and underscoring how one’s
own characteristics and competencies match the demands and requirements of the
work context led to interviewer perceptions of person-job fit. Non-verbal influence
strategies (e.g. dress, facial expression, eye contact) were also found to relate to positive
outcomes. McFarland et al. (2002) found soft influence tactics (rational persuasion,
inspirational appeals, consultation, ingratiation, and personal appeals) were positively
correlated with interview ratings whereas hard influence tactics (pressure, upward
appeals, and punitive threats) were not.
This study highlights the importance of looking at an employee from a variety of
perspectives. Although there was more agreement for within-workplace rating sources,
there were clear differences as well. Different perspectives may be good for
developmental purposes but, as with performance appraisal, using 360o information
for administrative purposes is a concern (Lepsinger and Lucia, 1997). Thus, collecting
others’ perceptions of a subject’s self-esteem for selection purposes is not
recommended. Initial impressions of someone else’s self-esteem are likely influenced
by the ability of the potential hire to manage impressions, not the ability of the
perceiver to accurately assess levels of self-esteem. However, it is critical to recognize
that in making staffing decisions, we may be unconsciously including our perceptions
of others’ self-esteem, which may inhibit good hiring or promotion decisions.
Summary
In sum, there is a distinction between the relationships found between self-esteem and
performance ratings when self-esteem is based on self-reports as compared to observer
perceptions. This study demonstrates that supervisors’, peers’, and customers’
perceptions (workplace sources) of self-esteem relate significantly to job performance
ratings whereas family and friend’ perceptions and self-reports of self-esteem do not.
Thus, workplace sources perceive high self-esteem as important to job performance.
The validity of self-esteem may be understated through reliance on the self-report
method alone. This study also found limited evidence that acquaintanceship led to
closer relationships between private and public measures of self-esteem and partial
support to indicate that viewing the subject in the same context leads to higher
agreement across rating sources.
