**Grant, A.M., Christianson, M.K., & Price, R.H. (2007).  Happiness, health, or relationships? Managerial practices and employee well-being tradeoffs. *Academy of Management Perspectives. *<https://doi.org/10.5465/amp.2007.26421238>**

- This paper delves into the complex trade-offs managers make between practices that boost happiness, health, or relationships among employees. It underscores that managerial practices which heighten one aspect of well-being can potentially undermine others, necessitating a nuanced approach to promote holistic employee well-being.

**Follow-Up Questions:**

- How should managers prioritize between employee happiness, health, and relationships?

- Can there be a universal approach, or should managerial practices be customized for each employee?

- How do organizational goals align or conflict with the objective of promoting employee well-being?

**Hobfoll, S. E., Halbesleben, J., Neveu, J. P., & Westman, M. (2018). Conservation of resources in the organizational context: The reality of resources and their consequences. *Annual Review of Organizational Psychology and Organizational Behavior*, *5*, 103-128. <https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-orgpsych-032117-104640>**

- The authors delve into the dynamics of resource conservation in organizations. They address how resources, both tangible and intangible, are vital for employees' performance and well-being. The effective management and conservation of these resources can influence organizational outcomes and employee health positively.

**Follow-Up Questions:**

1.  How can organizations ensure effective resource conservation while promoting employee well-being?

2.  What role do managers play in balancing resource allocation for organizational efficiency and employee health?

3.  How should organizations respond when resource conservation conflicts with employee well-being needs?

**Trougakos, J. P., Hideg, I., Cheng, B. H., & Beal, D. J. (2014). Lunch breaks unpacked: The role of autonomy as a moderator of recovery during lunch. *Academy of Management Journal*, *57*(2), 405-421. [https://doi.org/10.5465/amj.2011.1072](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.5465/amj.2011.1072)**

- The article examines the significance of autonomy in employee recovery during breaks. It underscores that allowing employees to have control over how they spend their breaks can lead to enhanced recovery and, subsequently, improved performance and well-being.

**Follow-Up Questions:**

1.  How can managers promote autonomy during breaks while ensuring that organizational objectives are not compromised?

2.  What are the potential benefits and challenges associated with giving employees total autonomy over their break activities?

3.  How can organizational policies be structured to support both employee autonomy and organizational productivity?

What does too much intervention or influence look like? What does not enough look like?
