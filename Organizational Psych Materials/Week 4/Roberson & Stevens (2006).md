To discern patterns of employee sense-making about workplace diversity, the authors analyzed 751
natural language accounts of diversity incidents from 712 workers in one department of a large
organization. Six generic incident types emerged: discrimination, representation, treatment by management, work relationships, respect between groups, and diversity climates. Consistent with hypotheses,
incidents that respondents viewed as negative, accounts from women, and those involving members of
respondents’ in-groups were more likely to cite justice issues. Partially consistent with research on the
linguistic intergroup bias, both negative and positive accounts involving out-group members and
accounts from men were more likely to be expressed using abstract verb forms. The authors discuss
future opportunities to integrate research on diversity, justice, and the linguistic category model.

The purpose of this study was to examine how people perceive
and interpret diversity incidents at work. Building on prior diversity climate research, which focuses on group differences in reactions to workplace diversity and diversity initiatives (see De
Meuse & Hostager, 2001; Mor Barak, Cherin, & Berkman, 1998),
we explored the process by which these differences emerge. Given
limited data on how people categorize various work experiences as
diversity related, we first attempted to construct a typology of the
conditions, events, and situations that people viewed as related to
diversity. Our second objective was to study how people interpreted such incidents. Toward this end, we explored two themes in
respondents’ accounts—namely, justice concerns and language
abstraction—to gain insights into diversity sense-making processes. These features of natural language accounts hold particular
promise for understanding diversity conflicts given that perceived
injustice may trigger strong emotional reactions and that language
abstraction yields important clues as to how incidents are organized and stored in memory. We also explored group differences
in these processes, which are likely to influence social interactions
and intergroup relations in organizations.

Perceiving Diversity in Organizations
From a sense-making perspective, organizations are conceived
as “networks of intersubjectively shared meanings” that are constructed and sustained through social interaction (Walsh & Ungson, 1991, p. 60). Such interaction often presents people with
equivocal events, which they attempt to interpret (Weick, 1979).
Thus, sense-making is defined as this attentional process of interpreting the interpersonal and interactive facets of organizational
life and assigning meaning to past events (Weick, 1995).
Grounded in identity construction and maintenance, sense-making
processes derive from an orientation toward situations that help
individuals to maintain esteem and consistency in their selfidentities (Ring & Van de Ven, 1989). As part of the identity
maintenance process, individuals categorize themselves and other
people into groups and use those groupings to help interpret
equivocal social situations. Because people tend to notice and rely
on visually prominent physical features or personal characteristics
to aid interpretation (Fiske & Taylor, 1991), race and gender may
be more likely than nonphenotypical characteristics, such as education or sexual orientation, to draw attention and serve as a basis
for spontaneous categorization (Jackson, Stone, & Alvarez, 1993).
Moreover, categorizations based on race and gender are identity
relevant (McCann, Ostrom, Tyner, & Mitchell, 1985) and as such
tend to confer higher or lower status on members of particular
racial and gender groups (Berger, Cohen, & Zelditch, 1972;
Brewer, 1979; Brewer & Kramer, 1985). Thus, to the extent that an
organization has employees with visibly diverse demographics,
such diversity is likely to be a significant feature in spontaneous
sense-making in that workplace.
Sense-making is also a social activity (Weick, 1995), one in
which individuals generate and share alternative explanations to
resolve ambiguities about what has happened and why. Research
on social networks suggests that people who are demographically
similar may have stronger interpersonal connections than do people who are demographically dissimilar (Ibarra, 1993, 1995). Thus,
it seems likely that interpretations of equivocal workplace events
will be shared more often within than across such demographic
groupings. This may prompt divergent sense-making about diversity incidents such that people who share a common frame of
reference due to ethnicity or gender generate interpretations that
are at odds with those of people in different ethnic or gender
groups. Particularly in monolithic and pluralistic organizational
cultures in which diverse employees are present (Ely, 1995),
conflicting accounts may come to epitomize how subgroups view
and interact with each other.
Given the potential impact of sense-making on selfcategorization and intergroup interactions, there is a need to identify the conditions, situations, or events that are spontaneously
categorized as being diversity related. Establishing the parameters
for such categorization processes will both aid in understanding
the genesis of workplace conflicts and provide key grounding for
building theory about identity at work. Moreover, there is a need
for systematic analyses of the themes that emerge in accounts of
diversity-related incidents. Such thematic analyses can indicate

What Conditions, Events, or Situations Are Perceived to
Be “Diversity Related”?
Existing research suggests that diversity perceptions are composed of individuals’ views and biases toward others, attitudes
regarding organizational policies and procedures used to promote
diversity, and beliefs regarding the qualifications of diverse employees (Kossek & Zonia, 1993; Mor Barak et al., 1998). Ely and
Thomas (2001) also identified the nature of race relations in the
work environment and the extent to which individuals feel valued
and respected by others as components of their diversity experiences in organizations. Although these studies have been helpful in
suggesting some situations or conditions that employees might
categorize as “diversity related,” they have relied heavily on research designs that may not capture the full range of such situations. Furthermore, they offer limited insight into how respondents
understood and stored interpretations of diversity-related episodes.
For these reasons, we adopted a complementary approach. By
analyzing respondents’ natural language descriptions of incidents
that they regarded as diversity related, we were able to assess a
wider potential range of incidents in their accounts. Moreover, we
were able to examine cues embedded in these accounts to pinpoint
two dimensions of their sense-making processes, specifically, the
salience of justice issues and level of abstraction at which events
were encoded.
On the basis of prior research, we anticipated that spontaneous
categorizations of incidents as being related to diversity would
likely arise from interactions among diverse employees, the nature
of their relationships, and experiences in which organizational
policies were invoked in connection with diversity. In particular,
we expected the emergence of issues consistent with leading
approaches to diversity management (Thomas & Ely, 1996), such
as stories or other accounts of discrimination, hiring preferences,
and the representation of diverse groups in the organization. Yet
because our work was exploratory, we remained open to the
possibility that a wider range of incidents than those described in
prior studies (e.g., actions taken by management, perceived benefits from working with diverse others) might be viewed by
employees as diversity related.

What Themes Emerge in Sense-Making About Workplace
Diversity?
Although employees’ sense-making about workplace diversity
has not been directly studied, prior research provides a sound
theoretical basis for focusing on two themes. One of these themes
is justice concerns, as research suggests that perceptions of fairness
play a key role in both organizational diversity initiatives (Mor
Barak et al., 1998; Thomas & Ely, 1996) and diversity-related
disputes (Goldman, 2001; Hogler, Frame, & Thornton, 2002). In
addition, social psychological research has shown that people’s
choice of concrete versus abstract verb forms when communicating their own and others’ behavior yields considerable insight into
their categorizations, evaluations, expectations, and attributions
(Maass, Salvi, Arcuri, & Semin, 1989; Semin & Fiedler, 1988).

Justice Concerns in Diversity Accounts
Prior studies have highlighted the centrality of fairness concerns
in diversity initiatives (Thomas & Ely, 1996), climates (Mor Barak
et al., 1998), and discrimination and harassment claims (Goldman,
2001; Hogler et al., 2002). Together with research arguing that
fairness is a socially constructed phenomenon (Lamertz, 2002),
these findings indicate that justice issues will be salient dimensions
of employees’ accounts of diversity-related incidents. Justice research suggests that at least three facets of fairness judgments—
distributive, procedural, and interactional justice—may influence
employees’ interpretations of organizational events (Colquitt,
2001). Assessments of distributive justice, or the fairness of one’s
outcome allocations (Deutsch, 1985), are based on comparisons of
whether one’s own and others’ outcomes conform to expectations
based on common distribution rules (Adams, 1965; Leventhal,
1976). Procedural justice, which refers to the process through
which allocation decisions are made, highlights the importance of
control over decision-making procedures and other system components (Leventhal, 1980; Thibaut & Walker, 1975). Finally,
interactional justice, or the quality of interpersonal treatment received when decisions are implemented, is used by individuals to
infer their own and their in-group’s relative status in the organization (Bies & Moag, 1986; Greenberg, 1993).
Because fairness is central to diversity management (Ely &
Thomas, 2001; Mor Barak et al., 1998), we expected that justice
issues would emerge in employees’ recollections of diversity incidents. Whereas current research suggests that distributive, procedural, and interactional justice are interchangeable and may
interact with one another in influencing work behavior (Goldman,
2003; Skarlicki & Folger, 1997), we anticipated that there might be
distinct relationships between types of diversity incidents and
facets of justice. For example, accounts of discrimination would be
more likely to raise concerns with distributive (i.e., allocation of
outcomes) than with procedural or interactional justice. Similarly,
accounts dealing with interpersonal interactions between diverse
employees may be more likely to cite interactional than procedural
or distributive justice. Given that our typology of diversity incidents emerged from the data, we could not predict in advance what
these relationships would be. Hence, our analyses of these data
were exploratory.
We were, however, able to offer specific hypotheses regarding
the prevalence of justice concerns as a function of account valence
(i.e., whether incidents were viewed as positive or negative), group
membership (i.e., whether incidents involved in-group or outgroup members), and respondents’ demographic backgrounds. In
terms of valence, there are several reasons to expect that justice
concerns would be more prevalent in negative than in positive
accounts. Attribution research suggests that people will engage in
sense-making analyses after experiencing unexpected or negative
events to determine the causes of such experiences (Pyszczynski &
Greenberg, 1981; Weiner, 1985; Wong & Weiner, 1981). Fairness
theory (Folger & Cropanzano, 1998, 2001) also suggests that the
presence of unfavorable outcomes or procedures will trigger attributional processes. More specifically, the theory suggests that

conditions of unfavorable outcomes or procedures activate counterfactual thinking, or comparisons of actual behavior or outcomes
with normative standards. Such comparisons produce a “would”
counterfactual, which is used to contrast the negativity of the
current situation, as well as counterfactual situations of what could
and should have occurred, the result of which will determine
whether a situation is interpreted as being socially unjust. These
thought processes and subsequent discussions of alternative interpretations are likely features of sense-making, which are less likely
to be activated under conditions of fair outcomes or procedures.
Thus, we offer the following hypothesis:
Hypothesis 1: Employees will be more likely to cite justice
concerns in accounts of diversity incidents that they view as
negative than they will in incidents seen as positive.
There are also likely to be systematic differences in the extent to
which justice concerns emerge in accounts that focus on in-group
versus out-group members. The relational model of justice (Lind &
Tyler, 1988; Tyler, 1989; Tyler & Lind, 1992) proposes that
people care about their relative status within social collectives—
and, by extension, the relative status of others who are similar to
them (Tyler, Degoey, & Smith, 1996). Because status within the
larger collective shapes identity and self-esteem (Tyler & Lind,
1992), the relational model predicts that people actively search for
information about how fairly they are being treated as evidence of
their respect and esteem within the collective. This logic suggests
that people will be more likely to recall and evaluate the fair or
unfair treatment of people like themselves (i.e., in-group members)
rather than that of dissimilar others when recounting workplace
diversity incidents.
Hypothesis 2: Employees’ accounts of diversity incidents
involving in-group members will be more likely to cite justice
issues than will their accounts of diversity incidents involving
out-group members.
Finally, research has suggested that people who belong to identifiably low-status groups may be more likely to monitor their
environments for affirmations of their identities (Tajfel, 1982; van
Knippenberg, 1984). Moreover, significant levels of past mistreatment generate expectations of future and more frequent mistreatment (Davidson & Friedman, 1995). Given observed or personal
experiences of bias or social isolation among these historically
excluded identity groups (Ibarra, 1995), we expected that women
and ethnic minorities would be more likely to actively attend to
and process information regarding how fairly they and others like
them have been treated in organizations than would men or ethnic
majority members.
Hypothesis 3: Minority employees’ accounts of diversity incidents will be more likely to cite justice issues than will
accounts by majority employees.

attributions about others’ behavior and, by implication, their expectancies for similar future actions. Semin, Maass, and colleagues

1988; Semin & Smith, 1999) have shown that people have wide
latitude in their use of interpersonal verbs that can be used to
describe self and others, and that this word choice is related to their
own and listeners’ causal attributions and expectations (Semin &
Marsman, 1994). According to the linguistic category model,
interpersonal verbs can be arrayed on a continuum of concrete to
abstract forms (Semin & Fiedler, 1988). At the most concrete level
are descriptive action verbs, which provide an objective description of action, offer no interpretation, and typically have one or
more physically invariant features (e.g., one kicks with one’s foot
and shouts with one’s mouth). Accounts using descriptive action
verbs are frequently attributed to situational factors and imply that
there is no expectation of similar future behavior. Interpretive
action verbs, in contrast, include greater depth of interpretation,
have pronounced evaluative components, and do not have physically invariant features—that is, many different actions could lead
to use of the same interpretive action verbs (e.g., harms could be
physical or psychological). Accounts using interpretive action
verbs are often attributed to the sentence subject, or actor. State
verbs, which presume knowledge of the actor’s state of mind, do
not maintain reference to any specific behavior or incidents (e.g.,
dislikes or prefers). Although state verbs typically evoke causal
attributions to the sentence object rather than the actor (Semin &
Marsman, 1994), they do convey expected similar future states of
mind from actors. Finally, adjectives refer to enduring qualities of
persons (e.g., [is] disagreeable or prejudiced) and represent the
highest level of abstraction. Their use is consistently associated
with dispositional attributions and, thus, a continued expectation of
similar future behavior.
The linguistic category model has several noteworthy implications for understanding how employees make sense of diversity
incidents at work. Although its correspondence with situational
versus dispositional attributions is not perfectly linear (Arcuri,
Maass, & Portelli, 1993; Semin & Marsman, 1994), the continuum
of verb forms does reflect and successfully communicate to others
what the speaker’s expectations are for similar future behavior.
Verb forms also play a significant role in the transmission and
maintenance of intergroup stereotypes, in the form of the linguistic
intergroup bias (Maass et al., 1989). The linguistic intergroup bias
reflects a tendency for speakers to describe the negative behavior
of out-group members and positive behavior of in-group members
using abstract verbs (suggesting expected stability), whereas they
characterize the positive behavior of out-group members and negative behavior of in-group members using concrete verbs (implying little expected stability). Its prevalence correlates consistently
with measures of implicit prejudice (Franco & Maass, 1999; von
Hippel, Sekaquaptewa, & Vargas, 1997). Studies have suggested
that the linguistic intergroup bias has both cognitive antecedents
(i.e., differential expectancies for in- and out-group member behavior; Maass, Milesi, Zabbini, & Stahlberg, 1995) and motivational antecedents (i.e., in-group protection; Maass, Ceccarelli, &
Rudin, 1996) and is particularly pronounced under conditions of
threat to social identity or perceived competition (Maass et al.,
1996).
In our context of employees’ accounts of diversity incidents, the
linguistic category model and linguistic intergroup bias lead to
several predictions. With regard to the language used to recount
different types of incidents, research shows that interpersonal
situations tend to generate more internal, stable attributions than do

noninterpersonal situations (Anderson, 1983a, 1983b). Therefore,
we anticipated that the linguistic intergroup bias effect would be
replicated in respondents’ unstructured accounts of diversityrelated incidents at work. Studies suggest that the most robust
effect is for differences in verb forms used to describe the negative
behavior of in-group versus out-group members, rather than differences in positive behavior (see Maass et al., 1989, 1995, 1996),
leading to our hypothesis:
Hypothesis 4: Employees will be more likely to use abstract
language in their accounts of negative diversity incidents
involving out-group members than they will in accounts of
such incidents involving in-group members.
Research also suggests that membership in groups that have been
relegated to lower status motivates stronger in-group protective
tendencies and thus more pronounced linguistic intergroup bias
effects (Maass et al., 1996). Because the social advantages of
higher status group membership are stable and not easily threatened, members of high-status groups exhibit less pronounced
linguistic intergroup bias tendencies (Maass et al., 1996). In their
perception of ambiguous intergroup interactions, men and Caucasians are less likely to interpret such events as involving prejudice
(Inman & Baron, 1996), suggesting that people with fewer expectancies for or experiences with prejudice have less developed
schemas for recognizing such treatment directed toward their
group. Inasmuch as members of historically excluded identity
groups may more frequently experience threats to their identity, we
would expect them to recall diversity-related incidents in more
abstract terms than majority group members.
Hypothesis 5: Minority employees’ accounts of diversity incidents will be conveyed in more abstract language than will
such accounts provided by majority employees.

Method
Sample
The sample consisted of 712 employees (89% of all employees) from the
circulation department of a large metropolitan newspaper. Participation
was voluntary, although the CEO endorsed the survey and scheduled paid
work time for all employees to participate. The sample was 70% male, with
an average age of 36 years (ranging from 18 to 63), an average firm tenure
of 8 years, and the following ethnic breakdown: 80% White, 10% Black,
7% Asian or Pacific Islander, 2% Latino or Hispanic, and 1% Native
American. Most employees (81%) held low-skilled or administrative jobs
(e.g., telemarketing sales for home delivery, actual delivery of papers to
homes and businesses, administrative support for these activities). In
addition, 33% of employees worked part time and 29% held at least a
college degree.

Respondents completed a survey as part of a diversity assessment. The
company had previously undertaken successful diversity initiatives in two
other departments (newsroom and sales), and senior managers were considering launching a diversity initiative in the targeted department. Employees were asked to complete the survey to help management determine

Qualitative Analyses
In analyzing the accounts of diversity-related incidents, we used two
different approaches. Given the absence of a preexisting taxonomy specifying the range of diversity-related incidents that occur in organizations,
we used an inductive content-analysis approach to develop categories of
the types of diversity incidents described by respondents. Independent of
this process, we used theoretically derived categorization systems for
analyzing the justice content and language abstraction in each incident.
Typology of diversity incidents. To explore the types of diversity
incidents recalled by respondents, we content analyzed verbatim responses
to the open-ended questions using pattern coding procedures (Krippendorf,
1980; Miles & Huberman, 1994). First, each author independently analyzed participants’ recollections of diversity incidents to identify common
themes and formed content categories that captured them. We identified a
total of 18 categories and had an overall agreement of 55%. An analysis of
the categories showed that most of our discrepancies were due to differences in level (i.e., superordinate vs. subordinate categories with the same
theme) rather than content. For example, one of us separated incidents
dealing with accusations of discrimination and those focused on fears of
discrimination accusations into two categories, whereas the other subsumed both types of incident into a single category. Collapsing these finer
grained distinctions into superordinate categories yielded 13 categories
organized around six main themes, with 78% agreement between us. We
then validated this categorization scheme using an independent researcher,
who recoded all incidents using the composite list of 13 categories. This
confirmatory coding yielded 86% agreement with our codes. All disagreements were resolved through discussion and consensus. Overall, the 13
categories and six main themes were retained for inclusion in our emergent
typology of diversity-related incidents.
Justice coding. To investigate justice concerns in respondents’ accounts of diversity incidents, we also categorized their accounts using a
coding scheme derived from the organizational justice literature. Using
established taxonomies of justice (see Colquitt, 2001; Leventhal, 1976,
1980; Lind & Tyler, 1988; Thibaut & Walker, 1975), we first developed a
comprehensive list of, and definitions for, all dimensions discussed in the
literature. We then coded a subset of respondents’ diversity accounts to
clarify and refine this coding scheme. The final coding scheme, which
included 14 categories, allowed the diversity incidents to be coded for all
relevant dimensions of justice (i.e., each account could be coded for more
than one type of justice concern). A summary of these justice codes and
their definitions can be found in Table 1. Using this system, one of the
authors and the independent researcher individually coded all diversity

Linguistic coding. To identify the degree of language abstraction in
participants’ descriptions of diversity incidents, we drew on research and
methods from the linguistic category model (Maass et al., 1989; Semin &
Fiedler, 1988). In prior research using this approach, participants have been
directed to write brief, complete sentences to describe a clearly identifiable
target’s actions or behavior (e.g., the behavior of characters in cartoons).
Each sentence is then coded for its verb form, and the codes for multiple
sentences are averaged (for examples of this system, see Fiedler, Bluemke,
Friese, & Hofmann, 2003; Maass et al., 1989, 1995, 1996; Semin & Smith,
1999; Semin, Montes, & Valencia, 2002; Wigboldus, Semin, & Spears,
2000).
In our attempts to apply these procedures to our data, we encountered a
number of difficulties. For example, respondents’ open-ended accounts
sometimes digressed into descriptions of background situations or venting
of personal feelings. A larger problem was that accounts frequently did not
conform to grammatical rules such that they did not include subjects or
verbs or did not identify a target person or situation. Consequently, our
initial attempts to apply the coding rules detailed by Maass et al. (1989)
yielded low interrater agreement (62%).
To accommodate the free-form nature of our data, we made two modifications to the Maass et al. (1989) procedures. Given our difficulty in
reliably distinguishing between state verbs and adjectives in the absence of
reference to specific subjects, situations, or objects, we decided to collapse
these two high-abstraction categories into one category. Our second revision was to code the entire account, rather than each individual sentence.
This adjustment enabled us to consider periodic digressions in respondents’
accounts as context rather than requiring us to weight them equally with all
other sentences in the account. Use of these revised coding rules, which are
shown in Table 1, resulted in interrater agreement of 91%.2

Discussion
Prior diversity research has primarily used survey methods to
examine the prevalence of differential treatment and the unique
experiences of minority employees. In contrast, we adopted a
complementary approach to elucidate how all employees interpret
their diversity-related experiences at work. In particular, we analyzed written accounts that employees themselves had designated
as reflecting positive or negative examples of diversity-related
incidents. This approach enabled us to both validate and extend
prior work by broadening the range of issues that might be studied
under the “diversity” banner. Although our data proved more
complex to analyze than those typically generated in survey re-

search, we were able to study unobtrusive indicators of employees’
interpretation patterns and offer preliminary guidance to future
researchers in how to treat such free-form data. Below, we summarize the key findings associated with each of our research
objectives, consider the limitations of our method, and outline
potential directions for theory, research, and practice.

Preliminary Taxonomy of Diversity Incidents
One objective of our study was to develop a taxonomy of the
types of incidents employees regard as being related to diversity.
By classifying the themes present in our natural language accounts,
we identified 13 total categories organized into six major types.
Consistent with prior diversity research, our respondents described
incidents involving discrimination, representation of diverse
groups in the hierarchy, and diversity climate. Supplemental analyses revealed distinct nuances in how these incident types were
recalled. For example, discrimination accounts were significantly
more likely to be recounted in concrete language and to mention
both distributive and procedural justice concerns. Conversely, representation accounts were more likely to be expressed in abstract
language and to cite procedural justice issues only, whereas diversity climate accounts were primarily abstract and showed no
relationship with justice. Thus, from a sense-making perspective,
employees were sensitive to the details of situations in which
people were denied jobs or promotions and looked to both processes and outcomes to understand why. In addition, they focused
on organizational processes designed to increase the amount of
diversity in the organization and expressed expectations for the
representation of diverse groups and the prevailing diversity climate to continue.
Three incident types that have received less attention in the
diversity literature are treatment by management, work relationships, and respect incidents. Although these might appear to be
similar at first glance, supplemental analyses also uncovered core
differences in employees’ interpretations. All three incident types

Although this department’s demographics are broadly representative of those found in many U.S. firms, it is possible that firms with

quite different patterns of incident types. Yet investigating the
relationship between different proportions of employee demographics and the types of diversity accounts provided by those
employees offers an avenue for exploration that is theoretically
rich and of practical significance.

Tests of Hypotheses on Justice and Language Abstraction
Patterns
Another objective of this study was to examine the justice
content and language abstraction associated with accounts of
diversity-related incidents at work. Our first hypothesis predicted
and found that justice issues were more prevalent in incidents
viewed as negative than in those viewed as positive. In particular,
negative diversity accounts were more likely to include mention of
distributive and procedural justice concerns. These findings are
consistent with justice research suggesting that counterfactual
thinking (i.e., about what might have been) in terms of processes
and outcomes may be activated in situations regarded as negative.
Future research might test this mechanism experimentally by probing individuals’ thought processes at different stages following
exposure to positive and negative diversity incidents such as those
identified in our typology.
Consistent with the relational model of justice (Tyler & Lind,
1992), we found support for our second hypothesis, which predicted that accounts of incidents involving in-group members
would be more likely than those involving out-group members to
cite justice concerns. Given that fair treatment of others who are
similar to oneself yields more information about one’s own relative standing within the social group, it is perhaps not surprising
that employees would be more likely to recall such incidents in a
diversity context. We also found partial support for our third
hypothesis, which had predicted greater prevalence of justice issues in accounts from minority than from majority group members.
More specifically, our results indicated that women were more
likely than men to cite distributive and interactional justice issues,
but that no differences emerged between ethnic majority and
minority group members. These results are consistent with re-

search that highlights gender differences in distributive expectations and preferences (Major, 1994) and interpersonal orientations
(Ford & Lowery, 1986). Future research should further explore
group differences in attention to justice and investigate whether
these findings generalize to other samples and contexts.
Beyond consideration of justice concerns, we also focused on
respondents’ use of concrete versus abstract language in their
accounts, as such language has been implicated in the generation,
maintenance, and transmission of stereotypes (e.g., Wigboldus et
al., 2000) and expectancies for future behavior (e.g., Fiedler et al.,
2003; Maass et al., 1995). Following theory and findings on the
linguistic intergroup bias, we hypothesized that respondents would
use more abstract language to recall negative incidents involving
out-group members than they would to recall negative incidents
involving in-group members. Although we did not find a significant interaction between account valence and in-group/out-group
focus (i.e., the full linguistic intergroup bias effect), we did obtain
a significant main effect for in-group/out-group focus and evidence of disproportionate use of abstract language in the recollection of negative events for out-group members relative to in-group
members. Note that the latter effect is similar to a pattern occasionally observed in the linguistic intergroup bias literature (e.g.,
Arcuri et al., 1993; Maass et al., 1995, Experiment 2).
Although negative incidents involving out-group members
were expressed more abstractly than were those involving ingroup members, it is interesting that our results showed the
same pattern for positive incidents. The latter effect is contrary
to findings on the linguistic intergroup bias and warrants additional investigation. If confirmed, this pattern of using abstract
language when recalling all incidents involving out-group
members suggests that employees may focus more on establishing and maintaining intergroup differences than on enhancing their own social identities. Some research suggests that
people may use abstract language to foster a unique social
identity, irrespective of the valence or social desirability of
in-group behavior (Maass et al., 1995). Thus, it is possible that
our findings reflect a subtle strategy for workplace differentiation—a dynamic that would be extremely difficult to address
through most existing workforce diversity initiatives.
Our final hypothesis predicted that, owing to the social disadvantages conferred on lower status groups, minority group members would experience stronger in-group protective bias and thus
would recall diversity incidents in more abstract terms than would
majority members. Analyses indicated a significant effect for
gender but not ethnicity; however, the gender effect was opposite
to our prediction. Men used more abstract language than did
women when describing diversity incidents. Consistent with research that suggests that infrequent events tend to be communicated in more abstract language (Semin & Smith, 1999), this
finding may signal gender differences in diversity incident accessibility in memory. An alternative possibility is that women in this
organization did not, as a whole, experience much identity threat
and that men in this sample viewed diversity incidents as reflecting
stable workplace behavior patterns. Additional work is needed to
clarify the explanation for this finding.

the justice literature. A recent article by Cropanzano, Byrne, Bobocel, and Rupp (2001) questioned how people generalize their
judgments about a single just or unjust event to broader conclu-

Benefits and Drawbacks of Methodology
One significant contribution of our study is the use of a methodology that complements earlier survey work by allowing respondents to describe in their own words the diversity incidents they
had observed. Few studies have used qualitative methods to investigate either diversity or justice phenomena (see Greenberg,
1986, for a notable exception to the latter), despite occasional calls
for their use in organizational—in particular, justice—research
(Lind, 2001; Shapiro, 2001; Taylor, 2001). For example, it is
difficult to gauge the salience of diversity or justice concerns when
survey questions cue respondents to consider such issues and on
preselected dimensions. Because we did not ask respondents to
focus on fairness issues or suggest the justice facets that might be
important, we can have confidence that fairness does indeed matter
in diversity-related interactions. Moreover, we were able to leverage well-established findings concerning the linguistic category
model to generate insights into respondents’ interpretations and
expectations.
By the same token, there are several disadvantages to our
methodology, as our results are constrained by the reliability and
validity of our coding procedures. Although both the justice and
language abstraction coding procedures were solidly grounded in
theory and applied iteratively to minimize the potential of significant measurement biases, we cannot rule out the possibility that
measurement error influenced our findings. For example, similarities in the justice codes (e.g., those for process control and voice,
or for bias suppression and consistency) may have inadvertently
amplified some patterns in the data. Alternatively, additional justice antecedents not included in the coding scheme may have
influenced how respondents perceived diversity-related incidents.
Although linguistic category models have been shown to be useful
for detecting the presence and transmission of intergroup stereotypes (see Maass et al., 1989), our results also do not allow us to
conclude that such models would be functional in other contexts or
with other types of data. For example, given that recent Supreme
Court cases (e.g., Gratz v. Bollinger [2003], Grutter v. Bollinger
[2003]) have expanded the legal concept of diversity beyond
gender and race, different patterns of results may emerge in data
measuring other diversity dimensions that are less visually salient
(e.g., education, sexual orientation, religion). Overall, additional
research is needed to examine the generalizability of our findings
across a variety of settings and employee populations.

389

Aside from these limitations and challenges, our study offers
several conceptual contributions. Being among the first to integrate
the sense-making, diversity, justice, and linguistic category literatures, we can identify promising avenues both within these literatures and at their points of integration. As one example, diversity
researchers have not considered factors that lead individuals to
identify with others who are similar on a given dimension and
whether such identifications remain stable over time. It seems
worth investigating whether salient workplace incidents involving
others with whom one shares a given characteristic might trigger
greater identification with that aspect of one’s demography. Similarly, research on social networks among diverse employees might
be augmented by analyses of communication patterns within networks as they react to specific diversity incidents and, in particular, how issues of justice are discussed. In addition to language,
such studies might examine the role of counterfactual arguments in
the formation of consensual views.
Practically, our findings suggest that incidents pertaining to
discrimination, representation, management treatment, work relationships, levels of respect, and the diversity climate are salient
and thus represent fulcrums for influencing how employees make
sense of diversity experiences. Further, open clarification of the
facts surrounding hiring or promotion decisions that could be
viewed as discriminatory seem important for maintaining or enhancing intergroup relations, given that such incidents are recalled
in more concrete terms. The study also highlights the role of justice
in the effective management of diversity. By emphasizing fair
distribution of outcomes (e.g., equality in disciplining diverse
employees), enactment of procedures (e.g., opportunities for diverse employees to participate in decision making), and interpersonal treatment (e.g., showing respect to all employees), organizations may be able to positively influence how employees
interpret diversity experiences. Further, because language abstraction plays a key role in diversity-related attitudes, greater care in
the language used to communicate diversity events and interactions might help root out biases, depolarize intergroup relations,
and improve the success of diversity initiatives.
