## How Goals Motivate Behavior {#slide-7}

Goals affect performance through  four mechanisms  (Locke & Latham, 2002):

Direct attention  to goal-relevant activities

Energize effort  (harder goals → more effort)

Increase persistence  over time

Stimulate discovery & use of task-relevant strategies

## Self-Regulation {#slide-11}

-   Behavior requires regulatory resources

Ego-Depletion/Regulation:  When we regulate our behavior we are deprived of mental resources, leaving fewer mental resources for other behavior

Previous acts of  self-control  drains ability to control future behavior

We might want to do something, but our self-control can be exhausted (like fatigue after exercise), which prevents us from achieving our goal.

Attitudes better predict behavior involving self-control when people have the mental energy to do so

## Dynamic Self- Regulation & Goal Hierarchies {#slide-12}

-  Work goals are hierarchical: actions → tasks → goals → higher-order needs
-  Cycle time differs: self-concept/identity = years; tasks = minutes/hours
-  Dynamic adaptation: abandon, postpone, or revise goals as environment shifts
-  Effort allocation = choices of direction, duration, intensity across goals (Neal et al., 2017)

## Locke & Latham, 2002 {#slide-20}

Goals motivate through:

- Directive function: Focus attention on goal-relevant tasks.
- Energizing function: Higher goals → more effort.
- Persistence: Difficult goals keep people working longer.
- Strategy discovery & use: Goals trigger search for better ways to perform.

## Neal et al., 2017 {#slide-23}

-  Cognitive resources are limited
-  Difficult goals can backfire when resource demand \> capacity.
-  People cope by:

<!-- -->

- Adding effort
- Switching strategy
- Relaxing goal difficulty
- Extending time or quitting

-  Models show strategy shifts more common than just "trying harder."
