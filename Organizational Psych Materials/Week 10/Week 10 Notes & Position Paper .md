**Week 10 (10/25): Emotional Labor & Emotional Regulation in the Workplace**

**Ashforth, B.E., & Humphrey, R.H. (1993). Emotional labor in service roles: The influence of identity. *Academy of Management Review*, *18*(1), 88 -- 115. [https://doi.org/10.2307/258824](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.2307/258824)**

- Ashforth and Humphrey (1993) delve into the dynamics of emotional labor in service-oriented professions, exploring how it influences workers\' identities. Emotional labor refers to the effort, planning, and control needed to express organizationally desired emotions during interpersonal transactions at work. The authors analyze how identity plays a significant role in modulating the impacts of emotional labor, possibly affecting job performance, job satisfaction, and the well-being of employees.

**Grandey, A.A. (2000). Emotion regulation in the workplace: A new way to conceptualize emotional labor. *Journal of Occupational Health Psychology*, *5*(1), 95 -- 110. [https://doi.org/10.1037/1076-8998.5.1.95](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.1037/1076-8998.5.1.95)**

- Grandey (2000) presents a fresh perspective on emotional labor, focusing on the regulation of emotions in the workplace. The author contends that understanding how employees manage their emotions can offer deeper insights into the effects of emotional labor. The paper examines the mechanisms through which employees modify their emotions to align with organizational expectations and the psychological and physical impacts of this process.

**Headrick, L., & Park, Y. (2021). Faking at work, struggling to be healthy at home: A model of surface acting and its relation with unhealthy eating and physical activity. *Journal of Occupational Health Psychology*. Advance online publication. <https://doi.org/10.1037/ocp0000317>**

- Headrick and Park (2021) connect the dots between emotional labor (specifically surface acting) and health outcomes outside the workplace. The authors propose a model explaining how the strain of masking or faking emotions at work can spill over into personal life, resulting in unhealthy eating habits and decreased physical activity. This paper underscores the pervasive impacts of emotional labor on individuals' overall well-being, extending beyond the confines of the workplace.

-Emotional Labor: The process of managing emotions to align with organizational or job expectations, which can have psychological and physical effects on employees.

-Identity Influence: The role of individual identity in mediating the effects of emotional labor, affecting how employees cope with and are impacted by such labor.

-Emotion Regulation: The mechanisms through which employees control and modify their emotions to meet workplace expectations.

-Health Impacts: The correlation between emotional labor (especially surface acting) and adverse health outcomes, such as unhealthy eating and reduced physical activity, highlighting the need for comprehensive approaches to mitigate these effects.

While the study of emotional labor has been deeply rooted in the context of customer service roles (Ashforth & Humphrey, 1993; Grandey, 2000), this research underscores the need to broaden this topic to a more diverse array of professional settings. Due to the multifaceted nature of emotional labor, that involves identity management and emotion regulation, it is not confined to the realm of customer interactions but can have applications in various occupational context.
