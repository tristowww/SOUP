Performing emotional labor impairs mood as well as regulatory control of employees, and we compare these
mechanisms to explain critical health-related behaviors: eating and exercise. Two studies examine the
relationship of surface acting at work with unhealthy eating and physical activity at home as mediated by
negative and positive affect. Emotion regulation (ER) self-efﬁcacy is tested as a moderator of the indirect
relationships. Also, the relative strength of the affect mechanisms and a depletion mechanism are explored.
Study 1 utilized a time-lagged between-person design to explore these relationships among 287 customer
service workers. Results show that surface acting has an indirect link with unhealthy eating via negative
affect and an indirect link with physical activity via positive affect. ER self-efﬁcacy moderates the path from
negative affect to unhealthy eating, thereby weakening the indirect effect of surface acting on unhealthy
eating via negative affect. Study 2 utilized a 5-week diary study of 469 teachers to replicate Study 1 at the
within-person level. Results show that surface acting has indirect effects on unhealthy eating and physical
activity via negative affect at the week level. Also, surface acting has an indirect effect on physical activity
via positive affect at the week level. Again, ER self-efﬁcacy cross-moderates (weakens) the indirect link
between surface acting and unhealthy eating via negative affect. The pairwise comparisons of the indirect
effects reveal no differences among the affect mediators (Studies 1 and 2); however, positive affect may
account for more of the effect of surface acting on physical activity than depletion (Study 2).

By proposing and testing the current model in two samples, we
aim to make several contributions to the literature. First, we
contribute to the growing body of research examining the afterwork behavioral consequences of surface acting. There is ample
evidence that surface acting is related to burnout and work-related
outcomes (see Grandey & Gabriel, 2015; Hülsheger & Schewe,
2011, for a review); however, few organizational research has
considered the after-work behavioral consequences of surface acting, in particular health behavior in the home domain. Although lab
studies have examined the effect of ER on eating (e.g., Hofmann
et al., 2007; Zyphur et al., 2007), these effects were tested only on a
narrow behavior of candy consumption, so evidence is still lacking
whether emotional labor is associated with employees’ broader
unhealthy eating. Also, to our knowledge, emotional laborers’
physical activity has not been examined in an applied setting either.
Thus, our study examining the link between surface acting at work
and unhealthy eating and physical activity at home will reafﬁrm the
intricate connection between work and home domains.
Second, we help to clarify the mechanisms by which surface
acting at work relates to unhealthy eating and physical activity at
home. Although previous lab studies have suggested that ER may
affect unhealthy eating and physical activity via depletion (Muraven
et al., 1998; Zyphur et al., 2007), this mechanism has received
increased scrutiny due to its lack of evidence (Hagger et al., 2016;
Vohs et al., in press). For example, Carter et al. (2015) found no

meta-analytic evidence to support that depletion necessarily leads to
self-regulation failure. Thus, in our study, we take an alternative
theoretical perspective to focus on the primary role of affect as COR
theory (Hobfoll, 1989) and action tendency theories of emotion
(Fredrickson, 2001; Frijda, 1986) suggest negative and positive
affect as mediators between ER and unhealthy behaviors. Nevertheless, we control for depletion as a possible competing mediator
and further explore the relative strengths of the mediators. By doing
this, we may determine, in essence, whether people engage in
unhealthy behaviors primarily as a way to cope with high negative
affect and low positive affect (threatened or lack of resources), or
from an inability to inhibit themselves due to exhaustion (depletion)
following surface acting at work.
Third, identifying possible boundary conditions (moderators)
is important to reﬁne theories and devise intervention programs
(Hitt & Smith, 2005; Whetten, 1989). While prior research has
explored ER self-efﬁcacy as a moderator between emotional labor
stressors and strain outcomes (e.g., Deng et al., 2017; Pugh et al.,
2011; Wang et al., 2011), research has yet to demonstrate whether it
is an important individual resource factor for modulating one’s
affective inﬂuence on health-related behaviors in the nonwork
domain (unhealthy eating and poor physical activity). Accordingly,
we test ER self-efﬁcacy as a second-stage moderator to make a new
contribution to the literature by exploring whether it can help
individuals manage their affective inﬂuence even after their work
has ended and further help them reduce the inﬂuence on their
important health-related behaviors.

have important practical implications for organizations that require
emotional labor. Although stress, in general, is known to impair
health through its inﬂuence on health behaviors (e.g., alcohol/drug
use, unhealthy eating, exercise), there has been little recognition of
the role of speciﬁc work stressors on these behaviors (Goh et al.,
2015). This is an important oversight given that workplace interventions for health behaviors (e.g., corporate wellness programs)

Background on Surface Acting and Unhealthy Behavior
According to emotional labor theory, emotion display rules in
organizations often dictate that workers show positive emotions to
satisfy customers, clients, patients, or students (Hochschild, 1983).
Displaying affective states that do not match one’s internal feelings
is known as surface acting (Grandey, 2000; Hochschild, 1983). A
resource-based perspective of emotional labor suggests that surface
acting requires employees to invest a great amount of resources
(affect, motivational energy) because it involves both suppressing
felt emotions and faking emotional expressions to meet a work goal
(Brotheridge & Lee, 1998; see Grandey & Gabriel, 2015, for a
review). Thus, employees may experience threatened or lack of
resources which can manifest in their emotional strain (Brotheridge
& Lee, 1998). COR theory further posits that when individuals face
threatened resources, they become motivated to restore the resources
and/or withdraw from further resources investment for resource
conservation (Hobfoll, 1989). In this study, we view employees’
high negative affect and low positive affect driving from surface
acting as their state of threatened resources and lack of valuable
resources (respectively), so they may be likely to eat unhealthy
foods to cope with the threat and withdraw from further resource
investment in physical activity at home. Similarly, it has been
suggested that health-related behaviors are often guided by affect,
above and beyond cognitive or attitudinal intentions toward health.
For example, research has demonstrated that affect is a primary
driver of eating behavior (i.e., “emotional eating”; Arnow et al.,
1995) as well as persistence in physical activity (Lawton et al.,
2009). Taken all together, we propose that affect may explain why
workers who engage in more surface acting are likely to approach to
unhealthy foods and avoid physical activity at home.

The Effect of Surface Acting on Affect
We ﬁrst hypothesize that surface acting may increase negative
affect and decrease positive affect because it threatens psychological
resources and undermines one’s sense of authenticity. According to
COR theory (Hobfoll, 1989), strain results from the threat or loss of
resources. Resources refer to the ones that an individual values and
strives to obtain and protect, and include conditional and objective
resources (e.g., social support, money) as well as physical and
psychological resources (e.g., energy, affect). Because surface
acting requires expending resources to fake and suppress emotions,
it is expected to harm psychological resources (e.g., affective
resources). As such, surface acting can be considered an upsetting
experience that may increase negative affect as well as decrease
positive affect. Along the same lines, according to emotional labor
research, individuals may draw on their positive affective resources
when they engage in emotional labor (Grandey et al., 2002).
Therefore, their positive affect may be a resource that is decreased
after engaging in surface acting at work. In addition, because surface

acting usually involves the suppression of negative feelings, individuals continue to experience this negative affect while faking
emotions, and their negative affect may remain unresolved. For
example, research has found that attempting to suppress negative
emotions causes individuals to think about the eliciting situation
even more, which ultimately increases negative affect (Gross, 2002;
Wegner, 1994).
Additionally, surface acting may harm affective resources
because it involves the experience of inauthenticity (Grandey &
Melloy, 2017). Inauthenticity or the feeling of being ingenuine
arises when there is a discrepancy between what one actually feels
and what they express that creates an internal tension (Hochschild,
1983). Inauthenticity may be upsetting to individuals because of the
emotional dissonance, and they are more motivated to maintain a
sense of harmony or oneness; as a result, this internal tension may
put them in a bad mood (i.e., increase negative affect; Erickson &
Wharton, 1997). Inauthenticity could also harm individuals’ positive self-perceptions because individuals may feel bad about themselves if they feel like they are being inauthentic. Hence, individuals
may experience decreased positive affect as well if they frequently
engage in surface acting at work.
Empirically, studies have also consistently found a positive link
between surface acting at work and negative affect. For example,
bus drivers’ daily surface acting had a positive link with state
negative affect (Scott & Barnes, 2011), while service workers’
surface acting during the workday was positively related to negative
affect at the end of the workday (Judge et al., 2009). However,
empirical evidence for the relationship between surface acting and
positive affect is less clear. For example, Yue et al. (2016) found
surface acting to be both positively related to negative affect and
negatively related to positive affect; Xanthopoulou et al. (2018)
found that daily surface acting was negatively related to ﬂow at the
end of the workday, which represents a state of absorption and
enjoyment at work; however, both Scott and Barnes (2011) and
Judge et al. (2009) failed to ﬁnd a signiﬁcant link between surface
acting and positive affect. Nonetheless, as COR theory suggests that
surface acting may be related to both negative affect and positive
affect, we hypothesize:
Hypothesis 1: Surface acting at work is (a) positively related to
negative affect and (b) negatively related to positive affect at the
end of workday during the week.

The Indirect Effect of Surface Acting on Unhealthy
Eating and Physical Activity via Affect
The COR theory (Hobfoll, 1989) states that individuals strive to
minimize resource loss and conserve their resources. Furthermore, it
holds that resource gains require resource investment. As such,
individuals who are experiencing high negative affect and low
positive affect after surface acting at work may be more motivated
to cope with the resource threat (eating unhealthy foods to repair the
mood) and less willing to invest their resources in pursuing physical
activity at home. In addition, action tendency theories of emotions
hold that affective experiences drive action tendencies that energize
and prioritize behaviors (Fredrickson, 2001; Frijda, 1986; Frijda
et al., 1989). Negative affect tends to have a hindering effect on
action tendencies, while positive affect tends to have a facilitating
effect. Accordingly, being in a bad mood (high negative) predisposes

individuals to engage in unhealthy behavior (Frijda, 1986; Frijda
et al., 1989) because when in an unpleasant state, they want to feel
better as quickly as possible, and many ways of feeling better involve
indulging appetites (e.g., unhealthy eating, being a couch potato).
On the other hand, positive affect is associated with broadening
one’s psychological resources and encourages individuals to engage
in action (Fredrickson, 2001). When individuals are in a good mood,
they gain cognitive resources that help them to engage in activities
that require motivation and effort. For example, in a good mood,
individuals were more likely to believe that their effort in diet and
exercise is worthwhile (Smith et al., 2018). In addition, when in a
positive affective state, individuals may draw on their positive mood
and energy to avoid unhealthy foods and engage in physical activity.
However, if they lack positive affect, then they may not be willing to
invest resources to avoid unhealthy foods or pursue physical
activity. Taken all together, being both in a poor mood (higher
negative affect) and a lack of positive affective resources (lower
positive affect) from surface acting may lead employees to eat more
unhealthy foods and avoid physical activity at home.
To our knowledge, there is no direct research evidence on the link
between employees’ surface acting and their eating behavior and
physical activity at home via negative or positive affect. However,
some evidence partially supported the link between affect and
unhealthy eating in within-subject research on nonwork samples
(e.g., adults, college students; Macht et al., 2004; Patel & Schlundt,
2001; Wolff et al., 2000). Relatedly, daily customer mistreatment
experiences (that may require emotional labor) were positively
related to service employees’ overeating behavior (eating too
much) at home via negative mood (Liu et al., 2017). The food
consumed in these cases usually comprises “junk food” and/or high
carbohydrates and low in nutrients. Also, consistent with COR and
action tendency theories of emotion, it is more often the case that
when individuals are in a high negative affective or a low positive
affective state, they will avoid physical activity. For example, diary
research has found that state negative emotions inversely predict
physical activity, whereas positive emotions positively predict
physical activity in a variety of clinical samples (e.g., Anton &
Miller, 2005; Emerson et al., 2018; Lemmon et al., 2007). Furthermore, individuals may be motivated to engage in more sedentary
behaviors, such as watching television or playing video games, to
relieve their negative affect and gain positive affect quickly and thus
avoid physical activity (Tice et al., 2001). Similarly, Sonnentag and
Jelden (2009) found that employees with higher situational constraints (i.e., conditions that make it more difﬁcult to one’s job)
during the work were less likely to engage in physical activity,
despite the fact that most employees perceived physical activity to
be highly useful for recovery from stress. Thus, combining COR
theory and action tendency theories of emotion as well as previous
research on affect and health behavior, we hypothesize:
Hypothesis 2: (a) Negative affect and (b) positive affect partially mediate the relationship between surface acting at work
and unhealthy eating at home during the week.
Hypothesis 3: (a) Negative affect and (b) positive affect partially mediate the relationship between surface acting at work
and physical activity at home during the week.

ER Self-Efﬁcacy as a Moderator
ER self-efﬁcacy is one’s belief about their ability to control their
emotions (Wang et al., 2011). Those with high ER self-efﬁcacy
believe that they can calm down quickly when they are upset and
have good control of their own emotions (Wang et al., 2011).
Because it involves regulating or managing emotions after they
are experienced, it is consistent with “response-focused” ER
(Gross, 2002). According to COR theory, self-efﬁcacy can serve
as a personal resource that helps individuals mitigate the effect of
resource loss (Halbesleben et al., 2014). In particular, because ER
self-efﬁcacy speciﬁcally involves one’s belief about their ability
to regulate their emotions, individuals may be less prone to engage
in affect-driven behaviors at home if they have higher ER selfefﬁcacy. For example, when individuals believe that they can
effectively regulate their negative affect on their own, they may be
less likely to turn to behaviors meant to immediately address their
poor mood, but instead, manage their emotions to reduce the
affective inﬂuence on their health behaviors. In addition, selfefﬁcacy is a personal resource in its own right and it further
broadens one’s cognition and attention that helps to build personal
resources in the form of positive affect (Fredrickson, 2008). Thus,
individuals’ conﬁdence in their ability to regulate their emotions
may allow them to bounce back from negative affect and regain
positive affect after surface acting at work so that they can
minimize the adverse inﬂuence of affective states on their action
tendencies at home.
While there is no direct evidence, prior research has suggested
that ER self-efﬁcacy may be important for buffering the effects
of affect on unhealthy eating and exercise. For example, in a lab
study, individuals who believed that their bad mood was changeable were less likely to engage in unhealthy eating (measured as
the number of cookies, pretzels, and cheese crackers consumed;
Tice et al., 2001). Similarly, Job et al. (2010) found that
perceived control over emotions negatively predicted unhealthy
eating in college students. In sum, the health behavior of those
who believe they can regulate their emotions well may be less
affected by their affective state. Furthermore, this implies that
the magnitude of current indirect effects of surface acting at
work on the behavioral outcomes at home via affect may differ
by the level of ER self-efﬁcacy. That is, high ER self-efﬁcacy
will weaken the indirect relationships between surface acting at
work and unhealthy eating and physical activity at home (i.e.,
second-stage moderation in the mediation paths). Thus, we
hypothesize:
Hypothesis 4: For those who have high levels of ER selfefﬁcacy, surface acting at work will have weaker indirect
relationships with unhealthy eating at home via (a) negative
affect and (b) positive affect versus those who have low levels
of self-efﬁcacy.
Hypothesis 5: For those who have high levels of ER selfefﬁcacy, surface acting at work will have weaker indirect
relationships with physical activity at home via (a) negative
affect and (b) positive affect versus those who have low levels
of self-efﬁcacy.

Sample and Procedure
We recruited participants for this study using Proliﬁc1 that is a
research subject recruitment platform online (Peer et al., 2017). We
posted an ad for our study on the platform and invited working
adults in the U.S. who provided service to those outside their
organizations (e.g., customers, callers, guests, students, patients,
or patrons) on a daily basis. After participants completed screening
questions based on our criteria, they were directed to complete the
Time 1 (T1) survey. The T1 survey assessed participants’ demographic information, including height and weight, their ER selfefﬁcacy, and their general experience of surface acting during the
workweek. A week later at Time 2 (T2), participants rated their
general level of negative affect and positive affect at the end of the
workday during the workweek. Another week later at Time 3 (T3),
participants reported their general level of depletion at the end of the
workday during the workweek as well as unhealthy eating and
physical activity in the evenings at home during the workweek.
Participants must have completed the previous survey to be invited
to take the next survey (i.e., T1 survey completion was required to
answer T2 survey, and so forth). In addition, we included attention
check items at each wave of data collection. After we removed those
who did not accurately complete the attention check items, we had
341 participants at T1, 307 participants at T2, and 289 participants at
T3. We excluded two participants due to their unmatched Proliﬁc
IDs across the time points. The ﬁnal sample had 287 individuals,
corresponding to a response rate of 65%.
The ﬁnal sample included roughly equal proportions of men
(52%) and women (48%) and was mostly white (82%). On average,
they were 35.93 years old (SD = 11.25), worked 38 hr per week
(SD = 10.33), and had worked in their job for 7.21 years (SD =
6.44). Participants represented customer service workers in a variety
of industries (retail 24%, restaurant 9%, healthcare 8%, hospitality
8%, telemarketing 5%, and recreation 5%). Their average body mass
index (BMI) was 27.49 (SD = 6.33), which is slightly lower than the
average BMI of 29.35 for adults in the U.S. (Fryar, Kruszon-Moran,
et al., 2018). For reference, a BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is
considered normal, a BMI between 25 and 29.9 is considered
overweight, and a BMI over 30 is considered obese (Centers for
Disease Control [CDC], 2020).

Negative Affect and Positive Affect at T2
Negative and positive affect were assessed with six items each
from the Positive and Negative Affective Schedule (PANAS;
Watson & Clark, 1994). This 12-item short PANAS version has
been used in previous multiwave studies and they have found
negative and positive affect to be distinct constructs that assess
an unpleasant, aroused state and pleasant, aroused state, respectively
(e.g., Hahn et al., 2014; Sonnentag et al., 2008). To assess negative
affect, participants rated the extent they felt “distressed,” “upset,”
“irritable,” “nervous,” “jittery,” and “afraid” at the end of the
workday in general during the workweek (1 = very slightly or
not at all to 5 = extremely; α = .82). To assess positive affect,
participants rated the extent they felt “active,” “interested,”
“excited,” “strong,” “inspired,” and “alert” at the end of the workday
in general during the workweek (1 = very slightly or not at all to 5 =
extremely; α = .89).

Unhealthy Eating at T3
Unhealthy eating is commonly measured in health psychology by
listing food items and asking participants how often they ate the
foods or whether they ate the foods in a given time period
(Devonport et al., 2019). In the organizational psychology literature,
Liu et al. (2017) used a checklist of unhealthy food items, which we
used as the basis for our own checklist development (8 items) in
combination with the list of unhealthy foods from the CDC’s dietary
guidelines (Centers for Disease Control, 2015), to determine
whether participants consumed unhealthy foods in the evenings
at home after work (e.g., “salty snacks such as potato chips,”
p. 1257). In our study, participants reported how often in general
during the workweek they ate the particular unhealthy food items
after they left work (1 = never, 2 = 1–2 days, 3 = 3–4 days, 4 =
almost every day). Sample unhealthy food items include: “fried food
(e.g., fried chicken, fried ﬁsh, French fries),” and “sweet snacks
(e.g., chocolate, candy, ice-cream, and pastry).” For scoring, the
responses were summed. Cronbach’s alpha was not appropriate to
provide a reliability estimate as it is a formative scale that includes
distinct types of food choices that might not be interrelated (Liu
et al., 2017).

ER Self-Efﬁcacy at T1
ER self-efﬁcacy was assessed with the four-item scale from Wang
et al. (2011). The scale assesses participants’ beliefs about their
ability to regulate their emotions in general (1 = strongly disagree to
5 = strongly agree). Sample items are “I can always calm down
quickly when I am very angry” and “I am quite capable of
controlling my own emotions” (α = .86).

Control Variables
We measured age, sex, and BMI in T1 survey to control for their
potentially systematic inﬂuences in our model as these demographic
characteristics have been found to relate to levels of unhealthy eating
(Albisu et al., 2011) and physical activity (Biddle & Mutrie, 2007).
To calculate BMI (CDC, 2020), we converted participant reported
weight (lb to kg) and height (feet/inches to meters) and then divided
weight (kg) with height squared (m2). In addition, we controlled for
another form of emotional labor known as deep acting (i.e., an
affective display that is congruent with inner affect; Grandey, 2000).
Deep acting has been found to have inconsistent relationships with
affect (Judge et al., 2009; Scott & Barnes, 2011) and to be largely
unrelated to resource depletion (Hülsheger & Schewe, 2011). Thus,
we chose to focus our hypothesizing on surface acting, but given its
possible effects on resources involved with self-regulation
(Grandey, 2000), we included deep acting as a control variable.
In addition, we controlled for depletion at T3, given it is a possible
mediator between surface acting and unhealthy eating and physical

Summary
We found that individuals’ general level of surface acting was
positively linked to their unhealthy eating at home (T3) via their
general level of end-of-work negative affect during the workweek
(T2). That is, when individuals are emotionally distressed from
faking and suppressing their emotions at work, they are more likely
to cope with high negative affect through indulging in unhealthy
foods at home. Moreover, ER self-efﬁcacy buffered the indirect link
between surface acting and unhealthy eating, such that the effect of
surface acting on unhealthy eating via negative affect was weaker
for those with high self-efﬁcacy but stronger for those with low selfefﬁcacy. On the other hand, we found that individuals’ general level
of surface acting was positively related to their physical activity at
home (T3) via their general level of end-of-work positive affect
during the workweek (T2). When positive affective resources are
depleted from surface acting at work, individuals are less likely to
engage in physical activity. ER self-efﬁcacy did not moderate the
indirect link between surface acting and physical activity. In addition, negative affect mediated the effect of surface acting on
unhealthy eating and positive affect mediated the effect of surface
acting on physical activity, but there were no signiﬁcant differences
among the indirect effects. This suggests that none of the

mechanisms (depletion, negative affect, and positive affect) are
stronger than the others.

Study 2
We seek to increase the conﬁdence of our ﬁndings and address the
methodological limitations of Study 1 in a speciﬁc occupational
context by conducting a constructive replication. A constructive
replication is a study that seeks to improve upon the ﬁndings of a
previous study by utilizing a design that allows for stronger conclusions while retaining the virtues of the previous study (Köhler &
Cortina, 2021). Thus, in Study 2, we conduct a weekly diary study in
elementary school teachers across 5 weeks to explore the withinperson indirect link of surface acting on unhealthy eating and
physical activity via negative and positive affect and ER selfefﬁcacy as a cross-level moderator. Again, we control for withinperson depletion as an alternative mediator to test the relative
strengths of the mediators. This design allows us to not only test
our hypotheses in more rigorously but also to better understand the
inﬂuence of method factors on the observability of the hypothesized
effects.
Teaching is an emotionally demanding profession (Keller et al.,
2014) and therefore is a relevant occupation for testing the link
between surface acting and health behaviors. The job often requires
teachers to regulate the emotions they display to meet the demands
and needs of their students and students’ parents (Sutton, 2007;
Zhang & Zhu, 2008). For example, according to a series of interviews and diary studies, teachers often must suppress their negative
emotions (i.e., anger, frustration) and fake neutral or positive
emotions (Sutton, 2004, 2007). As follows, during the interviews
(Sutton, 2004), teachers frequently discussed “holding in anger,”
“gritting their teeth,” “keeping themselves in check,” “looking at
their own tone,” and “not letting their frustrations affect their
teaching” (p. 384) as well as having to “get themselves up” and
preparing themselves to display “enthusiasm and energy” (p. 386).

We used the same scales from Study 1 to measure ER selfefﬁcacy (α = .86), age, sex, and BMI in the initial survey. Surface

Analytical Strategy
Given the nested structure of our data (weekly responses within
individuals), we used multilevel path analysis in Mplus 6.12
(Muthén & Muthén, 2007) to simultaneously estimate all path
coefﬁcients in our model. Following the recommendation of
Hoffmann et al. (2000), the week-level (Level 1) variables were
centered at each person’s mean scores to remove between-person
variances in these scores. The person-level (Level 2) variables were
centered at the grand means. At the week level, we controlled for the
direct effect of surface acting on unhealthy eating and physical
activity. At the person level, we controlled for the effects of age, sex,
and BMI on the mediators, unhealthy eating, and physical activity,
and the direct effect of ER self-efﬁcacy on unhealthy eating and
physical activity.4 To test our mediation hypotheses, we used the
Monte Carlo method to empirically generate distributions of the
speciﬁc indirect effects. Using Preacher and Selig’s (2010) interactive tool for indirect effects in 1-1-1 multilevel models, we generated 95% Monte Carlo CIs using 20,000 random draws. To test the
cross-level moderation effect of ER self-efﬁcacy, we speciﬁed its
effect at Level 2 on the random slopes of mediators on unhealthy
eating and physical activity at Level 1. To further test the conditional indirect effects, we used the Monte Carlo method (20,000
repetitions) to generate 90% CIs at high and low levels of ER selfefﬁcacy (±1 SD).

Summary
The results of Study 1 are largely replicated in Study 2 and
provide further support at the within-person level. Consistent with
Study 1, we found the signiﬁcant week-level relationships: Surface
acting was positively related to unhealthy eating at home via
negative affect and that surface acting was negatively related to
physical activity at home via positive affect. Further, we found that
surface acting was negatively related to physical activity at home via
negative affect at the week level. ER self-efﬁcacy cross-moderated
the week-level link between negative affect and unhealthy eating at
home, such that the indirect effect of surface acting on unhealthy
eating via negative affect was nonsigniﬁcant for teachers with high
ER self-efﬁcacy but signiﬁcant for those with low self-efﬁcacy. In
addition, the pairwise comparisons of the indirect effects indicated
that positive affect was a stronger mediator than depletion in
connecting surface acting with physical activity. However, none
of the other speciﬁc indirect effects of surface acting on unhealthy
eating and physical activity were signiﬁcantly different from one
another.

Discussion
The purpose of this research was to illuminate the process by
which surface acting may be associated with unhealthy eating and
physical activity and test ER self-efﬁcacy as a potential moderator. Consistent with the recent call for scholars to conduct more
constructive replication studies (Köhler & Cortina, 2021), we
explored these hypotheses in two studies. The ﬁrst study of
customer service workers used a time-lagged, between-person
design across 2 weeks. The second study used a within-person
design in a larger sample of teachers across 5 weeks. The results of
both studies suggest that surface acting at work is positively
related to unhealthy eating at home via negative affect and
negatively related to physical activity at home via positive affect.

physical activity at home via negative affect. Although we found

Theoretical Implications
Our ﬁndings contribute to the literature in several ways. First, by
examining the indirect relationship between surface acting and two
important health behaviors (i.e., unhealthy eating and physical activity)
via affective mechanisms, we broaden the outcomes examined in the
emotional labor literature. The predominant research on emotional
labor focused on burnout and work-related behaviors as outcomes of
surface acting, underexploring its extended effect on health-related
behaviors in the personal life domain (Grandey et al., 2019). More
broadly, research has done little to determine the pathways by which
speciﬁc work stressors may induce unhealthy behaviors. Few people
seem to engage in physical activity and refrain from unhealthy foods on
a regular basis (Blackwell & Clarke, 2018; Fryar, Hughes, et al., 2018),

and this lack of healthy behavior may be increasing mortality rates as
well as costing organizations a signiﬁcant amount of money each year
(Pfeffer, 2018). Thus, by linking surface acting to unhealthy eating via
negative affect and physical activity via positive affect, we expand the
scholarly knowledge of speciﬁc work stressors that can undermine
important health behaviors of employees.
Second, we contribute to the development of emotional labor
research by demonstrating support for negative affect as the primary
pathway by which surface acting at work relates to unhealthy eating
in the home domain and positive affect as the primary pathway by
which surface acting related to physical activity. There has been
controversy and mixed results regarding the mechanisms by which
self-regulation demands, such as surface acting, affect future activities that require self-regulation (e.g., Sayre et al., 2020). Researchers
have questioned whether individuals’ inability to engage in further
self-regulation after a self-regulation task is due to depletion or to
one’s mood caused by the exertion of self-regulation (Carter et al.,

important role affect versus depletion plays in whether individuals
turn to unhealthy foods and refrain from physical activity to cope
with emotional labor demands.
Third, we specify a possible boundary condition of the relationship between surface acting and unhealthy eating via negative affect.
The indirect relationship of surface acting on unhealthy eating was
signiﬁcant only at the low level of ER self-efﬁcacy, which suggests
that ER self-efﬁcacy can be a protective resource factor against
unhealthy food consumption as a result of surface acting and
negative affect. However, ER self-efﬁcacy did not moderate the
links between negative affect and physical activity nor between
positive affect and unhealthy eating and physical activity. One
possible explanation for this nonsigniﬁcant moderation is that
physical activity may require much more resources (effort, time)
than avoiding unhealthy food. Physical activity, especially the
dimension of vigorous activity, requires a signiﬁcant amount of
energy as well as involves a number of barriers to overcome (Biddle
& Mutrie, 2007). On the other hand, avoiding unhealthy foods may
require less effort. Thus, even though those with higher ER selfefﬁcacy may have more control over their affect and emotions, it
may be still difﬁcult for them to muster the additional effort required
of physical activity when they are in a negative affective or low
positive affective state.

Practical Implications
Regarding practical implications of the ﬁndings, this study
suggests that organizations, supervisors, and employees should
be aware of the detrimental cost surface acting can have on employees’ health behaviors via affect. The ﬁnding that affect is the
mechanism through which surface acting is associated with
unhealthy eating and physical activity presents potential avenues
for prevention and intervention programs. For example, managers
and frontline workers could get together to discuss how to reduce
their negative affect and increase their positive affect in general if
surface acting cannot be completely removed due to the nature of
their jobs. Also, importantly, in the present study, ER self-efﬁcacy
buffered the indirect effect of surface acting on unhealthy eating via
negative affect. Self-efﬁcacy is considered a malleable characteristic

that can be enhanced through proper training and experiences (Gist &
Mitchell, 1992); as such, previous research has demonstrated that ER
self-efﬁcacy can be improved through training (e.g., Buruck et al.,
2016; Slaski & Cartwright, 2003). Thus, similar training methods
may be applied to emotional labor workers to reduce the detrimental
effects of negative affect on important health behavior.

Limitations and Future Directions
This study has several limitations that may be addressed in future
research. First, using self-report may raise concern for common
method bias (Podsakoff et al., 2003). However, we had a few
preemptive approaches to reduce the concern, following Podsakoff
et al.’s recommendations (2003). For example, we did not use the
same response options across survey instruments (e.g., all
agreement-based scales) but diversify response options (i.e., frequency, degree, agreement, and application). Furthermore, we
included age, sex, and BMI as control variables in both studies
as well as deep acting. In addition, the signiﬁcant moderation effect
we found reduces the concern given that common method variance
decreases the sensitivity to detect moderation effects (Siemsen et al.,
2010). Nevertheless, future research may use multimethod data to
avoid the possible concern in future research. For example, we may
have spouses report unhealthy eating or physical activity.
Second, although we controlled for variables that might create a
spurious relationship between surface acting and unhealthy eating
and physical activity (i.e., age, sex, and BMI), we cannot rule out all
possible third variables that might make certain employees experience higher levels of unhealthy eating and lower levels of physical
activity. Also, individuals with a high BMI might have a tendency to
underreport eating or overreport physical activity (Rasmussen et al.,
2007), and individuals might inaccurately report more healthy
behavior over time in repeated assessment (Westerterp & Goris,
2002); thus, we included BMI in Studies 1 and 2 as a control
variable. However, there may be ways to reduce concern for the bias
reporting of health behaviors in future research. For example,
unhealthy eating could be evaluated via smartphones by having
participants scan their food items into diet tracking applications or
photographing their food and submitting the pictures to an online
food diary (McClung et al., 2018). In addition, physical activity
could be measured by wearable devices, such as ﬁtness tracking
watches (e.g., FitBit, Apple Watch), accelerometers, or heart rate
monitors (McClung et al., 2018). Additionally, the predictor, mediator, and outcome variables were measured at the same time each
week in Study 2, which limits causal inferences. We attempted to
address this concern in some ways in Study 2 as we consider the
relationships within individuals. For instance, we explored an
additional model controlling for the time-lagged effects of the
previous week’s levels of negative and positive affect, unhealthy
eating, and physical activity in Study 2. In addition, we ran another
model in Study 2 where we regressed unhealthy eating and physical
activity on time to account for any possible linear time trends in
unhealthy eating and physical activity. The direct effects of time on
unhealthy eating (γ = 0.07, p = .29) and physical activity (γ = 0.05,
p = .41) were not signiﬁcant, and the pattern of results was virtually
the same as the current results. Nevertheless, for better causal
inferences, future research should separate the time points of the
predictor, mediator, and outcome variables.

Third, given that surface acting, affect, unhealthy eating, and
physical activity may ﬂuctuate on a daily basis (Liu et al., 2017;
Scott & Barnes, 2011; Sonnentag & Jelden, 2009), in future research
it may be beneﬁcial to explore the within-person relationships at the
day level rather than the week level. However, in the present study, it
was not convenient for teachers to complete surveys every day of the
week. In addition, being able to explore these phenomena across
multiple weeks provides us with information on weekly patterns of
eating and physical activity that may have important implications for
longer-term health maintenance (Buchowski et al., 2004; Hill et al.,
2018). In contrast, most daily diary studies only last from 5 to 10
days (e.g., Sayre et al., 2020; Sonnentag & Jelden, 2009). Also,
because we focused on surface acting’s relationship with unhealthy
eating and physical activity via affect during the workweek (i.e.,
Monday–Friday), we were unable to determine if the weekend
offered an opportunity for workers to recover from surface acting
and engage in more health behaviors. Previous research has found
that recovery during weekends may play an important role in
reversing strain caused by job demands (e.g., Binnewies et al.,
2010). Thus, future research may include weekend assessments of
depletion, affect, recovery, and health behaviors.
Fourth, there may be concern for generalizability. In Study 1, we
utilized the research subject recruitment platform Proliﬁc to recruit
participants. Recruitment platforms, such as MTurk, have been
criticized for treating its participants poorly (Semules, 2018) and
poor data quality (Chmielewski & Kucker, 2020). Therefore, those
who utilize these platforms may not be representative of the general
workforce. However, Proliﬁc has some built in methods to reduce
this concern (i.e., participant identity veriﬁcation, a base pay for
participants, and decoupled prescreening questions from surveys to
reduce dishonest reporting) and has been shown to have demographics largely consistent with the U.S. workforce (Peer et al.,
2017). In addition, we included our own prescreening questions,
time separation of our variables, diversiﬁed response options, and
attention checks. In Study 2, our sample only included elementary
school teachers in the Midwestern U.S. who were mostly white and
female, thereby limiting its generalizability although our sample
represented the typical demographic proﬁle of elementary school
teachers. Thus, future research may explore the model in a variety of
different service industries. In addition, future studies may examine
our model in non-Western samples to explore its generalizability as
participants in both studies were limited to those in the U.S.
Although the rate of obesity is increasing across the world, it is
particularly high in the U.S. (World Health Organization, 2016).
One explanation is that adults in the U.S. and other Western
countries consume more sugary and processed foods and are less
physically active than other countries (Basu et al., 2013). Thus,
those in non-Western countries, where stronger healthier dietary and
physical activity norms are present, may be less sensitive to the
effects of affect on these health behaviors.
Fifth, our study is limited to the scope of health behaviors at
home. In future research, we may want to consider the effect of
surface acting via affect on health behaviors both at home and at
work. For example, a study by Sonnentag et al. (2017) found that
self-control demands relate to unhealthy snacking at work via affectregulation motives. Thus, future studies may explore whether
surface acting may affect these behaviors at work via affect or
any other possible mediators. Additionally, extending the outcomes
to the workplace will allow us to examine other organization-based

313

resources that may buffer the adverse effects of surface acting. For
example, the availability of healthy foods at work may decrease the
relationship between negative affect and unhealthy eating at work.
Alternatively, organizational health behavior climate (i.e., shared
perceptions of an organization’s approach to employee health
behaviors, including expectations and rewards to stimulate health
behaviors) may buffer the stress effects of surface acting on eating
and physical activity (Sonnentag & Pundt, 2016).

Conclusion
In summary, we explored the relationship between surface acting
and unhealthy eating and physical activity at home in two studies. In
both studies, we found that surface acting at work is positively
related to unhealthy eating via negative affect and negatively related
to physical activity via positive affect. Also, in Study 2, we found
that surface acting is negatively related to physical activity at home
via negative affect. It seems to cope with affect, individuals may
reduce motivation and effort in subsequent self-regulation at home
(i.e., healthy eating and physical activity). Important to note, we
found that one’s level of ER self-efﬁcacy minimizes the indirect link
between surface acting and unhealthy eating via negative affect in
both studies. In other words, those who feel conﬁdent that they can
effectively regulate their negative emotions may not easily allow
them to affect their eating. The present study adds to our knowledge
and theoretical development around how emotional labor relates to
health behaviors.
