ABSTRACT

Keywords:
Recovery
Working college students
Latent profile analysis
Well-being
Job demands

Recovery experiences are seen as key correlates of employee well-being at work. Yet, there is a
dearth of research on the recovery experiences of working college students, despite the fact that
these employees comprise a large and potentially at-risk percentage of the workforce. Building
from recent profile analytic work on recovery experiences conducted with full-time employees
(e.g., Bennett, Gabriel, Calderwood, Dahling, & Trougakos, 2016), we similarly take a personcentered, holistic view to understand profiles of recovery experiences amongst working college
students. In Study 1 (N = 520), we establish whether profiles of recovery experiences exist in
working college students. In an effort to replicate findings from Bennett et al. (2016), we also
consider whether job demands (e.g., role ambiguity, time pressure) and job resources (e.g., job
control) help in differentiating profile membership, and whether profile membership relates to
working college student well-being (e.g., work exhaustion, work engagement, somatic complaints) and turnover intentions. In Study 2 (N = 536), we aim to further extend the findings of
Study 1 by considering additional antecedents unique to the experiences of working college
students (e.g., work hours, organizational identification, school hassles, school flexibility), as
well as an expanded set of outcomes that consider effects at school and at the work-school interface (e.g., school engagement, school exhaustion, work-to-school conflict). Combined, our
results yield significant theoretical implications relevant to the generalizability of models of
recovery across occupational contexts, as well as substantial practical implications for the
maintenance of well-being and occupational health in working student populations.

There is extensive evidence to suggest that employee occupational health and well-being depend on the opportunity to recover
from work-related effort (Sonnentag, Venz, & Casper, 2017). Indeed, recovery experiences that cease work demands (psychological

in which recovery experiences correlate with indices of employee well-being, such as decreased fatigue (ρ = −0.18 to −0.39) and

However, we know little about how recovery experiences operate outside of the context of traditional, full-time, salaried workers
(e.g., Bennett et al., 2016; Kinnunen, Mauno, & Siltaloppi, 2010; Park & Fritz, 2015). To date, researchers have implicitly assumed

employees or knowledge workers” (p. 373), suggesting that the generalizability of recovery experiences across contexts remains an
open question. Thus, we seek to conceptually replicate and theoretically extend results concerning profiles of recovery experiences—person-centered, holistic profiles signifying how individuals jointly experience recovery across indicators (e.g., psychological
detachment, relaxation, mastery, control, and problem-solving pondering [see below for theoretical justification surrounding this
additional profile indicator])—in an under-represented population in recovery and occupational health research: working college
students.1
The experiences of working college students are severely under-represented in studies of occupational health (Calderwood &
Gabriel, 2017). Although the over-representation of college student participants in many areas of social scientific research has
received deserved criticism (e.g., Gordon, Slade, & Schmitt, 1986), there is good reason to suspect that our understanding of recovery
experiences and related correlates may be enriched by looking beyond the experiences of full-time, salaried employees to study
different kinds of work arrangements (see Scarpello, Huber, & Vandenberg, 1988, for a similar line of reasoning). Existing theoretical
work (Feldman, 1990; Katz & Kahn, 1978) and empirical evidence (Calderwood & Gabriel, 2017) calls into question whether occupational health models can directly translate to part-time employment contexts. Working college students are of particular interest
as part-time workers because they are at risk for a variety of mental health problems that are poorly understood (see the special issue
on college student mental health in Journal of Clinical Psychology, Castillo & Schwartz, 2013). Accordingly, an investigation of
recovery experiences and correlates in a sample of working college students can both evaluate the generalizability of recovery profiles
across non-standard employment contexts and identify avenues to enhance college student well-being.
In this research, we adopt a person-centered approach to examine recovery experiences for three primary reasons. First, as
outlined by Bennett et al. (2016), a person-centered approach clarifies how different recovery experiences are used in combination,
allowing for the derivation of profiles that differ quantitatively (i.e., in absolute level—meaning there is level-differentiation in the
profile indicators) and qualitatively (i.e., in relative comparisons between levels of the different recovery experiences that define a
profile—meaning there is shape-differentiation in the profile indicators). Thus, it is plausible that how time is spent after work (e.g.,
watching a movie) may elicit multiple forms of recovery experiences (e.g., psychological detachment and relaxation). This stands in
contrast to a variable-centered approach, in which each recovery experience is examined in isolation and it is not possible to efficiently model alternative combinations of recovery experiences that emerge amongst employees (see Wang & Hanges, 2011). Second,
a person-centered approach captures heterogeneity that goes unobserved in a variable-centered approach (Bennett et al., 2016;
Gabriel, Daniels, Diefendorff, & Greguras, 2015; Morin, Meyer, Creusier, & Biétry, 2016; Wang & Hanges, 2011), which allows for the
detection of subpopulations of recovery experience combinations that may exist in the working student context. In turn, these profiles
may relate distinctly to critical recovery-related antecedents and outcomes. Third, person-centered applications benefit strongly from
conceptual replications conducted in new samples and contexts (Wang & Hanges, 2011). Accordingly, if similar profiles of recovery
experiences emerge for working college students as are observed in full-time employee contexts, this would bolster past personcentered studies of recovery experiences (Bennett et al., 2016), while also enhancing our understanding of the relevance of recovery
profiles to working college students.
In the sections that follow, we first provide an overview of the theoretical background of recovery experiences and highlight
findings linking such experiences to well-being in full-time employee contexts. Next, we discuss the under-representation of working
college students in these studies, and outline why it is theoretically and practically important to understand the recovery experiences
of working college students from a person-centered perspective. We then provide an overview of Bennett et al.'s (2016) personcentered study of recovery profiles of full-time employees, followed by our initial investigation of recovery profiles of working college
students in Study 1; Study 1 was designed to conceptually replicate the profiles and correlates in Bennett et al. (2016) within a
working student sample. Finally, we detail an expanded theoretical discussion of correlates unique to working college students that
were the focus of Study 2.

Research on recovery experiences is largely predicated on a synthesis of two theories: the Effort-Recovery Model (ERM; Meijman

& Mulder, 1998) and Conservation of Resources Theory (COR; Hobfoll, 1989). The ERM states that work demands engender physiological and subjective load reactions that increase employee strain, with these effects being reversible via the process of recovery if
these demands cease (Meijman & Mulder, 1998). Relatedly, COR theory states that employees strive to obtain, retain, and protect
psychological resources (e.g., time, energy, self-efficacy; Hobfoll & Shirom, 2001), and that a loss or threat of loss of these resources
engenders stress (Hobfoll, 1989). Recovery research extends these ideas beyond the boundaries of the workplace, with time away
from work (e.g., in the evening, on weekends) theorized to allow employees the opportunity to recover from work demands and
restore lost or threatened psychological resources (Sonnentag, 2001). In turn, employees who effectively recover should maintain—or
even enhance—their well-being over time (e.g., lower exhaustion; increased work engagement; decreased somatic complaints;
Moreno-Jiménez, Rodríguez-Muñoz, Sanz-Vergel, & Garrosa, 2012; Sonnentag, 2001). Although much of this work is cross-sectional
and correlational precluding causal inferences (Sonnentag et al., 2017; Sonnentag & Zijlstra, 2006), time away from work, at a
minimum, correlates positively with well-being.
Employees benefit from time away from work when they engage in recovery experiences to restore resources and recuperate from
work stress (Sonnentag et al., 2017; Sonnentag & Fritz, 2007). To date, most research has focused on four key recovery experiences.
Two experiences help aid recovery by contributing to the cessation of work demands: (1) psychological detachment, a physical and
mental sense of being away from work; and (2) relaxation, a state of low activation and high positive affect. The remaining two
experiences aid recovery through the maintenance and generation of psychological resources: (3) mastery, the experience of challenge
and learning in non-work domains; and (4) control, a perceived freedom to decide how to spend leisure time (Sonnentag & Fritz,
2007). Recently, however, scholars have begun to suggest that alternative experiences after working hours may be restorative for
employees' resource-levels and well-being, such that problem-solving pondering (i.e., constructive reflections about work challenges/
events; Querstret & Cropley, 2012) may facilitate recovery by also replenishing psychological resources as employees think proactively about work (Cropley & Zijlstra, 2011).
Importantly, recovery experiences correlate with outcomes related to well-being including work emotional exhaustion (e.g.,
Blanco-Donoso, Garrosa, Demerouti, & Moreno-Jiménez, 2017), work engagement (e.g., Sonnentag, Binnewies, & Mojza, 2010), and
fatigue (e.g., Sonnentag, Binnewies, & Mojza, 2008). In addition, consistent with theoretical extensions of Sonnentag's (2001) recovery framework, such as the stressor-detachment model (Sonnentag & Fritz, 2015), evidence suggests that encountered job demands and job resources may influence the nature of employees' recovery experiences (e.g., Bennett et al., 2016; Blanco-Donoso
et al., 2017). Thus, although evidence of the causal direction of the relationship between recovery and well-being remains mixed (see
Sonnentag et al., 2008, and ten Brummelhuis & Bakker, 2012, for exceptions), correlational evidence indicates that, at a minimum,
there are potential well-being benefits for employees to gain when engaging in recovery experiences.
However, as noted by Sonnentag et al. (2017), one of the primary limitations of the recovery literature is its narrow focus on fulltime employees working in fairly traditional work arrangements, such as administrative staff. Accordingly, the foundations of recovery research are built on the experiences of full-time, salaried employees working in prototypical employment contexts, leaving an
open empirical question as to whether recovery experiences and related correlates will translate across less-traditional occupational
contexts. This omission in the recovery literature is part of a broader trend of under-representation of part-time employees in
organizational scholarship that has been recognized for nearly 40 years (Feldman, 1990; Miller & Terborg, 1979; Thorsteinson,
2003). Partial inclusion theory (Katz & Kahn, 1978) emphasizes that individuals who hold part-time work schedules tend to be less
included in the organization's social system than full-time employees, and may consequently experience organizational life differently
(Eberhardt & Shani, 1984; Miller & Terborg, 1979).
Partial inclusion may be more pronounced when considering working college students; as Feldman (1990) stated, “Part-time work
is much less central in the lives of students than it is to regularly employed adult workers” (p. 109). Limited support suggests that
these partial inclusion theory arguments apply to processes at the work/non-work interface. For example, extrapolating findings from
employees working part-time schedules, Chambel, Carvalho, Cesário, and Lopes (2017) observed that while heightened job demands
contribute to work-life conflict for full- and part-time employees, this association was significantly weaker for those working parttime.
To date, we know very little about how recovery experiences operate amongst working college students. In fact, only six studies
have measured recovery experiences in college student samples (Cheng & McCarthy, 2013; Ragsdale, Beehr, Grebner, & Han, 2011;
Reinecke, Klatt, & Krämer, 2011; Safstrom & Hartig, 2013; Schraub, Turgut, Clavairoly, & Sonntag, 2013; van Hooff & Baas, 2013);
this is in comparison to the dozens of studies that have focused on full-time employees (for a review, see: Sonnentag et al., 2017).
Cheng and McCarthy (2013) measured recovery processes in working students, finding that work conflict was most strongly associated with work satisfaction when students engaged in lower psychological detachment and avoidance coping (i.e., distancing
cognitively from the stressor). No other recovery experiences were measured by Cheng and McCarthy; thus, these findings do not
clarify the generalizability of recovery experiences, nor do they speak to possible ways that the multiple recovery experiences may
combine, which is a point we return to below. Four of the remaining studies focused on processes that do not pertain to work, such as
how students recover from school-related stressors (Ragsdale et al., 2011; Schraub et al., 2013) and how short-term experimental
interventions influence recovery (Reinecke et al., 2011; Van Hooff & Baas, 2013). Finally, Safstrom and Hartig (2013) used stressors
encountered at school as a proxy for job stressors in modeling relationships with recovery experiences, which may be problematic
given evidence that work and school stressors can manifest differently (Calderwood & Gabriel, 2017). Thus, past work has done little
to enhance understanding of the generality of recovery processes for working students and how demands tied to work may affect
students' recovery.
These questions have practical value given the pressing need to foster greater well-being amongst working students. Although we
do not know how working college students experience recovery, research is clear that these students experience dual demands from

2. Study 1: Extending recovery profiles to working college students
Until recently, scholars have largely considered how employees experience recovery as a set of isolated experiences after work,
and, in many cases, have overemphasized the relevance of psychological detachment in comparison to other experiences (e.g., Derks,
van Mierlo, & Schmitz, 2014; for a review, see: Sonnentag et al., 2017). Challenging this assumption, Bennett et al. (2016) posited
that employees may simultaneously experience multiple recovery experiences, capturing how psychological detachment, relaxation,
mastery, control, and problem-solving pondering experiences co-occur after work hours. This orientation towards recovery experiences fits within the broader stress appraisal literature (Folkman, Lazarus, Gruen, & DeLongis, 1986), which posits that it is possible
for employees to apply multiple appraisals to a single experience (e.g., appraising cooking a meal after work as having high levels of
relaxation and mastery, or reading a book in the evening as facilitating high levels of psychological detachment, relaxation, and
control). Further, focusing on profiles involves a person-centered view of recovery experiences (Gabriel, Campbell, Djurdjevic,
Johnson, & Rosen, 2018; Wang & Hanges, 2011), which offers a more comprehensive understanding of how individuals' experience
recovery after work relative to a variable-centered approach. However, beyond Bennett et al. (2016), limited work to date has
focused on how individuals undergo combined recovery experiences (e.g., Siltaloppi, Kinnunen, Feldt, & Tolvanen, 2011; Trougakos,
Hideg, Cheng, & Beal, 2014). As such, we continue to build from Bennett et al.'s (2016) work to examine whether profiles of recovery
experiences emerge amongst working college students.
Importantly, person-centered studies are inductive, in that the number and nature of the profiles is not known a priori (e.g.,
Bennett et al., 2016; Gabriel et al., 2015, 2018; Wang & Hanges, 2011). Thus, we do not make formal predictions about the nature of
profiles that may emerge in working college students. That said, in the absence of other guiding research, we begin with the possibility that we may be able to replicate the three recovery profiles that consistently emerged across Bennett et al.'s (2016) two studies
of full-time employees. First, Bennett et al. (2016) identified one profile of employees who reported higher relaxation, mastery,
control, and problem-solving pondering, with lower levels of psychological detachment. The authors labeled this profile recovering
ponderers, given that they were engaging in multiple forms of recovery with lower psychological detachment, along with heightened
thoughts about work. In their second profile, Bennett et al. (2016) identified employees who were leaving work behind; these employees engaged in high levels of psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control, along with the lowest problem-solving
pondering, suggesting that this group largely separated from work after hours. The third profile was a pondering profile, with employees who engaged in the highest problem-solving pondering, with lower levels of the remaining four recovery experiences.
Interestingly, Bennett et al.'s (2016) first study revealed a fourth profile—detach, low relax—in which employees reported moderate
levels of all five recovery experiences, the lowest of which were relaxation and problem-solving pondering. Yet, the authors were less
confident in this profile given that it did not replicate in their second study.
Despite this initial work, other combinations of recovery experiences are theoretically plausible, particularly when considering
the circumstances of working college students. For instance, according to partial inclusion theory (Feldman, 1990; Katz & Kahn,
1978), working college students may not feel overly embedded at work, which could limit their engagement in problem-solving
pondering. Paired with their competing demands tied to school, it may be the case that a profile in which all five experiences are low
is possible (i.e., lacking thoughts tied to work after hours yet not having time to fully engage in recovery experiences). Likewise, it is
also plausible that working college students may report profiles with high levels of psychological detachment and relaxation, yet low
levels of mastery and control given the limited ‘free time’ they may possess. However, given that no work to date has considered how
recovery experiences combine amongst working college students, our first research question aims to determine whether profiles of
recovery experiences exist in working student populations. Fitting with person-centered research that has adopted an inductive
approach, we first examine the following:
Research Question 1: Do profiles of recovery experiences (psychological detachment, relaxation, control, mastery, and problemsolving pondering) that are similar or dissimilar to Bennett et al. (2016) emerge in working student populations?
Further, part of the value of using a person-centered analytic approach is the ability to identify antecedents that differentiate profile
membership, as well as outcomes of the various profiles to help support the validity of the underlying profile structure. Thus, to build directly
from the work of Bennett et al. (2016) in a working student context, we focus on three work-specific antecedents that these authors
considered: role ambiguity, time pressure, and job control. These antecedents fit with the Job Demands-Resources Model (JD-R; Bakker &
Demerouti, 2007, 2017), which separates work experiences into either job demands or job resources. Role ambiguity and time pressure
represent two job demands that differ in their underlying experience; role ambiguity is characterized as a hindrance to work goals, whereas
time pressure is viewed as a positive challenge that can facilitate goals (Cavanaugh, Boswell, Roehling, & Boudreau, 2000; LePine, Podsakoff,
& LePine, 2005). Although job demands seemingly drain employee resources increasing the need for recovery as per the ERM, evidence from
Kinnunen, Feldt, Siltaloppi, and Sonnentag (2011; see also Bennett et al., 2016, 2018) suggests that these demands can also increase the

continued activation will make working college students more likely to belong to profiles in which low levels of prototypical recovery

We also consider whether the resource of job control differentiates profile membership. Job control should provide employees
with higher levels of agency during time spent at work, with this agency potentially spilling over into increased recovery after

Thus, fitting with our inductive approach, we aim to examine the following question:
Research Question 2: Do role ambiguity, time pressure, and job control differentiate profiles of recovery experiences amongst
working college students?
Finally, we explore work exhaustion, work engagement, and somatic complaints as outcomes of profile membership, similar to Bennett
et al. (2016), given their well-replicated associations with recovery experiences (e.g., Demerouti, Bakker, de Jonge, Janssen, & Schaufeli,
2001; Sonnentag et al., 2008) and the possibility that these work-related experiences also contribute to the well-being of working college
students. However, we also consider a fourth outcome that we view as pertinent to working students: turnover intentions. We view this as a
necessary theoretical extension of Bennett et al. (2016), given that it has long been recognized that the competing demands of work and
school contribute to elevated turnover rates in working students, relative to full-time, non-student employees (see Feldman, 1990, for an
overview of this issue). As such, we consider the following outcomes in relation to recovery profiles:
Research Question 3: Do profiles of recovery experiences amongst working college students differentially relate to indicators of
work well-being (e.g., work exhaustion, work engagement, somatic complaints) and turnover intentions?
2.1. Study 1 method
2.1.1. Procedure and sample
Students enrolled in undergraduate Psychology and Management courses at a large, urban Southeastern university who were at
least 18 years of age and working part-time were asked to complete an online study for course credit. To define part-time work, we
followed Calderwood and Gabriel's (2017) cut-off that part-time employees work at least 20 h per week and Bennett et al.'s (2016)
cut-off that full-time employees be working 32 h or more per week. Participants completed an online survey assessing recovery
experiences, job demands (e.g., time pressure, role ambiguity), job resources (e.g., job control), well-being (e.g., work exhaustion,
work engagement, somatic complaints), and turnover intentions. To ensure data quality, we had three quality control items in the
survey (e.g., “Please select ‘strongly agree’ to answer this question;” Bennett et al., 2016; Hauser & Schwarz, 2016; Kung, Kwok, &
Brown, 2018; Meade & Craig, 2012). Participants who missed any of these items were removed prior to data analysis.
In total, we received data from 687 working student employees. After removing 143 participants for working more than part-time
(> 31 h per week), 23 participants for missing quality controls, and 1 participant for not responding to items for the five profile
indicators, we retained a final sample of 520 part-time working students (75.7% retained).2 Our sample was majority female (64.7%)
and about half White (49.5%). Participants were 20.80 years old on average and worked an average of 23.42 h weekly. Participants
worked in a variety of occupations (e.g., sales associates; administrative assistants; entry-level managerial positions).
2.1.2. Measures
We directly mirrored the measures used in Bennett et al. (2016), and added an indicator of turnover intentions. All measures were
self-reported and administered in a randomized order. Responses were on a 5-point scale (1 = strongly disagree; 5 = strongly agree)
unless noted.
2.1.2.1. Recovery experiences. We used the Recovery Experiences Questionnaire (Sonnentag & Fritz, 2007). Participants evaluated
their agreement or disagreement with each statement in general, during time after work, with four items per recovery experience.
Example items are: “I forget about work” (psychological detachment, ω = 0.82); “I use the time to relax” (relaxation, ω = 0.96); “I learn
new things” (mastery experiences, ω = 0.91); and “I decide my own schedule” (control, ω = 0.89). Consistent with Bennett et al.
(2016), we assessed problem-solving pondering with four items adapted from Cropley, Michalianou, Pravettoni, and Millward (2012;
ω = .84). An item is: “During time after work, I find solutions to work-related problems.”
2.1.2.2. Role ambiguity. We used 10 items from Rizzo, House, and Lirtzman (1970; ω = .81) to measure role ambiguity. Items were
reverse scored when appropriate to reflect higher role ambiguity. An example item is: “I feel certain how I will be evaluated for a raise
or promotion.”

2.1.2.3. Time pressure. Time pressure was measured with five items from Spector and Jex (1998; ω = .85) on a 5-point scale

2.1.2.5. Work exhaustion. We used three items from Wharton (1993); see also Bennett et al., 2016) to assess work-related emotional

2.1.2.6. Work engagement. We used the nine item Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (UWES; Schaufeli, Bakker, & Salanova, 2006). The
UWES assesses each work engagement sub-dimension—absorption (e.g., “I am immersed in my work”), vigor (e.g., “At my job, I feel
strong and vigorous”), and dedication (e.g., “My job inspires me”). Fitting with recovery scholarship, we did not have theoretical
reasons as to why the sub-dimensions would operate differently as outcomes (e.g., Bennett et al., 2016; Sonnentag, 2003; Sonnentag,
Mojza, Demerouti, & Bakker, 2012). As such, we used the overall composite of engagement in our analyses (ω = 0.89).
2.1.2.7. Somatic complaints. We used a shortened version of the psychosomatic complaints scale from Spector and Jex (1998; ω =
.76) to evaluate somatic complaints. Participants rated the extent to which they experienced backaches, headaches, eye strain,
tiredness/fatigue, loss of appetite, and problems sleeping on a 5-point scale (1 = very slightly/not at all; 5 = extremely).
2.1.2.8. Turnover intentions. We used three items from Cropanzano, James, and Konovsky (1993; ω = .75). An item is: “I intend to
leave my organization within the next year.”
2.1.3. Analytic approach
We used latent profile analysis (LPA) in Mplus 8 (Muthén & Muthén, 1998-2015) to test for recovery profiles (see Asparouhov & Muthén,
2014a; Lanza, Tan, & Bray, 2013; Vermunt, 2010). Following Nylund, Asparouhov, and Muthén (2007), we began by extracting two profiles,
increasing the number of profiles extracted until model fit no longer improved. Each model used 5000 sets of starting values with 100
iterations; the best 200 sets were retained (Hipp & Bauer, 2006; Morin et al., 2016). Given issues with models converging when random
variances are modeled, variances of our profile indicators were fixed to be equal across profiles (Bauer & Curran, 2003; Chen, Bollen, Paxton,
Curran, & Kirby, 2001; Morin et al., 2016). In regards to determining model fit, we report the following statistics: Akaike Information
Criterion (AIC), Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), consistent AIC (C-AIC; calculated as BIC plus the number of free parameters modeled),
sample size adjusted Bayesian Information Criterion (SSA-BIC), Lo-Mendell-Rubin likelihood ratio test (LMR; Lo, Mendell, & Rubin, 2001;
recommended by Tofighi & Enders, 2007), bootstrap likelihood ratio test (BLRT; recommended by Nylund et al., 2007), and Entropy. For
LMR and BLRT, these statistics are evaluated in regards to their level of statistical significance (p < .05). For Entropy, values range from 0.00
to 1.00; values closer to 1.00 indicate greater classification accuracy (Jung & Wickrama, 2008). Recent simulations suggest that BIC, C-AIC,
and SSA-BIC should be relied upon when choosing a final profile solution (e.g., Diallo, Morin, & Lu, 2016, 2017; Nylund et al., 2007; Peugh &
Fan, 2013, 2015); as such, these indices were primarily used when selecting a profile solution, though we report the LMR, BLRT, and Entropy
values for completeness and consistency with Bennett et al. (2016). Additionally, in order to achieve theoretical parsimony in our final profile
solution, we ensured that the profiles extracted were not theoretically redundant with each other; for instance, we did not select a solution in
which one profile was represented twice with just slight quantitative differences (e.g., Howard, Gagné, Morin, & Van den Broeck, 2016).
The aforementioned profile extraction process constitutes the first step of the automatic three-step approach to LPA (Asparouhov
& Muthén, 2014a; Lanza et al., 2013; Vermunt, 2010). After obtaining the best fitting solution, we obtained most likely class
membership from the posterior distribution in the second step; this allows researchers to appropriately capture the error in profile
classification (Morin et al., 2016; Wang & Hanges, 2011). In the third step, we modeled our antecedents as predictors of profile
membership and profile membership as predictors of our outcomes. For antecedents, we used the R3STEP command (Asparouhov &
Muthén, 2014a; Vermunt, 2010), which uses multinomial logistic regression to determine whether a one-unit increase in an antecedent makes an individual more or less likely to belong to one profile compared to another. To aid interpretation, we calculated odds
ratios [OR] for each antecedent comparison, reflecting “the change in likelihood of membership in a target profile versus a comparison profile associated for each unit of increase in the predictor” (Morin et al., 2016, p. 246). The OR values can be positive or
negative; as noted by Morin et al. (2016), “an OR of 3 suggests that each unit of increase in the predictor is associated with
participants being 3 times more likely to be member of the target profile (vs. the comparison profile). ORs under 1 correspond to
negative coefficients and suggest that the likelihood of membership in the target profile is reduced (e.g., an OR of .5 shows that a one
unit increase in the predictor reduces by 50% the likelihood of membership in the target vs. comparison profile)” (p. 246). For the
outcomes, we used the BCH command (Bolck, Croon, & Hagenaars, 2004; see Asparouhov & Muthén, 2014b; Bakk & Vermunt, 2016);
analyses reflect whether one profile is statistically significantly different from other profiles per criterion. The BCH approach—an
advancement upon the former DCON approach developed by Lanza et al. (2013)—involves a weighted group analysis and prevents
shifts in latent profile membership during outcome analysis (Asparouhov & Muthén, 2014b). The R3STEP and BCH commands were
run separately in accordance with available automatic three-step procedures (Asparouhov & Muthén, 2014a; Lanza et al., 2013).

Combined, our results paint a complex picture pertaining to the recovery experiences of working college students. Consistent with
Bennett et al. (2016), three profiles emerged that were also found amongst full-time employees (detach, low relax; recovering ponderer;
and leaving work behind). And, similar to Bennett et al. (2016), we found limited support pertaining to job demands and resources
differentiating profile membership in the working student context. However, contrary to Bennett et al.'s (2016) findings, we did not
identify a pondering profile in this context, and instead found a moderate recoverer profile, suggesting that problem-solving pondering
may combine with other recovery experiences differently amongst working college students. Further, results tied to well-being
correlates of recovery profiles were all statistically non-significant, again running in contrast to Bennett et al.'s (2016) previous
findings. Specifically, they found that full-time employees in the leaving work behind profile had benefits in the form of lower
emotional exhaustion and somatic complaints in particular, and that employees in the recovering ponderer profile tended to report
higher levels of engagement at work. Given the limited support we found for these antecedent and outcome variables in Study 1, we
expanded our focus on additional correlates in Study 2 that may be of greater theoretical relevance to employees who are (a) working
part-time and (b) attending college full-time.
3. Study 2: Theoretically expanding profiles of recovery experiences in working students
The primary purpose of Study 1 was to delineate whether similar or dissimilar profiles of recovery experiences emerged amongst
working college students compared to primary work in full-time employees done by Bennett et al. (2016). In addition, we focused on
work antecedents and well-being correlates that aligned with Bennett et al. (2016). In Study 2, we expand the nomological network
surrounding recovery experience profiles by identifying factors unique to working college students that may better demarcate profile
membership. Additionally, we consider an expanded set of outcome correlates that tap directly into the work/school interface (e.g.,
Calderwood & Gabriel, 2017), in light of the limited support for recovery profiles in relation to work-related well-being observed in
Study 1.
Specifically, drawing from partial inclusion theory (Feldman, 1990; Katz & Kahn, 1978) and theory pertaining to boundary
management at the work/non-work interface (Ashforth, Kreiner, & Fugate, 2000; Clark, 2000; Matthews, Barnes-Farrell, & Bulger,
2010), we focus on how working college students' organizational identification, work hours, school hassles, and school flexibility
(ability and willingness) delineate profile membership. Theoretical and empirical extensions of partial inclusion theory (Katz & Kahn,
1978) as applied to working students (Feldman, 1990; Feldman & Doerpinghaus, 1992; Feldman & Weitz, 1990) suggest that when
working students identify less with the organization and/or work fewer hours, they tend to view their jobs as more temporary and out
of alignment with their educational experiences and long-term career plans. Thus, it is conceivable that lower organizational
identification and working fewer hours may predict membership in recovery profiles that entail lower levels of problem-solving
pondering (i.e., the leaving work behind profile). Furthermore, because work is theorized to be less central to the lives of working
college students in comparison to more traditional occupational groups (Feldman, 1990), encountering more school hassles (irritants
and problems in school life; see Peng, Schaubroeck, & Xie, 2015) may lead students to prioritize their psychological resources towards
dealing with school problems rather than work problems, entailing a reduced likelihood of membership in recovery profiles that
include at least moderate levels of problem-solving pondering (i.e., recovering ponderer and moderate recoverer profiles).
Moreover, the differentiation of recovery profiles in working students may also co-vary with their approach to managing the
boundary between work and school (Ashforth et al., 2000; Matthews et al., 2010). Boundary flexibility—both the ability and willingness to expand the domains of a boundary to meet demands in another domain—is a key construct underlying the maintenance of
work/non-work boundaries (Matthews & Barnes-Farrell, 2010). For working college students, their ability (i.e., capacity to change
how they meet school responsibilities to account for work demands) and willingness (i.e., inclination to change how school responsibilities are met to deal with work demands) to flex the school boundary may differentiate recovery profile membership. More
specifically, it is possible that working college students who are able and willing to flex the school boundary to meet work demands
would exhibit a reduced likelihood of membership in a recovery profile that entails ceasing connections from work after hours (i.e.,
the leaving work behind profile). As such, we consider the following antecedents of recovery profiles that may be particularly applicable to working college students:
Research Question 4: Do organizational identification, work hours, school hassles, and school flexibility (ability and willingness)
differentiate profiles of recovery experiences amongst working college students?
We also expand upon theorizing tied to the work/non-work interface (ten Brummelhuis & Bakker, 2012) to identify outcomes
associated with recovery experience profile membership in working college students. First, assuming that it is theoretically probable
for recovery experiences to spillover from the work to school domain (Frone, Russell, & Cooper, 1992; Netemeyer, Boles, &
McMurrian, 1996; ten Brummelhuis & Bakker, 2012), we examine whether membership in certain recovery profiles relates to differences in school-related well-being—specifically, school exhaustion and school engagement (Salmela-Aro, Kiuru, Leskinen, &
Nurmi, 2009). Similar to our focus on work emotional exhaustion and engagement, we position school exhaustion and engagement as
two key indicators of well-being for working college students; this is particularly the case given that school comprises a large part of
working college students' identity (i.e., being a student is a primary role for working college students; Ashforth et al., 2000; Clark,
2000).
Additionally, an inherent assumption in research pertaining to holding multiple roles (i.e., being an employee and a student) is
that these roles may conflict (Ashforth et al., 2000; Clark, 2000), generating work-to-school conflict for working students (Butler,
2007; Greenhaus & Beutell, 1985). Drawing from Butler (2007), work-to-school conflict occurs when participation in the school role
is impeded by work demands and/or responsibilities. In the event that working college students belong to profiles in which mastery

Research Question 5: Do profiles of recovery experiences amongst working college students differentially relate to indicators of
school well-being (e.g., school exhaustion, school engagement) and work-to-school conflict?
3.1. Study 2 method
3.1.1. Participants and procedure
Students enrolled in undergraduate Psychology, Management, and Marketing courses at three large, public universities in the
United States (two in the Southeast, one in the Southwest) who were at least 18 years of age and working were asked to participate in
an online study about work experiences for course credit. To be more inclusive of the experiences of working college students, we
relaxed the work hour requirements of Study 1 and allowed individuals to participate if they worked at least one hour per week in a
paid position. In other words, minimum and maximum requirements for work hours were not set in order to allow for more
variability in work hours such that this variable could serve as an antecedent of recovery profile membership. Similar to Study 1, we
included three quality control items and removed participants who missed at least one item.
We received data from 658 working college students. After removing two participants for incomplete data, 21 participants for not
providing a verifiable job title, and 99 participants for missing quality controls, we retained 536 students (81.5% retained). Our
sample was majority female (57.5%) and White (71.8%). Participants were 21.14 years old on average and worked an average of
21.16 h weekly across jobs; within their primary job, participants worked 19.55 h. Participants worked in a variety of occupations
(e.g., server; sales associate; cashier).
3.2. Study 2 measures
All Study 2 measures were on a 5-point scale (1 = strongly disagree; 5 = strongly agree) unless noted and were self-reported and
administered in a randomized order. In the event that participants worked multiple jobs, we asked them to rate each item reflecting
upon the job in which they worked the most hours (i.e., what we interpreted to be their primary job).
The same items and anchors from Study 1 were used in Study 2 for: psychological detachment (ω = 0.81), relaxation (ω = 0.95),
mastery (ω = 0.90), control (ω = 0.85), problem-solving pondering (ω = 0.80), role ambiguity (ω = 0.77), time pressure
(ω = 0.83), job control (ω = 0.80), work exhaustion (ω = 0.94), work engagement (ω = 0.91), somatic complaints (ω = 0.80), and
turnover intentions (ω = 0.79). To align with our added antecedents and outcomes pertaining to experiences associated with work,
school, and the work/school interface, participants in Study 2 were instructed to reflect on their perceptions of each item since the
beginning of the semester, as opposed to using a more “in general” time framing that might encompass work experiences during the
summer when there may be fewer school demands to balance with work.
3.2.1. Work hours
Participants listed the number of hours they worked per week on average. Importantly, 83 participants held more than one job.
Given that participants' other ratings were in relation to the job in which they worked the most hours, we used hours from their
primary job as an antecedent of recovery profile membership.
3.2.2. Organizational identification
We used three items from Leach et al. (2008; ω = .92). An item is: “Being a member of my organization is an important part of
how I see myself.”
3.2.3. School hassles
Nine items from Peng et al. (2015) captured school hassles. Participants rated their level of agreement about each hassle occurring
since the beginning of the semester on a 5-point scale (ω = 0.82). An example item is: “I have had too much schoolwork to do.”
3.2.4. School flexibility
Participants used a 6-point scale (1 = very untrue of me; 6 = very true of me) to rate school flexibility-ability (four items; ω = 0.73)
and school flexibility–willingness (three items; ω = 0.73)3; items were adapted from Matthews and Barnes-Farrell's (2010) workfamily flexibility scale. A school flexibility-ability item is: “If the need arose, I could work late without affecting my school responsibilities;” an item for school flexibility-willingness is: “I am willing to change my school related plans so that I can finish a work
assignment.”

3.2.5. School exhaustion and engagement
We used four items to measure school exhaustion (ω = 0.84) and nine items to evaluate school engagement (ω = 0.88) from

3.3. Study 2 results and discussion
Means, standard deviations, and correlations are presented in Table 6; fit statistics from the profile enumeration process are
presented in Table 2. We again considered the BIC and C-AIC statistics when selecting the statistically best-fitting profile solution, and
found that the BIC and C-AIC values declined until the four-profile solution and then began to increase. Further, the resulting profiles
were not theoretically redundant. We retained the four-profile solution; profile means from Study 2 are presented in Table 3 and
illustrated in Fig. 1 (dashed black line).
Similar to Study 1, the largest profile (47.1% of the sample) reflected the recovering ponderer profile in which students exhibited
relatively moderate- to high-levels of all profile indicators (relaxation M = 4.01; mastery M = 3.70; control M = 4.09; problemsolving pondering M = 3.78), with somewhat lowers levels of psychological detachment (M = 2.91; see Fig. 1). The second largest
profile (29.2% of the sample), also similar to Study 1, was the leaving work behind profile; here, individuals reported moderate-to-high
recovery experiences as described by Sonnentag and Fritz (2007; psychological detachment M = 3.88; relaxation M = 4.17; mastery
M = 3.03; control M = 4.15) paired with the lowest problem-solving pondering comparatively to the other three profiles (M = 2.67).
While the detach, low relax profile only emerged in one of Bennett et al.'s (2016) two studies, we again identified a profile quite
similar to this sub-population in Study 2 (17.0%). These individuals exhibited moderate- to high- levels of all recovery experiences
(mastery M = 3.80; control M = 4.17; problem-solving pondering M = 3.70), albeit with a slightly lower score than the mid-point of
the scale on psychological detachment (M = 2.88) that was paired with even lower levels of relaxation after work hours (relaxation
M = 2.12). Thus, although the psychological detachment score was slightly lower than the profile in Study 1, the qualitative shape of
the profile still fit the general detach, low relax trend exhibited in Study 1 and exhibited in Bennett et al. (2016). This profile was the
smallest in Study 1, but represented the third largest profile in Study 2.
The final profile (6.8%) did not conceptually fit the pondering profile from Bennett et al. (2016) and, as shown in Fig. 1, was not
similar enough in shape to be deemed a moderate recoverer profile as in our Study 1. This profile was characterized by fairly moderate
levels of psychological detachment (M = 2.93), mastery (M = 3.23), and problem-solving pondering (M = 3.13), but had notably
low levels of relaxation (M = 2.13) and control (M = 2.40). As such, we determined that this was a diminished recoverer profile—a
profile characterized by working college students who are not fully engaging in recovery experiences given the notable drops in
relaxation and control. Thus, in light of Research Question 1, results lend added support to the idea that, on the one hand, working
college students recover similarly to full-time employees after work. Yet, on the other hand, working college students seemingly have
lower levels of reliance on problem-solving pondering (i.e., no pondering profile) without the pursuit of higher levels of other recovery
experiences (i.e., belonging to the recovering ponderer profile).
Research Question 2 focused on job demands (role ambiguity, time pressure) and resources (job control) delineating profile
membership; Research Question 4 expanded the antecedents to identify factors that may be unique to working college students (work
hours, organizational identification, school hassles, school-flexibility ability and willingness). Results are in Table 4. Similar to Study
1 and Bennett et al. (2016), job control was not found to differentiate profile membership at a statistically significant level. As for role
ambiguity and time pressure, some differences in profile membership did emerge. For role ambiguity, working college students who
experienced a one-unit increase in role ambiguity had an increased probability of belonging to the diminished recoverer profile than
the recovering ponderer profile (OR = 0.63) and the detach, low relax profile (OR = 0.21). As such, role ambiguity seemingly related to
recovery being precluded after work hours (i.e., belonging to the diminished recoverer profile). Yet, for time pressure, working college
students were more likely to belong to the detach, low relax (OR = 1.65) or recovering ponderer (OR = 1.51) profiles than the leaving
work behind profile. Thus, time pressure ostensibly kept working college students more mentally connected to work in a potentially
positive way (i.e., engaging in some levels problem-solving pondering), precluding fuller off-job disengagement from work (i.e.,
belonging to the leaving work behind profile). In aggregate, time pressure may be a more positive demand for working college
students, whereas role ambiguity is more prohibitive to recovery in nature.
In regards to Research Question 4, work hours and school-flexibility willingness did not statistically differentiate profile membership. Other statistically significant effects, however, did emerge for the remaining antecedents. For organizational identification,
experiencing a one-unit increase in organizational identification co-varied with working college students having an increased
probability of belonging to the detach, low relax profile compared to the diminished recoverer (OR = 2.27) or leaving work behind
(OR = 2.12) profiles. Working college students reporting increased organizational identification were also more likely to belong to
the recovering ponderer profile than the diminished recoverer (OR = 1.79) or leaving work behind (OR = 1.67) profiles. Thus, having
higher levels of organizational identification precluded fully disconnecting from work during post-work time (leaving work behind),
but did minimize the likelihood that working college students were in the diminished recoverer profile. Similar to time pressure,

For school hassles, a one-unit increase in school hassles was associated with individuals being less likely to belong to the detach,

experiences unique to working college students differentiate profile membership—whereas time pressure, organizational identification, and school-flexibility ability make it more likely that working college students detach or think proactively about work (e.g.,
recovering ponderer; detach, low relax), higher role ambiguity and school hassles ostensibly preclude recovery experience pursuit (e.g.,
diminished recoverer).
Finally, Research Question 3 focused on our well-being outcomes and withdrawal specific to work (work exhaustion, work
engagement, somatic complaints, turnover intentions); Research Question 5 expanded our outcomes to consider processes at the
work-school interface (school exhaustion, school engagement, work-to-school conflict). In contrast to Study 1, all outcomes in Study
2 exhibited statistically significant differences between profiles (see Table 5).
For work exhaustion, results indicated that the diminished recoverer profile had the highest work exhaustion (M = 3.86) compared
to the detach, low relax (M = 3.03), leaving work behind (M = 2.96), and recovering ponderer (M = 2.84) profiles (all comparisons with
the diminished recoverer profile significant at p < .05). No other comparisons were statistically significant, suggesting that not fully
engaging in any type of recovery to a more pronounced degree was associated with impaired work exhaustion for working college
students.
Our remaining work outcomes detailed similar well-being impairments for students in the diminished recoverer profile.
Specifically, these employees experienced lower work engagement (M = 2.83) compared to the detach, low relax (M = 3.43) and
recovering ponderer (M = 3.43) profiles (comparisons significant at p < .05); the leaving work behind profile also had lower work
engagement (M = 2.74) compared to the detach, low relax and recovering ponderer profiles (p < .05). Thus, not engaging in higher
problem-solving pondering is seemingly negatively associated with work engagement for working college students. Likewise,
working college students in the diminished recoverer and detach, low relax profiles reported significantly higher (p < .05) somatic
complaints (M = 3.42 and 3.09, respectively) than the leaving work behind and recovering ponderer profiles (M = 2.67 and 2.70,
respectively). Finally, turnover intentions were lowest for the detach, low relax profile (M = 2.89) compared to the leaving work behind
(M = 3.46) and diminished recoverer (M = 3.52) profiles (p < .05); there were no differences observed between the recovering ponderer profile (M = 3.17) and the remaining profiles.
Combined, the work well-being and withdrawal outcomes suggest that the diminished recoverer profile exhibited higher work
exhaustion, lower work engagement, higher somatic complaints, and higher turnover intentions than the other profiles. This was
followed by students in the leaving work behind profile—although they had lower emotional exhaustion versus the diminished recoverer
profile, similar well-being issues occurred in regards to lower work engagement, as well as higher somatic complaints and turnover
intentions. Thus, the recovering ponderer and detach, low relax profiles seemingly had more beneficial work outcomes.
When considering our school-related outcomes (Research Question 5), a fairly similar pattern emerged, such that the diminished
recoverer profile had higher school exhaustion (M = 4.21) compared to all other profiles (p < .05); this was followed by the detach,
low relax profile (M = 3.81), the leaving work behind profile (M = 3.42), and the recovering ponderer profile (M = 3.36), of which the
detach, low relax and recovering ponderer profiles were also statistically significantly different (p < .05). The diminished recoverer
profile also exhibited lower school engagement (M = 2.73) than the detach, low relax (M = 3.13) and recovering ponderer (M = 3.06)
profiles (p < .05); these findings mirrored work engagement, such that working students in the detach, low relax and recovering
ponderer profiles had higher work and school engagement relative to the diminished recoverer profile. Finally, the diminished recoverer
profile reported the highest work-to-school conflict (M = 5.06) compared to all other profiles (p < .05), followed by the detach, low
relax (M = 4.02), recovering ponderer (M = 3.60), and leaving work behind (M = 3.51) profiles; of the remaining comparisons, the
detach, low relax profile exhibited higher work-to-school conflict than the latter two profiles (p < .05), and the recovering ponderer
and leaving work behind profiles were not statistically distinguishable for work-to-school conflict.
Thus, in terms of school-related outcomes, the problems continued for those in the diminished recoverer profile when expanding
the nomological network to include school-related outcomes. Yet, these results also painted a complicated picture for working college
students in the detach, low relax profile: while their work-related well-being and engagement is higher relative to some other profiles,
they do exhibit impaired well-being in the school domain relative to the recovering ponderer and leaving work behind profiles as they
reported higher school exhaustion (compared to the recovering ponderer profile) and greater work-to-school conflict.
4. General discussion
Despite emerging calls to expand the scope of recovery research beyond the boundaries of office workers employed in more or less
traditional schedules (e.g., Sonnentag et al., 2017), many questions remain as to how recovery processes—and recovery experiences
in particular—operate outside of full-time employment contexts. In the current research, we identified working college students as a
16

Journal of Vocational Behavior 115 (2019) 103329

A.S. Gabriel, et al.

useful comparison point to provide a rigorous test of the generality of recovery processes across occupational contexts. Across two
studies, results demonstrated that recovery experiences operate similarly and differently in a working college student context relative
to full-time occupational groups. Accordingly, our results have both answered questions regarding the generality of recovery experiences across occupational contexts, and yielded new questions regarding how working students experience recovery that can lay
the foundations for future research on work recovery, part-time work experiences, and the work/school interface.
4.1. Theoretical Implications
Arguably the most significant theoretical contribution of our work is the identification of similarities and differences in how
recovery experiences combine in working college student populations. As Bennett et al. (2016) had constrained their investigation of
recovery profiles to full-time employees, their work reflected an ideal starting point; thus, it is first worth considering how our results
align with those of Bennett et al. We identified two profiles—the recovering ponderer and leaving work behind profiles—that were
conceptually similar to two of the profiles that Bennett et al. (2016) observed in both of their full-time employee samples. Thus, our
work helps establish both the existence of these two recovery experience profiles and the generality of these profiles across occupational contexts. Interestingly, this aligns with prevailing theorizing underlying recovery experiences, which has placed emphasis on
the value of disconnecting from work during time away from the workplace (the leaving work behind profile; Sonnentag & Fritz, 2015)
and the potential value of directing proactive thoughts towards work during off-job time (the recovering ponderer profile; Cropley &
Zijlstra, 2011). Notably, the recovering ponderer group has represented the largest profile in all studies in which it has been observed
(i.e., both Bennett et al. (2016) studies and the two studies reported here). This may bolster the argument that psychological detachment has been overemphasized in past research relative to other recovery experiences (Derks et al., 2014), as at least a plurality
of employees (47.1%–58.9%) in four different studies have belonged to this profile that tends to have relatively lower levels of
psychological detachment and higher levels of proactive after-work thoughts.
Our results also bolster confidence in the existence of the detach, low relax profile, which Bennett et al. (2016) observed inconsistently in a full-time employee context. This profile, which is comprised of employees who report generally moderate levels of
recovery experiences but low levels of relaxation, was replicated in both of our studies of working college students. Further, it is
particularly interesting to note that the only occupational context in which this recovery profile has not been extracted to date is a
sample of full-time non-academic university employees (Study 2 of Bennett et al.), which is the exact type of employment context that
has been argued to have been over-represented in past variable-centered recovery research (Sonnentag et al., 2017). Thus, the detach,
low relax profile may represent a profile of recovery experiences that is less likely to emerge in the more traditional employment
contexts that tend to be represented in recovery research. While speculative, it is possible that greater predictability in work scheduling may facilitate relaxation, an observation that would be in line with work suggesting that variable shift workers exhibit greater
circadian variability in their physiological activation relative to stable shift workers (Åkerstedt, Pátkai, & Dahlgren, 1977). Future
research evaluating recovery profiles across work scheduling arrangements would be ideally suited to directly test this idea.
Critically, we also obtained evidence for two recovery profiles that were not observed by Bennett et al. (2016). The first, a
moderate recoverer profile, was observed in Study 1 only and was characterized by working college students who reported moderate
levels of all five recovery experiences. The second, a diminished recoverer profile, was observed in Study 2 only and bore some
similarities to the moderate recoverer profile (fairly moderate psychological detachment, mastery, and problem-solving pondering),
but differed in terms of having lower relaxation and control over leisure time. In seeking to ascertain why these differences may have
emerged, it is worth considering whether providing greater constraints around work hours in Study 1 (where the moderate recoverer
profile was observed) relative to Study 2 (where the diminished recoverer profile was observed) may have been relevant to the
extraction of these two profiles. Although we had no evidence to suggest that work hours differentiated profile membership within
Study 2, it is possible that allowing greater variability in work hours in Study 2 and including participants working relatively few
hours relative to Study 1 may have allowed for the inclusion of a larger number of participants who may perceive that they do not
work enough hours such that they need to recover from work (van Veldhoven & Broersen, 2003). This change in inclusion criteria
could also help explain why some differences were found for our well-being outcomes in Study 2, whereas no differences emerged in
Study 1. It is also worth noting that members of the diminished recoverer profile perceived substantially greater role ambiguity
surrounding their work role and reported more school hassles than members of the recovering ponderer and detached, low relax groups.
Thus, it is possible that the diminished recoverer group may struggle with how to define and balance work and school demands.
Understanding whether and under what circumstances the diminished recoverer profile emerges may be a particularly salient
research need as it relates to endeavors to enhance the well-being of working college students, as these student employees are at
elevated risk for impaired well-being at work and at school. In other words, the well-being impairments of these employees seem to
cut across the work and school domains, as manifested in elevated work and school exhaustion, lower work and school engagement,
and higher somatic complaints relative to most other recovery profiles in Study 2. Furthermore, the diminished recoverers reported
rather high levels of work–school conflict (a mean of 5.06 on a 6 point scale). While it should be acknowledged that this profile
constituted a small percentage of the overall sample (6.8%), these employees may be most at risk for the interfering influence of work
on school outcomes that has received the preponderance of research attention in the study of working while enrolled in school (see
Broadbridge & Swanson, 2005, for a review). Thus, from an intervention perspective, diminished recoverers are potentially the most at
risk for work-school conflict and corresponding well-being impairment; that said, future work is needed to establish the robustness of
this pattern of recovery experiences and corresponding antecedents and outcomes (Wang & Hanges, 2011).
In terms of our broader theoretical contributions, while the predictions of partial inclusion theory (Feldman, 1990; Katz & Kahn,
1978) have been empirically evaluated in relation to job attitudes (see Thorsteinson, 2003, for a review), our work is the first to

evaluate whether the reduced organizational identification theorized to characterize working college students manifests in how they
experience work recovery. Consistent with the prediction that being more identified with one's organization will manifest in greater
involvement and investment (Feldman, 1990), working students who reported greater organizational identification tended to belong
to recovery experience profiles that entailed moderate-to-high levels of problem-solving pondering (i.e., the recovering ponderer and
detach, low relax profiles). Accordingly, exploring avenues to increase organizational identification in working students, such as
enhancing perceptions of leader-member exchange and organizational support (Bennett et al., 2016; Sluss, Klimchak, & Holmes,
2008), may have downstream implications for keeping working college students proactively and positively engaged with work during
time away from the workplace.
Finally, extensive efforts have been targeted at understanding how employees manage the boundary between work and non-work
life (e.g., Bulger, Matthews, & Hoffman, 2007; Kossek & Lautsch, 2012; Kossek, Lautsch, & Eaton, 2006). However, knowledge of how
students manage the work-school boundary is limited. In the context of working while in school, our results suggested a co-variation
between an ability to flex the school boundary to meet work demands and membership in recovery profiles entailing problem-solving
pondering, relative to recovery profiles that entailed a cessation of thoughts directed towards work after hours. Yet, there may be
complexities underlying this relationship, as our results suggest that it was the ability (rather than the willingness) to flex the school
boundary that predicted recovery profile membership. Consistent with suggestions surrounding boundaries between work and nonwork (Kreiner, 2006), it may be that a full understanding of how work-school boundary management intersects with recovery
experiences will require incorporating preferences that working students hold regarding how work and school domains of life are
segmented or integrated together.
4.2. Practical Implications
Our results yield practical implications for working college students and employers. For the students, our Study 2 results suggest
that a moderate- to high-level of recovery experiences are needed to recover from one's part-time job. Thus, even when working fewer
hours than full-time employees, there is potential value in working college students reflecting on how to recover during time outside
of work. However, this may be particularly difficult for these employees, as time spent outside of work may be relegated to schoolrelated demands (Calderwood & Gabriel, 2017). Nonetheless, given the benefits associated with working students in the recovering
ponderer and detach, low relax profiles, there is value in finding time for leisure when striving to balance work and school demands.
For example, identifying a relaxing activity after work over which an individual has high control could place that working student in
the recovering ponderer profile, and thus be less likely to experience somatic complaints, exhaustion at work and school, and work-toschool conflict, in addition to feeling increased engagement in both domains.
Additionally, our results suggest that on-going work-related thoughts may not always be problematic. Indeed, three of the four
profiles exhibited similar problem-solving pondering, and in the case of recovering ponderers, this may have contributed to the
beneficial outcomes that this group experienced. Thus, while research has largely focused on the downsides of extended rumination
after hours (e.g., Rosen & Hochwarter, 2014), our results suggest that proactive thoughts after work may not inherently be problematic for working college students. Importantly, these results also fit with sentiments raised by Bennett et al. (2016). Thus,
working college students should recognize that it is not necessarily that they need to fully disconnect from work to recover, but that
there may be value in focusing on controllable aspects of work that can be addressed via problem-solving pondering.
Our results further suggest that it would behoove managers to help working college students feel a sense of identity with the
organization, as higher levels of organizational identity in Study 2 made working college students more likely to belong to the detach,
low relax or recovering ponderer profiles. These two profiles reported similarly high levels of work and school engagement, meaning
that identifying with one's job can pose benefits that spill from one role (i.e., a part-time employee) to the next (i.e., a full-time
student). Such an idea does run against tenets of partial inclusion theory, which notes that part-time work tends to be far less central
to the lives of working college students (Eberhardt & Shani, 1984; Feldman, 1990; Miller & Terborg, 1979). However, helping
working college students identify with their part-time job—and, potentially, see the long-term gains from their part-time employment—can have beneficial outcomes across work and school domains (ten Brummelhuis & Bakker, 2012).
Finally, managers of working college students may also want to consider the level of school hassles that their employees are
experiencing while working, given that increased school hassles lead to a higher likelihood to be in the diminished recoverer profile
than any other profile. Given that individuals in the diminished recoverer profile exhibited the highest turnover intentions and work
exhaustion, this poses a critical challenge for organizations. Since working students' class schedules change semester-to-semester and
school demands may change even week-to-week (e.g., increased hassles during weeks with exams or project deadlines), managers
who oversee employees who are working students should make efforts to have fairly flexible scheduling around these time periods.
That is, managers should consider assessing the school hassles students report week-to-week and, if at all possible, creating schedules
to best accommodate their needs during this time.
4.3. Limitations and future directions
As with any research, our studies need to be taken in light of their limitations. First, all data was self-reported and collected at the
same point in time, raising possible issues with common method bias and limiting causal claims. Importantly, we were focused
directly on working college students' recovery experiences and experiences at work and school, making students the best respondents
to properly understand this phenomenon. Further, as noted by Stanley, Kellermanns, and Zellweger (2017), “as the profile membership could not be guessed ex ante by our participants, social desirability concerns can be ruled out” (p. 97). Nonetheless, future

research building upon our work should consider (a) temporally separating measures (e.g., antecedents at Time 1; profile indicators
at Time 2; outcomes at Time 3), and (b) collecting data from secondary sources. Of interest would be collecting assessments of
demands from the supervisors themselves and even the professors that working college students have for specific classes Additionally,
secondary ratings of outcomes would help further extend the nomological network surrounding work and school outcomes of
working college students (e.g., course grades; supervisor performance ratings).
Second, all of the data came from students in the United States, which does not account for experiences of students in other
countries and cultures. In countries where higher education is low cost or free (e.g., Germany, Argentina), those who work part-time
may have different experiences managing the work/school interface, as these individuals may not need to work in order to pay for
college tuition. Furthermore, some countries, such as Germany, have legally-enforced limitations on the numbers of hours students
are permitted to work without having to pay into social security systems (Deutsches Studentenwerk, 2019). Thus, our findings may
not generalize cross-nationally, and replication in other countries is important.
Related to this point, we did not consider the industry students were working in. Indeed, we had over 200 unique job titles
represented in our samples, with some jobs seemingly having more emotional demands than others (e.g., customer service employees
versus mechanics) that could contribute to differences in well-being. Thus, it is possible that being in industries or jobs where
employees are engaged in more emotionally taxing interactions could act as an inhibitory agent to recovery experiences. Further
research should aim to uncover how industry, and related factors such as job position and status, might impact recovery experiences
in part-time contexts.
Fourth, we only considered the demands tied to working college students' primary job, despite the fact that 83 participants in
Study 2 reported working in multiple jobs at the time of the survey. Importantly, we had individuals focus their assessments on the
job in which they worked the most hours, as this was likely the most salient, proximal experience tied to their recovery. Nonetheless,
it would be of interest to understand how conflicts between jobs—in terms of competing demands or levels of identification—affect
recovery profiles. For example, it is possible that high organizational identification in one job precludes identification in another job,
with this tension creating inauthenticity that may preclude proper recovery (e.g., Caza, Moss, & Vough, 2018). As individuals
increasingly grapple with multiple work roles and identities, research on recovery should account for these possible discrepancies or
synergies.
Beyond the limitations noted above, we also encourage future scholarship to understand how recovery experiences might operate
differently in other contexts, such as hourly versus salaried employees. Two variables of note that should be considered are compensation and scheduling. It is possible that fixed amounts of compensation (i.e., hourly pay versus salary), even for the same number
of hours worked, could remove some of the financial strain on workers by removing the uncertainty of meeting financial needs.
Alternatively, as suggested by our findings related to school flexibility, the ability to flex work hours might be more important to this
working student population. Flexible scheduling practices in which work hours change daily or weekly may be seen as a beneficial or
stress-inducing aspect of work that could either reduce uncertainty or accommodate needs of employees. Given that our findings
showed both similarities and differences between the Bennett et al. (2016) full-time working population and working college students, there is reason to believe that these populations may have different considerations and stress-related reactions in relation to
compensation and scheduling practices.
Finally, we note that other analyses pertaining to LPA can be conducted to understand the nature of profiles of recovery experiences amongst working college students (e.g., additive tests of configural similarity, structural similarity, dispersion similarity,
distributional similarity across the samples; as an exemplar, see: Morin et al., 2016). In the event that scholars replicate our work and
find consistency in profile solutions across samples (versus our two studies where there were slight differences in the profile solutions), such tests could prove to be informative. This work would also be informative as scholars compare profiles across more diverse
cultures or occupational contexts (e.g., hourly versus salaried works).
5. Conclusion
Across two studies, our results support the idea that working college students do engage in conjoint use of recovery experiences
after working hours. Yet, these profiles differed slightly from prior work with full-time employees (e.g., Bennett et al., 2016) and were
also differentiated by factors attributable to part-time work contexts. Further, membership in recovery experience profiles had effects
for multiple role domains (i.e., work and school). Thus, our work represents a necessary first extension of examining recovery
experiences amongst working college students.
