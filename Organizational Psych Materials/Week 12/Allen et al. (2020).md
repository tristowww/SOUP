Associated With Work–Family Conflict

Through the lens of boundary theory, we systematically examined cultural context as a moderator of
relationships between work–family conflict and its key theoretical predictors (work/family hours and
work/family demands) and outcomes (job satisfaction, family satisfaction, and life satisfaction). We used
2 different approaches to examine cultural variation in the strength of work–family conflict relationships:
(a) individual cultural values (collectivism, power distance, uncertainty avoidance); and (b) regional
cluster configurations (e.g., Eastern Europe, South Asia). Our meta-analytic investigation is based on data
drawn from 332 studies (2,733 effect sizes) that represent 58 different countries. Consistent with
prediction, results revealed that collectivism moderated WIF/FIW and satisfaction outcomes such that
relationships were weaker in more collectivistic contexts than in less collectivistic contexts. Little
evidence was found to support power distance or uncertainty avoidance as individual cultural moderators.
Findings also indicated that work–family conflict relationships differ in strength as a function of regional
clusters, lending support to the use of configural approaches to examine cross-cultural variation. Overall,
our findings suggest that domain demands are a robust predictor of work–family conflict across countries
and that affective correlates to work–family conflict meaningfully vary in strength as a function of
cultural context.

Work–family conflict refers to a type of interrole conflict in
which participation in the work (family) role is made more difficult by virtue of participation in the family (work) role (Greenhaus
& Beutell, 1985). Work–family conflict directionally takes two
forms in that work interference with family (WIF) is distinguished
from family interference with work (FIW). Influential theoretical
models of the predictors and outcomes of work–family conflict
have been developed within the U.S. cultural context (e.g., Frone,
Russell, & Cooper, 1992). Based on these models, research consistently shows that key predictors of WIF and FIW include work
and family demands whereas key attitudes theorized as outcomes
include job, family, and life satisfaction (e.g., Amstad, Meier,
Fasel, Elfering, & Semmer, 2011; Ford, Heinen, & Langkamer,
2007; Shockley & Singla, 2011). What is less certain is the extent
that these relationships are consistent across different cultural
contexts. For example, some cross-national comparative studies
demonstrate that the strength of the relationship between WIF and
known correlates differs for those in more individualistic versus
more collectivistic cultural contexts (e.g., Spector et al., 2007)
while others find no differences across cultural groups (e.g., Hill,
Yang, Hawkins, & Ferris, 2004).
Given the growing body of work–family research conducted
outside the U.S., the time is ripe to meta-analytically examine
cultural variation in work–family relationships. Accordingly, with
boundary theory as a guide (Ashforth, Kreiner, & Fugate, 2000),
the main objective of the current study is to systematically examine cultural context as a moderator of relationships between work–
family conflict and its key theoretical predictors and outcomes. We
selected variables that help form basic theoretical models of work–
family conflict. These models suggest situational role domain
demands predict the occurrence of work–family conflict, and in
turn work–family conflict predicts satisfaction across life roles
(Aryee et al., 1999; Frone et al., 1992; Korabik, Aycan, & Ayman,
2017). On the predictor side we focus on quantitative (i.e., time
spent on work/family roles) and qualitative (e.g., work/family
pressure, role overload) work/family demands. Role demands have
been consistently demonstrated as robust predictors of work–
family conflict, with work role demands more highly associated
with WIF than with FIW and family role demands more highly
associated with FIW than with WIF (e.g., Byron, 2005; Ford et al.,
2007; Michel, Kotrba, Mitchelson, Clark, & Baltes, 2011). With
regard to outcomes we investigate affect-related variables reliably
associated with both directions of work–family conflict (e.g., Amstad et al., 2011; Shaffer, Joplin, & Hsu, 2011). Specifically, we
include job satisfaction, family satisfaction, and life satisfaction.
These predictors and attitudinal outcomes are established both
theoretically and empirically as part of the work–family conflict
nomological network (Allen, 2012). A secondary objective is to
update and extend meta-analytic effect sizes associated with this
set of theoretical predictors and outcomes of work–family conflict.

Our research makes several contributions to the literature. First,
our use of boundary theory as a theoretical guide advances the
theoretical work–family cross-national literature, as well as boundary theory. Most extant research relies on theory associated with a
specific cultural value without more broadly integrating those
cultural values into theory associated with work–family relationships. By using boundary theory as a foundation, we extend
theoretical insights beyond those provided in previous crossnational work–family research. For example, Spector et al. (2007)
based their hypotheses concerning work demands and WIF exclusively on collectivism. Moreover, none of the studies covered in
Shockley et al.’s (2017) qualitative review of cross-national work–
family research used boundary theory as a theoretical guide to help
explain culture as a moderator. By invoking boundary theory in
conjunction with specific cultural values, we are able to provide a
more comprehensive theoretical picture while simultaneously incorporating additional meaningful cultural values. Ultimately, by
providing an overall foundation based on boundary theory, we lay
the groundwork for hypothesis development that can extend beyond any singular cross-cultural value.
Second, our meta-analytic approach helps to address restrictions
associated with primary study research in which only a limited
number of cultural contexts can be feasibly represented. As noted
by Ollier-Malaterre and Foucreault (2017), cross-national studies
are typically focused on only two countries (e.g., Galovan et al.,
2010) or are based on small sets/clusters of countries (e.g., Spector
et al., 2007) and are thus vulnerable to sample selection bias.
Moreover, a variety of sampling strategies are used. For example,
some studies are based on participants from a single multinational
organization (e.g., Hill et al., 2004) while others are based on a

Such differences make qualitative comparisons across studies

Spector et al. (2007). For example, on the predictor side, Spector

Third, by examining national culture as a moderator, we contribute to the literature by providing a better understanding of the
boundary conditions associated with presumed work–family conflict relationships. A long-standing concern among cultural psychologists is that theories developed in one cultural context may
not be as applicable in other cultural contexts (Gelfand, Aycan,
Erez, & Leung, 2017; Gelfand, Erez, & Aycan, 2007). This is
particularly salient with regard to work–family conflict in that
societal expectations with regard to what it means to be a good
employee and a good family member vary across cultures. These
expectations can shape the way by which individuals understand
and enact their work and family roles. Our findings can be used to
direct future research efforts aimed at understanding the aspects of
national context that have the greatest impact on work–family
management.
Fourth, we use two cross-national comparative approaches: specific cultural values and regional country clusters. Both approaches
have been used to build and test plausible cross-national differences in primary studies, with varying results (e.g., Shockley et al.,
2017; Spector et al., 2007; Yang et al., 2000). By using both
approaches, we shed light on the extent each reveals novel and
distinct insights. Thus, our results can be used to inform which
approach might be most fruitful for guiding future work–family
research, an important contribution given the many lenses through
which cross-national differences can be examined (OllierMalaterre & Foucreault, 2017; Shockley et al., 2017). Additionally, by identifying the strength of work–family relationships
based on region, our results can be useful to organizations that
employ a multinational workforce. For example, understanding
how work–family relationships may vary in strength across different regions of the globe can help organizations in their efforts to
identify practices that may be most effective across different
contexts.

Theoretical Overview
Work and family roles are influenced by the cultural contexts
within which they are embedded (Ollier-Malaterre & Foucreault,
2017; Powell et al., 2009). Culture influences work behavior
(Taras et al., 2010) and family behavior (Behanyan, Marshall, &
Ferenczi, 2015). Culture refers to shared values, assumptions, and
beliefs held by individuals within a country that result from common experiences (House & Javidan, 2004; Triandis, 1995). Despite the recognition that culture matters, there is no single overarching theory or framework that unifies work–family conflict
related phenomena and cultural context. Instead, researchers rely
on theory associated with specific cultural variables. To wit, by far,

Our approach is different in that we use boundary management
theory in our theorizing and as an overall guide in our selection of
specific cultural dimensions for hypothesis development.
Boundary theory concerns the ways that individuals create,
maintain, or change boundaries in an effort to navigate the world
around them (Ashforth et al., 2000). It is rooted in cognitive
theories of social classification that focus on the meanings that
individuals assign to work and to home and the degree of ease
involved in transitioning between the two (Nippert-Eng, 1996).
Permeable boundaries are those that are more open and thus more
easily permit transitions from one role to another while impermeable boundaries are closed or fixed, making transitions across roles
more difficult (Allen, Cho, & Meier, 2014). Border theory as
described by Clark (2000) also underscores the importance of
transitions between work and family roles. Clark (2000) further
notes the important role of domain partners, referred to as border
keepers (e.g., coworkers, family members), in boundary management. Supervisors for example can establish a firm boundary that
segments family from work by not permitting personal phone calls
at work. In their formulation of boundary theory, Ashforth,
Kreiner, & Fugate (2000) suggested that cultural context influences the extent individuals view work and family as separate or as
integrated domains of life (Ashforth et al., 2000). More specifically, the fluidity and ease by which individuals can cross work
and family boundaries is thought to differ across cultural contexts.
Culture serves an important source of self-concept and the development of role identities. Border theory is silent on the role of
national culture, but notes that organizational culture can influence
boundary management (Clark, 2000). Thus, it too recognizes that
context matters.
Within the research literature, culture is hypothesized as playing
two potential theoretical roles (Kirkman, Chen, Farh, Chen, &
Lowe, 2009; Lytle, Brett, Barsness, Tinsley, & Janssens, 1995;
Tsui et al., 2007). One is as an independent variable and the second
is as a moderator. Both types of studies have appeared in the
work–family literature (Shockley et al., 2017). When the focus is
on culture as a moderator, different approaches can be taken. For
example, some scholars focus on regions (e.g., comparing North
American countries with East Asian countries; Oh et al., 2014).
Typically such comparisons are based on cultural values associated with regional clusters (e.g., North America is individualistic
while East Asia is collectivistic). Understanding cultural differences according to region can be helpful to multinational organizations that conduct business in specific regions of the world.
Another approach is to compare individual countries. In such
cases, nation is typically used as a proxy for culture and cultural
values may be inferred (the United States is individualistic while
China is collectivistic; e.g., Yang et al., 2000) or scores on a
cultural value of interest may be imputed (e.g., Haar, Russo, Sune,
& Ollier-Malaterre, 2014).

Cultural Moderators
Ashforth et al. (2000) proposed that collectivism, power distance, uncertainty avoidance, and gender egalitarianism could
moderate role transitions. In our process of selecting which of the

cultural values proposed by Ashforth et al. (2000) to include, we
considered the relationships that we were predicting would be
moderated by culture. Specifically, our examined relationships are
those that form basic work–family conflict models, demands as
predictors and satisfaction as outcomes. Of the four, we did not
develop hypotheses concerning gender egalitarianism because we
do not think it can be considered as a moderator without also
factoring in gender. To elaborate, if we were examining culture as
a moderator of the relationship between sex and WIF, then gender
egalitarianism would be a meaningful moderator. We next describe
how collectivism, power distance (PD), and uncertainty avoidance
(UA) values work to moderate work–family conflict relationships.
Collectivism refers to the way in which individuals within a
society relate to one another (Triandis, 1995). Societies in which
individuals are closely linked are described as collectivistic. Collectivistic societies tend to focus on others and are more embedded
in social networks that include extended family and groups
(Markus & Kitayama, 1998). According to boundary theory, in
collectivist societies individuals view work and family as interdependent domains that do not compete (Ashforth et al., 2000). The
central role of the family is stressed in both personal and in
business affairs (Nardon & Steers, 2009) and work is considered
an activity that brings honor to the family. This interdependency
strengthens relationships with role partners and can result in more
effortless transitions across role boundaries. Moreover, we suggest
that because of this interdependency, in more collectivistic societies there may be less contrast between role identities. Ashforth et
al. (2000) note that the greater the contrast between role identities,
the greater the potential difficulty of transitioning between roles. In
less collectivistic societies (also referred to as individualistic),
individuals see themselves as relatively independent and role
boundaries are more distinct, potentially increasing the contrast
across work and family roles. Individualists tend to focus on the
self and on personal achievement and work and family role identities are not interwined. Social behavior in individualistic cultures
is primarily determined by personal goals that may not overlap
with those of collectives such as the family or the work group
(Triandis, 1990).
Collectivism is thought to moderate work–family relations because in more collectivistic societies work is viewed as something
that is done on behalf of the family (Yang, 2005). That is, work is
viewed as contributing to the family rather than as competing with
the family, modifying the way conflicting demands are perceived
and appraised. Because work and family synergistically contribute
to one another, demands in one domain may be perceived as
relatively harmless and perhaps beneficial for the alternative domain. In contrast, in less collectivistic societies work is more likely
to be viewed as an activity done for the self in the service of
personal achievement (Lu, Gilmour, Kao, & Huang, 2006). Thus,
in less collectivistic societies, demands from one role domain are
more likely perceived as sources of conflict with the alternative
role. There is some evidence to support the notion that stronger
relationships exist between work demands and WIF in less relative
to more collectivistic societies (Lu et al., 2006; Spector et al.,
2007). Less research has investigated cultural differences in the
relationship between family demands and FIW, but similar patterns have been observed (Hill et al., 2004; Lu et al., 2006; Yang
et al., 2000). Based on theory and existing empirical evidence, we
pose the following hypotheses:

Collectivism has also been found to moderate the relationship
between work–family conflict and its theoretical outcomes. Ample
evidence shows that higher WIF/FIW is associated with lower job
satisfaction, work satisfaction, and life satisfaction (Amstad et al.,
2011; Ford et al., 2007). However, there is also evidence that the
strength of these relationships is stronger in less collectivistic
societies relative to more collectivistic societies (Lu et al., 2010;
Spector et al., 2007). Boundary theory suggests that conflicts
between interdependent roles are less impactful than those between more independent roles. This is because individuals have
less discrepancy between role meaning and engagement when
roles are interconnected. Thus, in more collectivistic societies that
view work and family as overlapping, conflicts may be less detrimental relative to less collectivistic societies. In addition, because individualists tend to focus on their own needs more so than
do collectivists, the negative reaction to the experience of work–
family conflict is likely to be amplified in less collectivistic relative to more collectivistic societies as work–family conflict is
viewed as a threat to their well-being (Aycan & Korabik, 2017).
Responses to work–family conflict may be attenuated in collectivistic cultures due to adherence to group loyalty and a focal
interest on the collective, across work and family boundaries
(Annor, 2015).
Hypothesis 3: The negative association between WIF/FIW
and (a) job satisfaction, (b) life satisfaction, and (c) family
satisfaction is weaker in more collectivistic countries than in
samples from less collectivistic countries.
Far less attention in the work–family literature has been given to
power distance and uncertainty avoidance. Power distance (PD)
refers to the extent that individuals accept an unequal distribution
of power in societies (Hofstede, 1984; House, Hanges, Javidan,
Dorfman, & Gupta, 2004). In higher PD contexts, power differences are legitimized and relatively few individuals are granted
access to resources and information. Respect and deference are
given to higher status individuals who may play the role of border
keepers such as supervisors and family elders (Ensari & Murphy,
2003). Based on boundary theory, differences in role and relationship boundaries are hypothesized to result in variation across
cultures with regard to relationships between work–family conflict
and its correlates. Higher PD cultures tend to have more formalized procedures and rules than do lower PD cultures (Nardon &
Steers, 2009), resulting in more rigid boundaries between relationship partners and between work and family roles. In contrast, in
lower PD contexts inequality is less tolerated (Kirkman et al.,
2009). Hierarchy and status are deemphasized, and employees
have more discretion with regard to how they carry out their work
and family roles. Moreover, employees in lower PD contexts
perceive those in authority to be more approachable and are more

likely to develop personalized relationships with supervisors (Tyler, Lind, & Huo, 2000).
Uncertainty avoidance (UA) refers to the extent a culture relies
on social norms, rules, and rituals in an effort to alleviate unpredictability (Hofstede, 1984; Sully de Luque & Javidan, 2004). In
higher UA contexts, rigid codes of behavior are enforced to reduce
uncertainty and ambiguity. In addition, individuals in higher UA
cultures tend to value predictability and consistency, whereas
individuals in lower UA cultures tend to prefer unstructured situations and guidelines that are general (Venaik & Brewer, 2010).
Similar to how lower PD cultures have less rigid boundaries, lower
UA cultures tend to have more flexible and permeable role boundaries (Ashforth et al., 2000). Both lower PD and lower UA cultures
eschew formalities and formal rules.
No known studies have examined PD or UA as moderators of
the demands and work–family conflict relationship. However,
there are several reasons to expect that the positive relationship
between demands and work–family conflict may be stronger in
higher PD and in higher UA societies than in lower PD or in lower
UA societies. Greater PD and UA enforces role boundaries. Rigid
role boundaries may create a communication gap between employees and their role partners, such as spouses and supervisors who
carefully guard the borders of their domains, making it difficult for
workers to request accommodations to address work–family needs,
resulting in exacerbated relationships between demands and WIF
(Clark, 2000; Ghosh, 2011).
Given that lower PD and UA contexts offer greater opportunity
to exercise control over work and family and enable the development of relational bonds with those in power, we expect the
relationship between demands and work–family conflict to be
mitigated in such contexts. Because lower PD cultures tend to
deemphasize hierarchy, they tend to provide employees with more
autonomy and discretion. This greater discretion can enable employees to better align their boundary management preferences and
behavior, helping to buffer the relationship between demands and
work–family conflict. Moreover, deemphasized hierarchy can increase the likelihood that border keepers understand the needs of
the employee and alleviate the likelihood that demands will be
poorly timed (Clark, 2000). Similarly, because lower UA cultures
tend to be less bureaucratic and more flexible with regard to rules
and rituals, we expect the strength of the relationship between
demands and work–family conflict to be reduced as such contexts
provide employees with a greater opportunity to modify the situation in a way that fits their work–family needs. Based on the
theory, we propose the following:
Hypothesis 4a: The positive association between WIF and (a)
work hours and (b) work demands, is stronger in samples from
higher PD countries than in samples from lower PD countries.
Hypothesis 4b: The positive association between WIF and (a)
work hours and (b) work demands is stronger in samples from
higher UA countries than in samples from lower UA
countries.
Hypothesis 5a: The positive association between FIW and (a)
family hours and (b) family demands is stronger in samples
from higher PD countries than in samples from lower PD
countries.

influential in relation to affect-laden constructs. It may be that rigid
role hierarchies intensify reactions to the occurrence of work–
family conflict. For example, the social differences that are emphasized in higher PD countries lend themselves to the suppression
of status-threatening emotions (Matsumoto, 1990). PD is also
associated with justice perceptions (Silva & Caetano, 2016). Employees react more strongly to perceived injustices in lower PD
contexts in which role boundaries are expected to be more fluid
than in higher PD contexts (Summereder, Streicher, & Batinic,
2014). In addition, differences in how individuals interpret and
express emotions as a function of PD have been found (e.g.,
Arrindell et al., 1997; Basabe et al., 2002). Matsumoto, Kasri, and
Kooken (1999) reported that when viewing photographs of others,
individuals from higher PD cultures gave lower intensity ratings to
negative emotion displays relative to participants from lower PD
cultures. The authors explain that in an effort to not disrupt group
harmony or status relationships, cultures that encourage and maintain power differences perceive less intensity in the expression of
negative emotions.
Given their predilection for orderliness and consistency, incidents of work–family conflict may be more disruptive to those in
higher UA contexts than those in lower UA contexts. By contrast,
because of their greater comfort with uncertainty, those in lower
UA contexts may be able to more readily adapt to and address
work–family conflict situations as they arise, therefore minimizing
the relationship between UA and affective reactions. Moreover,
research shows that greater UA is associated with less subjective
well-being and greater neuroticism (Steel, Taras, Uggerslev, &
Bosco, 2018; Sully de Luque & Javidan, 2004). Thus, individuals
in higher UA relative to lower UA contexts may have fewer
affective resources to deal with work–family conflict, exacerbating
the relationship between work–family conflict and affective reactions.
Overall, these findings suggest that affective reactions to work–
family conflict may be enhanced in lower PD and in higher UA
contexts. Thus, we propose the following:
Hypothesis 6a: The negative association between WIF/FIW
and (a) job satisfaction, (b) life satisfaction, and (c) family
satisfaction is stronger in samples from lower PD countries
than in samples from higher PD countries.
Hypothesis 6b: The negative association between WIF/FIW
and (a) job satisfaction, (b) life satisfaction, and (c) family
satisfaction is stronger in samples from higher UA countries
than in samples from lower UA countries.

Regional Cluster Configurations
Based on boundary theory, we hypothesized that collectivism
and PD will condition the strength of work–family conflict relationships. This is in line with conventional approaches used in the
work–family literature that examine individual cultural values

(e.g., Spector et al., 2007). However, societal values tend to
co-occur (Rockstuhl, Dulebohn, Ang, & Shore, 2012). Nations
consist of different historical constellations of institutions, policies, and cultural assumptions that impact the meaning that individuals attach to the connection between work and family and the
way individuals respond to conflicts between the two (Wharton &
Blair-Loy, 2006). For example, culture informs social expectations
and self-expectations that shape how individuals navigate their
work and family roles (Lee, Chang, & Kim, 2011). In addition,
countries differ in their structural approaches to the provision of
resources available for managing work and family (Allen, Lapierre, et al., 2014). For example, in countries such as Denmark and
Iceland, the government mandates paid parental and sick leaves,
while in the U.S. there is no federal mandated paid leave policy. A
configural approach recognizes that cultural components need to
be considered simultaneously in order to produce a more complete
understanding of the impact of cultural variation (Singelis, Triandis, Bhawuk, & Gelfand, 1995). One type of configural approach
is to examine regional country clusters. GLOBE clusters were
developed and empirically validated (via discriminant analysis) for
the purpose of creating a more holistic approach to understanding
similarities and differences among societies rather than focusing
on one dimension at a time (Gupta & Hanges, 2004). In addition
to cultural value similarities, countries are empirically grouped
based on their geography, ethnicity, and linguistic-religious similarities (Gupta & Hanges, 2004).
Table 2 summarizes the nine GLOBE dimensions and the nine
country clusters included in our study in a matrix format that
identifies where each country cluster falls along each dimension.
We also include national family related support as an institutional
country level variable that varies across clusters. For example, as
depicted in the table, the Anglo cluster overall is described as
competitive and results-oriented. It is moderate with regard to
assertiveness, future orientation, gender egalitarianism, humane
orientation, institutional collectivism, power distance, uncertainty
avoidance, low in in-group collectivism and national family related
support, and high with regard to performance orientation.
As noted by Gupta and Hanges (2004), regional country clusters
can assist with theory development as it permits researchers to test
the generalizability of empirical findings detected in one culture to
other cultures. By examining regional clusters as moderators our
intent is to generate additional theory as to the boundary conditions
associated with work–family conflict relationships. Given all the
possible comparisons of country clusters that could be made, we
take a more inductive approach while also building on our single
dimension hypotheses. As such we pose an overall research question with regard to cluster differences as well as more specific
research questions for which more particular outcomes may be
expected.

Cultural clusters are also distinctive in their provision of institutional support for blending work and family roles. Institutional
factors refer to public policies, laws, and regulations at the country
level that govern aspects of work such as paid vacations, paid sick
leave, and parental leave following the birth/adoption of a child
(Ollier-Malaterre et al., 2013). For example, countries in the Nordic European cluster (Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, Sweden) provide extensive paid leave supports, while countries in the
Anglo cluster (Australia, U.S., United Kingdom, New Zealand,
Canada) tend to provide fewer, or in the case of the U.S., no
federal mandated paid leave support (Allen, Cho, et al., 2014;
Trefalt, Drnovšek, Svetina-Nabergoj, & Adlešič, 2013). From a

boundary management perspective, such policies may enable
smooth micro and macro transitions between work and family
domains. For example, an expecting mother with a highly demanding job might use institutional supports to take sanctioned time off,
reducing the likelihood that her work demands instigate WIF.

demands and WIF.

Method
Several methods were used to conduct a comprehensive and
systematic search of the relevant work–family conflict literature.
Specifically, we conducted a keyword search on PsycINFO and
ProQuest Dissertation databases. Work–family interface search
terms included “work–family,” “work-nonwork,” and “work-life”
combined with “conflict,” “balance,” “spillover,” “interference,”
or “fit.” These terms were combined with correlate search terms
(i.e., work/family hours, work/family demand, work/family overload, job/family/marital/life/career satisfaction). In addition, steps
were taken to identify articles that may have been missed in our
database search and to identify unpublished work. Specifically, we
reviewed the reference sections of published work–family metaanalyses (e.g., Amstad et al., 2011), reviewed the reference section
of a comprehensive qualitative review of cross-national work–
family studies (Shockley et al., 2017), contacted work–family
scholars who conduct international work–family research, and
reviewed recent conference (e.g., Academy of Management; Society for Industrial and Organizational Psychology; Work–Family
Researchers Network) programs.

Eligibility Screening
Our search produced a total of 2,390 studies for initial screening. Studies were determined eligible if they (a) reported an effect
size convertible to r, (b) included a measure of directional work–
family conflict, (c) included one of our predictor or outcome
variables, (d) were written in the English language, and (f) reported effect sizes that could be separated by country.
We screened for multiple studies based on the same sample
(e.g., MIDUS) and in such cases used the study with the largest
sample size and/or with usable data. A total of 332 studies (2,733
effect sizes) that represented 58 different countries were eligible
for inclusion.

Coding and Statistical Procedures
Each study was independently coded by two of the study authors. Coding discrepancies were resolved through discussion and
reexamination of the data. Intercoder agreement was calculated
using Kappa and ICC(3). Agreement ranged from ICC(3) ⫽ .95
(agreement on effect size coding) to ICC(3) ⫽ .99 (agreement on
country).

Supplemental Analyses
Publication bias and outliers. We conducted additional analyses to determine the extent publication bias could influence our
results. Specifically, we examined forest plots to identify effect
size and sample size outliers. Effect size outliers were defined as
effect sizes that had nonoverlapping confidence intervals with
other study effect sizes. We identified seven effect size outliers
from two studies (Olson, 2014; Poelmans et al., 2003). Sample size
outliers were defined as discontinuous, extreme values when examining the distribution of sample sizes. We identified five sample
size outliers from two studies (Gan, Gan, Chen, Miao, & Zhang,
2015, N ⫽ 11,419; Liberman, 2012, N ⫽ 8,646). To test the extent
our results were influenced by outliers, we conducted all analyses
twice: once without sample size outliers and once without effect
size outliers. There were no substantive changes in the meaning of
results. Full results are available upon request.
To test the potential impact of publication bias, we conducted
trim-and-fill analyses for the relationships between WIF/FIW and
correlates using the metafor package Hunter-Schmidt estimator.
All confidence intervals around the meta-analytic correlations in
the trim-and-fill analyses overlapped with their corresponding
confidence intervals from the reported results. The relationship
between work hours and FIW decreased slightly (⌬r ⫽ .03) and
was no longer statistically significant (r ⫽ .01, 95% CI [.00, .03]).
The difference in magnitude between the reported and the trimand-fill analyses was minimal (maximum ⌬r ⫽ .07, average |⌬r| ⫽
.03). Overall, results are robust to outliers and to publication bias.
Other GLOBE dimensions. Our hypotheses that concerned
individual cultural values focused on collectivism, uncertainty
avoidance, and power distance. To provide a comprehensive examination of cultural values, we tested each GLOBE dimension as
a moderator. Findings are summarized in Table 10. Table 11
shows complete results for WIF and Table 12 shows complete
results for FIW. None of the additional cultural values examined
emerged as consistent moderators across predicted relationships.
Assertiveness was the only supplemental value with more than two
significant moderator effects. Specifically, the relationship between work demands and WIF was stronger in higher assertiveness
cultures than in lower assertiveness cultures while the relationship
between family demands and FIW was weaker in higher assertiveness cultures than in lower assertiveness cultures. In addition, the

relationship between WIF and job satisfaction was stronger in
higher assertiveness cultures than in lower assertiveness cultures.
The findings also provide a snapshot of what predicted work–
family conflict relationships appear to be more or less likely to be
moderated by culture. For example, none of the cultural variables
moderated relationships with regard to work hours and WIF, job
satisfaction and FIW, nor WIF/FIW and family satisfaction. The
predicted relationship most likely to be moderated was between
WIF and job satisfaction.
Time-lagged studies. Because the vast majority of primary
studies from which our analyses was drawn were based on crosssectional data, to assess the robustness of the main effect relationships, we analyzed effect sizes for studies that were time-lagged.
Results are shown in Table 13. The table includes variables that
involved k ⬎ 2 effect sizes and relationships that were predicted.
Two associations became nonsignificant. The association between WIF at Time 1 and family demands at Time 2 was
nonsignificant (r ⫽ .09, 95% CI [⫺.17, .37]) and the association between FIW at Time 1 and family demands at Time 2 was
nonsignificant (r ⫽ .10, 95% CI [⫺.18, .42]). We also computed differences between the cross-sectional and the lagged N
(diffN), k (diffke), and effect sizes (diffr; original value—
lagged value). For all but four meta-analyses, lagged analyses
were weaker than the main analyses (average |⌬r| ⫽ .11, SD
|⌬r| ⫽ .06). Overall these findings provide little evidence that
main analysis results are inflated.

Discussion
In recent years there has been a great deal of interest in examining the extent that theory and research concerning work–family
conflict, which has primarily evolved in the Western context,
generalizes to other cultural contexts (Korabik et al., 2017). Our
comprehensive meta-analysis provides broad insights with regard
to the extent that well-established work–family conflict relationships vary based on national context attributes. Several key findings emerged.
Based on boundary theory, we hypothesized that collectivism, PD, and UA moderated work–family conflict relationships.
We found limited support for PD and UA as stand-alone moderators. However, we found support for collectivism as a moderator of WIF/FIW and satisfaction outcome relationships.

more collectivistic and in higher power distance (hierarchical)
contexts than in more individualistic and in lower power distance
contexts. However, the findings also suggest that the impact of
cultural practices such as power distance on work–family relation-

culture as opposed to when examined in isolation. These findings
help demonstrate the value of examining regional clusters, in
addition to examining stand-alone cultural values.

clusters in that it is the only cluster classified in the GLOBE
low-PD band. Moreover, Nordic countries are known to provide
generous paid leave supports intended to help support family (e.g.,
paid parental and sick leave). It may be that the availability of
generous supports for family, the promotion of gender equity, and
the rejection of rigid hierarchies raise the expectation that work
will be immune from family interference. Thus, when FIW does
occur, it is especially frustrating, thereby reducing life satisfaction.

width of the confidence intervals across the regional clusters. As
illustrated in Figures 1 and 2, the confidence intervals associated
with observed effect sizes were consistently wide in the South Asia
and Eastern Europe clusters. South Asia is notable for its significant religious diversity and rigid stratification of society into
socioeconomic classes (Gupta et al., 2002). The Eastern Europe
cluster is also diverse in terms of economic history and religion
(Bakacsi et al., 2002). The economic and religious diversity that
exists in these clusters may create greater within cluster variation

in relationships between work–family conflict and its correlates.
Our findings suggest that examination of religion and economic
development in concert with cultural values is needed to obtain
more precise estimates of work–family relationships. For example,
religion plays a role in shaping beliefs regarding ideal family
arrangements and responsibilities within the family that may impact work–family conflict relationships (Rogers & Franzen, 2014).

Implications for Theory and Practice
Our results advance theory in several ways. While Ashforth et al.
(2000) speculated that the culture in which individual are embedded
would affect boundary management processes, little research has
applied boundary management theory within the cross-national work–
family research literature. Because culture establishes a set of norms
that helps individuals separate, define, and draw boundaries between
interpersonal relationships and roles, we theorized that boundary
management would be useful as a theoretical basis for understanding
cultural variation in work–family conflict relationships with key predictors and outcomes.
Our results suggest that boundary theory has explanatory value
in terms of affective correlates of work–family conflict in that our
findings with regard to work–family conflict and satisfaction outcomes generally align with boundary theory. As suggested by
boundary theory, our data shows that under contexts in which work
and family role identities are more interdependent than independent, interrole conflict is less impactful. That is, conflicts between
work and family are less likely to negatively relate to life satisfaction. Further, our findings suggest that collectivism may be the
cultural moderator that best aligns with culture-level boundary
management norms due to the variation in role interdependence
and harmony that lie at the heart of cross-cultural collectivism
differences.
Our findings provide less evidence of the utility of boundary
theory with regard to the relationship between demands and work–
family conflict. Our supplemental GLOBE analyses indicated that
cultural values moderated the relationship between work demands
and WIF, but none of the values were those identified by Ashforth
et al. (2000). It is probably unreasonable to expect than any single

(World Policy Center, 2018). However, based solely on national
policy we might then also expect differences between Latin American countries and Nordic or Eastern European countries, yet this
difference did not emerge. Instead, the configuration of culture
with policy may be driving differences. For example, Anglo individuals are categorized as performance oriented, while Latin
American countries are considered low on performance orientation. Perhaps it is the combination of high performance orientation
with limited institutional supports that sets Anglo countries apart
from Nordic and Eastern European countries. Overall, these findings suggest that institutional policies should continue to be considered as a factor that can explain between country differences in
the relationship between work demands and WIF. Primary studies

work–family relationships and further the development of work–

organizational work in global environments in that they help
determine limits and boundaries for the transfer of human resource
policies across societies (Gupta & Hanges, 2004). Our findings
that show that the relationship between work demands and WIF
generalizes across the globe. Thus, the recommendation that organizations implement policy to minimize long work hours and

Limitations
We acknowledge several limitations associated with the present study. One concern is the extent that reports of work–family
conflict, or any of the variables examined in the current study,
share the same meaning from one cultural context to the next.
Cross-national comparison studies do not routinely include tests
of measurement invariance (Shockley et al., 2017). As suggested by Allen, French, Dumani, and Shockley (2015), the
development of open repositories of cross-national data, translated measures, and reports of measures that demonstrate
measurement invariance across countries are needed to assist
scholars interested in cross-national comparative research, particularly those in resource-poor contexts. Such approaches are
consistent with growing calls for a more open and replicable
science base within organizational psychology (e.g., Grand et
al., 2018) and would enable researchers across the globe to
more readily conduct work–family studies (Gloss, Carr, Reichman, Abdul-Nasiru, & Oestereich, 2017).
The majority of the primary studies included in our analyses are
based on cross-sectional data, prohibiting firm conclusions with
regard to causal direction. Hours and demands are typically posited
as theoretical antecedents of work–family conflict while satisfaction is typically positioned as a theoretical outcome (e.g., Frone et
al., 1992). However, this theoretical positioning has been questioned. For example, occurrences of work–family conflict may
leave individuals predisposed to increased demands (Kinnunen,
Feldt, Mauno, & Rantanen, 2010). Our supplemental analyses that
examined lagged relationships suggest that these relationships are
robust. However, with an increase in the number of longitudinal
panel studies, theoretical assumptions with regard to the directionality of work–family conflict and its correlates may be more
thoroughly subjected to meta-analytic testing (see e.g., Nohe,
Meier, Sonntag, & Michel, 2015). As this body of literature
accumulates, future research is needed to determine if directionality varies across cultural contexts.
Our study also reveals limitations associated with the representation of work–family research across the globe. While we
were able to capture all but one (Sub-Saharan Africa) of the
regional clusters represented in the GLOBE study, some of the
sample sizes associated with the cluster analyses were small and
should be interpreted with caution. Our research underscores

that there is a particular dearth of work–family research from
Africa. Specifically, we identified only two studies from South
Africa and four from Nigeria. Thus, concerted efforts to create
collaborative opportunities that bring in researchers from across
the African continent are needed to better understand employee
work–family experiences in this developing region of the
world.

Future Research
The state of organizational research is such that culture is
typically measured at the country level (Gelfand et al., 2017). This
is consistent with the notion that culture is a top-down phenomenon. Cultural dimensions are used to describe differences between
countries (e.g., the U.S. is individualistic while China is collectivistic). Bottom-up research, which looks at how lower level properties aggregate to form collective phenomena is needed in addition to top down research, which investigates the influence of
higher-level contextual factors on lower-levels of the system (Kozlowski & Klein, 2000). Research within the work–family domain
has focused more on the latter, with researchers typically framing
studies in terms of understanding how societal values or organizational policies influence individual phenomena such as work–
family conflict. This perspective is reflected in the current study.
The examination of culture at the individual level within comparative work–family cross-national studies is rare (cf. Korabik et al.,
2017). This omission is important in that individual measures of
culture may help link national context with work–family conflict
(Allen et al., 2015). Such approaches are also needed to permit
evaluation of within country variability on cultural values of
interest, which could reveal new insights as well. Another important bottom up approach is using qualitative methods to better
understand work–family conflict as experienced by workers from
various cultural contexts. This may include research intended to
capture person-centric examinations of work–family conflict experiences and the meaning attached to these experiences by workers (Weiss & Rupp, 2011).
There are several ways to expand the scope of research
beyond the current investigation. Research shows that culture
may exert more or less of an impact under different conditions
(Taras et al., 2010). We examined correlates of work–family
conflict based on well-established relationships in the literature

experiences to date. As the literature on positive interdependencies between work and family roles (e.g., work–family

we recommend cross-national experience sampling studies that
can compare daily work–family conflict of individuals from
different cultural contexts. Such studies are needed to reduce
reliance on retrospective reports that are typically untethered to

time and that can better reveal relationships between work–
family conflict experiences and associated affective reactions
closer to the moment they occur.

Conclusion
The importance of cross-cultural theory has been repeatedly
emphasized in the field of organizational behavior (e.g., Gelfand et
al., 2017; Taras et al., 2010). Moreover, the simultaneous management of work and family roles is a concern to individuals
across the globe. Based on 2,733 effect sizes drawn from 58
different countries, we provide an update of meta-analytic effect
sizes associated with key predictors and outcomes associated with
work–family conflict and examine the generalizability of these
relationships though several different approaches. Our results reveal meaningful variation in work–family conflict relationships as
a function of culture and help identify the relationships that are
more culture sensitive than others.
