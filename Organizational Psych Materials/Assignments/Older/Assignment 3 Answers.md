- **Perceived Social Support:**\
  One widely used scale is the **Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support (MSPSS)**. This scale assesses perceived support from three sources: family, friends, and significant others.

*Sample Items:*

- "I can count on my friends when things go wrong."

- "My family is willing to help me make decisions."

**Citation:** Zimet, G. D., Dahlem, N. W., Zimet, S. G., & Farley, G. K. (1988). The multidimensional scale of perceived social support. *Journal of Personality Assessment*, *52*(1), 30-41.

- **Burnout:**\
  For measuring burnout in students, the **Maslach Burnout Inventory -- Student Survey (MBI-SS)** is commonly used. It assesses emotional exhaustion, cynicism, and academic efficacy.

*Sample Items:*

- "I feel emotionally drained from my studies."

- "I have become less enthusiastic about my studies."

**Citation:** Schaufeli, W. B., Martínez, I. M., Pinto, A. M., Salanova, M., & Bakker, A. B. (2002). Burnout and engagement in university students: A cross-national study. *Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology*, *33*(5), 464-481.

- Are there any changes that may be need to be made to any of these scales to measure a student population?

Both scales are designed for broader populations but are applicable to students with minimal modification. If needed, wording can be adjusted to reflect student-specific contexts, such as replacing \"work\" with \"academic responsibilities\" in the burnout scale. However, the core structure and validated items should remain unchanged to preserve the scales' reliability and validity.

- Why is important to try and use pre-existing or validated scales in research?

Using pre-existing or validated scales is crucial because these tools have undergone rigorous testing for reliability and validity. They ensure consistency across studies and help researchers compare results. Moreover, validated scales reduce the risk of measurement error and improve the credibility of the research findings.

**Limitations of AI in Research:**

- **Accuracy:** AI outputs depend on the quality of the input, and results may occasionally lack depth or relevance.

- **Bias:** AI tools may inadvertently reflect biases present in the data they are trained on, which can affect the output.

**Ethical Considerations:**\
Researchers should remain aware of the ethical implications of using AI, particularly around data privacy, potential biases in AI outputs, and over-reliance on AI-generated content. While AI can support the research process, it cannot replace the critical thinking and decision-making involved in rigorous scientific inquiry.
