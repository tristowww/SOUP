a.  **Culture vs. Climate (Denison, 1996)**

- What seems deeply rooted and value-driven (culture)?

- What feels more like the current mood or perceptions (climate)?

- Why might leaders find climate easier to shift than culture?

- **Culture (deep, enduring):** shared basic assumptions, identity-defining values (e.g., innovation, sustainability, "customer first," long-term mission).

- **Climate (surface/more malleable):** current employee perceptions of policies, leadership priorities, fairness, workload, safety, service focus.

- **Why climate easier to shift:**

  a.  Climate is shaped by **visible policies, practices, leadership communication, HR systems** and can change faster.

  b.  Culture is **rooted in meaning and history**; it's slow to evolve and reinforced socially.

✅ Full-credit answer: explicitly contrasts *deep/values-driven* vs *perceptual/mood* and states that climate is easier because it's tied to current leadership actions & HR practices, while culture is ingrained over time.

b.  **Schein's Levels of Culture**

- Give one example each of: artifact, espoused value, and underlying assumption.

- Do the visible artifacts align with stated organizational values? If not, what confusion could that cause?

- **Artifact:** tangible symbol or practice (e.g., open office layout, casual dress code, slogans, safety posters, sustainability reports).

- **Espoused value:** stated belief/ideal (e.g., "We value collaboration and transparency").

- **Underlying assumption:** taken-for-granted belief (e.g., "Employees should be self-directed"; "Growth is the top priority").

- **Alignment check:**

  a.  Good answer shows when artifacts **match** espoused values (e.g., "teamwork posters + collaborative meetings").

  b.  Or identifies **misalignment** (e.g., "Company says it values innovation but has rigid approval chains → confusion/disengagement").

✅ Full credit: correctly classifies each level and comments on consistency/mismatch.

d.  **Attraction--Selection--Attrition (ASA) --- Schneider (1987)**

- How might who the company attracts, selects, and keeps reinforce the current culture?

- If the organization wants culture change, what's one HR/talent practice you would change first?

- **Attraction:** people drawn to orgs that fit their values.

- **Selection:** hiring for cultural fit (explicit or implicit).

- **Attrition:** misfits self-select out or are pushed out.

- Good answer:

  a.  Explains how ASA keeps culture stable (e.g., "Netflix attracts risk-takers, selects independent thinkers, those uncomfortable with autonomy leave").

  b.  Suggests a talent lever to change culture (e.g., revise recruiting messaging, hire for different competencies, redesign onboarding, change promotion criteria).
