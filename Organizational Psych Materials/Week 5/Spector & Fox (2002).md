An emotion-centered model of voluntary work behavior
Some parallels between counterproductive work behavior
and organizational citizenship behavior

Abstract
We present a model that integrates findings from several areas to explain in parallel the voluntary
acts of counterproductive work behavior (CWB) and organizational citizenship behavior (OCB). CWB
is behavior, such as aggression or sabotage, intended to hurt the organization or its employees. OCB is
prosocial behavior intended to help. A variety of job/organizational conditions (constraints on
performance, job stressors, injustice, or violation of psychological contract) will elicit emotional
reactions. Negative emotion will tend to increase the likelihood of CWB and positive emotion will
increase the likelihood of OCB. CWB is associated with the personality characteristics of trait anger
and anxiety, locus of control, and delinquency. OCB is associated with empathy and perceived ability
to help. We discuss how management of emotion-eliciting conditions and events can help control
voluntary behavior in a way that leads to both employee and organization well-being. D 2002 Elsevier
Science Inc. All rights reserved.

1. Introduction
One of the major thrusts in organizational research has been directed toward understanding
the causes of employee job performance, which consists of assigned task-related activities. In
recent years, there has been a shift in emphasis to include voluntary behavior that goes
beyond task performance. Two independent streams of research have been developing. One
concerns voluntary altruistic or helpful acts that have the potential to enhance organizations,

commonly called organizational citizenship behavior (OCB) (Organ, 1988). The other
concerns voluntary, potentially destructive or detrimental acts that hurt colleagues or
organizations, which we (and others) call counterproductive work behavior (CWB). A close
examination of research addressing both forms of behavior suggests that there are parallels
that invite some integration of these two seemingly different areas. Our intent is to provide a
model that integrates both forms of behavior by underscoring parallelism in underlying
processes. At the same time, there are some important distinctions to be illuminated within a
common framework. As we will show, emotional reactions to work serve a central role in
both forms of behavior, and provide a unifying function in our model.
This proposed model for voluntary behavior includes both environmental and personal
factors as causes. Emotion mediates the effects of environmental conditions on behavior. As
situations, filtered through personal appraisals and perceptions, induce emotion, they affect
the likelihood that the individual will choose a form of either CWB or OCB. Negative
emotion increases the likelihood or facilitates CWB, whereas positive emotion tends to
facilitate OCB. Continued exposure to emotion-arousing events will heighten the likelihood
for the person to engage in behavioral response. Furthermore, these forms of voluntary
behavior leave far more room for the action of personal intentions than more constrained and
routinized task-related job performance that is the usual focus of our research attention (Staw
& Barsade, 1993). In addition, certain individual characteristics will make a person more or
less likely to commit CWB or OCB in response to such conditions. Although it is certain
that not all behavior is mediated by emotion, our model is limited to this case, which should
encompass a large share of such behavior that occurs in organizations. Our approach was to
base our model on existing findings concerning voluntary behavior, and so it is not
complete, but rather reflects the state of current knowledge about the role of emotions in
voluntary behavior.

2. Organizational citizenship behavior
Several authors have discussed how individuals may make voluntary contributions that go
beyond specified task performance or the psychological contract with the employer. These
behaviors are intended to help either people or the organization. Since they are not necessarily
job requirements, and might not be subject to rewards or punishments (although it is certainly
possible that OCB is noticed and rewarded), such acts are considered altruistic or prosocial
(McNeely & Meglino, 1994). Such behavior is most commonly called OCB (Organ, 1988),
although Borman and Motowidlo (1993) use the term contextual performance to refer to
much the same phenomenon. It has long been recognized that such behavior is essential for
effective organizational functioning, but little attention was given to it prior to Organ’s work
in the early 1980s (Bateman & Organ, 1983; Smith, Organ, & Near, 1983).
Various subcategories of OCB have been devised by different researchers. Organ et al.
(e.g., Smith et al., 1983) distinguished altruism from compliance. The former term refers to
acts intended to help, such as assisting another employee or volunteering to stay late.
Compliance is essentially conscientiousness and reliability, describing an individual who

comes to work on time and does what is required to the best of his or her ability. What makes
the latter OCB is that the person is going beyond what is specifically required in displaying
conscientiousness and initiative.
Van Scotter and Motowidlo (1996) described two similar dimensions of contextual
performance: interpersonal facilitation and job dedication. Facilitation is ‘‘cooperative,
considerate, and helpful acts that assist coworkers’ performance’’ (p. 525), whereas dedication
is ‘‘self-disciplined, motivated acts such as working hard, taking initiative, and following rules
to support organizational objectives’’ (p. 525). However, they found that dedication showed
too much overlap with job performance and facilitation and questioned if it was a viable
distinct construct.
Van Dyne and LePine (1998) developed a taxonomy of what they call extrarole behavior.
They distinguish behavior that is promotive (cause things to happen) versus prohibitive
(attempts to prevent things from happening), and that is affiliative (interpersonal) versus
challenging (involves ideas and issues).
McNeely and Meglino (1994) divided OCB into two categories according to the intended
beneficiary. One is designed to help other individuals (e.g., a coworker with a problem), and
the other is designed to help the organization (e.g., offer ideas to improve things). Whereas
they found that job satisfaction related equally to both forms of OCB, the pattern of relations
was different for other variables. Empathy was associated with individual but not organizational OCB. Perceptions of justice were associated with organizational but not individual
OCB. Thus, people who are empathetic by nature will tend to help others, while people who
feel that they are fairly treated will tend to help the organization. These varying patterns of
relations lend support to the idea that these two forms of OCB are distinct.

3. Counterproductive work behavior
CWB is behavior intended to hurt the organization or other members of the organization.
This includes acts with the potential for harm, such as avoiding work, doing tasks incorrectly,
physical aggression, verbal hostility (insults), sabotage, and theft. Some acts, such as
aggression and hostility, are directed at people, while others, such as doing tasks incorrectly
or sabotage, are directed at organizations. This parallels the OCB findings that there can be
different targets (McNeely & Meglino, 1994). Theft can be directed at either, depending
upon whether the theft is from an individual or organization. In many cases, a specific CWB
might be opposite to a specific OCB. For example, the OCB of coming to work conscientiously (on time and every day) has an opposite CWB of withdrawal from work reflected in absence and tardiness.
Various authors have studied CWB, but from different theoretical perspectives. This has
led to a proliferation of terms to describe what is an overlapping set of behaviors. Spector
(1975, 1978) was perhaps the first to take this approach in defining organizational aggression
as behaviors intended to hurt the organization. More recently, the terms aggression (Neuman
& Baron, 1998), antisocial (Giacalone & Greenberg, 1997), counterproductive (Fox &
Spector, 1999), delinquency (Hogan & Hogan, 1989), deviance (Hollinger, 1986; Robinson

& Bennett, 1995), retaliation (Skarlicki & Folger, 1997), and revenge (Bies, Tripp, & Kramer,
1997) have all been used to refer to behavior that fits our definition of CWB.
Differences in terminology reflect the theoretical bases of work done by these various
authors. Neuman and Baron (1998) and Spector (1978), as well as O’Leary-Kelly, Griffin,
and Glew (1996) based their work on the social psychological aggression literature. Hollinger
(1986) and Robinson and Bennett (1995) referred to deviance, which involves violation of
organization norms and rules. Hogan and Hogan (1989) took a criminological approach,
comparing incarcerated felons with a general college population to gain insights as to
distinguishing characteristics of individuals who engage in what they call organizational
delinquency. Skarlicki and Folger (1997) took an organizational justice approach, calling
CWB a form of retaliation. Bies et al. (1997) adopted a perspective that also includes
emotion. They propose that certain organizational events lead to negative emotion (e.g.,
anger) and cognition that under the right conditions will lead to revenge behaviors, some of
which are CWB.
CWB has been classified into subcategories using several different, but not entirely
conflicting schemes. Spector et al. (Chen & Spector, 1992; Spector, 1975; Storms & Spector,
1987) factor analyzed participant reports of behavior frequency and had categories of
aggression, hostility, sabotage, theft, and withdrawal. Robinson and Bennett (1995) conducted a multidimensional scaling study of CWB and derived two dimensions that were used
to classify it into four types. One dimension distinguished organizational from personal
targets, and the other concerned minor versus serious acts. Neuman and Baron (1998) used a
three-category scheme of hostility, obstruction, and overt aggression. Hollinger (1986)
distinguished production from property deviance. Fox, Spector, and Miles (2001) compiled
a comprehensive list of 64 CWBs that were gathered from several prior studies. Beginning
with the list from Fox and Spector (1999), they added items from Hollinger (1986), Knorz
and Zapf (1996), Neuman and Baron (1998), Robinson and Bennett (1995), and Skarlicki and
Folger (1997). Participant reports of behavior frequency were factor analyzed, resulting in
five dimensions, abuse of others (e.g., insults and nasty comments), threats (threatening
harm), work avoidance (e.g., tardiness), work sabotage (doing work incorrectly), overt acts
(theft). The first two dimensions target people, the next two target organizations, and the last
one targets both, but is more serious in nature.
These acts can also be classified as active versus passive. Active behavior is directed
immediately at the target, e.g., yelling at a supervisor. However, such acts are likely to be
punished, so quite often a person will resort to a passive approach, such as withholding
performance or withdrawal from work (e.g., absence or lateness). Whereas impulsive acts in
response to emotions are most likely to be active, long-term intentional acts are likely to be
passive, as when an employee purposely fails to warn a supervisor that there is a work problem.

4. The central role of emotion
Emotion serves an adaptive function in response to environmental events that have
implications for survival (Plutchik, 1989). Mandler (1975) and Simon (1967) argue that

emotion is a functional mechanism whereby highly organized, ongoing activity is interrupted to
force attention on urgent events that are relevant to physiological needs or that induce
disturbing cognitive associations, such as threats to esteem. The role of emotion is to energize
the individual physiologically and to induce appropriate action. Wallbott and Scherer (1989)
describe five subcomponents involved in the experience of emotion: evaluation of the situation,
physiological changes, motor expression, motivation for action, and subjective feeling states.
This five-component model suggests that individuals are active agents in their own
experience. They continuously monitor the environment, and when events are evaluated in
a particular way, physical and psychological changes are induced. Physiological arousal
increases, certain body movements and facial expressions occur (e.g., clenched teeth for
anger), the individual is motivated for action, and an emotion is experienced. This motivation
for action is likely to result in intentions to engage in certain behavior at a subsequent time.
Cognitive interpretation of the environmental events provides the basis for the particular
emotion experienced (Schachter & Singer, 1962). Lazarus (1982) suggested that if the
individual appraises a situation as enhancing well-being, a positive emotion will be
experienced, whereas a threat to well-being will induce a negative emotion. Appraisal is
influenced by personal factors that direct attention and filter experience. An individual in an
anxious state, for example, will be inclined to appraise a situation as threatening.
Emotion induces action tendencies that will tend to elicit behavior, but this does not
usually happen in a reflexive or nonpurposive manner with humans. In the case of strong
negative emotion, at times, an individual might act quite immediately and impulsively,
striking out at another person or object. More typically emotion acts to help formulate
intentions to engage in certain behaviors (Bies et al., 1997), or produces a readiness to act
later when certain events or situations are encountered. Quite typically, emotion motivates
behavior that will reduce negative feelings and enhance positive feelings. This can be
accomplished through direct action designed to change the environment directly, or what
Lazarus (1995) labels problem-focused coping. Whereas Lazarus tends to focus on constructive actions in response to stress, it is also possible to take direct action that might
eliminate the immediate cause of a negative emotion, but in a destructive way. This action
might harm another individual or a larger social unit. If a direct approach is not possible, the
individual might take actions designed to affect his or her emotions, without addressing the
situational cause. This emotion-focused approach (Lazarus, 1995), might involve a number of
actions, such as avoiding the situation, or striking back surreptitiously in retaliation. Such
behavior might serve no functional purpose other than to make the person feel better by
‘‘evening the score.’’ When a person makes a decision to engage in a behavior while in an
emotional state, it is quite possible that that particular emotion (e.g., anger) is not experienced
at the time when the CWB is engaged. In fact, the act of getting even might be associated
with a positive emotion.
OCB can be encouraged by positive emotion. Whereas negative emotion induces
avoidance tendencies to exit the situation, positive emotion induces approach tendencies to
remain. Furthermore, people in good moods will engage in behavior that will support their
moods. For example, people in such a state might choose to engage in an altruistic behavior
as a means of making themselves continue to feel good (Isen, 1984).

The workplace is an environment that can induce strong emotion, as it is the source of both
physical (e.g., money) and psychological (e.g., esteem) need fulfillment. Individuals in the
workplace will monitor situations, and those deemed as particularly relevant for enhancing or
hurting well-being will tend to induce emotion. That emotion will produce action tendencies
and intentions that might enhance positive and reduce negative states. Voluntary behavior
often serves these functions for individuals. A situation that induces a negative emotion will
increase the likelihood that CWB will occur, either to actively and directly attack the agent of
the situation (e.g., through intimidation and threats) or to passively and indirectly cope with
the emotion (e.g., by avoiding work or drinking alcohol). Oftentimes, a person who was made
to feel bad by another will engage in retaliation in order to make himself or herself feel better.
A situation that induces a positive state will have the opposite effect. It will encourage the
individual to become more involved in the workplace, which is a source of pleasurable
feelings. This includes OCB, which in and of itself can induce positive emotion.
It should be noted that we are not suggesting that emotion automatically or necessarily
invokes behavior, or that behavior will occur immediately. Rather emotion increases the
likelihood of certain behavior, which can occur under certain conditions. That is, they induce
action tendencies and intentions to act with behavior being carried out later when opportunities arise. In some cases, responses will be immediate, as when an employee assaults a
coworker in response to some provocation, but most CWB is not of this variety. For example,
Fox, Spector, and Miles (1999) found that direct assaults were far rarer in organizations than
less direct forms of CWB.

5. An integrated CWB/OCB model
5.1. Overview of the model
The conceptual model, illustrated in Fig. 1, views behavior as the outcome of an
interaction between an individual and the environment. This figure shows the general
process, but not the specific variables involved. For example, perceptions of the environment lead to emotion in a general sense, but not all environmental variables lead to
emotion. Connections among specific variables will be discussed after we provide this
general framework.
Although the causal flow in our model might be presumed to be from left (environment) to
right (behavior), this does not imply that causation is necessarily unidirectional, or that the
individual merely responds reflexively to conditions beyond personal control. This is
indicated by the double-headed arrows, showing that causal flow can be in both directions.
The figure shows categories of variables, which we discuss in greater specificity throughout
the paper. For example, environment consists of organizational constraints, role ambiguity,
role conflict, interpersonal conflict, and reward/punishment contingencies. Personality
includes conscientiousness, agreeableness, emotional stability (trait anxiety), trait anger,
and empathy. There are certainly many other relevant variables, but we limit our model to
only those for which empirical evidence exists, linking them to our voluntary behavior.

Beginning at the left, the model suggests that the environment is perceived and appraised
by the individual, but appraisal is influenced by the individual’s baseline emotional state
(Anderson, Deuser, & DeNeve, 1995), perceptions of control over the situation, and
personality. Appraisal, in turn, affects emotional state. Background state is caused by events
that have been ongoing, both inside and outside of the workplace. A conflict before work
with a family member, for example, will add to the baseline emotional state, and will affect
reactions to events experienced at work. Emotion, control perceptions, and personality
combined affect behavior, but behavior also can affect emotion and control perceptions.
Finally, behavior has an effect on the environment, which can cause a spiral effect whereby
behavior reinforces environmental conditions that facilitate the behavior. For example, a
supervisor is critical of an employee, who gets angry and talks back, which leads to more
criticism, and to more employee reaction, and the situation escalates into a serious conflict.
Attributions are an important component of appraisal in the process that leads to emotion
and behavior (Bies et al., 1997). As events unfold at work, it is not only what happens that is
important, but also an individual’s interpretation of the motives of the person/s who caused
the event. An accidental event will not typically lead to the same emotion or strength of
emotion as one that is perceived to be purposely caused. For example, we would likely get
angry if someone at work purposely stabbed us with a needle, but we experience a different
emotion if the person is a doctor giving us an injection.
Emotion plays a central role in this model. It is the nature of emotional response (negative
emotion for CWB and positive emotion for OCB) that determines a person’s action tendency.
However, emotion only induces a readiness to engage in behavior or an intention to act; other
factors must also be present. Individual differences are important, as certain personality
characteristics are associated with either CWB (e.g., alienation, Hogan & Hogan, 1989) or

OCB (e.g., empathy, McNeely & Meglino, 1994), as we will note in more detail.
Environmental conditions also help determine reactions. Not every instance of emotional
experience results in a CWB or OCB, but a combination of factors can enhance the likelihood
of occurrence should the opportunity arise.
The CWB and OCB boxes in the figure represent categories of behaviors and should not
be considered specific constructs. An individual who responds to emotion with a voluntary
behavior might elect to commit one or more different acts, depending upon constraints and
opportunities in the environment. Although the various behaviors within each category are
not necessarily manifestations of the same behavior, they might have the same function for
the individual. Thus, two employees who feel unfairly treated might decide to retaliate by
engaging in CWB to hurt the organization, but they might choose two different ways to
accomplish their objective, for example, one might take an active approach and destroy
property, whereas the other takes a passive approach and withhold effort, doing the absolute
minimum required to stay out of trouble with supervisors.
The target for behavior is predicated on the perceived agent of the situation that induced the
emotion, particularly for CWB. Thus, an individual who feels mistreated by a supervisor will
most likely direct CWB toward that individual rather than people in general. If that agent is
seen as part of the organization, the target might be broadened. Hence, an individual who
appeals to higher management and loses will likely blame the organization more than the
supervisor. In many cases, action directly against the target will be inhibited because of fear of
reprisal (hitting a supervisor can get one fired). In such cases, behavior is likely to be indirect,
perhaps surreptitiously attacking someone’s property or work instead of them, or acting in a
passive–aggressive manner, such as failing to complete an assignment correctly or on time.
The model shows parallel processes for CWB and OCB, beginning at the left with
environmental conditions or events. These are aspects of the physical and social environment
that people experience at work. As people encounter these conditions, they are perceived,
processed, and appraised. Some conditions/events are more likely to induce either positive or
negative emotion than others. For example, a computer crash will likely induce annoyance,
whereas being given a reward will likely induce feelings of pride. Negative emotion will
increase the likelihood of CWB, whereas positive emotion will increase the likelihood of
OCB. The effects of continued emotional experience will be summative, as repeated
annoyances will enhance the likelihood of CWB. It is unlikely that an occasional negative
experience will have much impact on behavior unless that experience is quite strong in
intensity, e.g., being demoted without just cause (at least in the individual’s view).
There is a critical distinction between the objective and perceived environments. People
often view situations differently, so situations that induce emotional responses in one person
will not necessarily induce them in another. Context and individual differences, as well as
background emotional state, are important factors. Both control over the situation and
individual personality can affect how people perceive situations. Personality is also a factor
in the individual’s emotion thresholds. Given the same perceived situation, some people (e.g.,
those high in trait anger or anxiety) will be more emotionally reactive than others.
Furthermore, background emotional state affects an individual’s threshold. Finally, both
control and personality help determine if emotion will lead to CWB or OCB.

Factors that are associated with CWB versus OCB can be (but are not always) opposites. For
example, fairness can induce positive emotion, whereas its opposite, unfairness, induces
negative emotion. On the other hand, some factors are not bipolar; for example, contingent
rewards might lead to positive emotion, but lack of this sort of program will not necessarily be
seen negatively. The same is true for personality characteristics. Some traits may work
oppositely, and be associated with both forms of behavior (but in different directions). The
limited research to date suggests that some personality traits relate to one form, but not the other.
Our model proposes that the basic process (although not necessarily the set of variables)
underlying CWB and OCB is similar, and that emotion plays an important role. CWB and
OCB can be oppositely affected by the same factors, and so we might expect that individuals
who are high on one might well be low on the other. Evidence for this is provided by Hunt
(1996) who investigated relations among nine dimensions of job performance. Data for the
study were supervisor ratings of positive and negative performance-related behaviors for
18,146 employees from 36 companies. Nine dimensions were identified, four of which
consisted of positive behaviors and five of which were CWBs. The positive dimensions all
related negatively with the negative, with correlations ranging from
.11 to
.73. Furthermore, Robinson and O’Leary-Kelly (1998) found that their measure of CWB correlated
.24 and .46 with two different measures of OCB.
However, it is also possible for an individual who engages in CWB on one occasion to
engage in OCB on another. Note that both the CWB and OCB literatures distinguish
organizational from personal recipients/targets. Hence, it is quite likely that an individual will
engage in one form of behavior for individuals and another for organizations. For example, a
person might feel unfairly treated by the organization, experience anger, and decide to steal
money from the company. The same person might have good relationships with coworkers who
are not blamed for organizational injustice, and offer to loan the same money to a colleague.
5.2. Environmental conditions related to emotion and voluntary behavior
The events and situations at work that induce emotion are quite varied, and these play a
role in CWB and OCB. There has been much research relating environmental factors to
affect-related variables. Most has focused on job satisfaction rather than specific emotion, but
many have explored emotional reactions, such as anger or anxiety. The majority of the studies
found in our literature have relied on self-reports of both job conditions and affect. Thus,
these studies give insights about relations between perceptions of the environment and
emotion, but it is not clear as to what extent this reflects the objective environment, nor is it
clear that the direction of causality is always from perception to emotion (Spector, 1992;
Taber & Taylor, 1990). Nevertheless, we can use this literature as the starting point from
which to derive a list of potential environmental conditions.
5.2.1. Organizational constraints
Spector (1978) suggested that job conditions, which interfered with an individual’s goal/
objective achievement or maintenance would induce feelings of anger and frustration, and
subsequently to CWB. Peters and O’Connor (1980) refined this idea in the context of work,

and defined organizational constraints as situations at work that inhibit task performance.
They discussed eight constraint areas that involved both physical and social aspects of work.
Research conducted on organizational constraints has tied it to both affect variables and
CWB. Spector (1997) conducted a small meta-analysis that found a mean correlation across
seven studies of .49 between constraints and the emotion of frustration (a form of anger).
Spector and Jex (1998) in another meta-analysis found that constraints correlated significantly
with anxiety (.38 across four studies). Constraints were most strongly related to frustration
(as an emotion), which is consistent with Spector’s (1978) contention that interference with
ongoing behavior will lead to emotions of anger and frustration.
Constraints have also been linked to CWB. Aggression, hostility, sabotage, and withdrawal
(Chen & Spector, 1992; Storms & Spector, 1987), and theft (Chen & Spector, 1992) were
related to constraints. Using the Robinson and Bennett (1995) four-way typology of deviance,
Fox and Spector (1999) found that constraints related significantly to minor personal, serious
personal, and minor organizational, but not serious organizational deviance.
5.2.2. Role stressors
Job stressors are conditions that are associated with negative emotion at work (Spector,
1998), and role stressors have been the most frequently studied. Role ambiguity is the extent
to which an individual is uncertain about what is expected of him or her, whereas role conflict
involves simultaneous demands that interfere with one another and make it difficult to carry
out the job (Katz & Kahn, 1978). One might expect that the uncertainty of role ambiguity
would be met with a certain amount of anxiety and unease. Role conflict overlaps with
constraints in that it represents situations that make covering all responsibilities difficult, and
so it should be related to anger.
Both of these role variables have been associated with affect. Jackson and Schuler (1985)
in their meta-analysis of role variables reported that both role ambiguity and role conflict
relate to tension/anxiety. Role ambiguity and role conflict have been found to relate to both
anxiety and frustration/anger at work (Chen & Spector, 1991; Jex & Spector, 1996; Spector,
1987; Spector, Dwyer, & Jex, 1988; Spector & O’Connell, 1994). The magnitude of relations
for both role variables seems to be similar for anxiety and anxiety/tension, but role conflict
has stronger relations than role ambiguity with emotional frustration. Furthermore, role
ambiguity and conflict relate to reports of CWB classified as aggression, hostility, sabotage,
and theft (Chen & Spector, 1992), and role ambiguity relates to organizational and personal
CWB (Frone, 1998).
5.2.3. Interpersonal conflict
Getting into arguments and fights with others and poor treatment by coworkers and
supervisors are among the most frequently noted job stressors in organizations (Keenan &
Newton, 1984). As a job stressor, interpersonal conflict is expected to be associated with
negative emotion. In their meta-analysis, Spector and Jex (1998) reported mean correlations
in the .30s between interpersonal conflict and anxiety, depression, and emotional frustration.
Conflict (Chen & Spector, 1992) and abusive treatment (Frone, 1998) have been found to
relate to various forms of CWB.

5.2.4. Justice
Perceptions of equity or justice have been studied in relation to both CWB and OCB, and,
here, we find exactly opposite relations. Recent work on distributive and procedural justice
has underscored the importance of fair treatment in employee decisions to engage in both
CWB and OCB. According to equity theory (Adams, 1963), an individual who experiences
inequity or injustice will attempt to restore balance using a number of mechanisms, including
reduction of task behaviors. Since job performance is often highly constrained and under
close organizational control, it is more likely that voluntary OCB will be reduced.
Conversely, Organ and Ryan (1995) found that fairness was associated with OCB. In their
meta-analysis, the mean correlation was .185 for altruism across 20 studies, and was .221 for
compliance across 15 studies. When they divided OCB according to intended target,
McNeely and Meglino (1994) found that justice was related to organizational OCB, but
not personal OCB.
On the negative side, injustice has been shown to relate to CWB with both quasiexperimental and self-report survey studies. Greenberg (1990) conducted an experiment in which
the fairness perceptions concerning a pay reduction were manipulated. Where employees
were given a reasonable and fair explanation, the reduction had limited effects, whereas when
an inadequate explanation was given, theft rates and withdrawal increased. This study
illustrates how appraisal of situations and attributions for events are important. Using a survey
methodology, Frone (1998) showed that feelings of distributive injustice were associated with
both organizational and personal CWB. Similarly, Skarlicki and Folger (1997) and Skarlicki,
Folger, and Tesluk (1999) found that distributive, interactional, and procedural justice were
all associated with their global measure of retaliation, which fits our concept of CWB.
Greenberg and Barling (1999) showed that justice related more strongly to aggression against
supervisors than coworkers or subordinates. Fox et al. (2001) also found a link between
justice and CWB.
5.2.5. Psychological contract
A psychological contract is the belief held by an employee that certain commitments and
obligations exist between him or her and the employing organization (Rousseau, 1995).
Contracts concern what is expected to be given to the employer (e.g., attendance and specific
task performance) and what is to be gotten in return (e.g., benefits and salary). However, the
contract can go far beyond tangibles and can involve treatment and work assignments. It is
possible for an employee to feel that the contract is being met or exceeded, as well as being
violated when the job does not meet expectations.
Employee perceptions of the state of the contract (met, exceeded, or violated) have
important implications for behavior. Although there is limited empirical research, there is
reason to expect that both CWB and OCB are affected by the contract. Robinson and
Morrison (1995) conducted a longitudinal study relating perceptions of contract violation
with OCB. They found that high levels of violation were associated with low levels of OCB
assessed a year apart.
Although the contract, in general, is important as an indicator of the quality of relationship
between an employee and organization, the relationship with the immediate supervisor may

280

be even more important. Settoon, Bennett, and Liden (1996) assessed organizational support,
which they argued reflected the psychological contract and leader–member exchange quality.
They found that the latter was more strongly related to OCB than the former. Conversely,
support was more strongly related than exchange quality to organizational commitment.
Similarly, Randall, Cropanzano, Bormann, and Birjulin (1999) found that employee reports of
organization support related to OCB assessed by supervisors.
Contract violation, as with other organizational conditions that prevent an employee from
achieving what is desired, is likely to lead to negative emotion, such as anger (Morrison &
Robinson, 1997). These emotional states, in turn, are expected to lead to CWB. Only limited
empirical support can be found for these linkages. Robinson and Rousseau (1994) found that
contract violation was associated with job dissatisfaction and intention to quit, but they did
not assess emotion or behavior. They used a longitudinal design that showed that intention to
quit assessed cotemporaneously was significantly correlated with contract violation, but
intention to quit assessed prior was not. This pattern provides somewhat stronger support that
violation causes intent than would be possible with a cross-sectional design.
5.3. Relation of affect variables with CWB and OCB
Our model suggests that emotion will have effects on CWB and OCB. Negative emotion
(e.g., anger or anxiety) will encourage CWB; whereas positive emotion (e.g., enthusiasm or
pride) will encourage OCB.
5.3.1. CWB and emotion
Most of the research on CWB and emotion has been limited to anger and feelings of
frustration, which are a form of mild anger. A small number of studies have looked at a broader
range of emotions, finding similar relations. Chen and Spector (1992) found that frustration
was significantly related to interpersonal aggression and hostility, but not to sabotage, theft, or
absence. They found stronger relations with a measure of anger, which yielded significant
relations for all four CWB scales. Storms and Spector (1987) found significant relations of
frustration with aggression, hostility, sabotage, and withdrawal. Using the organization versus
person dichotomy, Fox and Spector (1999) found relations of frustration with both subscales,
with the larger correlation for organization than person (.36 vs. .23).
Fox et al. (2001) used the Job-Related Affective Well-being Scale (JAWS) that assesses a
wide variety of emotion at work (Van Katwyk, Fox, Spector, & Kelloway, 2000). Negative
emotion included anger, anxiety, boredom, and depression, whereas positive emotion
included cheerfulness, contentment, enthusiasm, and happiness. The overall negative emotion
scale was related to both organization and person targeted CWB, as well as all five subscales
of abusive behavior, overt (serious) acts, threats, work avoidance, and work sabotage.
Positive emotion related only to organization targeted CWB and work sabotage, both in
the expected (negative) direction.
Cropanzano, Howes, Grandey, and Toth (1997) related CWB to high levels of job tension,
somatic tension, fatigue, and burnout. The job stress literature (Spector, 1998) provides
evidence that these strain measures all reflect negative emotion to some extent.

281

Most of these studies have relied on a cross-sectional, single-source, questionnaire
methodology, leaving open the possibility that biases might have affected the results. One
study using a different approach is consistent with these findings. Heacox (1996) related a
measure of employee emotional frustration with independent supervisor reports of employee
CWB. She found a significant relation between both variables, suggesting that employees
who report feeling frustrated at work are more likely to be noted by supervisors as having
engaged in CWB.
5.3.2. OCB and emotion
It has been well established in the social psychological prosocial literature that positive
mood is associated with helping behavior (Baron, 1996; Isen, 1984; Penner, Midili, &
Kegelmeyer, 1997; Salovery, Mayer, & Rosenhan, 1991). Isen (1987) notes, however, that if
the person believes that helping will induce negative emotion (e.g., depression), it might
inhibit helping (Isen, 1987). Thus, expectation of future emotion can be an important element
in behavior. It should be kept in mind, however, that most of these studies involve induced
moods and helping a stranger, rather than someone with whom the subject had an ongoing
relationship. These studies reflect immediate responses rather than future behavior.
The range of emotion-related variables studied by OCB researchers has been quite limited,
with most focusing on general mood rather than specific emotional states. George (1991)
showed that positive mood was associated with OCB. There have been several studies that
related OCB to job satisfaction, but this is an attitudinal variable rather than a measure of
emotion. For example, Organ and Ryan (1995) reported a mean correlation between job
satisfaction and two types of OCB. Altruism correlated .237 and compliance correlated .216.
McNeely and Meglino (1994) found an almost identical correlation of job satisfaction with
OCB targeting the organization (.25) and people (.26). Munene (1995) found relations of
OCB with several attitudinal variables including job satisfaction in a sample from Nigeria,
thus extending these findings beyond western cultures.
5.4. Personality differences
Researchers have recognized that there are important individual difference variables that
contribute to voluntary behavior. Here, more attention has been given to the negative CWB
side, in an attempt to determine characteristics that can be used in selection to reduce this
behavior by avoiding the hiring of individuals who are CWB prone. An industry of integrity
testing has arisen to accomplish this end. Most of the research on OCB is from the social
psychology literature and concerns prosocial behavior in general, although a few work studies
can be found.
5.4.1. CWB and personality
Most of the personality studies concerned with CWBs were attempts to validate integrity
tests for use in employee selection. Ones, Viswesvaran, and Schmidt (1993) conducted a
meta-analysis of such studies, and found that they were able to predict a variety of CWBs.
Ones and Viswesvaran (1998) argued that these tests assess three of the Big Five personality

282

P.E. Spector, S. Fox / Human Resource Management Review 12 (2002) 269–292

dimensions — conscientiousness, agreeableness, and emotional stability. However, the very
general nature of these tests makes it difficult to determine the precise personality characteristics that are important.
Hogan and Hogan (1989) used the Hogan Personality Index (Hogan, 1986) to identify four
personality characteristics that distinguished college students from felons. Alienation,
hostility to rules, social insensitivity, and thrill-seeking impulsiveness were combined into
the Employee Reliability Index. They reported results of several studies relating this index to
a variety of negative behaviors, many of which reflect CWB. As expected, reliability related
negatively with CWBs and their consequences, such as discharges from work and being
counseled for aberrant behavior (see also Hogan & Hogan, 1995).
More specific affective traits, such as trait anger and anxiety, have also been shown to be
related to CWB. Fox and Spector (1999) found that trait anger correlated significantly with
both organization (r=.50) and person (r=.56) CWB. Trait anxiety correlated somewhat lower,
.40 and .20, for organization and person, respectively. Fox et al. (2001) found that trait anger
related to several forms of CWB with stronger relations with abusive behavior, work
avoidance, and work sabotage, than for overt acts and threats. Relations with trait anxiety
tended to be somewhat smaller, with less variability across the five CWB scales. These
findings are supported by research that ties neuroticism to general antisocial and criminal
behavior (see Collins & Griffin, 1998).
5.4.2. OCB and personality
The personality variable that has been most studied in relation to altruism or prosocial
behavior in general is empathy, which is the ability to comprehend and experience
vicariously another person’s emotions (Salovey & Mayer, 1990). Empathy has been shown
to predict helping behavior in a number of settings (e.g., Davis, 1996; Eisenberg & Miller,
1987; Penner et al., 1997). Penner et al. have developed a measure of what they call the
prosocial personality (see Penner, Craiger, Fritzsche, & Freifeld, 1995). Their inventory
produced two factors: empathy and helpfulness. The latter factor described individuals who
reported a willingness to help, were able to distance themselves emotionally from another in
distress, and felt capable of offering assistance (high self-efficacy and feelings of control).
Allen (1998) found that both subscales of the Penner et al. scale predicted willingness
of college students to serve as mentors for high school students interested in majoring
in psychology.
In the work domain, McNeely and Meglino (1994) showed that empathy was related to
OCB when the target was another person, but not an organization. Thus, the type of OCB
may be critical in determining whether empathy is relevant. Allen (1999) found that the
empathy and helpfulness subscales of the Penner et al. (1995) prosocial personality scale
were correlated with 116 female accounting professionals’ willingness to mentor and past
experience as a mentor. Hogan and Hogan (1989) found that their Employee Reliability Index
related positively to various indicators of performance, some of which may have included
OCB. On the other hand, in their meta-analysis, Organ and Ryan (1995) found quite small
relations of Big Five dimensions of conscientiousness, agreeableness, and emotional stability
(negative affectivity), as well as positive affectivity with OCB. The largest correlation was .16

P.E. Spector, S. Fox / Human Resource Management Review 12 (2002) 269–292

283

with conscientiousness, but when studies using self-ratings of OCB were omitted, the mean r
was only .041.
Personal values are a type of individual difference variable that might be important
contributors to OCB. Collectivism is such a value, in that it reflects an individual’s tendency
to value the welfare of the group more than the welfare of self. Moorman and Blakely (1995)
assessed collectivism and OCB, both with self-reports, in a sample of American financial
service employees. They found a significant association, with collectivists being more likely
to report OCB. Allen (1999) found that female accounting professionals’ collectivism was
associated with willingness to mentor. Of course, these studies needs replication with an
alternative means of assessing OCB other than self-reports.
5.5. Control
A critical element in voluntary behavior is control over the specific work situation or
environmental condition that is causing the emotional response. Again, control works
oppositely for CWB versus OCB. High control increases the likelihood of positive behavior
and reduces the likelihood of negative, and low control has the opposite effects. In fact,
control itself can be an environmental condition that induces emotional reactions, although
the relation can be quite complex. Insufficient control can lead to feelings of anger and
anxiety, and eventually to learned helplessness and associated depression (Seligman, 1975).
Too much control can be overwhelming and can lead to anger and anxiety. However, we do
not presume that too much control, and associated responsibility, is likely to lead to CWB, as
such behavior is typically a reaction to situations of low control.
Emotion can have an impact on control-related variables, such as self-efficacy (Bandura,
1977). Self-efficacy is the belief in one’s capability to perform a particular task, which, in a
sense, is the belief that one has control over task accomplishment. Forgas and Bower (1988)
found that mood affected self-efficacy. Individuals in a positive mood felt more competent,
and individuals in a negative mood felt less competent in cognitive, motor, and social tasks.
5.5.1. CWB and control
Empirical support for the control–CWB link is limited. Fox et al. (2001) found that
perceived autonomy related significantly to organization forms of CWB (work avoidance and
work sabotage), but not person forms. This might be expected since autonomy has to do with
control over the work itself, and thus targets for CWB are the organization rather than
coworkers. Heacox (1996) found the personality variable locus of control (the tendency to
believe one has control) related to aggression against others. Similarly, Perlow and Latham
(1993) showed that locus of control predicted which employees would be terminated for
abusing clients of a residential facility. Both of these studies used non-self-report measures of
the CWB. Finally, Fox and Spector (1999) found that locus of control related to both
organization and person CWB. In all three of these studies, externals (individuals who tended
to believe they had low control) were more likely to engage in CWB. Storms and Spector
(1987) found only small relations of locus of control with CWB with only one of four
dimensions significant, but all were in the direction of externals reporting more. However, they

284

P.E. Spector, S. Fox / Human Resource Management Review 12 (2002) 269–292

found that locus of control served as a moderator of the relation between frustration and CWB.
Externals were more likely to respond to frustration with CWB than were internals. It should be
noted that locus of control has to do with general feelings of control, in contrast to autonomy
that is task specific, and therefore would be expected to relate to a wider range of CWBs.
5.5.2. OCB and control
There is little research addressing the control issue with OCB, and so the connection at this
time is speculative and in need of investigation. However, as noted earlier, Penner et al.
(1995) found that feelings of control and self-efficacy were part of their prosocial personality,
so it seems reasonable to expect that control is relevant to OCB, that is, people only help if
they feel they can be effective.

6. Practical implications of the model
Voluntary behavior is a vital element in organizational functioning. On the positive side, it
has been recognized that OCB is essential for effectiveness (Borman & Motowidlo, 1993), as
formal task performance and assigned duties do not comprise everything required for
successful organizational functioning (such as cooperation, flexibility, initiative, and innovativeness). Furthermore, it has been shown that frequency of OCB in workgroups predict
subsequent objective productivity (Podsakoff, Ahearne, & MacKenzie, 1997). On the negative
side, estimates of costs due to various forms of CWB have been staggering. Greenberg (1997)
cites estimates of US$200 billion per year in employee theft alone just in the US. Clearly, it is
in the best interest of organizations to discourage CWB and encourage OCB.
Our model suggests that a focus on employee emotion may be an effective way to address
voluntary behavior. Organizational policies and practices should be informed by the possible
emotional effects on employees. This is not to say that all negative situations must be
avoided, but rather that attempts to minimize the negatives and maximize the positives are
likely to result in higher OCB and lower CWB. Potential emotional impact should be part of
the decision process, even when extreme actions are taken. For example, too many
organizations seem to maximize the anxiety of downsizing by not considering the emotional
toll on employees. They announce coming reductions months in advance, and then
deliberations are kept secret until implemented. Often, layoffs are carried out in wave after
wave, rather than all at one time. This process serves to induce extreme anxiety that can keep
employees in an emotional state for a year or more. How long will it be after the final wave of
layoffs before employees relax and conclude the storm is over? During this time, we would
expect CWB to increase and OCB to decrease as people lose commitment and look elsewhere
for employment.
As noted by Sauter, Lim, and Murphy (1996), there is a tendency to compartmentalize
employee performance and employee well-being as separate concerns that are in conflict. For
example, very little research on job stress and employee well-being has considered the impact
of job stressors on the organization (Jex, 1998). Most researchers seem to have concluded that
there is not a strong causal link from job satisfaction to job performance, or perhaps not even

P.E. Spector, S. Fox / Human Resource Management Review 12 (2002) 269–292

285

much of an association (e.g., Iaffaldano & Muchinsky, 1985). Indeed, required job
performance might not be strongly affected by well-being, in large part because such
behavior is highly constrained and scrutinized by management. Employees are careful to
appear to do their jobs sufficiently well to avoid management intervention. Furthermore, Jex
(1998) notes that the job stressor–job performance link is extremely complex, with many
possible moderating influences. He notes that this relation is likely mediated by emotional
states, and that relations are likely stronger with voluntary aspects of performance (see a
similar argument by Staw & Barsade, 1993). It is such voluntary behavior that is not under
close management control that is likely to be most associated with well-being. Of course,
constraints in the environment can also inhibit voluntary behavior (e.g., surveillance cameras
in retail establishments inhibit theft), but, often, such constraints change the nature, and
perhaps not the target, of such behavior. Particularly with CWB, individuals will often persist
in finding ways to harm their organizations once they formulate intentions to act, although it
is likely such behaviors will be indirect or passive.
Organizational scholars have begun to recognize that employee and organizational health
and well-being are not antithetical. For example, Jaffee (1995) defines a healthy company as
one that is effective in the traditional sense (e.g., profitable) but at the same time promotes
employee well-being. He provides several examples of how organizations have achieved both
simultaneously. Sauter et al. (1996) discussed characteristics of what they call healthy work
organizations. They argue that both organizational effectiveness and employee well-being
(reflected in reduced job stress) are associated with an emphasis on employee needs (e.g.,
career development), fairness, cooperation, and reduction of conflict.
As we note in our model, poor well-being is associated with negative emotion and mood,
and such affective states are important components in voluntary behavior. Effective organizations need individuals who will respond constructively to situations that make them feel
bad, and who are willing to go beyond the prescribed tasks they are given. Designing the
organization to avoid bad feelings and enhance good feelings, to the extent that is reasonable,
will in the long run produce both employee and organizational well-being. Our model provides
some hints as to where efforts should be directed, specifically by minimizing constraints on
performance, increasing job autonomy, reducing job stressors, establishing fair and just
procedures, and fulfilling the psychological contract. This is not to say that all job stress
must be eliminated, or that every employee must feel good about every action taken. Rather
employee feelings should be one consideration and not necessarily the last consideration.
In the US, organizations have tended to address this problem via employee selection
procedures in an attempt to hire those who are likely to perform OCB (e.g., be team players)
and avoid hiring those who are likely to perform CWB. The Hogan and Hogan (1989)
Employee Reliability Index is a selection device that may do both simultaneously. However,
the selection model will only take us so far. To be effective, we must have a large applicant
pool for each position, a situation that is becoming increasingly rare for all but the lowest
skilled jobs. Furthermore, selection is not an effective substitute for good working conditions,
and even the most empathic employee may restrict OCB in the face of poor, unjust treatment.
Selection procedures can be helpful, but will achieve more if those individuals who are
chosen will have a work environment that encourages and supports their full efforts. Just as

selection represents the beginning for a new employee, it should represent only the first step
in a comprehensive approach to reducing CWB and enhancing OCB.

7. Research directions
Research on voluntary behavior is still in its early stages. Compared to the thousands of
studies available on job performance, far fewer can be found on CWB and OCB. The study of
such behavior is not without significant challenges. In particular, it is difficult to study CWB
that remains carefully hidden from management view, as such behavior can result in
disciplinary action if not arrest.
The OCB studies have provided some insights about variables that are related to it, and
most have used designs that go beyond single source. Most studies of OCB have avoided the
potential mono-method limitation by having OCB assessed by supervisors rather than
subjects themselves. This may help minimize certain biases that might distort correlations
of the OCB measure with other subject-reported variables. However, supervisors are not
necessarily in the best position to know about all the OCB done by their subordinates, and
thus their reports may not be very accurate (see Frese & Zapf, 1988). Additional work is
needed using a variety of methods to assess OCB from a variety of sources, e.g., by surveying
potential recipients of personal OCB. Targeting a single OCB behavior, such as Allen’s
(1998, 1999) focus specifically on mentoring might allow a more in depth look at possible
causes. Furthermore, more longitudinal designs and quasiexperimental studies should be
conducted. For example, the effects of organizational changes on OCB could be investigated
using a pretest–posttest design. Even better would be a design in which affected employees
were compared to counterparts not affected by a change.
CWB studies have, by necessity, been limited in most cases to single-source participant
reports. Since CWB tends to be behavior that is carefully hidden, in many, if not most cases,
only the perpetrator has knowledge of what was done. Lee (1993) suggests that research on
sensitive topics such as this requires a great deal of creativity and may require innovative
methods. He advocates using a multitude of approaches, both qualitative and quantitative.
The existing anonymous survey studies have provided a fairly consistent picture of CWB.
Furthermore, the small number of studies that have used different approaches have tended to
support these single-source studies. For example, Heacox (1996) found that individuals
identified by supervisors as having engaged in CWBs independently rated themselves as
higher on work frustration than individuals not so identified, which is precisely what research
has shown using all incumbent reports.
It goes without saying that researchers need to go beyond cross-sectional, single-source
designs. Two approaches seem feasible. First, Heacox’s methodology could be used by
having coworkers or supervisors identify individuals who have engaged in CWB. Such data
could be tied to participant reports of perceptions and emotion. Second, longitudinal studies
might help show the impact of organizational changes. An assessment of CWB before and
after a change, even if aggregated to a group level as did Greenberg (1990), would be
feasible. It might also be possible to match data across time within participants by having

P.E. Spector, S. Fox / Human Resource Management Review 12 (2002) 269–292

287

them generate their own unique code numbers. This sort of research introduces ethical
concerns that must be part of the design. Confidentiality is of particular concern with the
Heacox methodology. Researchers must include strong safeguards to keep management from
finding out who has been identified as having committed certain acts if coworkers are
surveyed. This sort of research is least risky if done by outside researchers over whom
management has no power.
To fully explore our model, research that looks simultaneously at both CWB and OCB is
needed. This can be done most easily with participant surveys, but studies that go beyond this
methodology are needed. One possibility would be to ask supervisors or others to assess both
CWB and OCB and link such data to participant reports of other variables. An example of
this is Hunt (1996) in which ratings of both types of behavior were taken. Such data might
provide useful insights if related to variables from other sources, such as employees or
organizational records. Organization level data could be explored at the level of work groups,
e.g., branches for a bank.
Research is needed that looks at a wider range of emotions. As noted earlier, OCB studies
have looked mainly at general positive mood, while most CWB studies have looked at anger/
frustration. Furthermore, it would be helpful to explore the possible connections of positive
emotions to CWB and negative emotions to OCB. We might expect positive emotion to inhibit
CWB and negative emotion to inhibit OCB, but empirical support for these links is lacking.

8. Conclusions
In this paper, we have presented an integrative model of voluntary behavior that includes
both counterproductive and prosocial forms. We propose that CWB and OCB are the result of
both employee and organization variables, and that they have many similar causes. This
parallelism makes it useful to think about both as forms of voluntary response to
organizational conditions that have emotional meaning for people. Research conducted so
far has shown that many of the same organizational conditions (or at least their perception)
are associated with CWB and OCB, although in opposite directions. Interestingly, on the
employee side, personality variables tend to be different for the two forms of voluntary
behavior. Affective traits (e.g., trait anger and anxiety) are associated with CWB but not
OCB. Empathy is the main correlate identified to date for OCB. Negative emotions are
associated with CWB, whereas positive emotions are associated with OCB.
Our model recognizes the central role of emotion in organizational behavior, particularly in
areas that are more under employee control. Required job performance is typically highly
constrained and scrutinized, leaving little room for employees to do other than what is
required by their supervisors. CWB is typically hidden and is committed by individuals at
their own discretion. OCB may be less hidden, but since by definition, it is not required,
employees are free to engage in it or not. These are conditions that maximize the possible
relations with emotion and other motivation-related variables.
Our model also recognizes the importance of individual perception and appraisal of
organizational conditions and events. Idiosyncratic interpretation of the environment produces

288

P.E. Spector, S. Fox / Human Resource Management Review 12 (2002) 269–292

variability in employee response. A situation seen as fair by one person might be seen as unfair
by another. Thus, perceptions are not always tightly linked to objective features of jobs. This
means that employee reports will continue to be a vital methodology, as this is the best way to
assess people’s perceptions and other phenomenological experience. However, a crucial
question that needs further research throughout our field concerns how objective aspects of
the job result in particular perceptions, such as fairness or violation of psychological contract.
The advantage of this model is that it integrates research from two different streams of
work, both of which involve forms of voluntary behavior. In so doing, we show that an
approach to managing emotion can be useful for encouraging positive and inhibiting negative
forms of behavior. Our model suggests that the underlying process is similar for CWB and
OCB, although some of the specific variables might differ. We provide a framework that
integrates and organizes existing empirical work concerning CWB and OCB, and that can
help guide future work.
A limitation of our model is that it only addresses emotion-based behavior. Although much
of CWB and OCB may be mediated by emotion, people may also engage in such behaviors for
a variety of instrumental reasons. For example, theft can occur out of need rather than in
response to negative feelings toward the organization. Some forms of CWB can be chosen as a
conscious strategy to achieve some objective, e.g., using intimidation to get someone to do
something. Similarly, OCB may be part of an individual’s self-promotion strategy in pursuit of
career advancement. Nevertheless, existing research shows that emotion is associated with
much voluntary behavior, whether the behavior occurs immediately and reflexively or whether
it results in the decision to act later. From a practical perspective, the management of conditions
that tend to induce a particular emotion can go a long way toward reducing CWB and
enhancing OCB, and thereby improving both employee and organizational well-being.
