The role of fun features in training has yet to be systematically examined from an academic
perspective. The purpose of this paper is to aid academic research and training practice by addressing four important issues. First, we discuss the meaning of fun in the context of workplace
training. Second, we review and critique the existing research on fun features in training. Third,
based on Kahn's (1990) theory of psychological engagement, we propose a conceptual model to
guide research to help increase our understanding of the role of fun features in training. Fourth,
we discuss opportunities for future research as well as practical implications and caveats. Our
intent is to provide a stronger theoretical basis for understanding and researching fun features in
training and to provide more nuanced guidance for training practice.

1. Introduction
“Training has to be fun. Simple as that. To inspire changed behaviors and adoption of new practices, training has to be interesting,
motivating, stimulating, and challenging”.
–– Michael Hackett, Co-Founder, LogiGear Corporation
“Most people would agree that health and safety can be a very dry subject. It can be a very serious business, but you can also have
fun while you're learning about it.”
–– Dianne Grote Adams, Owner and President, Safex Consulting
“Learning should be one of the most joyful things we do, and so I do everything I can to make the experience enjoyable. I make a
lot of use of spontaneous (not canned) humor.”
–– Malcolm Knowles, Training Expert

learning experiences (French, 2017; Hackett, 2017; Hooker, 2015; Knowles, 1996; Marshall, 2013; Morrison, 2014). In a similar vein,
a variety of practitioner oriented articles attest to fun's value with such titles as Training Disguised as Fun & Games (Fehrenbach, 2013),
Making It Fun: Tips for Training Today's Pilots (McMahon, 2009), and Privacy Education and Training—Make It Fun! (Strauss, 2016).
Moreover, a number of books focus on how to incorporate fun into the design and delivery of learning experiences, including The Big

learn more, and they will have more favorable reactions about the experience. This belief motivates many training professionals to try
to make training fun as well as educational in order to enhance the overall quality of the learning experience.
Despite this belief in the value of fun, fun may also be an undesirable feature of training. Some studies have shown that manipulation of training delivery to maximize fun is unrelated to learning, or at best, results in similar learning outcomes as delivery
methods that do not intentionally focus on fun (Bretz & Thompsett, 1992; Sitzmann & Johnson, 2014; Sitzmann, Song, & Wang,

Rieber (1996) has argued that fun is often misconstrued as only appropriate for children and is inappropriate for adults to experience
in the workplace. Further, time at work devoted to training often means less time for completing more pressing job responsibilities.
When fun features are included in a training session some trainees might feel that the session itself is a waste of time, and they instead
may prefer to be back on the job attending to more serious, pressing business. In this respect, rather than engaging trainees, fun
features may have the opposite unintended eﬀect.
We contend that fun features may have value in training, but incorporating them is not necessarily straightforward and should be
judiciously employed. Fun features have received some academic attention, but the existing body of research is relatively small. Our
understanding of how fun training design inﬂuences learning outcomes is limited. The overall goal of this paper is to provide a
stronger theoretical basis for understanding and researching fun in the context of workplace training and to provide more nuanced
guidance for applied practice. Although fun features may have value because they lead to positive trainee reactions, they may also
lead to instrumental gains, such as greater knowledge and skill acquisition, if properly incorporated. To help advance research and
practice on fun features and training, the purpose of this paper is four-fold: 1) clarify what constitutes fun in a training context; 2)
review previous research on fun features in training; 3) provide a theoretically-based conceptual model of fun and training eﬀectiveness; and 4) identify several areas for future research.
2. What is fun?
When studying fun in the context of training, it is important to deﬁne what constitutes fun. The term fun is often used in everyday
vernacular, and individuals may instinctively know what is meant by fun. However, is important to delineate diﬀerent perspectives
for conceptualizing fun to provide the necessary construct clarity to guide research endeavors. In the existing body of workplace
research that is not training speciﬁc, the dominant way of conceptualizing fun is as environmental factors or features of the work
environment of social, playful, and amusing nature (e.g., Fluegge, 2008; Karl, Peluchette, & Hall, 2008; Karl, Peluchette, & Harland,
2007; McDowell, 2004; Tews, Michel, & Allen, 2014). Researchers who conceptualize fun as a feature of the work environment have
focused on diﬀerent levels of speciﬁcity. For example, Karl et al. (2007) refers to a global holistic evaluation of whether a workplace
provides enjoyment, amusement, or pleasure as experienced fun. Other researchers have focused on speciﬁc features of the work
environment that potentially lead to an individual's internal state of fun. From this perspective, Fluegge (2008) deﬁnes fun as “any
social, interpersonal, or task activities at work of a playful or humorous nature” (p. 15). Similarly, Ford, McLaughlin, and Newstrom
(2003) characterize fun as a “work environment that intentionally encourages, initiates, and supports a variety of enjoyable and
pleasurable activities” (p. 22). Facets of fun include such elements as fun activities, coworker socializing, manager support for fun,
personal freedoms, and fun job responsibilities (e.g., Fluegge, 2008; Tews et al., 2014; Tews, Michel, Xu, & Drost, 2015).
Drawing from the general workplace fun literature that primarily examines fun features of the work environment, we will focus
on fun features of the training environment in the review that follows. Fun features of the training environment refer to speciﬁc
design and delivery elements intended to provide enjoyment, amusement, or pleasure as a part of the learning experience. Previous
research has demonstrated that fun features of the training environment reﬂect design and delivery elements that are social, entertaining, humorous, or playful in nature (Tews, Jackson, Ramsay, & Michel, 2015). Tews et al. demonstrated that fun in learning
environments includes two broad dimensions—fun activities and fun delivery. Fun activities encompass a variety of hands-on exercises
and ways to promote social involvement among trainees, such as games, competitions, playing of music, and sharing of food. Fun
delivery is more instructor-focused involving the use of humor, creative examples, and storytelling.
It is important to acknowledge that another way of thinking about fun is the experience of positive aﬀect, a state of “high energy,
full concentration, and pleasurable engagement” (Watson, Clark, & Tellegen, 1988: 1063). This perspective is likened to “having fun.”
In this vein, Pryor, Singleton, Taneja, and Humphreys (2010) characterize fun as “the pleasure you experience while you are involved
in some action such as doing something, seeing something, or even relaxing” (p. 294). Notwithstanding Pryor et al.'s (2010) deﬁnition, the extant body of fun research does not characterize positive aﬀect as a dimension of fun, but rather as an outcome of fun
features of the work environment (e.g., Fluegge, 2008). We acknowledge the importance of positive aﬀect in training eﬀectiveness,
which we include in our conceptual model discussed later in the paper. We do so because positive aﬀect helps determine the extent to
which fun features of the training environment are in fact enjoyable.

3.1. Instructor-led training
Instructor-led classroom training remains the most popular training method. Training's annual survey shows that instructor-led
classroom training accounts for 41% of the total training hours delivered by organizations compared to 30% for online training and
less than 5% for social learning and mobile learning (Training Industry Report, 2016). Despite the popularity of face-to-face instruction, we still have much to learn about the delivery style that trainers should use to enhance learning, including how to harness
fun to create an eﬀective learning environment.
We do know that trainer expressiveness is important. Enthusiastic, friendly, and expressive instructors have been shown to have a
positive inﬂuence on learning (Towler & Dipboye, 2001). Trainer expressiveness can be considered a fun feature of the training
environment because it promotes enjoyment, amusement, or pleasure as a part of the learning process. Supporting the importance of
trainer expressiveness, Rangel et al. (2015) found that expressive and stimulating delivery style was positively related to high levels
of engagement and transfer intentions. Rangel et al. also found that trainees' preferred learning style had an important inﬂuence on
the relationship between trainer expressiveness and outcomes. Trainees who preferred an experiential approach reported higher
transfer intentions when content was delivered in an expressive and experiential manner. Furthermore, Sitzmann, Brown, Casper,
Ely, and Zimmerman (2008) demonstrated that instructional style (which included humor) and human interaction (interaction with
the instructor and other trainees) were the best predictors of trainee reactions.
Powell and Andresen (1985) suggest that instructor humor is valuable for many reasons in a learning environment. Powell and
Andresen argue that humor creates a positive classroom environment, encourages learner involvement, holds learners' attention,
fosters cognitive development, and enhances the quality of instructors and students' lives. Based on their narrative review of empirical research, they concluded that humor helps increase interest, illustrates material, and reinforces learning content. Similarly, an
instructor's use of humor has been found to reduce anxiety and enhance learning and creativity (Browne, 2013; Pomerantz & Bell,
2011).
Based on the work of Martin, Puhlik-Doris, Larsen, Gray, and Weir (2003), Romero and Cruthirds (2006) describe diﬀerent humor
styles that individuals deploy in organizations to varying degrees. These ﬁve styles include aﬃliative, self-enhancing, aggressive, mild
aggressive, and self-defeating humor. Aﬃliative humor focuses on enhancing group cohesiveness, which includes inside jokes and nonhostile practical jokes. Self-enhancing humor is focused on enhancing one's own image relative to others. Aggressive humor relates to
belittling others to make the initiator feel better at another's expense, typically leading to negative outcomes. Mild aggressive humor
involves gentle teasing and humorous reprimands and may potentially lead to positive outcomes. Lastly, self-defeating humor involves
ridiculing oneself to help alleviative status diﬀerences. This framework highlights that not all humor is the same. Humor can be used
to achieve many objectives, and research should examine how trainers' use of diﬀerent humor styles inﬂuences psychological engagement and learning.
Bretz and Thompsett (1992) compared trainee reactions and learning across two delivery methods (traditional lecture-based and
integrative learning) and a no-training comparison group using a Solomon four-group design. The integrative learning approach
included games such as Charades, stories, poetry, and background music intended to create a relaxing and fun learning environment
and facilitate positive aﬀect. Trainee reactions in the integrative learning condition were signiﬁcantly greater than those who experienced the traditional lecture-based delivery approach. Trainees who experienced the traditional lecture-based course and those
who experienced integrative learning were found to learn more than the comparison group. However, learning was not signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent for trainees who experienced integrative learning compared to those who experienced the traditional lecture-based delivery
method.
Tews et al. (2015) examined how instructors' use of fun was related to learner engagement among undergraduate students. They
found that instructors' use of fun could be characterized by two dimensions: fun activities (e.g., playing music, games, and group
competitions) and fun delivery methods (e.g., storytelling and humor). They assessed how instructors' use of fun activities and fun
delivery methods, along with peer socializing and instructor praise, were related to cognitive, emotional, and physical engagement.
Cognitive engagement refers to intensity of attention, concentration, and focus. Emotional engagement refers to an aﬀective state
characterized by energy and positive emotions when involved in an activity. Physical engagement refers to active participation and
the exertion of energy and eﬀort (Rich, LePine, & Crawford, 2010). Fun delivery was found to be positively related to each dimension
of engagement. However, fun activities were not. Fun delivery exhibited the strongest eﬀect with emotional engagement, followed by
cognitive engagement, and physical engagement. Thus, fun delivery appears to most strongly impact aﬀective outcomes.

3.2. Online learning and games
In the fun and training research domain, research on online learning, speciﬁcally games, has received the most attention. A
number of terms have been used for games including serious games, games for learning, and simulation games, among others. Such
games integrate elements of recreation and play to teach knowledge, skills, and behaviors.
Sitzmann (2011) conducted the one meta-analysis focused speciﬁcally on games in an adult learning context. This meta-analysis,
based on 65 studies, examined the eﬀectiveness of computer-based simulation games relative to a comparison group. Sitzmann's
results demonstrated that trainees taught with simulation games exhibited higher post-training self-eﬃcacy, declarative knowledge,
procedural knowledge, and retention. This meta-analysis did not directly address the role of fun, but it did demonstrate that activity
level (e.g., practice and engaging with the game), but not entertainment value, signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced learning. Trainees learned less
from simulation games than the comparison group when the comparison group received engaging instruction as a substitute for the
simulation game. These ﬁndings highlight that games are most eﬀective when they focus on active participation and that they are not
necessarily an equivalent substitute for live instruction. In addition, these results suggest that fun may not be necessary for learning to
occur from participating in games.
Lopes, Fialho, Cunha, and Niveiros (2013) conducted a review of computerized games used for leadership development. The
review focused on games where learners interacted with a simulator to make decisions and perform actions that lead to diﬀerent
outcomes. Lopes et al. argued that designers of business games should make more concerted eﬀorts to describe the theoretical basis
for the relationship between game play and practice. Furthermore, they highlighted that experiential activities using business games
for leadership development do not necessarily lead to greater learning. Lopes et al. noted that only a few studies actually focused on
the results achieved by participants through pre-test and post-test evaluations and stress the need for future evaluation eﬀorts.
Connolly, Boyle, MacArthur, Hainey, and Boyle (2012) conducted a narrative review of the impact of computer games and serious
games on learning and engagement. Connolly et al. concluded that players appear to enjoy digital games and that future research
should examine their motivational features. However, the eﬀectiveness of digital games in enhancing knowledge acquisition was not
strong because those who participated in the games did not learn more than those in a control group. When game-based learning was
integrated with other learning methods, knowledge acquisition was enhanced. The ﬁndings and conclusions of Connolly et al. (2012)
suggest that games alone may not enhance learning. Rather, games likely have a positive inﬂuence on learning when they are
integrated with other training methods.
Pe-Than, Goh, and Lee (2014) examined how motivational needs contribute to enjoyment when participating in human computational games (HCGs). HCGs are entertainment-oriented applications that include embedded computational tasks. HCGs have
been used in diﬀerent domain areas including collecting photos of real-world locations to construct 3-D models (Photo City), generating photos and texts of geographic locations for map navigation (EyeSpy), and collecting Facebook users' opinions on songs (Herd
It). Pe-Than et al. demonstrated that learners may not universally perceive games as fun. Drawing on the work of Deci and Ryan
(2000), Pe-Than et al. found that learners' enjoyment depends on the extent to which games fulﬁll learners' autonomy, competence,
and relatedness needs. Furthermore, games inﬂuenced enjoyment when they allowed users to perform activities they desired, facilitated social belongingness, and matched users' skill levels.
Two studies by Sitzmann and her colleagues investigated the role of fun and aﬀect in course satisfaction and learning in online
courses. Sitzmann and Johnson (2014) highlight the complexities of fun in training. Sitzmann and Johnson found that some fun
features (e.g., jokes, stories, and video clips) did not improve trainee reactions or directly aﬀect knowledge acquisition. However,
they were found to alleviate negative aﬀect, which in turn facilitated learning, but caused trainees to reduce time on the learning task
itself. Sitzmann et al. (2017) conducted four experiments using a within-subjects design to examine the relationship between course
satisfaction and learning. The studies included manipulations of diﬃculty of performance assessment, feedback, and instructor engagement. Overall, their results suggest that eﬀorts to design training activities to enhance satisfaction, and in turn learning, may be
unwarranted. Speciﬁcally, they found that course satisfaction did not have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on learning. Regardless of how
much they learned, students were more satisﬁed after receiving favorable than unfavorable performance feedback. Instructor engagement (which included making humorous remarks while discussing important course content) did have a positive relationship
with course satisfaction, but not learning. Furthermore, they found limited support for the relationship between positive aﬀect and
course satisfaction and no support for the relationship between positive aﬀect and learning. High positive aﬀect was related to course
satisfaction, and low negative aﬀect was related to course satisfaction.
3.3. Experiential learning
Experiential learning is one type of training method that often incorporates play and emphasizes fun. Experiential learning can be
broadly considered learning through reﬂection based on doing or engaging in experiences. Research on the role of fun in experiential
learning has considered how fun might enhance learning. This includes experiential learning such as outdoor adventure training that
is physically demanding and challenging (e.g. climbing and ropes courses) and less physically strenuous programs that emphasize
coordination, delegation, problem solving, and communication among team members (e.g., cooking courses).
Ewert (1989) argued that it is important to create conditions to promote high levels of engagement for experiential learning to be

eﬀective, and fun serves to engage trainees. One of the challenges of experiential learning is that learners tend to focus on the
challenge and fun they experience while participating, and as a result, they fail to see that the experiences they have during training
are metaphors for speciﬁc team and organizational challenges. That is, experiential learning involves far transfer in which training
exercises serve as metaphors rather than replications of the problems and issues faced in the work environment (Baldwin & Ford,
1988). Another challenge is that trainees may perceive experiential learning as a frivolous and childish endeavor and fail to fully
engage in the activity. For learning and transfer to occur in experiential learning, it is imperative to show its relevance beyond being a
fun activity.
Kolb and Kolb (2010) emphasized that a dedicated learning space for play is necessary for deep learning to result from experiential learning. They introduce the concept of ludic learning space, which refers to a space where play can thrive. The ludic learning
space is rooted in ethology, anthropology, psychology, and philosophy (e.g., Gadamer, 1992; Huizinga, 1950; Nachmanovitch, 1990).
The underlying concept is that play needs to occur in a space that is free from social pressure, structure, and evaluation. For play to
result in deep learning, the process should encourage learners to take ownership of their learning based on personal standards of
excellence, allow for play over time to allow for new perspectives and insights, and be valued similarly. Based on a case study of a
loosely organized softball league over ﬁfteen years, Kolb and Kolb conclude that deep learning occurs if learners have a space in
which they can self-organize, establish boundaries for recursive play free of time boundaries, and can pursue their intrinsic interests
while being themselves.
Kark (2011) presents a conceptual framework for understanding how fun is initiated using play as a design feature in outdoor
adventure training and strategy games. Similar to Kolb and Kolb (2010), Kark emphasizes the importance of a ludic play space. Play
frees managers to consider new possibilities, experiment and develop new skills, or alter their self-image free from the expectation
that they are required to change. Kark provides several testable propositions about the role of play and skill development and the
conditions that aﬀect this relationship. Consistent with the notion of ludic play space, contexts with high psychological safety are
expected to enhance the eﬀects of play on leadership development. Kark cautions that experiential learning activities that incorporate
play need to include reﬂection to increase awareness and meaning of what was learned.
Addressing the learning and transfer of training challenges inherent in outdoor adventure training, Meyer (2003) emphasizes that
four “territories of experience” need to be included to help trainees focus on learning and transfer and not merely on having fun.
These four territories of experience help learners realize incongruence between their espoused and actual behavior, which leads to
the felt need to learn and subsequently apply what was learned back in the work environment. Speciﬁcally, learners need to be
physically and mentally engaged in experiential learning (performing); be involved in a debrieﬁng immediately afterward focused on
how the experience was a metaphor for a workplace issue (assessing); reﬂect on what was learned and the implications for the
workplace (visioning); and complete action plans to articulate how new knowledge and skills can be applied (strategizing). Using case
studies of outdoor-adventure learning, Meyer (2003) illustrates how the four territories of experience can be applied in training
practice. Meyer (2003) highlights that outdoor adventure training as well as other types of experiential learning involves much more
than designing fun into the focal activity itself. Implementing the “four territories of experience” into experiential learning requires
careful thought, attention, and the allocation of suﬃcient time to address these issues.
3.4. Conclusions
Several conclusions can be drawn from our narrative review on fun features of the training environment. First, compared to other
areas of training research, far fewer studies have investigated how fun features of the training environment inﬂuence learning
outcomes (for example, over one hundred ﬁfty studies have been conducted on training motivation). Second, there are mixed ﬁndings
on fun's relationship with trainees' experiences and training outcomes, although trainee characteristics and the combination of
training methods used likely aﬀect these relationships. Third, researchers have not adopted a common theoretical basis for understanding fun training design and the area lacks a conceptual framework to motivate future studies. The lack of research, mixed results
of studies that have been conducted, and the lack of a common theory- based conceptual framework for studying fun in training have
all contributed to our lack of understanding of why and how fun features of the training environment inﬂuence learning outcomes. In
the next section of the paper we discuss how Kahn's (1990) theory of psychological engagement can help us understand how fun
inﬂuences training eﬀectiveness. Following this discussion, to motivate future research on fun in training we present a conceptual
model grounded in Kahn's theory that describes the mechanisms through which fun inﬂuences knowledge and skill acquisition.
4. Psychological engagement as a theoretical foundation for understanding the relationship between fun and training
eﬀectiveness
In his seminal work, Kahn (1990) describes psychological engagement as “the harnessing of organization members' selves to their
work roles; in engagement, people express themselves physically, cognitively, and emotionally during role performances” (p. 964).
By extension, training engagement can be characterized as the harnessing of trainees' selves in learning endeavors where they
immerse themselves physically, cognitively, and emotionally. Kahn articulates three psychological conditions that are needed to
promote engagement—meaningfulness, safety, and availability. Meaningfulness refers to a sense that one will receive a return on
investment for his or her eﬀort. Safety refers to being able to express one's self without fear of negative repercussions. Availability
refers to possessing the resources to invest in on-the-job activities. Noe, Tews, and Dachner (2010) drew on Kahn's work to describe
how these three conditions of engagement are important in learning contexts. Meaningfulness motivates individuals to exert eﬀort
toward learning knowledge and skills. Safety helps overcome individuals' fear of failure and feels of anxiety that can inhibit learning.

We argue that fun could enhance each of these conditions of engagement. Fun may increase positive aﬀect among trainees, and

bonds among trainees. These social bonds help learners feel more comfortable with one another. As a result, they are able to have

instill availability by energizing trainees. By increasing positive aﬀect in general, fun could energize trainees. In addition, when fun
involves time oﬀ task from the focal leaning task, fun provides a break to reenergize individuals for further learning. In the conceptual
model of fun and training eﬀectiveness presented below, Kahn's conditions of psychological engagement serve a central role, building
on these arguments presented above.

5. A conceptual model of fun and training eﬀectiveness
Our conceptual model is shown in Fig. 1. Fun features of the training environment are depicted as inﬂuencing course satisfaction
and cognitive and skill-based outcomes, but their inﬂuence is not direct. That is, fun features of the training environment will impact
positive aﬀect and psychological engagement, which will then inﬂuence motivation to learn, a well-established precursor to course
satisfaction and cognitive and skill-based outcomes (Bauer, Orvis, Ely, & Surface, 2016; Colquitt, LePine, & Noe, 2000; Noe, 1986). A
feedback loop is also presented in the model, emphasizing that the attainment of cognitive and skill-based outcomes results in greater
positive aﬀect (Klug & Maier, 2015). Furthermore, the model depicts trainee characteristics (generational cohort, the Big Five
personality dimensions, and goal orientation), the match between trainee skills and learning demands, and organizational climate
and culture as moderators in the relationship between fun and positive aﬀect and psychological engagement. Such variables likely
inﬂuence the extent to which trainees value and are receptive to fun in a learning context. Including these variables may help us
better understand the mixed results previous found in research on the relationship between fun and learning outcomes.

5.1. Fun training features and positive aﬀect
As the model shows, when training incorporates fun design and delivery elements, trainees are more apt to experience positive
aﬀect. According to aﬀective events theory (Weiss & Cropanzano, 1996), transitory or ongoing favorable events tend to elicit positive
emotional reactions; whereas, unfavorable work events elicit negative emotional reactions. To the extent that features of the training
environment are perceived as entertaining, humorous, or playful in nature, trainees are more apt to experience positive aﬀect.
Supporting the fun-positive aﬀect relationship, research on multimedia learning environments has shown that positive aﬀect can be
induced through incorporating design principles that elicit positive emotions (Um, Plass, Hayward, & Homer, 2012). Regardless of
whether a fun event during training is highly signiﬁcant or less noteworthy, in general the more positive events experienced, the
higher levels of positive emotion the person should feel (Conway & Briner, 2002). Not all positive emotions are necessarily germane
in the context of fun research. Rather, it is those emotions with higher levels of activation (e.g., happiness, pleasure, excitement) as
opposed to lower levels of activation (e.g., contentment and serenity) (Posner, Russell, & Peterson, 2005). It is important to highlight,
as suggested by Roseman, Spindel, and Jose (1990), that the appraisal of an event, as opposed to the event itself, determines the aﬀect
that is experienced. Some trainees may perceive a group teambuilding activity or game as enjoyable. Such an activity could be
anxiety producing for others. The moderators discussed later will help illuminate when fun leads to greater positive emotions.

5.2. Positive aﬀect and course satisfaction
Aﬀective events theory (Weiss & Cropanzano, 1996) distinguishes between aﬀect and satisfaction, yet acknowledges that aﬀect
inﬂuences satisfaction. Research supports the relationship between positive aﬀect and satisfaction (e.g., Erez & Isen, 2002). When
individuals experience greater levels of positive emotion, they are more likely to view an event as a positive experience. In a training
context, fun may be an eﬀective means to promote course satisfaction, and we suggest that this relationship is mediated through
positive aﬀect. This proposition is supported by research on trainee reactions that has found that when trainees experience greater
levels of happiness, pleasure, and enjoyment from features of the training, they should be more satisﬁed with the training program
itself (Brown, 2005; Harman, Ellington, Surface, & Thompson, 2015).
5.3. Positive aﬀect, psychological engagement, and motivation to learn
The model proposes that positive aﬀect resulting from fun features of training, in turn, impacts engagement, motivation to learn,
and subsequent course satisfaction and cognitive and skill-based outcomes. These relationships are supported by research on trainee
reactions discussed earlier (e.g., Brown, 2005; Sitzmann et al., 2008), training motivation (Colquitt et al., 2000), and studies directly
examining positive aﬀect. For example, positive aﬀect has been found to be related to motivation to learn and transfer of trained skills
(Machin & Fogarty, 2004; Naquin & Holton, 2002; Venkatesh & Speier, 1999). Studies have also shown that positive aﬀect can have a
positive relationship with attention, cognitive processing, and retrieval of information from long-term memory, both of which result
in enhanced learning (e.g., Brown, 2005; Isen, Daubman, & Nowicki, 1987; Isen, Johnson, Mertz, & Robinson, 1985). Furthermore,
some research suggests that learning is associated with pleasant aﬀect, but not anxious aﬀect (Daniels, Boocock, Glover, Hartley, &
Holland, 2009), supporting the importance of aﬀect in training contexts.
Kahn's (1990) conditions of engagement (meaningfulness, safety, and availability) are additional focal variables in the model. We
argue that fun features of the training environment could enhance each of these conditions of engagement, both directly and indirectly through positive aﬀect. Fun features of the training environment likely enhance meaningfulness because they facilitate ﬂow
(deep absorption in an activity), whereby individuals engage in behaviors because they are intrinsically motivating
(Csikszentmihalyi, 1996). One of the means through which ﬂow is derived is from activities that provide enjoyment and interest, and
fun is intended to absorb individuals in the learning experience. Flow likely heightens positive aﬀect among trainees, and such mood
enhancement could increase the meaningfulness trainees place on training content. Previous research has consistently demonstrated
that positive aﬀect helps individuals to view opportunities more favorably (Lyubomirsky, King, & Diener, 2005). Fun features of the
training environment may promote safety by making the learning environment less threatening. When fun involves social interactions and teambuilding activities, they could enhance safety by increasing social bonds among trainees (see Kolb & Kolb, 2010). These
social bonds help learners feel more comfortable with one another. Furthermore, fun features of the training environment likely
enhance safety by increasing positive aﬀect. To the extent that fun is playful and relaxed, trainees' anxiety may be reduced, and as a
result, they likely experience more positive aﬀect and become less concerned about protecting their self-images. Lastly, fun may
instill availability by energizing trainees. By increasing positive aﬀect in general, fun could energize trainees. Learning may deplete
individuals' psychological resources due to the inherent diﬃculties in acquiring new knowledge and skills. Fun, however, may make
learning more enjoyable, interesting, and active. For example, Brown (2005) found enjoyment of a lecture and perceptions that the
material was fun were positively related to their attention to the material presented and intent to use it. In addition, when fun
involves time oﬀ from the focal learning task, fun may provide a break to reenergize individuals for further learning.
5.4. Cognitive and skill-based outcomes and positive aﬀect
The model proposes a feedback loop between cognitive and skill-based outcomes and positive aﬀect. That is, trainees success in
obtaining new insights, knowledge, and skills at any time during training boosts their positive aﬀect, which further enhances their
motivation to learn and learning for the remainder of the program. This feedback loop is supported by studies demonstrating that
successful goal pursuit is intrinsically rewarding and enhances feelings of well-being, especially when outcomes are personally
relevant (Csikszentmihalyi, 1996; Deci & Ryan, 2000; Klug & Maier, 2015).
5.5. Trainee characteristics, skills-learning demands match, climate, and culture as moderators
Training practitioners have suggested that to maximize training eﬀectiveness it must be designed considering generational
preferences about how they want to learn (see Gale, 2015; Sharma, 2016). The workforce includes ﬁve diﬀerent generations each of
which have been characterized as having similarities as well as diﬀerences based on how they were parented and the events occurring
around them (e.g., Lyons & Kuron, 2013). In particular, fun may be especially important for Millennials, those born between 1981
and 1995. The greater importance of fun for Millennials may be rooted in their biological age, as Millennials are currently younger
individuals who typically value fun more strongly than older individuals do. Furthermore, fun is thought to be more important for
Millennials than other generational cohorts because of their relatively carefree upbringing (Alsop, 2008). Millennials grew up in a
period of economic prosperity, their parents were very attentive to their needs, and extracurricular involvement was central to their
upbringing. Furthermore, Millennials are often characterized as valuing social involvement and balancing play with work (Carless &
Wintle, 2007; Cennamo & Gardner, 2008). Finally, fun in training might also be important among Millennials given their reliance on
playing games for enjoyment (Alsop, 2008; Tews et al., 2015).

The Big Five personality dimensions, which include agreeableness, openness to experience, extraversion, emotional stability, and
conscientiousness (Barrick & Mount, 1991), are additional trainee characteristics that likely inﬂuence the extent to which fun
matters. Previous research has demonstrated that these variables are important in learning contexts, inﬂuencing decisions to participate in learning and motivation to learn (e.g. Barrick & Mount, 1991; Colquitt et al., 2000; Major, Turner, & Fletcher, 2006; Orvis
& Leﬄer, 2011). Because trainees with high levels of agreeableness are more friendly and cooperative (Barrick & Mount, 1991), they
will likely be more amenable to fun than their more antagonistic counterparts will. Trainees who are high in openness likely are more
receptive to fun because such individuals are more broadminded, creative, and curious (Barrick & Mount, 1991). Trainees with high
level of extraversion are sociable, gregarious, assertive, ambitious, and active (Gough, 1987). As a result, extraverts are likely to be
more psychologically engaged and experience more positive aﬀect compared to trainees who are more introverted, particularly when
fun involves social interactions with others. Similarly, because fun activities often create a sense of disorder and vulnerability, we
would expect that trainees who have high levels of emotional stability would be more receptive to fun because such individuals are
more secure and conﬁdent (Costa & McCrae, 1985).
Conscientiousness has the most consistent and strongest relationship of the Big Five with motivation to learn and knowledge
acquisition (Colquitt et al., 2000). However, incorporating fun may not always be judicious when trainees are high in conscientiousness. Because fun features of training can make it appear to lack structure and order, fun may not always be valued by those
high in conscientiousness, who are typically highly self-disciplined, cautious, critical, and organized (Barrick & Mount, 1991; Hogan
& Ones, 1997). Trainees high in conscientiousness likely will ﬁnd the lack of clear directions and rules that are often part of fun
design and delivery to be undesirable and confusing, leading to less positive aﬀect and psychological engagement (Gully, Payne,
Koles, & Whiteman, 2002). Furthermore, their critical nature suggests that they might view fun training design features as extraneous
and believe they distract them from understanding and attaining key learning points and objectives.
The importance of the Big Five in the context of fun also draws on research on personal need for structure, deﬁned as the tendency
to prefer clarity and order, and to avoid uncertain or ambiguous situations (Neuberg & Newsome, 1993). Those with a high need for
structure tend to be higher in conscientiousness, less open to experience, and have lower emotional stability (Neuberg & Newsome,
1993). Pundt and Venz (2017) found that the positive eﬀects of leaders' humor on followers' commitment was stronger for followers
who were low in need for structure. Furthermore, leaders' humor had a negative relationship with followers' disengagement for those
low in need for structure, whereby humor resulted in lower levels of disengagement for followers low in need for structure. Although
Pundt and Venz (2017) research examined humor in leadership, their results suggest that conditions that create informality in a
situation, like fun features of the training environment, may be demotivating for individuals who have a high need for structure and
value personal control.
Trainees' goal orientation, how individuals interpret and respond to achievement situations (Dweck & Leggett, 1988), is another
moderator that should be considered because of its importance inﬂuence in learning contexts (Payne, Youngcourt, & Beaubien,
2007). Goal orientation can be separated into learning goal orientation and performance goal orientation (Dweck, 1986; Dweck &
Leggett, 1988). Learning goal orientation refers to a preference to grow and develop one's competence by acquiring new skills and
mastering new situations. A performance orientation reﬂects a desire to demonstrate competence and be positively evaluated by
others, which can be further divided into prove and avoid dimensions (Elliott & Harackiewicz, 1996; VandeWalle, 1997). A prove
goal orientation focuses on demonstrating competence; whereas an avoid goal orientation focuses on not revealing incompetence and
avoiding negative ability judgments from others.
Learning goal orientation likely inﬂuences the extent to which fun matters. Trainees with a high learning goal orientation are
inherently more interested in acquiring skills and knowledge in training programs than individuals with low levels of learning goal
orientation. Because training design and delivery that incorporates fun further enhances the joy and pleasure of learning, trainees
with a high level of learning goal orientation should respond more favorably to the learning experience. Similarly, trainees with a
high prove goal orientation should also experience enhanced positive aﬀect and psychological engagement because of fun. Fun design
elements involving social interaction and competition give individuals with a high prove goal orientation the opportunity to acquire
knowledge and skill that meets their desire to show their competence and gain approval from others. Conversely, individuals with a
high avoid goal orientation should react unfavorably to fun. Fun training features often include social interaction and introduce
ambiguity into learning which gives these individuals the unwanted opportunity to potentially reveal a lack of competence. For
example, asking others for assistance and trying to identify novel solutions for challenging problems or game scenarios likely will be
perceived as threatening by individuals with a high avoid orientation because they present opportunities for failure, revealing skill
and knowledge weaknesses to others.
Fun may help to enhance the meaningfulness of training because it enhances trainees' interest and intrinsic motivation which are
precursors to experiencing ﬂow. However, the extent to which trainees experience optimal ﬂow depends on the match between their
skills and the demands of the situation (Admiraal, Huizenga, Akkerman, & Dam, 2011; Csikszentmihalyi, 1997). In a training context,
fun may have its largest inﬂuence on engagement for trainees who perceive a match between their ability to learn and the challenges
inherent in the learning context. When there is such a match, trainees may experience less anxiety and be more receptive to fun. Also,
fun could be important when trainees experienced boredom, that is, when they have the ability to learn, but feel the training content
is not inherently interesting. For bored trainees fun can enhance their interest and enjoyment in learning. Conversely, for apathetic
trainees who lack the ability to learn and are disinterested in the training content, fun may be perceived as a distraction. For these
trainees, at best fun likely has no inﬂuence, and at worst, a negative inﬂuence on engagement.
Climate and culture, factors outside of the formal training context, are also included in the model because they inﬂuence motivation to learn and learning outcomes (Tracey, Tannenbaum, & Kavanagh, 1995; Tracey & Tews, 2005). According to Schneider,
Gonzalez-Roma, Ostroﬀ, and Est (2017), climate refers to employee perceptions based on experiences with an organization's policies

and practices; culture is considered the shared values and assumptions that underlie an organization's actions and its focus. We
suggest that a speciﬁc type of climate (psychological safety) is important for understanding the relationship between fun features of
training and psychological engagement. A climate of psychological safety is characterized by trust, respect, concern for others, and
conﬁdence in members' abilities (e.g., Edmondson, 1996, 1999). Teams research shows that psychological safety can mitigate team
members' fears of rejection or disapproval when speaking up, helping, or engaging in other social interactions in which there is the
potential to be judged by other team members or made to feel awkward (e.g., Kozlowski & Ilgen, 2006). The inﬂuence of fun features
of training, especially those that involve social interaction, on positive aﬀect and psychological engagement may be stronger in a
climate of high psychological safety.
Finally, we believe that it is important to consider the moderating role of the “tightness” or “looseness” of an organization's
culture. Drawing from cross-cultural research (Gelfand, Nishii, & Raver, 2006), organizational cultures that are “tight” have strong
norms and low tolerance for deviant behavior, while “loose” cultures have weak norms and high tolerance of deviant behavior. We
expect that in organizations where trainees perceive that humor and fun at work are at a minimum tolerated and perhaps even
encouraged and valued, the inﬂuence of fun features of training on trainees' positive aﬀect and psychological engagement will likely
be stronger than in organizations perceived to have a “tight” culture in which fun is not openly encouraged and where work rules and
spending time on task are valued.
6. Opportunities for fun and training research
The conceptual model shown in Fig. 1 represents a ﬁrst step in providing an individual-level, theoretically based framework to
stimulate research on how fun inﬂuences training outcomes. To build our understanding of the role of fun training design on training
outcomes, researchers need to ﬁrst evaluate the feasibility of the relationships illustrated in the model. Studies using both betweenand within-subjects designs are appropriate for testing the model or the proposed relationships shown. Studies using betweensubjects designs are appropriate for examining whether diﬀerences between trainees in their perceptions of fun training features are
related to diﬀerences in positive aﬀect, motivation to learn, psychological engagement, and learning outcomes. However, withinsubjects designs may be more appropriate because they can consider the role of time (Shipp & Cole, 2015). Between-subjects designs
cannot adequately assess the temporal ordering or precedence in which data on the variables shown in the model should be collected.
Speciﬁcally, trainees perceive fun features of the training environment, which in turn lead to positive aﬀect and heightened conditions of engagement, resulting in motivation to learn and cognitive and skill-based outcomes. Between-subjects designs are inadequate for examining within trainee variability in cognitive and skill-based outcomes that likely occurs as a training course or
program evolves and can be explained by within person diﬀerences in motivation, engagement, and positive aﬀect resulting from fun
features of training.
Next, based on this body of work, researchers should propose alternative models and other potential moderators that incorporate
empirical ﬁndings of individual-level relationships. Adopting a multilevel perspective could also be useful. Future work needs to
consider team and organizational perceptions of climate for psychological safety and culture “tightness” or “looseness.” We believe
that the development of a multilevel model for understanding how fun training design inﬂuences learning outcomes would follow a
similar evolution as our understanding of the complexity of training transfer and training eﬀectiveness (see Kozlowski, Brown,
Weissbein, Cannon-Bowers, & Salas, 2000; Sitzmann & Weinhardt, 2015). Such advances were only made possible because of what
we learned from the results of studies conducted at the individual-level of analysis as well recognizing their limitations.
It is also important to recognize two realities that have implications for research on the relationships shown in the model. One
reality is that trainees' positive aﬀect, engagement, and motivation to learn likely vary during the time they are in a training course or
program. Future research needs to examine not only how fun features inﬂuence learning outcomes over the course of a training
program, but also to examine how and when during training fun features have a potentially negative impact on learning.
Experiencing fun early in a training program may help arouse trainees emotionally, stimulating their engagement and subsequent
motivation to learn and learning. However, the positive inﬂuence of fun on engagement, motivation to learn, and learning might be
eroded over time if it is included across the entirety of a training program. Testing these relationships requires the use of longitudinal
and time series designs including multiple measures of the variables included in our model. Such designs would allow for the use of
latent growth modeling, periodogram, and spectral analysis which can be used to identify the eﬀects of fun features of training on
positive aﬀect, engagement, motivation to learn, and learning outcomes at various points in time (Bliese & Ployhart, 2002; Jebb &
Tay, 2017). Another reality is that the relationship between positive aﬀect and learning outcomes is likely dynamic and reciprocal, as
our model suggests. That is, positive aﬀect can inﬂuence learning, and incremental gains in knowledge and skills can strengthen
trainees' positive aﬀect, which in turn, can further enhance motivation to learn. Future research is needed to examine such dynamic
relationships.
In our conceptual model, we have proposed Kahn's (1990) theory of psychological engagement as a central theory for examining
fun and training. However, other theoretical frameworks suggest alternative mechanisms for understanding fun's inﬂuence on
cognitive and skill-based outcomes are also worth considering. Fredrickson's (2001) broaden-and-build theory of positive emotions
posits that positive emotions broaden a person's momentary thought and action repertoires, which can build intellectual, social,
psychological, and physical resources. Because fun promotes the experience of positive emotions in learning environments, fun may
lead to the development of appropriate resources to facilitate cognitive and skill-based outcomes. Future research could explicitly
assess how fun training features promote positive emotions and the development of diﬀerent resources to facilitate motivation to
learn and desired learning outcomes.
Ryan and Deci's (2000) self-determination theory (SDT) may also be useful for examining the role of fun in training contexts. One

central focus of SDT is that when the three needs of competence, autonomy, and relatedness are satisﬁed, intrinsic motivation is
increased. In a training context, motivation to learn can be enhanced by the degree to which the training design and delivery helps
trainees meet these three needs (Sheldon, Turban, Brown, Barrick, & Judge, 2003). Training scholars are encouraged to replicate and

relatedness and, in turn, cognitive and skill-based outcomes.
Finally, based on a cognitive load perspective Um et al. (2012) present two competing hypotheses concerning the relationship
between fun design and delivery and learning that should be tested in future research. Their emotions as extraneous cognitive load
hypothesis suggests that fun imposes an extraneous cognitive load on learners that impedes cognitive processing, resulting in less
learning. This might be especially the case for trainees in online learning who lack prior knowledge of the training content (Sitzmann
& Johnson, 2014). However, Um et al.'s (2012) emotions as facilitators of learning hypothesis suggests that fun enhances learning by
stimulating positive emotions that enhance cognitive processing, recall, and motivation to learn. The emotions as facilitator hypothesis
is congruent with the emphasis we have on fun in our conceptual model as an antecedent to psychological engagement and positive
aﬀect, which in turn leads to motivation to learn, course satisfaction, and enhanced cognitive and skill-based outcomes.
Research is needed to enhance our understanding and measurement of fun in training design and delivery. Work is needed to
further establish the validity of Tews et al.'s (2015) 13-item, two-dimensional measure of fun activities and fun delivery. This scale
represents the only currently available measure designed to assess multiple elements of fun features of the training environment.
Given that this scale was developed and validated with a focus on undergraduate education, future research should further validate
this scale in employment contexts to assess its generalizability. It would also be worthwhile to consider including items regarding
feedback and results from participating in fun yet challenging activities in addition to the activities themselves. For example, including items related to receiving a fun reward, such as a novel prize or earning the highest score on an outdoor adventure training
challenge, could be useful additions in future scale development eﬀorts. Without valid and reliable scales that can explicitly and
adequately measure fun in learning environments, it is unquestionably diﬃcult to determine the potential value of fun in terms of its
antecedents and consequences for learning and transfer of training. We strongly encourage researchers to invest in developing
psychometrically sound measures.
Fun is a multifaceted construct. Given the mixed results of previous research, additional work is needed to understand not only if
fun facilitates or impedes learning, but how fun inﬂuences learning in diﬀerent types of training and what aspects of fun are responsible. For example, the results of Bretz and Thompsett (1992) and Tews et al. (2015) suggest that at least for instructor-led
training, fun delivery, and using humorous and novel ways to get learners attention may enhance engagement more than fun activities (e.g., games). Studies are needed to determine whether the inﬂuence of fun is similar in instructor-led, online, and experiential
learning. In addition, research that experimentally manipulates fun delivery and fun activities should be conducted to provide a more
robust test of cause-and-eﬀect relationships. Future research needs to more carefully diﬀerentiate the role of diﬀerent types of fun on
engagement, aﬀect, and learning. Furthermore, research is needed to examine fun relative to other forms of support, such as training
climate and trainer or peer support. Examining fun relative to these other aspects of support will serve to validate the incremental
value of fun and enhance our understanding of its nomological network. From a practical perspective, such research would be useful
for providing an evidence-based recommendation to trainers about the value of incorporating fun into training design and delivery.
7. Practical implications and caveats
As most trainers are certainly well aware, getting individuals to engage in the learning process is not necessarily an easy endeavor
and rarely occurs by chance. Rather, engaging learners requires careful planning, thought, and attention. Without question, the
foundation of eﬀective training is focal content grounded in a careful needs assessment, identiﬁcation of learning goals, development
of a training context that facilitates learning, and support for transfer of training. Yet, many practitioners believe that incorporating
fun into learning environments is a viable means to engage trainees. While fun may certainly have value, it is by no means a panacea
or magic bullet. Fun is not a substitute for sound design and delivery. Rather, fun should be incorporated into the design and delivery
of training to support the objectives of the learning experience.
Because we currently have an incomplete picture of the role of fun in training design and delivery, trainers should carefully
consider the role of fun and proceed judiciously. Trainers must ask themselves why are they incorporating fun? For example, trainers
should consider whether fun is intended to support the delivery of focal learning content, enhance learner reactions, promote
bonding, provide a break from learning, create a more relaxed learning environment, or some combination of these. In addition,
trainers should ensure that fun is a complement to the learning experience, rather than the focus itself. Current research suggests that
if trainees become too consumed with fun, they likely pay less attention to achieving cognitive and skill-based outcomes. Trainers
must determine the appropriate level and type of fun for their speciﬁc learning contexts.
Conventional wisdom suggests that fun is desirable from both the trainer and the trainee perspective. Both trainers and learners
may believe that fun training design and delivery leads to enhanced knowledge and skill acquisition. However, the reality is that
research on fun training design and delivery is limited. Fun may facilitate or inhibit learning, and further work is needed to determine
the eﬀects of fun. It is our hope that this paper and our conceptual model will stimulate critical thinking and serve as a springboard
for future research to inform both scholarly understanding and training practice.
