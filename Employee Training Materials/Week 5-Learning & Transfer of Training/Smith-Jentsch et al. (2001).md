Investigating the Gap Between Learning and Transfer

A key proposition within the Baldwin and Ford (1988) transfer
of training model is that work environment variables moderate the
degree to which trainees transfer what they have learned in training. At least two studies have tested this proposition previously
(i.e., Rouiller & Goldstein, 1993; Tracey et al., 1995). However,
neither study found evidence of the expected Learning X Environment interaction. One possible explanation for this, offered by
Tracey et al., was that the measures of learning (i.e., knowledge)
and on-the-job transfer (i.e., behavior) used in their study were not
significantly correlated (i.e., participants with greater knowledge
after training did not demonstrate greater transfer). Thus, the
knowledge measure may have been a deficient indicator of trainees' potential to use the skills learned in training. A more direct
method of assessing posttraining potential might involve rating
trainees' use of desired behaviors during a role-play exercise or
task simulation. This may give a more accurate indication of the
disparity between trainees' ability to apply a newly trained skill
and their actual use of the trained skill on the job.
However, isolating the causal impact of a single work environment variable on the gap between posttraining potential and typical
performance on the job is further complicated by the lack of
experimental control possible in an organizational setting. For
example, Brinkerhoff and Montesino (1995) noted that even
though they randomly assigned supervisors to hold pre- and posttraining discussions with trainees not all of those who were asked
to participate agreed to do so. Supervisors who were willing to try
the experimental intervention may well have been those who also
conveyed support for the trained skill through other means such as
providing greater opportunities for transfer. Thus, it is unclear how
much of the observed group differences in transfer was a result of
the pre- and posttraining discussions themselves and was not a
result of a more global factor of supervisory support. Given this
and other limitations noted by the authors, it was argued that
additional research is needed to discover which specific actions
taken by transfer-supportive supervisors directly lead to increased
transfer of training. The present study was designed to address this
need. Specifically, we obtained behavioral measures of posttraining potential and more typical performance in the identical team
task, where opportunities to perform a trained skill could be
controlled and one specific element of support could be manipulated by confederate team members.
Maximum and Typical

Performance

Sackett, Zedeck, and Fogli (1988) noted that motivation to exert
effort is artificially heightened within formal assessment situations
through demand characteristics such as (a) an explicit awareness of
being evaluated, (b) instructions to maximize effort, and (c) a short
enough measurement period to allow focused attention. Thus, it
was argued that the resulting measure is a better indicator of
maximum performance or what an individual can do than of typical

performance or what he or she will do on the job. However, it has
also been noted that the maximum-typical performance distinction
is more accurately viewed as a continuum rather than a dichotomy,
with assessment situations being arrayed to the degree that they
allow individual differences in motivation to affect performance
(DuBois, Sackett, Zedeck, & Fogli, 1993). If this logic is followed,
behavioral ratings obtained in a posttraining simulation should be
better indicators of transfer on the job to the degree that the same
variables affecting motivation to transfer in the workplace influence behavior in the simulation.
Given that the demand characteristics listed by Sackett et al.
(1988) maximize efforts to be evaluated highly, trainees will focus
their attention on identifying and demonstrating those behaviors
they deem to be instrumental to that goal. However, even when
individual differences in overall motivation to perform are constrained (ceiling effect), motivation to adopt a particular behavior
strategy may still vary. For example, all participants in an assessment center are acutely aware that they are being evaluated.
However, Kleinmann (1993) noted that in some cases participants
are made aware of the targeted skill and associated behaviors that
are to be assessed while in others they are not. Such awareness was
referred to as skill transparency. Kleinmann found that ratings of
the same skill in two separate exercises were more highly correlated when the targeted skill was not transparent in either exercise
than when the targeted skill was transparent in one exercise but not
in the other. In the latter case, he argued that convergent validity
may be reduced because one exercise (high transparency) captures
skill alone whereas the other (low transparency) contains additional variance accounted for by the participants' judgment regarding the appropriateness of using the targeted skill given the situation presented. Kleinmann went on to suggest that, because
on-the-job behavior is motivated by the same type of situational
judgment, ratings obtained from assessment center exercises containing low skill transparency may demonstrate greater correlations with on-the-job performance than those with high skill
transparency.
In an on-the-job transfer environment, trainees are not typically
given specific instructions to demonstrate their mastery of a newly
learned skill just prior to task performance. Instead, they are
generally required to judge for themselves if and when use of a
new skill will be instrumental to their success on the job. This
decision to attempt transfer or not to attempt transfer in a given
situation is expected to contain some amount of variance that is
accounted for by stable individual differences. However, situationspecific cues (e.g., behavior of peers and supervisors) play a role
as well. Formal assessment exercises that explicitly focus trainees
on demonstrating mastery of a trained skill in an exercise where all
those involved (e.g., role players, fellow assessees) clearly accept
and expect such behavior eliminate variability associated with
such factors. Thus, behavioral measures obtained in these exercises should be a better indicator of what trainees maximally can
do after training given the opportunities presented to them than
what they typically will do in similar situations on the job. However, when targeted skills are not made transparent to trainees, the
resulting measure should better reflect both their ability and willingness to transfer to similar situations. In summary, although
overall motivation to perform may be unavoidably maximized in
formal assessment situations, individual differences in a person's
willingness or tendency to adopt a particular behavioral strategy to

The present study adopted this approach to obtain directly
parallel indicators of learning and transfer. Half of our participants
were explicitly instructed to demonstrate their mastery of a trained

Hypothesis I: Ratings of a trained skill obtained during the identical

Consistent with the findings from previous research, we expected that the training program in question would effect greater
posttraining potential than would be reflected by an assessment of
more typical performance given the identical opportunities to

gap in performance could be causally tied to specific acts of
support exhibited by confederate team leaders.

Team Transfer Environments
Results from numerous studies have indicated that perceptions
of support for training at the work group level are an important
predictor of transfer (Facteau, Dobbins, Russell, Ladd, & Kudisch,
1995; Tracey et al., 1995; Tziner, Haccoun, & Kadish, 1991). In
fact, perceived work group- or team-level support has been shown
to be more strongly related to transfer than organizational-level
indicators in at least two studies (Facteau et al., 1995; Tracey et al.,
1995). These findings are consistent with both conceptual and
empirical work on levels of analysis that suggest individual behavior is most affected by variables closest to the individual
(Rousseau, 1985). Furthermore, the trend toward implementing
work team structures (e.g., autonomous work groups, quality circles) is likely to increase the impact that support from peers and
team leaders has on transfer. Thus, even though perceptions of
both organizational-level and team-level support systems have
been shown to explain unique variance in transfer behavior
(Tracey et al., 1995), efforts to change the work environment by
targeting team leaders and immediate supervisors may provide the
greatest return on investment.
Team leader support. Support for training from those who
directly lead or supervise trainees on the job has long been cited as
an important factor in the transfer process (e.g., Fleishman, 1953;
House, 1968). However, as noted by Baldwin and Ford (1988),
clear operational definitions of the specific actions taken by supportive supervisors and team leaders have lagged far behind anecdotal evidence of their importance. More recent research, however, has begun to specify multiple ways in which these key
stakeholders can support trainees' ability and willingness to transfer. It has been suggested, for example, that trainees' ability to
transfer can be improved by providing them with opportunities to
perform trained tasks on the job (e.g., Ford et al., 1992), offering

reminders and coaching (e.g., Brinkerhoff & Montesino, 1995;
Decker, 1982), and modeling trained behaviors on the job (e.g.,
Decker, 1980). Additionally, it has been argued that team leaders
influence trainees' motivation to transfer through participative
goal setting (Wexley & Nemeroff, 1975), extrinsic rewards (e.g.,
bonuses, promotions), and social cues and consequences (Rouiller
& Goldstein, 1993, Tracey et al., 1995). The present study tested
the proposition that informal reinforcement (or punishment) of
behaviors consistent with the goals of training by team leaders will
moderate the degree to which an organization can expect behavior
in a specific team transfer situation to reflect trainees' true capacity
(potential) to transfer given opportunities presented (i.e., team
leader support will lead to typical behavior that is more in line with
what the average trainee can do after training under maximum
performance conditions). Thus, our second hypothesis stated:
Hypothesis 2: The disparity in posttraining behavior exhibited in the
identical task under maximum and more typical assessment conditions
will be greater for trainees with nonsupportive team leaders than for
those with supportive team leaders.

Our remaining hypotheses test linkages within a conceptual
model of the process by which objective differences in team leader
reinforcement lead to climate perceptions that, in combination with
trainee characteristics, explain significant variance in transfer behavior (see Figure 1). Our typical performance condition was
designed to approximate the transfer environment in a more controlled setting. Thus, the following hypotheses were tested using
these participants only.
Team transfer climate. Team transfer climate is defined here
as individual perceptions of the degree to which a particular group
of teammates accepts and expects the use of behaviors learned in
a specific training program. This definition implies that the same
team may be viewed as supportive of transfer from one training
program (e.g., safety or technical training) but not from another
(e.g., participative decision making or feedback skills training).
Moreover, given reliable differences in transfer climate across
work teams, the same trainee may selectively choose to transfer or
not to transfer what they have learned in training when working
with different teams of individuals.

Developments in the area of social cognition, and in particular
the script-processing notion, can be used to explain how observable acts of team leader support lead to perceptions of team
transfer climate that motivate trainees to transfer or not to transfer
what they have learned. The notion of the performance script
contends that individuals develop and store context-specific
knowledge about conventional behavior and event sequences (e.g.,
role-based interactions, task performance) (Gioia & Manz, 1985).
Thompson and Luthans (1990) suggested that these scripts or
"socially constructed realities" (p. 328) develop as a result of the
cognitive matching of antecedents, behaviors, and consequences
that we experience directly and vicariously through observation
and organizational story telling. Repeated exposure to similar
episodes becomes the building block for a generalized performance script that is stored in semantic memory. It has been argued
that this generalized performance script is then used to interpret
cues and consequences in a new work environment and guides
one's performance accordingly (Gioia & Poole, 1984).
The choice between competing performance scripts in the workplace is sometimes a conscious one. However, it can also be
unconscious. Work in the area of cognitive social psychology
suggests that essentially automatic, schema-based information processing plays a significant role in the enactment of many organizational behaviors (Langer, 1978; Lord & Smith, 1983; Taylor &
Crocker, 1981). In other words, employees do not actively process
all information unique to a situation to decide how to behave.
Instead, they often draw broad conclusions quickly and with relatively little active information processing based on observed cues
and consequences (Gioia & Manz, 1985). Our study investigated
the degree to which informal reinforcement (or punishment) observed vicariously by trainees would lead them to draw broad
inferences about team transfer climate that, in turn, would motivate
them to transfer or not to transfer. Thus, as illustrated in Figure 1,
our third hypothesis stated:
Hypothesis 3: Perceptions of team transfer climate will mediate the
relationship between experimentally manipulated team leader support
and posttraining behavior for trainees in the typical performance
condition.

Trainee Characteristics
Although script processing helps us to understand how observable cues and consequences lead to perceptions of team transfer
climate, most transfer situations contain enough ambiguity to
allow for individual trainees' characteristics to color their interpretations of these objective cues. Consistent with this notion, a
number of studies outside the training literature have found substantial individual differences in the way jobs are perceived even
when formal job descriptions and tasks are relatively constant (cf.
O'Reilly, Parlette, & Bloom, 1980). Furthermore, individuals' job
attitudes (e.g., satisfaction with coworkers) have been shown to be
relatively stable over time despite changes in employers and occupations (cf. Staw & Ross, 1985). Together, these findings suggest that attitudes and perceptions about the work environment can
come as much from internal states of the individual as from
external cues and may explain why in previous studies individual
level perceptions of climate have failed to cluster into socially
meaningful groups (e.g., Patterson, Payne, & West, 1996).

Many authors have argued that perceptions of climate are a
product of complex interactions among observable elements of the
workplace and the values, needs, and other characteristics of
organizational members (Hellreigel & Slocum, 1974; James &
Jones, 1974, 1976; Schneider, 1983a, 1983b). However, little is
known about the impact of trainee characteristics on perceptions of
team transfer climate.
Predispositions toward training content. It is likely that certain personality characteristics may lead two trainees to view the
same transfer environment as more or less supportive. Specifically,
trainees may project their own beliefs regarding the appropriateness of a newly learned skill onto their teammates. Furthermore,
confirmation biases may lead trainees with certain personality
characteristics to perceive the same situational cues as more or less
meaningful. For example, a trainee who reports a positive predisposition toward handling conflict situations assertively may seek
out or attend to cues that confirm his or her beliefs about the
appropriateness of using assertiveness in a given transfer setting
while ignoring evidence that disconfirms his or her beliefs. As
depicted in Figure 1, our fourth hypothesis stated:
Hypothesis 4: In the typical performance condition, trainees' predisposition toward the content of training will account for unique variance in perceptions of team transfer climate after accounting for
variance predicted by objective differences in team leader support.
Specifically, trainees who score higher on an assertive personality
inventory prior to assertiveness training will perceive their team
transfer climate as being more supportive of transfer than will those
who score lower on that inventory.

In addition to influencing perceptions of the transfer environment, trainee characteristics may also impact trainees' ability and
willingness to change or ignore nonsupportive aspects of their
transfer environment. For example, those with an internal locus of
control may be more resilient to the demotivating effects of a
nonsupportive team transfer climate.
Locus of control. Locus of control refers to an individual's
tendency to attribute control over his or her outcomes either to
himself or herself (internal) or the environment (external) (Rotter,
1966). Tziner et al. (1991) suggested that work environment constraints may have a stronger impact on the behavior of trainees
who generally feel a lack of personal control. As evidence of this,
these authors found that internals benefited more from a relapseprevention module designed to enhance training transfer than did
externals. In another study, Storms and Spector (1987) found that
externals were more likely than internals- to demonstrate antioutput behaviors in reaction to frustrations. Thus, there is some
evidence to suggest that internals may be more likely than externals to use newly trained skills regardless of whether they perceive
that their transfer environment is supportive. In contrast, externals'
use of trained behavior may be much more influenced by the
perceived environment. As depicted in Figure 1, our fifth hypothesis stated:
Hypothesis 5: The relationship between perceptions of team transfer
climate and posttraining behavior will be moderated by trainees' locus
of control for participants in the typical performance condition. Specifically, perceptions of team transfer climate will be a better predictor
of behavior for trainees with a more external locus of control.

Participants
Participants were 80 licensed pilots recruited from an aeronautical
university. This university provides individuals with flight training as well
as aviation-related degrees designed to aid them in obtaining jobs in
commercial and military aviation. Participants' flight experience ranged
from 27 to 270 flight hours, with an average of 119.12 flight hours. The
average age of the participants was 21 years and ranged from 19 to 36.
Ninety-five percent of the participants were between 19 and 26 years old.
Eight of the participants were women. These participants were evenly
distributed across experimental conditions. The proportion of women in the
present study (10%) mirrored that in the student population at the aeronautical university and in the aviation industry (Glaysher, 1993).

Design
The hypotheses put forth in this study dealt with the effects of trainee
characteristics and aspects of the team transfer environment on participants' use of behaviors learned in training, not with whether the training
strategy (i.e., behavior role modeling) would be effective at improving the
trained skill (i.e., assertiveness) or whether the targeted skill would support
effective team decision making. These questions have been studied elsewhere (see Hartel, Smith & Prince, 1991; Jentsch, 1997; Smith-Jentsch,
Salas, & Baker, 1996). However, learning is a necessary prerequisite to
transfer of training. Thus, our study was designed to enable us to determine
whether the training course did in fact affect performance. Because the
typical performance condition required that participants believed their
teammates were also nai've participants and not confederates following a
preplanned script, a repeated-measures design was not feasible. Thus, a 2
(trained, untrained) X 2 (maximum, typical) X 2 (supportive, nonsupportive leader) between-subjects factorial design was used.

Procedure
Participants provided demographic information (i.e., age, gender, flight
hours) and completed two personality inventories (i.e., predisposition toward assertiveness, locus of control) at the time they were recruited for the
study. Half of the participants performed the team task before being offered
the opportunity to receive assertiveness training while the other half
performed the task 1 week after receiving the training. Team leader support
was manipulated during a pretask brief. Participants completed a team
transfer climate survey after this brief but before the team task. After the
task, participants were debriefed as to the purpose of the study and were
given feedback on their performance. Trainees received $10 for their
participation and signed for the release of their videotape for research
purposes.
Assertiveness training. Trainees received 2 hours of classroom training
that followed a behavioral role-modeling format (i.e., lecture, observation
of models, practice, and feedback). Participants were trained in groups of
2-6 individuals. Each participant was given one opportunity to practice
using assertiveness to deal with a 10-min role-played cockpit situation.
This also took place in the classroom and did not involve the simulation of
any technical aspects of flight.
Numerous studies (Lorr & More, 1980; Lorr, More, & Mansueto, 1981;
Skatsche & Kien, 1989; Smith-Jentsch et al., 1996) have reported evidence
that assertiveness consists of multiple, relatively independent dimensions
(i.e., directiveness, independence, social assertiveness, defense of interests). Behaviors associated with two of these dimensions (i.e., indepen-

dence, directiveness) are considered to be particularly important for effective team performance (Smith-Jentsch et al., 1996). These two dimensions
were discussed, demonstrated, and practiced in the present training program as separate dimensions of assertiveness that are important for aircrew
performance. The first dimension, independence, involves the ability and
willingness to resist conformity. This could involve, for example, stating
and maintaining an unpopular opinion or providing negative feedback to
another teammate in a constructive manner. The second dimension, directiveness, involves the ability and willingness to initiate action. This could
involve, for example, offering suggestions or taking on responsibility for
another teammate's tasks when needed.
Team task. Participants' assertiveness was assessed during a 35-min
computer-based flight simulation (Microsoft Flight Simulator, Redmond,
WA) that has been used previously to train and evaluate aircrew coordination in numerous studies (see Bowers, Salas, Prince, & Brannick, 1992).
This task required participants to perform all technical aspects of flight
(e.g., altitude control, navigation, safety checklists) in addition to communicating with their crew and with air traffic control.
In the present study, all participants were assigned the position of copilot
in a three-person aircrew. Each time, two confederate teammates acted in
the roles of captain and flight engineer. Fourteen different male pilots acted
as captain, flight engineer, and air traffic control on a rotating basis. These
confederates received several weeks of standardization training and pilot
testing prior to actual data collection. All participants were made aware that
their teammates were pilots from the same aeronautical university. Even
though a few participants recognized a teammate from having seen him
around campus or at a social gathering, none of the participants were
personally acquainted with or had ever flown with any of the confederates.
During the simulation, communications from air traffic control, the
captain, the flight engineer, and the voices of other aircraft were tightly
scripted to ensure that the trainees had equal opportunities to perform
trained behaviors. Four scripted trigger events were embedded into the
simulation. These events were developed by commercial pilots on the basis
of actual aircrew mishaps (Hartel et al., 1991). Each of the four events
required trainees to demonstrate assertiveness in order to safely complete
their flight. For example, in one event, the captain flew through his
designated altitude, putting the crew at risk of possible collision. The
confederates' behavior during the simulation was identical across experimental conditions. Furthermore, confederates were not aware of which
participants were trained or untrained and which were in the maximum or
typical performance assessment condition.
Assessment conditions. Participants in both the maximum and typical
performance conditions were aware they were being videotaped and evaluated. Additionally, all participants were instructed to do their best to
successfully complete the simulated flight plan they were assigned. However, only those in the maximum performance condition were explicitly
told that success would depend on their ability to effectively use assertiveness in response to the situations presented to them by instructors who
would play the roles of captain and flight engineer. Participants in this
condition were informed that their teammates were confederates to eliminate any inhibitions that would interfere with our ability to capture a
measure of their maximum capacity to use the trained skill given the
opportunities that were presented.
In contrast, participants in the typical performance condition were simply instructed to coordinate with their teammates to the best of their ability.
Thus, these participants were left to their own devices in determining just
what effective coordination meant in the context of the exercise. Furthermore, participants in the typical performance condition were led to believe
that their teammates were simply fellow assessees who had been recruited
in the same manner that they themselves had been. This was done so that
we could investigate the potential impact of team leader support on their
use of trained behaviors. During the experimental debrief, these participants were asked whether they had been aware during the simulation that

Team leader support. Published studies of cockpit climate (Ginnett,
1986; Komich, 1985), accident reports (Ha'rtel et al., 1991), and critical
incidents obtained from pilots within the participant population were
reviewed to develop an ecologically valid manipulation of team leader
support. These sources aided us in determining both (a) an appropriate
setting for the manipulation and (b) a realistic dialogue for crew
interaction.
It has been estimated that at least 60% of pilots working for U.S.
commercial airlines regularly fly with different crew members (Sharp,
1993). Often times, crew members meet for the first time at the preflight
brief and are required to fly as a crew in as little as 20 min. Thus, the
preflight brief was deemed an appropriate setting for manipulating team
leader support for training transfer. All dialogue between the confederate
crew members was tightly scripted. In all conditions, the confederate flight
engineer disagreed with the captain on an aspect of their flight plan and
offered an alternative suggestion. In fact, the flight engineer's suggestion
was actually incorrect. In the supportive leader condition, the captain
politely explained why the suggestion was incorrect and positively reinforced him by stating, "This time, you were incorrect, but next time, I may
be; so, don't be afraid to speak up. It's the things that you don't say that
may get us into trouble." In contrast, trainees in the nonsupportive leader
condition observed the captain ridicule the flight engineer. "No, no, no, you
are wrong! It is always an odd number plus 500 heading east. (Pause). Isn't
that part of the private pilot test? I mean, that is basic pilot knowledge."

Measures
Predisposition toward the content of training. Participants completed
items from the Lorr and More (1980) assertiveness inventory to assess their
predisposition toward two dimensions of the trained skill. Eight items were
used to determine trainees' predisposition toward each dimension (e.g.,
independence: "I nearly always argue for my viewpoint if I think I am
right"; directiveness: "I am usually the one who initiates activities in my
group"). Participants indicated their level of agreement with these statements on a 6-point Likert scale. Previous research has demonstrated that
scores on this inventory do in fact correlate with participants' tendency to
use assertiveness to resolve conflicts during a team task (Smith-Jentsch et
al., 1996). The internal consistency of responses within each subscale was
estimated using Cronbach's alpha (.69 for independence, .76 for
directiveness).
Locus of control scale. Participants completed Rotter's (1966)
Internal-External (IE) scale. This scale consists of 23 items in a forcedchoice format. Scores are calculated by summing the total number of
externally oriented responses chosen. Scores range from 0 through 23 with
low scores representing internality and high scores representing externality.
The internal consistency of items within this scale was estimated to be .75
using Cronbach's alpha.
Team transfer climate perceptions scale. The measure of team transfer
climate used in this study consisted of seven 7-point Likert scales anchored
with bipolar adjectives. These adjectives were intended to describe perceptions regarding the appropriateness of using the two dimensions of
trained behavior. To identify these adjectives, we held a focus group with
pilots drawn from the same population as the study participants. Perceptions of the appropriateness of the first dimension (climate for independence) were assessed by three items anchored by the adjectives flexible/
rigid, comfortable/tense, and forgiving/unforgiving. The internal
consistency of items within this subscale was estimated to be .93 using
Cronbach's alpha. Perceptions of the appropriateness of the second dimension (climate for directiveness) were assessed by four items anchored by
the adjectives participative/authoritative, open/closed, respectful/disrespectful, and trusting/distrustful. The internal consistency of items within
this subscale was estimated to be .97 using Cronbach's alpha.

Event-based performance effectiveness ratings. Behaviorally anchored
rating scales (BARS) were developed to evaluate how effectively trainees
applied the trained skill in response to the four key events in the simulation.
A group of three commercial pilots who developed the simulation events
were used to identify behavioral anchors for the BARS after reviewing the
performance of 10 pilot participants. These anchors were used to train
assessors to reliably evaluate the degree to which participants responded to
key events using the trained performance script (i.e., assertiveness) or a
counterscript (i.e., submissiveness). Each BARS required assessors to
assign a rating of 1 (highly ineffective; more consistent with the counterscript) through 5 (highly effective; more consistent with the trained script).
Two commercial pilots rated each participant using these scales. One of
these pilots provided ratings during the simulation and was not condition
blind. The second rater assigned ratings while viewing performances on
videotape and was condition blind. Interrater reliability for effectiveness
ratings assigned at the four key simulation events was estimated using
Pearson's product-moment correlation. Interrater reliability estimates for
ratings on the four BARS were .95 (Event 1), .93 (Event 2), .93 (Event 3),
and .88 (Event 4). Thus, the two raters' ratings were averaged for each
BARS. To estimate the consistency of participants' performance across the
four events in the simulation, we computed coefficient alpha on these four
averaged event ratings. Internal consistency estimated in this manner was
.71. Given that trainees' performance was shown to be fairly consistent
across the four key events, these event ratings were averaged to arrive at an
overall effectiveness rating for the simulation. This overall effectiveness
rating was used in subsequent analyses.
Frequency of trained behaviors. Two condition-blind raters viewed
videotapes of the simulated flights to assess the number of times participants displayed behavior consistent with the trained skill. This measure
differed from the event-based effectiveness measure in two ways. First,
trained behaviors exhibited throughout the team task (not just at preplanned
events) were included. Thus, frequency ratings were expected to be more
sensitive to differences in motivation to use the trained skill (due to climate
or locus of control). Second, the frequency ratings were multidimensional.
This allowed us to examine the impact of study variables separately on the
two dimensions of assertiveness emphasized in training. Two undergraduate psychology students who were trained to identify and discriminate
between behaviors consistent with independence and directiveness provided these ratings.
Interrater reliability for the frequency ratings was estimated using Pearson's product-moment correlation. These reliability estimates were .84 for
behaviors coded as independence and .93 for behaviors coded as directiveness. Thus, the two assessors' ratings were averaged for each dimension. These average dimension scores were used to test our experimental
hypotheses. As can be seen in Table 1, the correlation between frequency
counts of behaviors coded as independence and directiveness were modest
but significant, as was the correlation between the paper and pencil
measures of trainee predisposition toward the two dimensions. These
findings are consistent with previous research that has repeatedly found the
two dimensions to be correlated but distinct components of assertiveness
(Lorr & More, 1980; Lorr et al., 1981; Skatsche & Kien, 1989).

Discussion
Results from the present study complement and extend those
found in previous research on transfer of training. First, one
specific component of team-level support for transfer (i.e., informal reinforcement by a team leader) moderated the disparity
between maximum and more typical performance in the identical
team task after training. Specifically, those in the typical performance condition who had supportive team leaders demonstrated
use of the trained skill that was equivalent to their maximum
performance counterparts. In contrast, trainees in the typical performance condition who had nonsupportive leaders demonstrated
significantly fewer trained behaviors than their maximum performance counterparts.
As predicted, perceptions of team transfer climate were found to
mediate the team leader's impact on trainees in the typical performance condition. Moreover, despite the large impact of team
leader support on perceptions of team transfer climate, individual
differences in predisposition toward the content of training contributed unique variance as well. Specifically, trainees who scored
higher on an assertive personality inventory prior to the experiment tended to perceive their team transfer climate as being more
supportive of assertiveness than those scoring lower on the inventory, after team leader condition was taken into account. Finally,

Investigating the Gap Between Learning and Transfer
Behavioral ratings in the team task simulation only correlated
with climate perceptions and with transfer to the cockpit for those
participants in the typical performance condition. These findings
are consistent with the notion that our maximum-typical performance manipulation affected the degree to which individual differences in motivation to use the trained skill were free to vary.
Specifically, the fact that team transfer climate predicted the use of
trained behaviors in our typical performance condition suggests
that, although behavior was assessed in a controlled environment,
participants in this condition were indeed affected by the perceived
expectations of their peers. Conversely, nonsignificant correlations
between climate perceptions and behavior for those in the maximum performance condition suggested that in this condition individual differences in motivation to use the trained skill were
constrained (ceiling effect) as intended by explicit knowledge that
all those involved in the simulation (i.e., evaluators, teammates)
accepted and expected such behavior. Thus, mean ratings in this

most likely to have transfer problems.

Practical Implications

Many employee involvement (El) programs are considered parallel participation structures because they require individuals to
work within temporary teams that exist in parallel to their regular
day-to-day activities (Mohrman & Lawler, 1989). Organizations
generally intend for team skills training provided in conjunction
with these programs to be "transportable" (Cannon-Bowers, Tannenbaum, Salas, & Volpe, 1995, p. 364) to both short-term El
groups as well as work teams associated with regular job functions
(Tesluk, Fair, Mathieu, & Vance, 1995). However, one implication
of our findings is that transfer of team skills training may be quite
situation specific. In other words, employees who are required to
work with different teams on a regular basis may selectively
choose (consciously or unconsciously) to transfer or not to transfer
from one day to the next. Fortunately, results from this study also
suggest that leaders and immediate supervisors can facilitate trans-

trainers in a continuous learning environment (Tannenbaum,
1997). We recommend a number of interventions that organizations can make toward this end.
Team leader support. First, leaders need to be made aware of
the powerful impact that their actions (however subtle) may have
on subordinates' willingness to transfer. In this regard, it is important to note that trainees in our study did not directly experience
reinforcement or punishment for using the trained skill. They
simply observed their team leader's reaction toward another teammate. Thus, they learned vicariously what type of behavior was
accepted and expected of them. This suggests that leaders who
consciously seek out opportunities to publicly reinforce transfer
may have a far-reaching impact on team members.
Second, for team leaders to reinforce transfer attempts, they
must first be able to recognize behaviors that are consistent with
the goals of training. Because team transfer climate is specific to
each new training program, this requires an ongoing partnership
between trainers, team leaders, and supervisors. Finally, stroking
team members for effective transfer can be uncomfortable for
some team leaders, particularly for those who have traditionally
viewed their primary function as being one of commanding and
controlling rather than coaching and morale building. Thus, organizations may increase their return on training investments by
providing their team leaders with training that highlights simple
but effective strategies for soliciting and reinforcing transfer attempts. This training should include opportunities to practice roleplaying workplace situations and to receive feedback on their skill
as a facilitator.
Tannenbaum, Smith-Jentsch, and Behson (1998) tested the effects of such a training program on the briefing behavior of a group
of Navy officers. Results indicated that leaders who participated in
this seminar were more likely to solicit and reinforce suggestions
made by their team members during a series of subsequent postexercise debriefs. Thus, there is some evidence to suggest that
team leaders, even in an environment as regimented as the military, can be trained to consciously manage team transfer climate.
Trainee characteristics. Interactions among trainee characteristics and the work environment, such as the one reported here,
help to explain why some team members choose to transfer even
when their leaders exhibit nonsupportive behaviors while others do
not. One implication of this is that at-risk trainees may be targeted
for special interventions both before and after training. First,
pretraining discussions (e.g., Brinkerhoff & Montesino, 1995) that
make supervisor support for training explicit may help to combat
the tendency that some trainees have to allow their own negative
predispositions toward the content of training to color perceptions
of team transfer climate. Second, posttraining relapse prevention
seminars or self-regulation strategies may help to make individuals
with an external locus of control more resilient to perceived
organizational constraints. Finally, organizations may take trainee
characteristics into account when assigning employees to teams
following a new training program. For example, those least likely
to be inhibited by nonsupportive transfer climates (e.g., highly
internal trainees) may be chosen for assignments to teams led by
individuals who are known to be skeptical about a training
program.

Training evaluation. Results from this study also have implications for practitioners involved in training effectiveness evaluation and, more broadly, in the assessment of interpersonal skills in
role-play exercises or task simulations. Specifically, these findings
suggest that the predictive validity of behavioral ratings obtained
in such situational exercises will be greater if targeted skills are not
made transparent to assessees. However, given that learning is a
precondition of transfer, assessment of maximum performance
under conditions of high skill transparency may be useful in
determining whether a training course has been effective in allowing individuals to acquire new skills. If not, this may suggest that
the course does not provide adequate opportunities to practice. On
the other hand, assessment of trained skills under conditions of low
skill transparency may help to reveal problems in trainees' willingness to transfer or in their ability to identify situations where
transfer is appropriate. Additionally, when employees are trained
and evaluated together with their primary work teams, performance in exercises characterized by low skill transparency may
uncover team climate perceptions that run counter to the goals of
training and threaten to hinder transfer on the job. Thus, assessment of a training program's effectiveness in a task simulation
under both conditions of low and high skill transparency may
allow for the diagnosis of a variety of factors that can result in poor
transfer and may likely require different types of interventions
(e.g., increased opportunities to practice, relapse prevention training, team leader interventions).

Study Limitations
A number of factors should be considered when generalizing the
findings reported here. First, the impact of team leader support on
posttraining behavior was assessed in a simulated work environment and not in an organizational setting. A second factor to
consider is that participants had no past or future working with
their teammates. Furthermore, participants had very little time to
assess their teammates' expectations of them before performing
together as a team. This is quite common for commercial aircrews,
teams that operate in high-stress environments (e.g., emergency
medical teams), task forces, and other short-term decision-making
groups within organizations. However, many work teams remain
together for extended periods of time. To change preexisting
perceptions of team transfer climate, developed over the course of
months or years, more than one act of support is likely to be
required.
A third related point is that only two levels of team leader
support were examined. These represented opposite ends of the
continuum. This together with the fact that participants had no
prior experience with their team is likely to have inflated the
impact that the team leader's actions had on climate perceptions
(R2 = 75-83). Yet, despite this fact, predispositions toward the
content of training still explained unique variance. Our study did
not include a condition where a teammate's transfer attempt was
ignored or where no teammate attempted to transfer. It is possible
that trainee characteristics may have played an even stronger role
in determining perceptions of team transfer climate in such ambiguous or neutral environments. Alternatively, in the absence of
blatant cues to the contrary, trainees may have relied on their
perceptions of organizational-level or professional climate regarding the appropriateness of assertiveness.

A fourth point involves our operationalization of maximum and
typical performance. In our study, this involved the simultaneous
manipulation of both skill transparency and awareness of teammates' confederate status. We chose to make participants in the
maximum performance condition aware that their teammates were
confederates in addition to making them aware of the targeted skill
to eliminate social inhibitions that would prevent us from assessing
their maximum capacity to use assertiveness. However, we are
unable to say how much of the difference in performance between
the two assessment conditions was due to skill transparency and
how much was due to the lack of social inhibitions induced by
knowledge of confederates.

Directions for Future Research
The present study used nontraditional methods for testing the
proposition that team leader support moderates the degree to which
trainees transfer what they have learned in training. A controlled
task simulation, and a between-subjects design, was used to obtain
directly parallel indicators of learning and likelihood of transfer. In
contrast, previous field research has adopted correlational withinsubjects designs. Results from our study were consistent with those
found in these more traditional studies of transfer. Moreover, the
many controlled features of the present study (e.g., condition-blind
coders, confederate teammates) enabled us to isolate the causal
effects of one aspect of team leader support. Future research may
use similar methods to test the causal effects of other work environment variables that have been correlationally linked to transfer
in field settings.
A critical assumption within our study was that we could manipulate the degree to which performance in the identical task
simulation reflected potential to use the trained skill (learning) and
more typical use of the trained skill on the job (transfer). Differential correlations among behavioral ratings in the simulation and
self-reported transfer on the job provided some evidence to support
this notion. However, additional research is needed to further
examine relationships between behavioral ratings collected in task
simulations that vary on the maximum-typical performance continuum and other more traditional measures of learning (e.g.,
knowledge and attitude scales) and transfer (e.g., supervisor and
peer ratings).
Finally, the trainee characteristics and team-level variables examined in our study had inconsistent effects across two dimensions
of the trained skill. This highlights the importance of collecting
multidimensional measures of transfer behavior in addition to
overall measures of performance effectiveness. Researchers should
continue to use such measures to expand our understanding of the
specific effects of various training inputs on transfer. For example,
when do trainees generalize observed reinforcement of one dimension within a trained skill to others? Such research is needed to
further understand when and why the same trainee may choose to
transfer or not to transfer when working with different groups of
teammates.
