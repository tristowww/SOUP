## What is coaching and mentoring? {#slide-2}

A training strategy that involves pairing experienced professionals with newer employees or experienced professionals to: 

Help them adapt to the environment and culture of the workplace.

To help them settle into the surroundings and get off to a good start. 

To provide them benefit from personal guidance on specific job duties, processes or responsibilities.

## Coaching {#slide-5}

Interactive process through which managers and supervisors aim to solve performance problems or develop employee capabilities

## Mentoring {#slide-6}

"...someone who helps someone else learn something that he or she would have learned less well, more slowly, or not at all if left alone." -- Chip Bell, author/consultant

Mentoring helps develop tacit, or "sticky," knowledge

The scope of mentoring is vastly greater than coaching -- coaching is a subset of mentoring

Mentoring addresses the whole person and his or her career

## Key Differences {#slide-7}

                               Coaching                                                                       Mentoring
  ---------------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Key Goals                    To correct inappropriate behavior, improve performance, and impart skills      To support and guide personal growth
  Initiative  for  Mentoring   The coach directs the learning                                                 The  protégé is in charge of the learning
  Volunteerism                 Volunteering  not necessary (although agreement to participate is essential)   Mentor  and protégé both volunteer
  Focus                        Immediate problems and  opportunities                                          Long-term personal career development

  Relationship                 Coach is often the boss                                                        Mentor  is rarely the boss.

## Coaching versus mentoring {#slide-8}

  COACHING                       MENTORING
  ------------------------------ ----------------------------------------------------
  Slightly more formal           Not always formal
  Career coach or professional   Typically a superior in your field or organization
  Client-led                     More personal relationship
  Structured Process             Often less structured
  Focused on accountability      Focused on knowledge sharing

## Coaching Myths {#slide-9}

Myth:  Coaching is for losers, a last-grasp effort before being shown the door.

Reality:  Coaching is for winners who seek to go to the next level.

Myth:  Coaching is about filling leadership behavior gaps.

Reality:  Coaching is about an Impossible Future and changing your life.

Myth:  Coaching is a separate leadership development activity.

Reality:  Coaching integrates leadership development and results.

## Coaching Myths {#slide-10}

Myth:  The coach is a process consultant who asks questions from a distant.

Reality:  The coach is like a sports coach on the playing field, doing whatever it takes to win.

Myth:  Coaching is an activity that happens in annual reviews.

Reality:  Coaching requires continuous, but not continual communication.

## Characteristics of a good mentor {#slide-18}

-  Willingness to share skills, knowledge, and expertise.
-  Demonstrates a positive attitude and acts as a positive role model.
-  Takes a personal interest in the mentoring relationship.
-  Exhibits enthusiasm in the field.
-  Provides guidance and constructive feedback.
-  Respected by colleagues and employees in all levels of the organization.
-  Sets and meets ongoing personal and professional goals.
-  Values the opinions and initiatives of others.
-  Motivates others by setting a good example.

## Benefits & Challenges {#slide-20}

## Benefits (1/2) {#slide-21}

Coaching and mentoring can provide an array of benefits for organizations of all sizes

Retention

Helps encourage loyalty to the company. 

When experienced professionals help  mold  the career of and provide opportunities for mentees, these individuals may feel a greater sense of connection and commitment to the business. 

Personal Development

Can help guide an employee along on his/her career path

Allows individuals to resolve issues and concerns within a trusted and confidential relationship 

Helps reduce frustrations on a personal level and improve the job satisfaction.

## Benefits (2/2) {#slide-22}

Team Efficiency

Allows managers to identify the weaknesses and strengths of each employee

Allows the organization to plan available resources to maintain smooth functioning when employees request vacation or take a sick day.

Versus Training

In the context of training budget cuts,  coaching and mentoring allows you to "do more with less"

One US study showed that while training alone increases productivity by 22.8%, for training combined with coaching the figure was nearly 90%.  Serves as a bridge between training and implementation.

## Challenges (1/2) {#slide-23}

Most barriers to effective mentoring and coaching stem from: 

Issues of organisational culture where the prevailing culture is not sympathetic to mentoring and coaching, or does not fully understand it. 

Personality issues between those involved in mentoring and coaching programmes. 

## Challenges (2/2) {#slide-24}

Challenges to effective mentoring/coaching include:

Poor matching of mentors or coaches to their protégés 

Lack of managerial support at higher levels

Resentment from those not chosen to participate in mentoring and coaching programmes, perhaps due to a perception of favouritism

The creation of unrealistic expectations as to what mentoring and coaching can achieve 

The blurring of role boundaries, for example, between the role of manager and mentor. 

## Allen et al., (2017) {#slide-25}

Taking stock of two relational aspects of organizational life: Tracing the history and shaping the future of socialization and mentoring research

Key Themes :

- Early research established foundational theories, including stage models and organizational socialization tactics.
- The 1990s saw the development of measures for socialization, testing of interactionist perspectives, and emphasis on individual differences and proactive behaviors of newcomers.
- From 2000 onwards, there was a focus on the dynamic nature of socialization, including the role of individual differences, the impact of socialization on various levels of organizational outcomes, and the exploration of negative socialization experiences.

## Allen et al., (2017) {#slide-26}

Mentoring:

-  Historical Context : Workplace mentoring research gained momentum with  Kram's  (1985) work, focusing on developmental relationships that aid career progression and personal growth.
-  Key Themes :

<!-- -->

- Initial research aimed to establish the benefits of mentoring and explore its role in supporting the careers of women and minorities.
- The 1990s expanded the scope to include diverse mentorship forms, the impact of individual differences on mentoring outcomes, and the structural and relational differences between formal and informal mentoring.
- More recent research has explored the mechanisms of learning within mentoring relationships, best practices for formal mentoring programs, and the multifaceted outcomes of mentoring for both mentors and protégés.

26

## Allen et al., (2017) {#slide-27}

Future Directions:

-  The authors suggest bridging the socialization and mentoring literatures to deepen the understanding of how these processes interact and influence each other.
-  They call for research that better captures the dynamic nature of both processes across time, utilizing longitudinal, computational, and event-based methodologies.
-  There is a need for developing multilevel theories and models to understand how socialization and mentoring impact and are influenced by individual, dyadic, team, and organizational levels.
-  Addressing causality through experimental and quasi-experimental designs is crucial to disentangle the direction of effects in both fields.

27

## Allen et al., (2017) {#slide-28}

Takeaways:

-  Both organizational socialization and mentoring are critical processes that facilitate employee adjustment, development, and success within organizations.
-  While significant progress has been made in understanding these processes, there remain substantial gaps, particularly in understanding their dynamic nature, multilevel impacts, and causal relationships.
-  Future research should adapt to changing work environments and technological advancements to remain relevant and informative for organizational practices.

## Feldman &  Lankau  (2005) {#slide-29}

Executive coaching: A review and agenda for future research 

Overview:

-  Executive coaching has grown significantly as a development intervention for managers over the past decade. Despite its popularity, empirical research and theoretical frameworks on the processes underlying effective coaching are limited.
- 

Definition and Distinctiveness:

-  Executive coaching is generally seen as a short- to medium-term relationship focused on improving an executive\'s work effectiveness through one-on-one counseling, using 360-degree feedback to address strengths and weaknesses.

## Feldman &  Lankau  (2005) {#slide-30}

Coaches and Executive Clients:

-  The qualifications and backgrounds of executive coaches vary widely, and the field currently lacks standardized credentialing. Coaches come from diverse backgrounds including psychology, business, and other professional fields.

Outcomes of Coaching:

-  The desired outcomes from executive coaching include changes in managerial behaviors and enhancements in organizational effectiveness. However, rigorous empirical studies validating these outcomes are scarce, and the field is calling for more systematic research to measure the impact of coaching.

30

## Feldman &  Lankau  (2005) {#slide-31}

Phases of Coaching Relationship:

-  T he coaching process usually involves phases of data gathering, feedback, periodic coaching sessions, and evaluation. These phases are aimed at setting developmental goals, providing feedback, and implementing and assessing development plans.

Approaches to Coaching:

-  Different coaching approaches are used, including psychodynamic, behaviorist, person-centered, cognitive therapy, and systems-oriented approaches, each with its own focus and intervention strategies.

## Feldman &  Lankau  (2005) {#slide-32}

Future Research Agenda:

-  The article outlines several areas for future research, including clarifying the distinctions and overlaps between executive coaching and other developmental relationships .

 

Takeaways:

-  While executive coaching holds promise as a tool for leadership and organizational development, there is a critical need for more theoretical and empirical work to understand its mechanisms, effectiveness, and best practices.
-  F uture research should aim to provide a stronger evidence base for the practice of executive coaching, addressing the current gaps in understanding and contributing to the field\'s professionalization and efficacy.
