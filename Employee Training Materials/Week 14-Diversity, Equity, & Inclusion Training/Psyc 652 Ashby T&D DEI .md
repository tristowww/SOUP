6. I Am, But I Am Not
This group activity helps break down stereotypes in the workplace:
•Have each person in the group create two columns on a sheet of paper
•In the first column, they should write down something about who they are (for example, their

are

1.Behavior Change Model: The study supports a behavior change
model where attitudes precede intentions, which in turn influence
actions. It found that behavior change was more pronounced among
groups with supportive attitudes toward women before the training,
while attitude change was more prominent in less supportive groups.
2.Limitations and Skepticism: There are reasons to be skeptical about
the effectiveness of brief diversity trainings. Attitude changes may be
influenced by demand effects, and behavior change was limited,
particularly among dominant group members. Conducting the study in
only one organization limits generalizability.
3.Potential Benefits: Diversity training may have other benefits not
measured in the study, such as signaling the value of diversity and
setting norms of inclusion. The study found that training prompted
women to connect with more senior women, suggesting potential
benefits in promoting proactive behavior.

2.Mediating Role of Belief in a Just World: Belief in a just world acts as

3.Challenges in Developing Cross-Race Friendships: Despite the

whether diversity education can help individuals overcome the
challenges of developing cross-race friendships.

RAGINS, B. R., & EHRHARDT, K. (2020, JULY 23). GAINING PERSPECTIVE: THE IMPACT OF
CLOSE CROSS-RACE FRIENDSHIPS ON DIVERSITY TRAINING AND EDUCATION HYPOTHESES
S3

Objective and Design: Study 3 explores the impact of a "day in the life" perspective-taking essay exercise on enhancing
participants' willingness to develop cross-race friendships and other prosocial diversity outcomes. Conducted in a field
setting with an experimental research design, the study aimed to bridge social psychology research with practical
diversity education.
Method: Participants were shown a photo of an individual demographically different from themselves and asked to
write a narrative essay from that person's perspective (treatment group), whereas the control group wrote without
adopting the target's perspective. The effectiveness of this perspective-taking exercise was assessed at the course's
end, comparing it against control for changes in prosocial diversity behaviors.

Theoretical Contribution: The study tests theories explaining the effectiveness of perspective-taking exercises,
specifically examining whether outcomes are mediated by empathic concern for the exercise's target or a cognitive
self-other overlap (including the target in the perceiver's self-representation).

1.Main Findings: The perspective-taking exercise increased empathic
concern but not self-other overlap. It led to a greater willingness to
develop cross-race friendships and engage in diversity voice but did
not significantly impact comfort confronting racist comments through
empathic concern.
2.Exploratory Analyses: The effectiveness of the exercise did not vary
significantly based on participant race, gender, or existing cross-race
friendships. The diversity course itself improved willingness to develop
cross-race friendships and diversity voice but had a negligible effect
on comfort confronting racist comments.

RAGINS, B. R., & EHRHARDT, K. (2020, JULY 23). GAINING
PERSPECTIVE: THE IMPACT OF CLOSE CROSS-RACE FRIENDSHIPS ON
DIVERSIT Y TRAINING AND EDUCATION TAKEAWAYS & FUTURE
IMPLEMENTATIONS – OVERALL
1.Validation of Relational Theory: The studies validate the proposed
relational theory of diversity education, emphasizing that while
diversity courses increase awareness of the importance of perspective taking, actual improvement in perspective-taking abilities was notably
higher among those with pre-existing close cross-race friendships.
2. Mediating Role of Belief in a Just World: This belief significantly
mediated the effect of close cross-race friendships on perspectivetaking abilities, with those having such friendships entering the course
with a set of beliefs that predisposed them to benefit more from the
diversity training.
3. Robustness of the Friendship Effect: Attempts to explain the
findings through alternative explanations (e.g., racial prejudice, need
for social distance) did not alter the fundamental relationship,
suggesting a unique cognitive and motivational impact of close crossrace friendships on diversity education outcomes.

GEBERT, D., BUENGELER, C., & HEINITZ, K. (2017). TOLERANCE: A
NEGLECTED DIMENSION IN DIVERSIT Y
TRAINING?. ACADEMY OF MANAGEMENT LEARNING & EDUCATION
TAKEAWAYS & FUTURE IMPLEMENTATIONS
• Traditional corporate diversity training often doesn't work well because it doesn't effectively address barriers
like people being too rigid in their views or not speaking up about their differences. The authors propose a new
approach focused on tolerance, where everyone's right to express their values is respected, and open dialogue
is encouraged.
• They suggest implementing this approach in MBA courses, where students can learn through discussions and
experiences. The goal is to reduce rigid thinking and increase openness to different viewpoints.
• The authors also emphasize the importance of trainers' beliefs in shaping the effectiveness of diversity training.
They suggest that a diverse group of participants and experiential learning can promote tolerance and
understanding.
• Overall, the paper highlights the potential of a tolerance-centered approach to improve corporate diversity
training, but also acknowledges challenges and the need to consider different training objectives and beliefs.
