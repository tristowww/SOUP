As companies are driven to reshape their established practices with the impact of advancements in
information and communication technologies, corporate training emerges as a distinct area requiring
technological transformation. This exploratory study aims to examine the attitudes of a sample of 106 of
the top 500 corporations in Turkey regarding the usage of e-learning for corporate training. Findings
show that most of these ﬁrms currently have a hesitant attitude toward this development. Through
factor analysis, the main advantages for using e-learning in corporate training have been classiﬁed as
employee commitment and motivation, convenience and accessibility, customization and outsourcing,
and cost effectiveness while the disadvantages have been grouped as personal and organizational.
Consequently, attitudinal differences toward e-learning between companies have been explored according to two scale-related variables (employee size and company revenue) and two variables about
organizational readiness (the existence of a Learning & Education Department and the current use of elearning).

1. Introduction
The effects of developments in information and communication
technologies are rapidly permeating into nearly all activities and
processes in companies. Considering that corporate training is one
of the main business functions in most organizations, studying the
diffusion of technology into this realm is worthwhile. Actually, the
penetration of technology into the world of education is not
prevalent only in an organizational context. Distance learning
programs are ﬂourishing immensely in various areas such as high
school and university education, adult education and lifelong
learning programs. In this environment, companies are expected to
give serious thought and consideration regarding a technological
reformation in the way they design and deliver training programs
for their employees.
The advancement of technology utilization in corporations for
employee training is a twofold issue. On one side, it can be expected
to improve efﬁciency greatly as a result of achieving the desired

Bucciarelli, Muratore, and Odoardi (2010) have grouped European

distance learning market since it is has the largest percentage
(16.6%) of young population in Europe (TUIK, 2014), an Internet
penetration rate of 59.6% (Internet World Stats, 2016) and an
employment rate of approximately 50% in the 15e64 age range
which is approximately 53 million of the total population as of 2015
(TUIK, 2015).
In light of the fact that the global corporate e-learning market is
booming and the conjecture that Turkey has a serious potential in
this area, this research aims to explore perceptions about and
attitude toward the usage of e-learning in corporate training in
Turkey. More speciﬁcally, the research questions of this study are:
RQ1. What is the general attitude toward the use of e-learning for
corporate training in organizations?
RQ2. What are the advantages and disadvantages perceived by
companies regarding utilizing e-learning for corporate training?
RQ3. What are the differences in the advantages and disadvantages perceived by companies according to employee size, company
revenue, existence of a Learning&Education Department, and the
current use of e-learning?
This study aims to contribute to the current literature in the ﬁeld
of corporate e-learning in a number of ways. One of the main
contributions is to draw a more organizational perspective to elearning adoption rather than an individual one. Most studies in the
literature test attitudes toward e-learning by using various technology adoption models and asking respondents to state their
personal opinions and attitudes regarding various aspects of elearning. In this study, alternatively, key people from organizations
have been contacted to represent their company's outlook toward
this new learning system instead of their personal experience or
approach.
Another important contribution is the exploratory nature of this
research. Currently, there are many valuable studies in the current
literature testing numerous technology adoption and motivation
theories in the e-learning context. Integrative and extended models
are used and important ﬁndings have been reached. However,
because of the theory-testing nature of such work, existing scales
have been adapted to the e-learning environment and although
general behavioral approaches have been captured, context speciﬁc
issues have remained secondary or untapped. This study has made
an evaluation of a comprehensive set of contextual advantages and
disadvantages of corporate e-learning and has focused on more

tangible aspects of adoption rather than the behavioral dimensions
which have been studied already in many attempts. This approach
has resulted in an assessment of the importance of various operational issues different from the personal and behavioral sides of the
topic.
Finally, this study has examined the drivers and drawbacks of elearning for companies that are at the very early stages of adoption.
Instead of posing a limitation, this actually provided a projective
nature to the study regarding the opportunities and challenges
surrounding the e-learning market. Although studying the satisfaction, success or performance of companies or employees who
have already welcome this technology is valuable, understanding
the views of non-adopters or early adopters as they see this
development from a distance is also one of the important alternative perspectives and contributions of this study.
2. Theoretical background
The current state of the literature about corporate e-learning is
characterized by different approaches. There are studies that have
utilized a speciﬁc technology adoption model or an integration of
multiple models to evaluate the success and diffusion of e-learning
systems in organizational environments.
One such framework is the IS success model of DeLone and
McLean (1992) where the system quality and information quality
of an information system precedes system use and user satisfaction
which, in turn, determine its impact on individuals and ultimately
on organizations. Wang, Wang & Shee (2007) have developed and
validated a multiple-item scale to assess e-learning system success
(ELSS) based mainly on the IS success model with an addition of
service quality as an antecedent and a more comprehensive
approach to measure system use, user satisfaction and the net
beneﬁts of system use. Chen (2010) has examined the relation between e-learning system use and overall job outcomes also by
utilizing the IS success model and has found that the model can
explain individual beneﬁts of e-learning usage in terms of speciﬁc
job outcomes, i.e. task completion, job satisfaction, and job performance. In another attempt, IS success model has been integrated
with social cognitive theory and motivation theory to explain critical
success factors for e-learning in developing countries as judged by
ICT experts and faculty (Bhuasiri, Xaymoungkhoun, Zo, Rho, &
Ciganek, 2012). While three dimensions of success, namely, infrastructure and system quality, course and information quality, and
institution and service quality have been included in the study from
the IS success model perspective, four additional dimensions which
are e-learning environment, learner characteristics, instructor
characteristics and extrinsic motivation have also been added from
social cognitive and motivation theories.
As in most studies regarding the acceptance and adoption of a
speciﬁc technology, one of the most popular models that has been
used in the corporate e-learning literature is the Technology
Acceptance Model e TAM (Davis, 1989). Research attempts to utilize
TAM for this context took an integrative or expansive approach. Lee,
Hsieh & Chen (2013) have tested an extended TAM model where
organizational support, computer self-efﬁcacy, prior experience in
using computer technology and task equivocality have been identiﬁed as the antecedents of perceived ease of use (PEOU) and
perceived usefulness (PU). Findings show that all four antecedents
have full or partial effects on the two main independent variables of
TAM. Similarly, Hsia, Chang and Tseng (2014) have added locus of
control as an antecedent to these two variables and found that both
effects are signiﬁcant. Mohammadi's (2015) study, although it is not
conducted in an organizational context, provides another successful example of an integrative attempt where the above-mentioned
IS success model approach and TAM are combined. In his work, the

H. Kimiloglu et al. / Computers in Human Behavior 72 (2017) 339e349

author has conglomerated educational quality, service quality,
technical system quality and content and information quality from
different sources in the literature being inspired by the IS success
model. He has used these four quality antecedents alongside PEOU
and PU to predict user satisfaction, usage intention and actual usage
of e-learning systems. Variations of the TAM model take many
other different forms. In Cheng, Minhong, Moorman, Olaniran, and
Chen (2012) work, authors have divided perceived usefulness into
two dimensions as individual and social learning and have hypothesized that both dimensions will have positive effects on
intention to use e-learning at work. Moreover, the antecedents of
PEOU have been added from an expectancy theory perspective as
managerial support, job support and organizational support for elearning.
Another technology adoption model that has been used in this
research avenue is Uniﬁed Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology e UTAUT (Venkatesh, Morris, Davis, & Davis, 2003). Yoo, Han
& Huang (2012) have situated the variables of UTAUT into extrinsic
and intrinsic motivation categories. Effort expectancy, attitude towards e-learning, and anxiety have been categorized as intrinsic
motivation variables while performance expectancy, social inﬂuence and facilitating conditions have been used as extrinsic motivation variables. Intrinsic motivators has been found to have a
stronger effect on behavioral intention to adopt e-learning in the
workplace than extrinsic motivators. Motivational theories have
also been utilized in integrative approaches. Intrinsic value, selfefﬁcacy, and test anxiety were used in Joo, Lim, and Kim's (2012)
study as learners' motivational factors while PEOU and PU were
used as environmental factors affecting learning ﬂow and
achievement.
In the literature, more speciﬁc and parsimonious studies can be
found to study the effectiveness of corporate e-learning programs
as well. Cheng, Minhong, Yang, Kinshuk, and Jun (2011) have used a
competency-based learning approach and studies perceived individual learning support and perceived peer collaborative learning
support as the two main antecedents of intention to use e-learning
systems and both have been found to have a strong predictive
power on usage intention. As an example of another more speciﬁc
and niche approach, Rodriguez and Armellini (2013) have
concentrated only on the comparative importance of learnercontent, learner-learner, and learner-teacher interactions in the
effectiveness of corporate e-learning. Findings show that learnercontent interaction is most important followed by learner-learner
and learner-teacher interactions.
Although many studies have used a theory-testing approach in
the literature, this research has taken a more exploratory
perspective. Considering the novelty of the corporate e-learning
market in Turkey, assessing perceptions about a comprehensive set
of advantages and disadvantages was seen important in order to
depict the current state of company attitudes toward this development. Therefore, the literature has been scanned also from the
perspective of a wide number of studies examining the pros and
cons of organizational e-learning systems. Findings about how
these issues are grouped together and the emerging categories of
variables show close resemblance with previous studies and
parallelism with related theories.
The most commonly mentioned advantages in the literature are
the ﬂexibility of e-learning programs and efﬁciency, especially in
terms of cost minimization and time saving. However, higher-order
issues such as the permanence of learning material, personalization
of the learning process or enhancement of employee and customer
satisfaction also appear as important positive aspects of e-learning
compared to traditional methods. As for the disadvantages, while
technical problems and technology resistance prevail, questions
about the lack of sociability, quality considerations and

3. Methodology of the study
Due to the exploratory nature of this study, a rather lengthy
questionnaire was used as the method of data collection. It was
constructed mainly according to the existing issues arising from the
current literature and research examples conducted in different
contexts. Opinions of e-learning experts and a pilot study conducted on ﬁve corporations helped the creation of the ﬁnal form of
the tool. A total of 19 questions in the questionnaire were related to
this particular study. The initial three questions were about the
proﬁle of the respondent (age, gender, and department worked in).
Respondents who ﬁlled out the survey on behalf of their companies
were employed in managerial positions mostly in the Human Resources and Learning & Education Departments as well as some
others that were still all relevant to the subject area of the research
(nHR ¼ 67 (63.8%); nLED ¼ 18 (17.1%); nOTH ¼ 20 (19%)). The
respondent group was fairly equivalent in terms of gender distribution with males slightly exceeding females (nM ¼ 59 (55.7%);
nF ¼ 47 (44.3%)). It was a young respondent group, approximately
half of which is in the 30e40 age range with the rest fairly closely
distributed in the below 30 and above 40 groups (n<30 ¼ 25 (23.8%);
n30-40 ¼ 51 (48.6%); n>40 ¼ 29 (27.7%)).
This part was followed by questions related to the general
proﬁle of the corporations (province, number of employees, sector,
revenue, type, and location) as well as the current state of elearning in the sample. Companies were asked questions about the
existence of a Learning & Education Department in the company,
the number of employees in this department, the current level of
and budget allocated to e-learning. Responses to these questions
can be found in the company proﬁle results.
The next section included questions about the perceptions of
the companies related to the importance of advantages and disadvantages of e-learning compared to traditional learning for their
company. The questions related to the advantages (19 items) and
the disadvantages (12 items) were rated on a 5-point interval scale
from “very unimportant” to “very important”.
The data were gathered through an online survey, formed
through Google Documents application and the survey link was
sent to the top 500 corporations determined by a reputable Turkish
monthly business and economy magazine, for notiﬁcation. Initial
contact with the top 500 corporations was made through e-mail.
The body of the message included an explanation about the purpose of the study and a statement of conﬁdentiality of the information provided by the respondents. This ﬁrst round resulted in 45
responses in three weeks. An intense follow-up process through a
second round of e-mails and telephone calls generated an additional 61 returns adding up to an overall response of 106. Since the
target population was the top 500 corporations in Turkey, considering the extensive nature of the questionnaire, a response rate
slightly higher than 20% is accepted as adequate.
Respondents contacted in each company were asked to answer
the questions with an organizational identity, representing the
company's current approach toward e-learning rather than personal opinions. For this reason, care was taken to make sure that the
survey was answered by a relevant manager from the Learning&Education or Human Resources Department in the company.
Although this created an important challenge and slowed the data
collection process, it was important to get the corporate perspective including positive views and resistance areas instead of the
individual opinions of these respondents.

4. Findings of the study

4.1. Attitudes toward E-Learning for corporate training

The proﬁle of the companies represented by the respondents in
the sample is shown in Table 3. As shown in this table, most of the
companies that have been included in the sample are located in
Istanbul, while there are also a considerable number of companies
from other cities in Turkey. In terms of employee population, 43.4%
have 500 or less employees while another substantial 39.4% have
500e2000 employees. There are only 17 companies with an
employee size of 2000 or higher. This is a good distribution for this
study in the sense that it includes organizations with different
employee populations that might have varying needs and approaches toward e-learning. The same idea is valid for company
revenue and it is important that there is not a very dominant
concentration in any one of the revenue groups. These companies
are nearly totally from the private sector with an exception of only
four subjects from the public sector. They are mostly multiple
location entities and the sectors they come from are distributed
diversely which is also crucial to achieve the heterogeneity needed
in this study.
While 60% of these companies have a Learning & Education
Department, the rest of them do not have one. The level of elearning applications in these companies is currently not very high.
In 44%, there are currently no e-learning applications, 45% use it at a
preliminary level and only a minority of 11% have common use of elearning applications. Consequently, those companies who have
spared a part of their training budget to e-learning have allocated
less than 25% to e-learning in total except for a very few exceptions.
This shows how preliminary e-learning investments are in these
companies.

An important issue investigated in this study is the overall
attitude of companies toward using e-learning for corporate
training. Managers have been asked to state their preference toward learning approaches, their general disposition toward elearning, and their expected usage of e-learning in the near future.
As seen in Table 4, with respect to preference toward learning approaches, most of the contacted organizations prefer blended
learning, which is a combination of traditional and e-learning for
corporate training. While a very minor group of four companies
have made a particular preference toward e-learning, another minor group has shown an intention to stick to traditional learning as
the most preferable approach for employee training.
As for the general attitude of the organization toward e-learning,
most of them are doubtful about its advantages and there are
conﬂicting views in organizations regarding this issue. Meanwhile,
there is a small group of organizations that have a totally negative
approach stating that they ﬁnd it inefﬁcient while another group of
companies believe that e-learning is effective and efﬁcient.
Findings about the expected usage of e-learning in the next three
years have shown that, although there is a minority of ﬁrms that
aim to stick to traditional learning mostly for the near future, a
larger part of the contacted companies is ready to adopt e-learning
to some extent in the short run. Overall, these results show that,
with the exception of a small group of companies holding a strong
position against adopting e-learning, most organizations have a
current tendency or intended future outlook that is mainly positive
toward e-learning.

early stages of adopting this innovation. There are many existing
theories about categorizing adoption stages such as Rogers (1983)

4.2. Advantages of E-Learning
One of the major objectives of this research is to investigate the
importance attached to the main perceived advantages and disadvantages of e-learning for corporate training programs. For this
purpose, contacted professionals in companies have been asked to
state the importance they attribute on behalf of their companies to
19 advantages and 12 disadvantages of e-learning. Consequently,
factor analyses have been conducted on the list of advantages and
disadvantages examined. Tables 5 and 6 show the relevant ﬁndings.
The 19 items measured with a ﬁve-point interval importance
scale assessing the perceived advantages of e-learning have been
grouped into four factors as a result of the factor analysis. The KMO
test of sampling adequacy is 0.818 which is higher than the
acceptable threshold of 0.500 and Bartlett's Test of Sphericity is
signiﬁcant at the 0.001 level which shows the suitability of the data
for factor analysis. The total variance explained by these four factors
is 71.2%. As seen in Table 5, this analysis has produced four factors
signifying the major areas of advantages in e-learning.
Factor 1 has been named Employee Commitment and Motivation
since it includes advantages that are mostly related with employees' acceptance of and involvement with e-learning. Factor 2
named as Convenience and Accessibility hosts issues that are more
relevant to the practical advantages of e-learning because of its
wide availability and time and place-free nature. Factor 3 which is
named Customization and Outsourcing includes advantages related
to the ﬂexibility of the content of e-learning and the opportunity to
receive superior training through being able to outsource this
function based on the use of e-learning technologies. Finally, Factor
4, Cost Effectiveness, covers two issues directly pinpointing the low
cost advantage of e-learning.
Descriptive ﬁndings depict that all of the advantages of elearning have been found important by the contacted professionals
in the companies. Thus, although e-learning is currently used at a
preliminary level in most organizations, managers' attitude toward
this learning approach is mainly positive and the expected usage of
e-learning can be anticipated to be higher in the near future.
With a comparative perspective, it can be seen that being able to
train employees in their own convenient time, the opportunity to
access a large number of employees, and being able to conduct elearning without the need to travel are perceived to be the most
crucial advantages showing that the practical beneﬁts of e-learning
are dominant. However, the fact that conducting e-learning will
attach a learning organization proﬁle to the company has also been
found important, signiﬁes that such intangible and more strategic
aspects of adopting e-learning are also essential for companies.
These ﬁndings are strongly parallel to previous studies in which
various antecedents have been identiﬁed and their impact on the
adoption of e-learning have been evaluated. The high importance
attached to the Employee Commitment and Motivation factor
conﬁrm the importance of intrinsic motivators encountered in
previous studies which have adopted motivational theories. Similarly, Convenience and Accessibility and Customization and
Outsourcing factors relate tightly to perceived usefulness which has

4.3. Disadvantages of E-Learning
The 12 items measured with a ﬁve-point interval importance
scale assessing the perceived disadvantages of e-learning have
been grouped into two factors as a result of the factor analysis. The
KMO test of sampling adequacy is 0.827 which is higher than the
acceptable threshold of 0.500 and Bartlett's Test of Sphericity is
signiﬁcant at the 0.001 level which shows the suitability of the data
for factor analysis. The total variance explained by these two factors
is 58.1%. As seen in Table 6, this analysis has produced two factors

signifying the major areas of disadvantages in e-learning. Factor 1
has been named Personal Disadvantages as it includes difﬁculties
that employees are expected to experience during the conduction
of e-learning such as challenges related to concentration and
communication. It also includes personal barriers that employees
might have against this learning approach such as their lack of
acquaintance with and their overall negative prejudice against it.
On the other hand, Factor 2, Organizational Disadvantages, comprises difﬁculties that may be experienced from the company
perspective such as not having the necessary infrastructure, qualiﬁed team, supportive management and competent employees to
beneﬁt from e-learning. It also includes practical issues such as the
difﬁculty of ﬁnding the right e-learning program so that a wise
investment is made and furthermore, protecting the illegal propagation of the e-learning material. Thus, companies are faced with
challenges both at the personal and behavioral side of the issue and
the technical and practical applicability of e-learning in the
company.

H. Kimiloglu et al. / Computers in Human Behavior 72 (2017) 339e349

Descriptive ﬁndings display that the importance levels attached
to the disadvantages of e-learning are evidently lower compared to
the advantages. This conﬁrms the positive outlook companies have
toward adopting e-learning. The obstacles that have been perceived
as relatively more important are mostly the personal issues
regarding the lack of face-to-face communication, difﬁculty to
conduct e-learning in a disciplined manner and motivation and
concentration difﬁculties. This ﬁnding is parallel with previous
studies that have shown the importance of the social aspects of elearning. Participants in such programs want to maintain sociability
and interaction and fear of losing the social value of the learning
process is an important drawback.
On the other hand, issues like the lack of technological infrastructure and skilled employees for e-learning or the difﬁculty of
keeping e-learning material proprietary have not been perceived as
important disadvantages. These results are also very promising
since they show that companies do not refrain from e-learning
because they do not have the necessary resources or strategic
commitment for this investment, but mainly because of the challenges related to conducting this approach in an efﬁcient and
effective manner.
Findings of this study about the advantages and disadvantages
of e-learning are important from one other perspective. Although
companies are currently at early stages of e-learning adoption, the
perceived advantages mostly outweigh the expected disadvantages. This is parallel to Herzberg's two-factor theory where there
are two groups of factors instigating motivation in organizational
environments: motivational factors and hygiene factors. Motivational factors are in line with the four advantage factors and hygiene factors are in line with the two disadvantage factors explored
in this study. While it is essential to reduce dissatisfying hygiene
factors to increase motivation, it is crucial to ﬂourish the satisfying
motivational factors in the long run. This study also conﬁrms this
perspective by showing that once personal and organizational
barriers are overcome, this new technology can penetrate into the
learning processes and culture in a company much more deeply
and ﬂuently.

5. Discussion and conclusion
The purpose of this exploratory study has been to ﬁnd out the
current and expected future outlook of e-learning in a sample of top
500 companies in Turkey to shed a light on the state of this industry
and the major issues surrounding its adoption for corporate
training. One of the main themes that emerge from the ﬁndings is
that most companies are willing to embrace e-learning for corporate training but they want to embed it more as a complementary
technology to accompany and enrich their traditional training
practices. The imminent tendency to prefer blended learning as the
most ideal training approach is a strong indicator of this.

This ﬁnding provides an interesting insight in terms of organizational change. According to Lewin's (1951) early theory of change
management, change can happen effectively only when existing
structures that can create barriers against change are dissolve and
reshaped. This is called the unfreeze-change-refreeze pattern.
However, Weick and Quinn (1999) propose that change does not
necessarily have to be “episodic” where organizational are inertial
and change is discontinuous and intentional. Instead, change can
also be “constant”, evolving and cumulative and in such cases, organizations are more self-organizing. The strong tendency toward
blended learning shows that transition to e-learning must happen
in this incremental pattern.
Another main trend that is evident according to these ﬁndings is
that regardless of their scale, most companies have a positive
outlook toward e-learning and are currently on the verge of
infusing it into their established practices. The perceived advantages are very prominent: a very convenient, highly cost-effective
and customizable training opportunity with strong potential to
capture substantial employee readiness and commitment. Besides,
attaining a learning organization proﬁle as leading businesses is
also a main drive. However, the disadvantages pull companies back
from rushing into this approach. Especially, human-related issues
prevail in the factors that hinder companies from adopting elearning. Additionally, companies are aware that for an e-learning
package to be effective, it must suit the company needs very well, a
competent team must be able to implement it and management
support is essential.

348

H. Kimiloglu et al. / Computers in Human Behavior 72 (2017) 339e349

These ﬁndings are important from two aspects: organizational
and personal. While previous studies in the literature have focused
heavily on behavioral adoption issues, this study shows that from
an organizational perspective, there are many important elements
to be considered for e-learning adoption. Cost-effectiveness, functionality, customizability and maintainability are the most prominent ones. Companies producing e-learning solutions must focus
more on totally customizable, ﬂexible packages that organizations
can adopt according to their needs and requirements.
On the other hand, personal factors are also very important.
Special attention must be paid to the importance of sociability and
interactivity in the e-learning environment. Companies do not
want to turn the learning experience into an automated, totally
systemized but non-human process. Therefore, it is also crucial that
the e-learning market hosts solutions with two-way and many-way
interactivity and real-time presence simulating the real learning
environment as much as possible. Such products will combine the
convenience and cost efﬁciency of this medium with virtual
engagement. Advanced technological approaches such as
augmented reality or intelligent human-computer interfaces can be
expected to prevail in this domain in the near future. Additionally,
social networking technologies must be embedded in these systems to assimilate employees to a more natural and engaging
learning environment.
Findings of this study show that, interestingly, once companies
step into adopting e-learning even at a preliminary level, most of
the previously mentioned obstacles begin to diminish. This
research has shown that companies that have some level of experience in e-learning perceive the advantages much more strongly
compared to those who have never used it at all. Similar to the
diffusion of most technologies in the market, once the resistance
threshold is passed, acceptance of and attitude toward the new
technology rapidly improves. This is the expected picture for the
Turkish corporate training market which seems to be in a stasis
with the pros and cons outweighing each other but awareness
about and willingness toward adopting this technology. Companies
should see the bigger picture and avoid approaching e-learning as a
cost-cutting technology but focus on its long-run diffusion and
sustainability in the company. Thus, it can become an integral part
of company technology and learning practices and continuous
performance assessment tools and incentives can accompany this
instead of occasional one. This promising state provides great opportunities for e-learning solution providers in the Turkish market.
The main limitation in this study is the small sample size
regarding the currently low level of adoption of e-learning in the
corporate training market in Turkey. As the industry evolves and
companies gain more experience in the ﬁeld, future research can be
conducted on the long-term impact of adopting e-learning in
companies. A more diverse and larger sample can be used to capture the potential from different company proﬁles, i.e. the potential
in SMEs. A wider variety of innovation characteristics must be
researched for this ﬁeld. For example, untapped variables from
innovation diffusion theories (i.e. trialability and observability dimensions of Rogers (1983)) or the reputation and brand image
enhancement value of using e-learning are some intrinsic motivators from an organizational perspective that yet remain to be
researched. Qualitative studies are also suggested to delve deeper
into perceptual issues, especially resistance barriers to ﬁnd out
ways to overcome them. Employee expectations in addition to
managerial objectives should also be captured to ensure how
higher employee motivation can be achieved in this area. Case
studies where a large scale reformation or an extensive e-learning
project is implemented could enlighten the transformation challenges with a real-life outlook.
