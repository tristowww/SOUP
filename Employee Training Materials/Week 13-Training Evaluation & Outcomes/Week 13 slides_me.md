## Training Return on Investment (ROI) {#slide-14}

-   ROI  = (Program Benefits -- Program Costs) / Program Costs
- 

Utility analysis: 

ΔU = (T)(N)(dt)( SDy ) -- (N)(C)

- 

14

  Symbol   Meaning
  -------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Δ U      Utility gain --- the net dollar value or benefit of the training
  T        Duration (in years) that the training effect is expected to last
  N        Number of individuals trained
  dt       Effect size --- how much better trained workers perform than untrained
  SDy      Standard deviation of job performance in dollar terms (i.e., variability in productivity value)
  C        Cost of training per person

## Giangreco et al., (2010) {#slide-19}

Are we doing the right thing? Food for thought on training evaluation and its context

Reevaluates the Kirkpatrick model of training, arguing that it is outdated and overly ridged for modern organizations

Critiques of the Kirkpatrick Model:

 Oversimplified and incomplete:  The model is both too simple to capture organizational context and too complex to be actually used in dynamic environments.

 Causal ambiguity:  No empirical consensus exists supporting the cause-effect flow from

 reactions → learning → behavior → results.

 Misplaced hierarchy:  The belief that results are always the "most important" ignores the unique goals of different training initiatives.

19

## Giangreco et al., (2010) {#slide-20}

20

::: {.smartart .list1 layout="list1"}
**Practitioner recommendations**

- Align evaluations with training goals and needs
- Reaction-level data is valuable for motivation, engagement, and feedback
- Soft skills training may not need formalized evaluation

**Researcher recommendations**

- Shift from reactive models (ROI or higher-level criteria) to more exploratory/context-dependent research 
- Explore intermittent evaluations for training and growth outcomes
- Focus on smaller organizations and different learning contexts 
:::

Structural Assessment:

## Kraiger  et al., (1995) {#slide-22}

Pilot Study Findings:

-  Trainees were 11 students in a grad multivariate class
- Scores increased for structural assessment after training, but these did not predict task performance 12 weeks later
- 

Implications:

- Using Structural Assessment depends a lot on instructional design
- However, traditional learning tests (multiple choice) may miss higher level cognitive changes (knowledge integration and abstraction)
-  Validity of Structural Assessment depends on content alignment and perceived relevance for transfer

## Sitzmann & Weinhardt (2019) {#slide-23}

Approaching evaluation from a multilevel perspective: A comprehensive analysis of the indicators in training effectiveness

Proposes a comprehensive framework for evaluating training effectiveness across multiple levels of analysis

Key Takeaways: Beyond Individual Learning

- Limited Scope of Traditional Evaluation:  Solely focusing on trainee reactions (satisfaction) and learning outcomes (knowledge gain) provides an incomplete picture. These traditional approaches neglect the broader impact of training on teams and the organization.
- Multilevel Perspective:  Expand evaluation scope to consider the impact of training at three levels:

<!-- -->

- Macro Level:  Organizational level including indicators across the whole company or business -- best for evaluating broad patterns of training
- Between-Person  Level:  Differences between individuals . Aggregates personal experiences across employes -- good for identifying which types of employees benefit from training
- Within-Person Level:  Individual level, tracking what happens within a single person across time -- best for moment-to-moment changes in a person's behavior or attitudes

## Training Utilization {#slide-25}

25

::: {.smartart .list1 layout="list1"}
**Training Enrollment**

- Between-person level: distinctions are made among employees to determine which employees enroll in training.
- Within-person level: the decision to enroll in training
- Macro: overarching percentage of employees that enroll in training.
- The theory of planned behavior

**Attrition from Training**

- Electing not to complete training lessons (within- person level) or training programs (between-person level), and the ratio of employees who enroll but do not complete training (at the macro level)
