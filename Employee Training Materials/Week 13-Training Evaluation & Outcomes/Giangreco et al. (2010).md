Abstract
Purpose – This paper aims to advance the debate regarding the use of training evaluation tools,
chiefly the Kirkpatrick model, in reaction to minimal use of the tools reported in the literature and the
economic changes that have characterised the industrialised world in the past 20 years.
Design/methodology/approach – The main argument – the need to design new evaluation tools –
emerges from an extensive literature review of criticism of the Kirkpatrick model. The approach is
deductive; the argument emerges from extant literature.
Findings – The main findings of the literature review show that the major criticisms of the
Kirkpatrick model, though rigorous, are not relevant in today’s post-industrial economy. Issues of
complexity, accuracy and refinement, which are relevant in stable industrial organisations, must be
revised in the new economic world.
Research limitations/implications – This paper is based on a literature review and presents a call
for new research. As such, it is not grounded in original empirical evidence, beyond that presented in
the cited articles.
Practical implications – The paper calls for training evaluation tools that align better with modern
organisational reality. If the research community responds to this call, the results will benefit
practitioners directly. This paper also presents practical advice about the use of existing evaluation
techniques.
Originality/value – A new angle on criticisms of existing training evaluation systems does not
reiterate classic criticisms based on logic and mathematics but rather takes a pragmatic and economic
approach. Thus, this paper offers evidence of theoretically grounded paradoxes of the consequences of
existing criticisms of training evaluation.

1. Introduction
When organisational behaviour and human resource management scholars approach
the topic of training evaluations, they agree on a starting point: Kirkpatrick’s
Hierarchical Model of Training Outcomes (1959a, b, 1960a, b, 1967, 1996a). According
to Kirkpatrick’s goal-oriented model, reactions, learning, behaviour and results

Level 2, learning, pertains to the logics, methodologies and techniques acquired by
trainees, without considering their job-related application. Level 3, or behaviour,
relates to the real usage of new principles and practices learned by trainees to modify
and improve their behaviour and performance at work. Finally, level 4, results, entails

the impact of training on, for example, costs, productivity, quality or morale,
depending on the desired results of the training. The model is hierarchical, because the
perceived satisfaction of trainees (reactions) has an impact on the inclination to study
and learn, which in turn can drive behaviour to the extent that it generates
organisational results. During the almost half a century since its first version, the
Kirkpatrick model has significantly influenced the development of other models (e.g.
Warr et al., 1970; Hamblin, 1974; Cannon-Bowers et al., 1995; Kaufman et al., 1995;
Molenda et al., 1996; Phillips, 1997, 2003; Cascio, 1999) and dominated debate on the
topic (e.g. Noe, 1986; Plant and Ryan, 1992; Olsen, 1998; Warr et al., 1999; Blanchard
et al., 2000; Tracy et al., 2001), as well as stimulating contributions from many
practitioners (e.g. Swanson and Sleezer, 1987; Talbot, 1992; Mann and Robertson, 1996;
Albernathy, 1999; Toplis, 2001; Tyler, 2002). In a similar way, it has prompted a certain
amount of criticism (e.g. Clement, 1982; Alliger and Janak, 1989; Tannenbaum and
Woods, 1992; Brown, 2005; Sitzmann et al., 2008), including Holton’s (1996) attack and
Kirkpatrick’s (1996b) response.
For almost half a century, the Kirkpatrick model also has been the focal point of
discussions about how and why training should be evaluated, and research has tried to
extend it in different directions. Nevertheless, modern society and the economic
infrastructure in which we move today are much different than those in which
Kirkpatrick developed his model. Today, the principal agent of the economy is not the
corporation, as it was 50 years ago. New technologies and social emancipation give the
individual a much more dominant role in the economic playing field (Friedman, 2005).
Tightly coupled organisational systems, which characterised the time when the first
training evaluation models were developed, have been replaced by loosely coupled
networks of individuals, in which mobility is both a skill and a requirement. New terms
also characterize the economy, such as the e-lance economy (Malone and Laubacher,
1998), the knowledge economy, or the post-industrial economy (Sculley and Byrne,
1987). In this emerging world, the knowledge worker is the centre of productivity and
economic life and must be nurtured, challenged and constantly pleased to remain with
an organisation (Alvesson, 2000). In this world, the individual also must constantly
learn, just as organisations must become learning organisations (Senge, 1990).
Learning (and training) is the key to survival, but are the traditional ways to measure it
still valid and relevant?
The effects on training, its meaning and its evaluation of the societal changes that
continue to emerge largely have been ignored by the research community. Yet
practitioners likely have incorporated these changes into their modus operandi,
without reconsideration of the reasons and the impacts of their decisions.
This article presents a discussion of training evaluation that takes into account the
consequences of socio-economic changes, with the aim of opening a debate about the
future challenges, for researchers and practitioners alike, when dealing with training

Along these lines of thought, we introduce the extremely relevant and persistent

and some counter-explanations that, in the end, converge with the same concluding

advancement of relevant theory? The second and more general objective of this article
is to contribute to the debate about the balance among the need to identify measures,
the feasibility of doing so and the usefulness of such information. To what extent in the
post-industrial economy should training be evaluated? And how?
This article is organised as follows. In the next section, we discuss the major limits
ascribed to the application of the Kirkpatrick model. We then focus on the matter of
training performance measurement both for human resource management (HRM)
scholars and practitioners, debating the feasibility issue and the added value offered by
the training evaluation process. We conclude by questioning the benefits that the
model provides to training stakeholders and offer suggestions for four key elements to
consider when designing new training evaluation systems.
2. Alleged limits of the Kirkpatrick Model
To open the debate, we start by revisiting the classic criticism of the Kirkpatrick
model, in light of recent societal changes. Of the many scholars who have commented,
Bates’s (2004) extensive analysis emphasises three major limits of the Kirkpatrick
model and its use:
(1) The oversimplification and the incompleteness of the model.
(2) The untested presence of a cause-effect relationship among the different levels.
(3) The unproven progressive importance of information moving from the first
level to subsequent ones.
2.1 The oversimplification and incompleteness of the model
In relation to the first limitation, we might argue that the model, rather than being
oversimplified (Alliger et al., 1997), is perhaps too complex. Abundant literature
follows the lines drawn by the Kirkpatrick model and converges on one main
conclusion: Companies are not keen to evaluate training programmes fully (Mann and
Robertson, 1996; Wang and Wilcox, 2006), so they tend to remain at level 1 and only
evaluate reactions. The poor usage of the full model appears confirmed by the
relatively low rates of companies that undertake an evaluation that goes up to level 4:
15 per cent according to Dixon (1990), 20 per cent as stated by Shelton and Alliger
(1993), 31 per cent as indicated by Olsen (1998), 2 per cent according to Pershing and
Pershing (2001) and Lee and Pershing (2002), 7 per cent by Van Buren and Erskine
(2002), 11 per cent for Sugrue (2003) and 13 per cent as indicated by Sugrue and Rivera
(2005).
Traditional literature cites several reasons that companies do not use the higher
levels based on in rationales typical of the industrial economy: lack of necessary

Another argument for the minimal application of the full model is its
incompleteness. Even if applied up to level 4 – or level 5, which considers the
return on investment (ROI) of training (Cascio, 1999; Phillips, 2003) – the Kirkpatrick
model cannot take into consideration the influence of both contingency and external
factors (Taylor et al., 1998), nor all the contextual and organisational elements
(McEvoy, 1997). In a similar manner, it ignores the influence of HRM policies and
practices inside the organisation, as well as the factors that affect individual conditions
(Tracy et al., 1995). Thus, though the problem of incompleteness exists, its solution
with an augmented model would probably increase the already critical problem of
usability. We cannot include some or all of the allegedly missing elements (e.g. Kraiger,
2002) without increasing the complexity of the model and thus limiting its use in the
real business world even further.
The most common answer to the problems of incompleteness and simplicity has
been to augment the model, either vertically with more levels or horizontally with more
contextual and organisational elements. Some examples of these extensions include
system-based evaluation training models, such as the CIRO (context, input, reaction,
outcome) model (Warr et al., 1970), CIPP (context, input, process, product) model
(Worthen and Sanders, 1987), IPO (input, process, output, outcome) model (Bushnell,
1990) and TVS (training validation system) approach (Fitz-Enz, 1994), all of which try
to deal with some of the critiques of Kirkpatrick’s model.
In our view, this debate should not focus on the model and its development but
rather on a rediscovery of the roots of the development of the model itself. The usage
characterizes the validity of these tools, not just the mathematically validated
correlations. By adopting this angle, we can highlight that the model may not be too
simple but rather too complex; it may not be incomplete but rather too rich for the
organisations of our times. With these apparently paradoxical statements, we attempt
to find the roots of the debate about evaluation systems and thus reasons to further
research on this topic.
2.2 The untested presence of cause-effect relationships among different levels
The second limitation, the lack of a proven cause-effect relationship among the outputs
of the four different levels, receives support from some scholars (e.g. Alliger and Janak,
1989). Moreover, the Kirkpatrick model has been the subject of harsh criticism with
regard to the possibility of identifying a correlation between trainees’ satisfaction with
training and the level of knowledge, and then the modification of behaviours, leading to
the production of results (e.g. Holton, 1996). On this point, Kirkpatrick has been a little
contradictory. In an early version of the model, he noted some intrinsic limitations and
gaps, pointing out that readers would need to consider the four dimensions as isolated
and manage them one by one. Yet almost 30 years later, in response to Holton’s (1996)
article, Kirkpatrick (1996b, p. 23) asserted that “my model (or taxonomy) shows the

relationship among the four levels.” The debate remains open, and the results are quite
contradictory. For example, according to Tan et al. (2003), negative reactions predict a
higher degree of learning, whereas Russ-Eft et al. (2005) find that negative reactions
predict lower learning. According to McEvoy (1997), trainees often tend to complete their
training in a state of euphoria, such that their immediate favourable reaction is much
higher than feedback reactions measured later. Dixon (1990) also concludes that good
reactions do not necessarily ensure good learning. Analysing the same relationship cited
in Cannon-Bowers et al.’s (1995) model of training effectiveness, Rowold (2007) reaches
the same conclusion. Finally, in an extensive and more recent nomological analysis of
trainee reactions, Sitzmann et al. (2008, p. 21) state that “it is inaccurate to claim that
reactions do not matter with regard to learning”, which again underlines the complexity
and ambivalence of the cause-effect direction among the different levels (Hook and
Bunce, 2001). In conclusion, though some connection might exist among the different
levels, the issue remains controversial (e.g. Alliger and Janak, 1989).
To further this debate, we might argue that the causal link sought among
Kirkpatrick’s levels results from a mechanistic ideal, appropriate for the industrial
economy, rather from than an objective need in the post-industrial economy. The main
outcome that we obtain from the apparently controversial results regarding the link
among the levels is the recognition of a very high level of contextualisation and
possible interpretation of results. This conclusion might seem aberrant in an industrial
society, but it is perfectly acceptable in the post-industrial one. The contradictory
results of these studies point to the need to re-evaluate evaluation systems within the
new emergent context, in which cause-effect relationships are a secondary need and
flexibility of use and adaptation are primary.
2.3 The unproven progressive importance of information moving between levels
According to the industrial economy way of thinking, if each level is capable of
producing significant outputs, the level of importance should be increasingly more
significant from one level to the next, implying that the level 4 results are the best
measure (Lorenzet et al., 2005). In the post-industrial economy though, the issue is not
higher results but rather different natures. Training programmes are not always
designed to produce outputs and significant information for all four levels (Alliger and
Janak, 1989). As often happens, a company might organise training courses aimed only
at developing a good team spirit and reinforcing staff motivation (level 1: reactions).
Alternatively, a logistics company could develop a safety-training course for forklift
truck drivers, because a recent law made it obligatory for them to pass a national
qualifying exam (level 2: learning). The company managing the security of an airport
might train its staff about new procedures regarding liquids and personal computers in
carry-on luggage, with the objective of ensuring staff members pay more attention to
details (level 3: behaviour). Finally, a public hospital may train its radiology
technicians in the use of a new computer tomography machine that will allow them to
conduct more tests per day (level 4: results).
In these examples, a discussion about the progressive importance of the different
levels becomes of secondary significance compared with the purpose of training and its
interpretation by the employer and employees. Even in a traditional, rational thinking
context, it would be utopian to assume that the effect of the training action stops at a
given level, without influencing the others (e.g. Seyler et al., 1998). For example,

In conclusion, the Kirkpatrick model certainly has limitations. The question that
remains is the way in which these limitations might be overcome. The characteristics
of the post-industrial economy and the elevation of the role of the individual mean that

the most obvious solution might not be the most appropriate one. Augmenting the

the model remains completely open to debate. Augmenting the model actually could
mean decreasing the number of levels, moving away from causal relationships or even
eliminating the different levels.
Different directions in the discourse on evaluation will affect the world of practice as
well as the world of theory, and both should discuss and be ready to act on the
resultant challenges. The following section addresses practitioners and researchers
individually, leading into some propositions for further work in this area.
3. Questions and reflections for practitioners
In light of the economic trends discussed previously and the controversial impact of
limitations of the Kirkpatrick model, we focus on two issues of extreme importance to
practitioners: the value and costs of training evaluation programmes.
The principal question relates to the usefulness of training evaluations. It is
essential to understand the extent to which added value derives from the complex
process of evaluation (Eseryel, 2002). Furthermore, the notion of value clearly differs
for various professional figures, including training designers, trainees and the direct
managers of trainees (Brown and Gerhardt, 2002). People who take these roles form a
heterogeneous group, with different perceptions of the value of training evaluation
(Blanchard and Thacker, 2007). If they believe that a benefit exists, they also want to
understand all the costs of training evaluation.
The initial attempt to clarify the cost and value questions requires a conceptual
framing of training within the overall realm of HRM. Thus, a first essential point is
establishing the goals of training correctly to ensure the correct choice of measurement
tools (Sitzmann et al., 2008). If, for example, the goal of a training course is to increase a
sense of membership in the organisation, an assessment of trainee reactions would be a
proper measuring tool, because these reactions are important indicators of trainees’
receptivity to attitude changes (Sitzmann et al., 2008). If a company wants to assess the
outputs of the training initiative fully, it should consider potential psychological side
effects, which may have negative consequences on the organisational climate.
Applying a training evaluation process often entails psychological tensions that exert
different intensity, related to the level of measurement, especially when related to
learning evaluations conducted through tests and exercises (consider the stress of
sitting for an examination), to which adults normally react with psychological tension
and resistance (Andrews, 2004). Such evaluations also may create excessive
expectations for trainees, because the formal acquisition of new competences

This risk also exists if the desired outcome pertains to two subsequent levels (learning
and behaviour). Again using the example of training to increase a sense of
membership, will the use of an extensive evaluation system really maximise the
benefits of the training initiative? The answer is clearly negative, but this line of
argument also can be generalised to highlight a contingent and contextual dimension
of evaluation that must constantly be taken into account. The value of an evaluation of
training is not universal but rather is part of the goal that the different stakeholders
have for the training itself (Sitzmann et al., 2008).
Furthermore, most organisations focus on evaluating reactions, because the efforts
required to conduct formal evaluations at subsequent levels involve tangible and
intangible costs that might be higher than the potential benefits (Cullen et al., 1978;
Green, 1997). Accurate and proper evaluations of training programs demand the use of
additional resources, whether money or time. Organisations that make evaluation part
of their standard management tools require accuracy in terms of both content and
process. Content refers to the monitoring of several hard variables (e.g. productivity,
quality, material costs) and soft variables (e.g. improved job satisfaction, improved
teamwork, increased organisational commitment), collected both prior to and after the
training (McKillip, 2001). Process accuracy instead involves the use of test and control
groups, for example (Lilly, 2001).
The need for parsimony in the use of these resources becomes particularly cogent in
times of economic contraction, “when . . . most decision makers will be more impressed
with an indication that a programme taught employees new skills, changed employees’
behaviours, and contributed to improved performance than with the knowledge that
trainees liked the training” (Tannenbaum and Woods, 1992, p. 68). Yet a paradox again
emerges. During economic contraction, training is among the first investments to be
reconsidered (Taylor et al., 1998), especially in small and medium-sized enterprises
(SMEs) in Europe (Gray and Lawless, 2000), which requires more information about
the impact of training, even though there is little training to evaluate, and companies
are not interested in funding a process to monitor it.
In conclusion, the cost and value of training remain complex issues that require
specific thinking, modified from case to case. They cannot be hypothesised – as many
practitioners will appreciate – as linear, direct relations between costs and outcomes.
In many cases, spending less actually may lead to better results. Furthermore, and in
line with the post-industrial debate, the same training evaluation cannot be used for
different trainees, as if they were a homogeneous group, because their reactions and
expectations depend on the initiator of the training (e.g. direct manager). In general,
this argument suggests moving towards evaluations that are tailored to
manager-trainee tandems, for which cost and value considerations change from case
to case within the framework discussed in this section.

The literature on training evaluation is characterised by a very significant
phenomenon: The number of studies that address all four levels of the Kirkpatrick model
is considerable, especially compared with the small number of companies that apply the
integral model. Several of these studies recommend augmentations of the existing

number is a neat package, a trainer’s dream! In some cases, it can be obtained. In other
cases, while seductive, it may not be worth the effort” (Lilly, 2001, p. 1).
Not surprisingly, the number of published studies that deeply investigate only level
1 of the Kirkpatrick model is very small, even though “anecdotal and other evidence
suggest that reaction measures are the most widely used evaluation criteria in applied
settings” (Arthur et al., 2003, p. 235). Despite the widespread use of level 1,
investigating something this “simple” is not really attractive to researchers, and much
of the previously presented discussion highlights the poor prestige of level 1 research.
Thus, the choice not to use higher-level evaluations is not associated with positive
reflective skills but rather with negative and lesser capabilities (i.e. companies do not
do more because they do not have the skills or money or because they do not
understand the model). Yet participants can provide:

Considering its widespread use and potential benefits, investigating trainees’ reactions
offers great potential, which does not necessarily imply that evaluating reactions is
sufficient to assess training but rather that in some contexts, it represents an
acceptable and enriching solution. Despite this potential, journals are still reluctant to
publish studies that focus solely on trainees’ reactions (Arthur et al., 2003).
The direction of previous research on training evaluation underlines an ethical
problem that Bates (2004) refers to as the principle of beneficence. If our duty, as HRM
scholars studying training evaluation, is to determine whether training is effective and
what measures need to be taken to improve the training process, then the debate and
interest in training evaluation may need to shift both contents and process. On the one
hand, many scholars acknowledge that the Kirkpatrick model is incomplete (e.g. Tracy
et al., 1995). Yet on the other hand, we also should question whether academics help
HRM practitioners when we insist on dedicating research efforts to analysing very
advanced dimensions (e.g. ROI of training; Cascio, 1999; Phillips, 2003), which tends to
produce results and implications that interest a very restricted group of companies.
This overemphasis on the relationship between training and company results –
summative evaluation as a rational thinking that fits the industrial era – over time turns
attention away from analysing actual work situations and workers’ dynamics (Brown

focus on more exploratory studies. This research should scrutinise the reasons
associated with both training and its evaluation and reconceptualise them in a
post-industrial society. Why do people get sent to training? How do employees make
sense of this training? Does training help keep employees with a company? Should
training evaluation test organisational goals or employee sensemaking? What
measures are really important in the modern world?
This approach will require different research methods that extend beyond the
numbers produced by the American Society of Training and Development (ASTD)[1]
and move into the offices of workers and human resource managers. We call for case
studies and ethnographic research that can re-establish the fundament on which to
build new, more relevant work. This fundament eventually may appear similar to the
one we currently use, but the minor differences and unique details might have
profound impacts on the way we interpret and appreciate training.
5. Discussion
In the end, we highlight the need to close the gap between the post-industrial reality of
businesses and the academic world, which would enable HRM practitioners and
scholars to exchange the mutual benefits of their work. Kirkpatrick’s (1959a, b)
“hierarchical model of training outcomes” may be paying the price for its fame. Its
global use has made it universal, but as is true of anything that achieves this status, the
premises on which it was created have become so embedded in practices that they now
are practically invisible. As times change, the post-industrial world incrementally has
“grown out” of the model, eventually coming to a point at which the model may lack
the capability to describe the true complexity and diversity of reality.
Similarly, researchers need to pay more attention to the context of use of a training
evaluation and its real goals. Criteria should attempt to segment and discriminate among
the different types of training, and thus the evaluation purposes. Focusing on outcomes
stimulates both HRM practitioners and scholars to recognise the elements that
differentiate analyses, design, development and implementation of training, depending
on the size of the company, the type of audience, the nature of the contents and other
situational factors. In a similar manner, training evaluation approaches, methodologies
and techniques must be devised and customised accordingly. As Santos and Stuart
(2003, p. 27) point out, “such [existing] frameworks are often overly deterministic, are
insensitive to workplace context and typically obscure as much as they reveal”. We
recommend moving away from a deterministic, instinct-based use of evaluation tools
and towards more reflective approaches, typical of post-industrial economy, in which
existing models become just one of many tools available and in which, for example,
non-evaluation might be just as effective as a formal, level 4 evaluation. We propose four
elements of interest in the post-industrial knowledge economy that we should consider

5.1 Size of the company
Shared evidence reveals that whereas large firms tend to use levels 3 and 4, SMEs often
stop at level 1 (reactions). These differences become more evident when we consider
experiences and studies in the USA and Europe; the former generally are based on
ASTD samples, which feature US companies with more than 100 employees. In
contrast, Plant and Ryan (1992) investigate a sample of 600 European companies and
find that none of them use evaluations above level 3.
Although they are very important in terms of their turnover, profit and large
workforce, ASTD companies are not representative of businesses worldwide. Europe is
defined by the small enterprise of the post-industrial world, with 10 million SMEs that
employ 66 per cent of the workforce and represent 55 per cent of overall turnover. In
Italy, the European country with the most SMEs (3.5 million), Giangreco et al. (2008) find
that smaller firms engage in fewer use evaluations beyond level 1. However, rather than
dismissing this choice a negative or incorrect managerial decision, we should turn to
SMEs for inspiration regarding why reaction evaluations might produce positive results.
Training evaluations in SMEs thus might provide considerable added value, because
these companies suffer the scarcest resources and can perceive the clear connection
between actions and the results of training better than can large organisations.
5.2 Type of audience
Trainees that take different positions in the business and professional life cycle, such
as pre-employees, new recruits, workers in development, workers in reconversion or
workers in exit, all require training that uses different didactical methodologies and
techniques (Costa and Giannecchini, 2005). Other segmentation criteria for trainees,
such as hierarchical positions, current performance or potentials, similarly imply
different approaches and actions. Training oriented to different populations of workers
necessitates more customised approaches to evaluating the training initiatives. For
example, in segments of knowledge workers, training provides a tool for motivating
and retaining employees (Alvesson, 2000). In this case, typical of post-industrial
organisations, reaction evaluation effectively assesses the training objectives.
5.3 Contents of training
Whether a training initiative attempts to act on the area of knowledge (savoir),
capabilities (savoir faire) or attitudes and behaviours (savoir être) influences the
didactical methodologies used and the training outputs. For example, a training
initiative meant to reinforce team spirit among a group of knowledge workers will
produce outputs very different in nature and temporality than a training course
focused on the correct use of new software. Available quantitative results in the short
term (software course) versus qualitative results in the mid- and long term (team
building) should influence the design and use of a training evaluation system.

the level of unionisation is high (e.g. Italy, France), training and its evaluation become
significant issues in both industrial relations and bargaining. In such countries,
learning evaluations – through tests, evaluation interviews and exams – and the
evaluation of attitudes and behaviours – through pilot tests, simulations, follow-up
questionnaires and self-assessment – can be perceived as too intrusive, especially by
the unions (Lilly, 2001). This perception leaves space for only evaluations of reactions
and organisational results. Learning more about this issue would be helpful for both
HRM scholars and practitioners.
Finally, we agree with the pragmatic approach of some scholars (e.g. Sackett and
Mullen, 1993) that indicates it is better to evaluate imperfectly rather than not evaluate at
all. Practitioners know well that training choices, changes and decisions must proceed
through imperfect and perfectible processes (Sackett and Mullen, 1993). However, the
size of the firm, type of trainees, content of training and other elements deeply influence
the choices of HRM practitioners in terms of planning and implementing training
initiatives; in a similar manner, these factors should be considered driving forces in the
context of training evaluations. This perspective is especially critical in light of
practitioners’ de facto requests for help, including calls for more feasible and customised
solutions. Because the final aim of any researcher is to produce benefits for someone
(Bates, 2004), HRM scholars should first deepen understating of training evaluations in
the modern context and then, with the help of practitioners, find feasible solutions that
offer the optimum cost-opportunity ratio, while also avoiding the paradox that a better
job is the enemy of a good job. That is, our message is not to discourage companies from
using complex and sophisticated evaluation tools (Morgan and Casper, 2000); rather, we
want to highlight that, similar to other processes, the choice of auditing and control
systems for training must be coherent with the reality analysed instead of based on the
blind application of universal models. This thinking may encourage further research into
intrinsically relevant models that can asses training and that should emerge from the
field rather than being imposed top-down, mindful of small economic realities, a
workforce that is highly mobile and intellectually strong, organisations that are
becoming flatter and responsibilities that keep increasing. In other words, these studies
should start from the discussion presented herein and take seriously the implications of
the post-industrial economy, including imagining elaborate models that stop at (an
enhanced) level 1 or that have only one level but ensure that level is relevant, feasible and
economically sound.
6. Final considerations
The discrepancy between what HRM practitioners do to evaluate training and what
HRM scholars study continues to grow. It is time to bring these positions closer
together, in an effort to understand their reciprocal needs. On the one hand, HRM
scholars should concentrate more on providing customised and feasible solutions. On
the other hand, HRM practitioners need to be more open-minded in assessing training
and try to overcome the polarised paradox of either disproportionate expectations of
training as a panacea for all problems (i.e. no measure is good enough!) or perceiving

training merely as a drain on the resources of the organisation and the time of Are we doing the
individual employees (i.e. why bother?).
right thing?
As companies enter and function in the post-industrial economy, research should
follow the directions indicated by a few scholars (e.g. Brown and Gerhardt, 2002;
Brown and Reed, 2002) who maintain that what we need to assess is the use of training
and how it contributes to company performances (and therefore employee
performance) (i.e. Brinkerhoff, 2003), rather than assessing the training itself.
