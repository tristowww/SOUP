Impact of Task Experience and Individual Factors
on Training-Emphasis Ratings
J. Kevin Ford, Eleanor M. Smith, Douglas J. Sego, and Miguel A. Quinones
This study included 2 sets of analyses examining the impact of task experience and individual
factors on task ratings of training emphasis. Aerospace ground equipment mechanics in the U.S.
Air Force completed surveys 8 months and 12 months after formal training. Mechanics rated how
much formal training they would recommend for a sample of tasks. In the cross-sectional analyses,
with tenure constant at 12 months, an individual's breadth of experience (number of tasks performed) and level of self-efficacy were found to have significant effects on training-emphasis
ratings. The change analyses examined factors influencing changes in ratings of training emphasis
from 8 to 12 months on the job. Results indicated that mechanics whose breadth of experience or
self-efficacy perceptions increased over time tended to increase their ratings of training emphasis.
Implications for understanding the task analysis rating process are discussed.

Training-needs assessment is the first step in the systematic
development of a training program. It addresses issues of where
in the organization training is needed (organizational analysis),
what needs to be taught in the training program (task analysis),
and who needs to be trained (person analysis; Goldstein, 1986,
1991).
The task analysis phase of the training-needs-assessment process is critical, as it provides information necessary for the development of a training plan. This plan often specifies the training
program's content, the emphasis placed during training on the
various content areas, and the setting of the criterion of success
relevant to the training content (Ford & Wroten, 1984). As
noted by Goldstein (1986), a task analysis results in a description of the work operations performed on the job and the conditions under which the job is performed. A task analysis can
focus on the actual tasks performed or the knowledge, skills,
and abilities needed to perform those tasks (Goldstein, 1991).

see McGehee & Thayer, 1961; Moore & Dutton, 1978), a popular method for identifying specific job duties is the task inventory approach (Christal, 1974; Christal & Weissmuller, 1988;

Although personnel and training research have advanced understanding of factors affecting task ratings, there are several
limitations that need to be addressed. First, work experience
has been defined narrowly as tenure on the job. Measures of job
tenure, though easy to obtain, focus on an individual's time on a
job rather than on the actual experiences an individual gains
while on the job (Ford, Sego, Quinones, & Speer, 1991). The
focus on actual experiences can expand the understanding of
why differences in tenure may be related to differences in task
analysis ratings. Perhaps more important, from a levels-of-analysis perspective, is that tenure is not necessarily the appropriate
measure of experience to use when examining the effects of
work experience on task analysis ratings. Because these ratings
are being made at the task level of analysis, experience should

also be measured at the task level {e.g., see Rousseau, 1985). By
using tenure as a measure of experience, one may be missing
task-level variance that influences task analysis ratings. In the
present study, we examined whether variations in tasklevel experiences do in fact influence task analysis ratings.
A second limitation of the existing literature is that it has
been cross-sectional in nature. Sanchez (1990) suggested that
task ratings such as frequency, importance, and difficulty to
learn may change as an incumbent masters the job. He cited
evidence that engineering technicians with low tenure tended
to be more lenient in their ratings of the difficulty of learning a
task than incumbents with high tenure. Similarly, Landy and
Vasey (1991) found that police officers with more years of experience rated themselves as being more frequently involved in
noncriminal interventions than did officers with less tenure.
The cross-sectional nature of the research, though, does not
allow for the examination of factors that affect changes in task
ratings over time. In this study we present both cross-sectional
and longitudinal analyses. The two sets of analyses provide evidence for the impact of various factors on task ratings as well as
for the extent to which within-rater changes in task ratings are
related to individual changes in tenure and task-level experiences.
A third limitation is that the existing research has mainly
focused on the effects of demographic characteristics, such as
race, sex, and educational level, on task ratings. This research
(mainly from the personnel selection literature) has been driven
more by important practical issues, such as test fairness, than
by theoretical considerations. Several researchers have called
for the incorporation of more psychologically meaningful variables into this type of research (e.g., Ford & Noe, 1987; Landy &
Vasey, 1991; Sanchez, 1990). It has been suggested that variables
such as ability, self-efficacy, and rater cognitive complexity are
not only of more theoretical interest, but may also have a larger
impact on task ratings than do demographic variables (Ford &
Noe, 1987; Landy & Vasey, 1991). In our research we focused on
cognitive ability and self-efficacy as two psychologically meaningful factors that may affect task analysis ratings.
To summarize, Harvey (1991) cited the need for a broader
perspective on the study of factors that influence task analysis
ratings. The purpose of the present study was to expand this
type of research by examining the impact of task-level experiences and individual characteristics on one type of task analysis
rating: recommended training emphasis. Recommended training-emphasis ratings indicate how much emphasis should be
placed on a task in a formal training program for newcomers to
an organization (Christal, 1974; Ruck et al., 1987). Our rationale for including the factors expected to affect recommended
training-emphasis ratings is given below.

Task Experience
Research has indicated that individuals at the same level of
tenure show differences in the amount of task experience obtained on the job. For example, Schmitt and Cohen (1989) empirically demonstrated that individuals holding the same job at
the same level of tenure differ in terms of the number of tasks
performed and the frequency of task performance. Similarly,
Ford, Quifiones, Sego, & Sorra (1992) found a large amount of

variation in the breadth, or number of trained tasks that trainees were allowed to perform on the job, as well as in the activity
level, or how often trainees performed these tasks. An important question to be answered is how these differences in task
experience for individuals at the same level of tenure affect
their recommended training-emphasis ratings.
In regard to task experience, we hypothesized that variations
in the breadth and activity level of on-the-job experiences
would be related to differences in recommended training-emphasis ratings. In particular, we expected that incumbents who
had performed a large number of tasks on the job (high
breadth) would see the utility of training for those tasks in a
formal training setting. Thus, individuals with greater breadth
of experience should rate training needs as higher relative to
those with lower breadth.
Furthermore, although it was more speculative, we hypothesized that the activity level, or the number of times each task is
performed on the job, would also affect task analysis ratings.
Tasks that are performed more often on the job may be given
higher ratings of recommended training emphasis by incumbents than those tasks performed less often.
In addition, we hypothesized that changes in task experiences would be positively related to changes in recommended
training-emphasis ratings. Individuals whose task experiences
increase over time will see more of a need for formal training
and will rate training needs as higher, compared with individuals whose task experiences remain more stable over time.

Individual Characteristics
Self-efficacy is the belief or confidence in one's capability to
perform a task (Bandura, 1977). It is a dynamic judgment reflecting a variety of personal and task-related performance determinants as well as individual attributional processes (Gist &
Mitchell, 1992).
Self-efficacy has been associated with learning and performance in a number of settings (Gist & Mitchell, 1992). For
example, self-efficacy has been found to be related to learning
in the training setting as well as to the transfer of skills to the
job (e.g., Ford et al., 1992; Gist, Schwoerer, & Rosen, 1989; Gist,
Stevens, & Bavetta, 1991). In addition, Gist et al. (1989) found
that individuals attending a training program may gain confidence and self-efficacy as a result of success in training.
In regard to task ratings, we hypothesized that individuals
with high self-efficacy perceptions were more likely to rate
training needs as higher. High self-efficacy individuals are confident in the skills that they have gained (Gist et al., 1989).
These skills have been gained through formal training and
other learning experiences on the job. It is likely that high selfefficacy individuals would attribute their confidence, in part,
to the formal training they received. This would result in higher
ratings of recommended formal-training emphasis by high selfefficacy individuals compared with low self-efficacy individuals.
In addition, self-efficacy is a fluid and changeable construct
(Gist & Mitchell, 1992). Thus, we also hypothesized that individuals who became more self-efficacious over time would provide task ratings that suggested a greater need for formal train-

ing than those individuals who tended to remain fairly stable in
their perceptions.
It has been suggested that the cognitive ability or knowledge
level of the rater may affect task analysis ratings (Landy & Vasey, 1991). Cognitive ability has been found to be a strong predictor of learning in a training program and of subsequent performance on the job (e.g., Schmitt, Gooding, Noe, & Kirsch,
1984). Therefore, we examined the impact of cognitive ability
on recommended training-emphasis ratings. Because highability individuals may benefit most from training, we hypothesized that they would be more positive about the need for
training and thus provide greater training-emphasis ratings.

Research Design Issues
A strength of this study is our examination of the impact of
multiple factors on recommended training-emphasis ratings.
Conceptually, task experience, self-efficacy, and cognitive ability are distinct factors. In addition, Gist and Mitchell (1992)
have suggested that ability and task experience are only two of a
number of internal and external cues that may influence an
individual's self-efficacy perceptions. Finally, empirical research has shown that task-experience factors, such as breadth
and activity level, are not highly related to perceptions of self-efficacy or cognitive ability (Ford et al, 1992).
From a theoretical perspective, the three factors provide alternative explanations for why task analysis ratings, such as recommended training emphasis, might be affected by various
rater characteristics. For example, if the factors of breadth and
activity level affect task ratings, this suggests that individuals
attend to quantifiable aspects of their task experiences when
making decisions regarding what tasks should be trained or
emphasized in a formal program. On the other hand, if self-efficacy has a major impact on task-emphasis ratings, this suggests that the emphasis ratings reflect, in part, the psychological orientation of the individual toward his or her own level of
performance mastery. Cognitive ability effects would suggest
that task analysis ratings are inherently bound up with more
stable rather than (or in addition to) the more dynamic or
changeable characteristics of self-efficacy or task experiences.
Consequently, this study provided a unique opportunity to examine which type or types of explanations are more plausible.
To examine these issues, we conducted two sets of analyses.
In the cross-sectional analyses, we held tenure constant to examine the effects of task-level experiences and individual characteristics on ratings of recommended training emphasis. For the
change analyses, we examined the impact of within-person
changes in task-experience factors and in individual characteristics on changes in training-emphasis ratings.
Method

Sample
Participants were aerospace ground equipment (AGE) mechanics in
the U.S. Air Force. AGE mechanics use powered and nonpowered
equipment to repair the various aircraft used by the U.S. Air Force. The
equipment varies from small jet engines used to start jet aircraft to tow
bars used when moving aircraft. New recruits attend an 18-week formal training program that teaches a variety of tasks that AGE mechan-

585

ics are likely to perform during their first job assignment. Upon graduation, mechanics are sent to an operational base.
AGE mechanics were sent a task inventory to complete 8 and 12
months after graduating from the formal training course. For the first
set of analyses, we used the data collected at 12 months. One year is the
minimal amount of time that the Air Force deems necessary for mechanics to learn their job well enough to be promoted to the next level
of responsibility. Thus, 12 months was an appropriate time period for
obtaining ratings of recommended training emphasis.
A total of 196 out of a possible 450 surveys were returned (44%
response rate) at 12 months. Cognitive ability scores were available for
180 of the mechanics who completed surveys at 12 months. Thus, the
final sample size for the first set of analyses was 180 mechanics; 92% of
these mechanics were men.
For the second set of analyses, we analyzed the data collected at 8
and 12 months. A total of 118 of the 196 mechanics who completed
surveys at 12 months had also completed surveys at 8 months. Therefore, the second set of analyses were completed for the 8-12 month
matched sample of 118 mechanics; 91% of these mechanics were men.

Measures
Tasks. To reduce survey length and increase the potential for a high
response rate, we used a stratified sampling plan. The plan resulted in
the selection of 34 of the 99 trained tasks (Ford & Sego, 1990) that
differed in terms of task difficulty (Christal, 1974).
Recommended training-emphasis ratings. The dependent variable
in this study was the training-emphasis ratings. For each of the 34
tasks, we asked respondents "How much formal training emphasis (i.e.,
structured training) do you recommend for first-term airmen in the
AGE career field?" Respondents rated emphasis on a 9-point scale,
ranging from extremely low (1) to extremely high (9) emphasis.
Training-emphasis ratings are typically collected as part of the U.S.
Air Force task inventory approach (Christal, 1974) and are used as one
input into training design decisions. Research has found that trainingemphasis judgments are highly predictive from other task ratings, such
as consequences of inadequate performance, task difficulty, and relative time spent (Ruck et al., 1987). Research also supports the reliability of the ratings, as independent raters have shown high agreement
(Ruck et al., 1987; Stephenson, 1978).
The correlational matrix of the recommended training-emphasis ratings for the 34 tasks we studied was subject to common factor analysis
with squared multiple correlations as communalities to produce a reduced set of reliable task-rating factors. We used the scree test to determine the number of factors. We then rotated these factors using varimax rotation. An examination of the breaks or discontinuities in the
pattern of eigenvalues and an examination of the interpretability of the
factors suggested a five-factor solution. Interpretations of factors were
made based on factor loadings above .50. We eliminated tasks with
high loadings across multiple factors from subsequent analyses, which
resulted in the elimination of 14 tasks. The five factors reflect major
job duties of the AGE mechanics.
On the basis of our analysis of item content, we labeled the five
dimensions and computed their reliabilities for the 12-month sample
as follows: Hydraulics (ft = .89), Compressed Gas (ft = .91), Electrical
(p = .84), Basic Manual (pi = .78), and Periodic Inspections (ft = .88).
The reliabilities for the 8-12-month matched sample were very similar
to those for the 12-month sample. Table 1 presents the tasks for each
dimension, along with descriptive statistics for the 12-month sample.

performed each of the trained tasks on the job. Mechanics provided
these judgments after both 8 months and 12 months on the job.

Activity level. We asked respondents to indicate how many times
they had performed each task since graduating from technical training. We calculated activity level for each dimension by summing the
number of times each task was performed. An examination of these
sums indicated a nonnormal distribution of scores. To reduce this
problem and to maintain the continuous nature of the variable, we
performed a square-root transformation on the activity-level scores.
Self-efficacy.
The respondents completed an eight-item self-efficacy measure. Similar to Jones (1986), we conceptualized and measured self-efficacy as an individual's confidence in his or her ability to
succeed and perform well on the job. An example item was "I feel that I
can perform all the tasks of an AGE specialist at the 5-skill level."
Mechanics indicated their level of agreement with these statements on
a 7-point scale, ranging from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (7).
For the 12-month sample, the mean scale score for the self-efficacy

measure was 5.99 (SD = 0.79). The scale was highly reliable (a = .86).
For the matched sample, the 8-month mean scale score was 5.84 (SD =
0.82), and the 12-month mean scale score was 6.03 (SD = 0.78). In
addition, the reliabilities for the 8-month and 12-month matched sample were quite adequate <ft = .82 and .83, respectively).
Cognitive ability. A measure of general cognitive ability was obtained from the Armed Services Vocational Aptitude Battery (ASVAB), which is used for military enlistment and classification decisions. We used the Armed Forces Qualification Test (AFQT), a composite of verbal ability, mathematical knowledge, and arithmetic
reasoning subtests of the ASVAB, to measure ability. The AFQT has
consistently shown reliabilities in the .90s (Welsh, Kucinkas, & Curran,
1990). The mean AFQT score for the 12-month sample was61.74 (SD =
15.14), which was very similar to the mean AFQT score for the total
possible sample of mechanics who were sent surveys (M = 60.72, SD =
15.61).

Analyses
For the cross-sectional analyses, hierarchical multiple regression
equations were computed separately for each of the five task-rating
dimensions of training emphasis for the 12-month sample. In Step 1,
the two individual difference factors of self-efficacy and ability were
entered. In Step 2, the two task-experience variables were entered into
the regression equation. The order of entry reflects causal priority
(Cohen & Cohen, 1983). Recent research indicates that ability and
self-efficacy have some influence on the task experiences gained by
incumbents (see Ford et al, 1992). Therefore, by entering ability and
self-efficacy first, the independent effects of the experience factors
(breadth and activity level) could be examined.
The change analyses focused on the impact of changes in self-efficacy and task experience on changes in training-emphasis ratings. Ability was not examined in this set of analyses, as it was only measured at
one point in time.
The use of change scores can be problematic, as the change scores are
dependent on prescores (Cronbach & Furby, 1970). Cohen and Cohen
(1983) suggested using the postscore as a dependent variable and the
prescore as a covariate when predicting change. In the present study,
there were three prescore measures that had to be used as covariates to
examine the effects of changes in individual and task factors on
changes in task ratings of training emphasis.
The analysis plan required removing the effects of the initial level of
self-efficacy and task experience (measured at 8 months) from the 12month training-emphasis ratings. In addition, the 8-month measure of
training emphasis was used as a covariate. This analysis plan produced
measures of self-efficacy, task experience, and training-emphasis ratings that only reflected change.
Hierarchical multiple regression analyses were computed separately
for each of the five training-emphasis-rating dimensions. In Step 1, the
independent and dependent variables measured at 8 months were entered as covariates. For Step 2, the self-efficacy measure taken at 12
months was entered. In Step 3, the measure of task experience taken at
12 months was entered into the regression equation.

A closer examination of the results revealed that self-efficacy

the two individual characteristics. Although ability was not related to training-emphasis ratings, self-efficacy had a strong,

breadth of experience. Breadth was a significant influence for
three of the five task-rating dimensions. The positive beta
weights indicated that the more tasks performed by the mechanics for each of the three dimensions, the higher the ratings
of training emphasis. Activity level had a significant, positive
influence on training-emphasis ratings for only the Basic Manual task dimension. Nevertheless, breadth had a stronger influence than activity level for the Basic Manual task dimension.
Change Analyses
The second set of analyses examined the impact of changes
in self-efficacy and breadth of experience on changes in training-emphasis ratings. Activity level was not included in the
change analyses because of the high correlation between activity level and breadth and the small relationship found between
activity level and recommended training-emphasis ratings in
the cross-sectional analyses.

Discussion
We conducted this research to examine the extent to which
task analysis ratings are influenced by task-experience and individual factors. In the cross-sectional analyses, we examined the
influence of ability, self-efficacy, breadth of experience, and
activity level on ratings of recommended formal training made
by individuals with the same level of tenure. The results showed
that there were differences in task-level experiences across individuals when tenure was held constant and that these differences were related to recommended training-emphasis ratings.
As we hypothesized, individuals who had the opportunity to
perform more tasks during the first 12 months on the job (high

TRAINING-EMPHASIS RATINGS

breadth) rated formal training-emphasis needs as higher than
did those with less breadth of experience. Activity level was a
less influential factor. As we also hypothesized, individuals
higher on self-efficacy rated tasks as needing greater emphasis
in a formal training program than did those lower on self-efficacy. Cognitive ability was not related to recommended training-emphasis ratings.
The change analyses investigated how changes in an individual's task experience and self-efficacy because of increased tenure on the job might lead to changes in recommended training
emphasis. The results indicated that changes in breadth of experience and self-efficacy were related to changes in training-emphasis ratings.
As expected, individuals who reported an increase in the
breadth of their task experiences also tended to increase their
ratings of formal-training emphasis. In addition, individuals
who became more self-efficacious over time tended to increase
their ratings of the need for formal-training emphasis.
Research Contributions
Our research makes at least three contributions to the literature on task analysis ratings. First, our findings highlight the
importance of studying task-level experience factors for their
impact on task analysis ratings, such as training emphasis. Past
research has typically used tenure as a surrogate measure for
the work experiences gained by individuals. Our research indicated that there was much variability in the task-level experiences of incumbents with the same job tenure and that this
variability was predictive of task ratings of recommended
training emphasis. This evidence supports the notion that individuals consider some of the more quantifiable aspects of their
experience when making task analysis ratings, such as recommended training emphasis.
Second, this study has expanded the research on task analysis
ratings by examining within-person changes in task ratings.
Although researchers have suggested that these changes may
occur (e.g., Sanchez, 1990), past research has been limited by its
use of cross-sectional designs. The present study shows that
changes in task experience and individual characteristics are
systematically related to within-person changes in one type of
task rating. Our results indicate the importance of conducting
longitudinal analyses that allow for the study of change issues
in task analysis ratings. For example, research is needed that
identifies when task analysis ratings stabilize and how person,
task, or job factors affect the length of time it takes for these
ratings to stabilize.
Third, our findings support recent calls for the incorporation
of more psychologically meaningful factors into task analysis
research (e.g., Harvey, 1991; Landy & Vasey, 1991). Our results
indicate that perceptions of self-efficacy can affect beliefs
about the usefulness or importance of formal training programs for developing job skills. Of course, the boundary condition for our results is that individuals had been trained on the
tasks they rated. It is unclear whether the same results would be
found for individuals who had not been trained in tasks they
rated. Nonetheless, these results support the proposition that
an individual's interpretation of his or her performance capabilities is an important factor influencing task analysis ratings.

589

This suggests that task analysis ratings of what should be
trained may be more in the eye of the beholder than has previously been acknowledged in the literature.
Issues for Future Research
On the basis of our results, we can identify a number of issues
that future research could address. One issue concerns the generalizability of results. Future research should examine whether
similar results would be obtained using different types of tasks.
In addition, this study focused on one job in the military; research is needed to determine whether these results will generalize to additional jobs in the military and to other organizational settings.
A second issue concerns the task-experience measure we
used. Breadth and activity level focus on the amount of experience obtained on the job. However, the amount of experience is
just one aspect of a person's experience on the job (Ford et al,
1992). Another dimension of experience is the type of tasks the
individual performs on the job. For example, individuals may
have similar breadth and activity levels but differ greatly in
whether the tasks they perform are difficult or easy ones. It is
likely that individuals would provide different training-emphasis ratings for tasks that have the same activity level but that
vary in terms of task difficulty. This suggests that task difficulty may moderate the relationship between task experience
and task analysis ratings.1
A third issue concerns the measurement of self-efficacy. We
used a measure of self-efficacy similar to that used by Jones
(1986) that focused on an individual's confidence in his or her
ability to succeed and perform well on the job. Although they
provided a useful general index, the self-efficacy perceptions
we collected were at a different level of analysis than were the
task-experience factors. Future research should measure self-efficacy perceptions for each task performed on the job. This
would allow for direct comparisons of the impact of task-experience factors and self-efficacy on task analysis ratings.
A fourth issue concerns the quality of the training-emphasis
ratings provided. The results indicated that those incumbents
who are active in their job duties provide quite different judgments of what tasks should be trained than do incumbents with
low task experience. Nevertheless, it is not clear whether the
ratings provided by individuals with greater breadth of experience or higher self-efficacy would lead to a better quality training-needs-assessment plan than the ratings provided by those
with lower breadth and self-efficacy.
To conclude, this study has advanced understanding of the
relationship between rater characteristics and task analysis ratings by examining the impact of three distinct factors—task
experience, self-efficacy, and cognitive ability—on one type of
task analysis rating, recommended training emphasis. The results indicated that both quantifiable aspects of experience
(breadth) and psychological factors (self-efficacy) affect task
analysis ratings. The incorporation of additional task and rater
characteristics and further theory development will broaden
