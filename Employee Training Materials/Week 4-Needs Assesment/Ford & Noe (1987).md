Self-assessment research has concentrated on validity and psychometric issues in selection and appraisal contexts. Little is known about factors that
affect self-assessment of training needs. The present study examines the
influence of managerial position (level, function) and managerial attitudes
toward training utility on self-reported training needs. A training-needs
survey consisting of items related to basic managerial, interpersonal, administrative, and quality control skills was completed by 506 lower and
middle level managers across four functional areas. Correlational and regression analyses indicated that managerial level and function had some
effect on reported administrative training needs, while function and attitudes towards training utility had some impact on self-reported training
needs for quality control. Level, function, and attitude were not related
to reported basic managerial and interpersonal training needs. The implications and limitations of the results are discussed, and future research
directions are presented.
A systematic approach to training requires the assessment of training
needs, the development/implementation of the program, and the evaluation and continual modification of the training process (Goldstein, 1986).
Training-needs assessment is a critical component of the training system
(Goldstein, 1980; Hinrichs, 1976; McGehee & Thayer, 1961; Moore &
Dutton, 1978) because it provides data to determine who is to be trained,
what training programs are needed, and how the results of training programs are to be evaluated.
There are a variety of data sources and measurement tools available
for the assessment of training needs. Sources of training-needs assessment
infonnation at the individual or person analysis level include perfonnance
data (e.g., productivity, accidents, customer complaints), work sampling,
and job-knowledge tests (see Moore & Dutton, 1978, for a more detailed

Evaluation of Self-Assessment Information
While the usefulness of self-assessments of training needs has been
suggested, empirical research is lacking. Research on self-assessments
in the industrial/organizational psychology literature has concentrated on
comparing the validity and psychometric characteristics of self-assessment
ratings with other predictors or types of performance infonnation.
The validity evidence indicates that self-assessments of abilities and
skills are moderately related to performance criteria (Ash, 1980; DeNisi
& Shaw, 1977; Levine, Flory, & Ash, 1977) and are at least as predictive
of outcomes such as job perfonnance, achievement-test scores, and course
grades as are other sources of information (Mabe & West, 1982; Shrauger
& Osberg, 1981). Research on psychometric characteristics indicate that
self-reports of skills and performance tend to be more lenient than ratings
made by others (Holzbach, 1978; Kirchner, 1965; Lawler, 1967; Thornton,
1968) but tend to exhibit less halo (Klimoski & London, 1974; Kraiger,
1985; Lawler, 1967; Parker, Taylor, Barrett, & Manens, 1959). While
some studies have found a limited amount of convergence of self- and other
ratings (Heneman, 1974; Prien & Liske, 1962; Williams & Seiler, 1973),
a majority of studies have noted a general lack of convergence (Holzbach,
1978; Klimoski & London, 1974; Lawler, 1967; Thomton, 1968).
A limitation of the existing research is that little is known about factors that affect self-assessment ratings in applied settings (Heneman, 1980;
Levine, 1980). The present study examines the impact of three factors on
the self-assessment of training needs by managers. Two factors, hierarchical level and function, are characteristics of the manager's position in the
orgaiiization. The third factor, attitude towards the utility of training, is an
individual characteristic.

Influence of Managerial Level and Function
The importance of examining the impact of hierarchical level and function on managerial training needs is highlighted by the literature on managerial roles, behaviors, and required knowledges, abilities, and skills. Researchers contend that top, middle, and lower level management perform
substantially different patterns of leadership functions, with technical skills
becoming less important than administrative and external monitoring skills
as one moves up the hierarchy (Argyris, 1964; Dunnette, 1986; Katz &
Kahn, 1978). Mintzberg (1980) suggests that managerial roles have different saliencies depending on the functional specialty of the manager.
Empirical evidence supports these contentions. Paolillo (1981) found
that level and function were related to the salience of managerial roles.
Pavett and Lau (1983) surveyed CEOs, as well as middle and lower level
management, and found that external roles (liaison, spokesperson) were
significantly more important for higher management levels, while leader
roles (interpersonal relations, motivational activities) were more important
for lower levels. Basic supervisory skills such as listening, verbal communication, and written communication were found to be important for
successful performance regardless of level and function. Recent research
also indicates that the importance of specific skills and knowledges (e.g.,
technical aspects) differs by level and functional specialty (Pedigo, 1986).
Gomez-Mejia, McCarm, and Page (1985) surveyed 600 managers across
three levels and 20* functions and found large differences in behavioral
activities by level and function.
The literature on managerial roles and activities suggests that training
needs may also differ across managerial levels and functions. Surprisingly, only one study was found that examined differences in self-assessed
training needs by managerial position. Bemick, Kindley, and Pettit (1984)
examined self-assessed training needs of managers for technical, human
relations, and conceptual skills (Katz, 1955). Managerial level (lower, middle, upper) affected self-perceived training needs, with upper management
reporting higher needs for conceptual training and lower level managers
reporting higher training needs for technical skills. The profile of training
needs for middle management was more closely related to the upper than
to the lower management training-needs profile. While the study by Bernick et al. (1984) was an initial research attempt, the findings were limited
by the small sample-to-variable ratio and by the examination of only level
differences in self-assessed training needs.

Attitudes Towards Training Utility
The motivation of the job incumbent to report performance deficiencies accurately has been shown to affect the quality of self-assessment
responses (Mabe & West, 1982; Shrauger & Osberg, 1981). Similarly,
an individual's attitude towards the usefulness of training programs may
influence the quality of self-assessed training needs. For example, it has
been suggested that attitudes about the need for a specific type of training
program affect trainee motivation to leam (Hicks & Khmoski, 1984; Noe,
1986). Therefore, it is reasonable to hypothesize that a manager's overall
attitude about the utility of training programs should be related to the level
of self-assessed training needs. Managers who believe that the quality of
training programs is poor and that the skills emphasized are not job relevant should deemphasize their training needs by reporting lower training
needs than managers who perceive training as useful regardless of actual
training needs. To the authors' knowledge, no research has examined the
relationship between attitudes towards training utility and perceived training
needs.
The present exploratory study examines factors that influence selfassessed training needs. It is expected that level, function, and attitude
towards training utility account for significant amounts of variance in selfassessment ratings. It is also expected that the attitudes will have an influence on ratings beyond the effects of level and function. Regardless of
position, managers who perceive that training has little utility for improving performance or leading to valued outcomes should repon lower training
needs than managers who see training as beneficial.
Method
Sample
Training-needs surveys were sent to 887 lower and middle level managers in one division of a large manufacturing company. Of the 586 surveys
returned, 506 were useable, for a 57% response rate.
Procedure
To develop a training-needs assessment survey, managerial job analyses
and the training objectives for the managerial programs cun-ently being
conducted were examined. This step generated a list of 70 managerial skills
(e.g., "Recognizing and praising high perfonnance", "Following the proper
procedures in grievance situations"). Twenty-two upper level managers
and trainers added, deleted, and edited skills to insure that the list of skills

needed to perform effectively as a manager in that division was complete.
On the basis of this input, the survey was revised to include 80 skill items.
The revised survey was sent to upper level managers for distribution
to lower and middle level managers in the division. Managers were told
that their responses would be used to help develop training programs that
better met the needs of the managerial staff. Anonymity was assured, and
no demographic (e.g., sex, age, race) information was obtained from survey
respondents. Surveys were completed individually, on company time, and
returned directly to the training department.
Measures
Training needs. Participants were asked to consider each skill item on
the training-needs survey ("To what extent do you have a training need in
this area?") and respond to each item using a five-point Likert-type scale
(1 = to no extent; 5 = a very large extent). Development of scales for the
training-need items are described in the results section.
Level and function. The training-needs survey asked respondents for
their organizational level and for their job classification code (function).
Based on this information, the sample was categorized into 344 lower level
managers (first-line supervisors) and 162 middle level managers (secondor third-line supervisors). The sample was also categorized into four functional specialties based on the job classification codes. The four functional
areas were Administration (n = 45), Reliability/Engineering (n = 74),
Manufacturing (n = 161), and Materials Handling (n = 206).

Discussion
This study examined the infiuence of managerial level, function, and
attitudes towards training utility on self-assessed training needs. The results indicated small but significant effects for level, function, and attitudes
on reported training needs. Lower level managers reported higher needs
for administrative skills (interpretation of union guidelines, safety issues,
inventory control) than did middle level managers. Reliability, Manufacturing, and Materials Handling managers reported higher needs for quality
control training than did managers with Administrative functions. Managers who perceived training to be worthwhile reported higher training
needs for quality control skills. Level, function, and attitudes were not
systematically related to reported training needs for basic managerial skills
and interpersonal skills.
Implications of Results
The results suggest that structural characteristics like managerial position have some impact on self-assessed training needs. The results complement previous research on the effect of managerial position on managerial
roles in that differences in required knowledges and skills were refiected in

differences found in self-assessed training needs. For example, lower level
managers were found to desire more "technical" supervisory (administrative) training in areas such as union guidelines, safety issues, and inventory
control than did middle level managers. Managers in functional areas
such as Reliability and Materials Handling, which require technical skills,
desired more training in quality control skills than did managers in other
functional areas. By identifying differences in training needs hy managerial
position, training departments can reduce training costs incurred hy having
managers attend training programs that are relevant to the managers' job
demands and problems.
Reported training needs for basic supervisory and interpersonal skills
were not related to level or function. This result complements Pavett and
Lau's (1983) finding that basic managerial skills were equally important for
success across managerial levels and functions. The results of the present
study suggest that for many skills, training needs may generalize across
lower and middle levels of management as well as across functions.
The results for attitudes on training utility suggest that more emphasis
should be placed on examining managerial attitudes and their impacts on
self-reported training needs. In the present case, attitudes were generally
favorable towards training utility. Nevertheless, attitudes were related to the
level of reported needs for training in quality control skills. Managers with
negative attitudes towards training may report less need for training than
do managers with positive attitudes towards training. Possibly, managers
who do not perceive training to be useful may not receive the training
necessary to remain current in their fields. This is especially relevant when
information from self-assessments is weighted considerably in scheduling
training programs. Therefore, for managers with a negative attitude towards
training, training-needs information from additional sources is especially
important to confirm the results of the self-assessments.
Collecting attitudinal information may also be useful for diagnostic purposes. Managers forced to attend programs that they believe do not address
their training needs are likely to become dissatisfied with training and lack
the motivation to leam and transfer skills (Hicks & Klimoski, 1984; Noe
& Schmitt, 1986). Poor attitudes towards training within departments or
functional areas suggest that trainers must carefully analyze the work environment, past training practices, and the characteristics of the employees
to uncover the causes of the negative attitudes. Interventions must then
be developed to improve these attitudes prior to conducting the training
program (Ford, 1984).

Future Research Directions
The results of this study indicated that managerial position had only
a small impact on self-reported training needs. If one assumes that the
reported training needs are accurate, the results indicate that managerial
training needs are for the most part generalizable across level and function.
On the other hand, the responses may not be accurate, and training needs
may, in fact, differ greatly by managerial position. For example, managers
may have lacked insight into their own training needs. Or, managers may
not have been motivated to provide accurate self-assessments. An examination of means in Table 5 suggests the possibility of a central-tendency
response bias. Managers may have been unable or unwilling to distinguish
between skills that needed to be trained and those that did not.
Future studies using self-assessments should consider incorporating
Shrauger and Osberg's (1981) recommendations for increasing the usefulness of self-assessment information through motivating the assessors to
be accurate, facilitating recall of previous experiences, and requiring explicit decisions to be made. For example, managers could be informed that
the self-assessments would be verified against supervisor reports, or asked
to recall situations where they feh that they had inadequate training and
to consider the skills involved. Self-assessment information could also be
compared with performance appraisal infonnation, supervisory assessments
of training needs, or work samples to determine whether factors such as
negative attitudes result in less agreement between self-reported and other
sources of needs-assessment information.
The attitude-towards-training utility measure used in this study also
needs to be examined. Future research is needed that explores the dimensionality of the construct. Possible dimensions include the usefulness of
the training for the current job, usefulness for career progress, satisfaction
with the administration of the training programs, and perceived supervisory
support for training. The antecedents and consequences of these attitudes
towards training also need to be explored. Individual characteristics such
as need for achievement, self-efficacy, and career salience may infiuence attitudes towards training. Also the importance of work-environment factors
such as task constraints, supervisor-subordinate relations and social support need to be determined. The consequences of attitudes toward training
on factors such as motivation to leam, leaming, behavioral change, and
transfer of skills to the job also need to be explained. For example, motivation to leam in a training program may be influenced by the extent to
which trainees find themselves in programs focusing on skills congruent
with self-perceptions of their needs.
To conclude, training practitioners and researchers have not adequately
studied the effects of attitudinal or organizational factors on self-assessed
