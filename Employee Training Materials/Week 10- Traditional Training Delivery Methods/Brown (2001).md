USING COMPUTERS TO DELIVER TRAINING: WHICH
EMPLOYEES LEARN AND WHY?
KENNETH G. BROWN
Department of Management and Organizations
University of Iowa

Compared to traditional instructor-led training, computer-delivered
training typically offers learners more control over their instruction.
In learner-controlled environments, learner choices regarding practice
level, time on task, and attention are expected to be critical determinants of training effectiveness. To examine the effect of learner choices
in computer-based training, a study was conducted with 78 employees
taking an Intranet-delivered training course. Learner choices were assessed and predicted with goal orientation (mastery and performance)
and learning self-efficacy, as well as age, education, and computer experience. Results indicate considerable variability among trainees in
practice level and time on task, which both predict knowledge gain.
Performance orientation interacted with learning self-efficacy to determine practice level, and mastery orientation had an unexpected negative effect. Implications for the use of computers to deliver training
and for future research are discussed.

The reduced cost and increased capabilities of computer technol-

ogy have triggered dramatic increases in various forms of computerdelivered instruction (Bassi & Van Buren, 1999; Filipczak, 1996; Owston, 1997), including computer-based training (e.g., Williams & Zahed,
1996), Web-based training (e.g., Hall, 1997), multimedia learning environments (e.g., Lawless & Brown, 1997), and e-learning (e.g., Berry,
2000). Although there are some technical distinctions among these
types of training, they all involve the use of computers as the dominant
medium for delivering instruction to learners.

A feature that often distinguishes instruction delivered by computer
from more traditional instructor-led training is the possibility for trainees
to individualize their learning experience and do what theywant with the
training, when they want it (Filipczak, 1996). Individualization involves
a shift in responsibility from instructors to learners because learners control important features of instruction, including the amount of practice
and the time spent on task. Ford and colleagues have noted that the shift
toward learner control of training demands a shift in research attention
toward the active role that the learner plays in determining training outcomes (Ford & Kraiger, 1995; Ford, Smith, Weissbein, Gully, & Salas,
1998).
In this study, how individuals engage instruction during a computerbased training' course is conceptualized as learner choices. Learner
choices are decisions regarding the level and focus of effort exerted
during a learning opportunity. Choices are reflected in behavior (i-e.,
what the learner decides to do with the system, such as the amount
and duration of practice) and cognition (i.e., what the learner decides
to think about while working with the system, such as the amount of
off-task attention). Despite the centrality of behavioral choices in classic (Weiss, 1990) and more recent (Ericsson, Krampe, & Tesch-Romer,
1993; Wegner, 1998) learning theories, these constructs are seldom examined empirically. Although recent work has explored the effects of
cognitive choices (Fisher & Ford, 1998; Kanfer & Ackerman, 1989), behavioral choices are typically held constant in classroom or laboratory
settings (e.g., Kanfer & Ackerman, 1989), or they are unexamined in
field research because of the difficulty of collecting the data (e.g., Noe &
Schmitt, 1986; Warr & Bunce, 1995). In computer-based learning environments, the computer can be used to record learner behaviors that involve interacting with the system. Thus, computer-based training serves
as both a stimulus toward and a method for the study of choices that
learners make during learner-controlled training opportunities.
Research is needed to examine the effects of learner choices on
learning from computer-based training, and to find constructs that predict these choices. Because developing computer-based training is timeconsuming and expensive (Hall, 1997), organizations should focus their
efforts toward employees who make effective choices. Understanding which choices lead to learning, and predicting who will make these
choices, are therefore important areas for theory development and training practice.

To date much of the research on learning from training delivered by
computer has been atheoretical (Bates, Holton, & Syler, 1996; Montazemi & Wang, 1995). Research in this area has traditionally focused
on features of the technology, such as the interface, rather than on
learner characteristics and the learning process (Kommers, 1996; Yang
& Moore, 1995). Consequently, there is a dearth of theory-driven research demonstrating which learners make choices that benefit their
learning in these environments, and by extension which learners will
benefit from the implementation of computer technology as a delivery
mechanism for training.
Based on past research, constructs with potential for predicting
learner choices include goal orientations and learning self-efficacy. Goal
orientation theory describes differences among learners in preferences
regarding task difficulty, reasons for engaging in learning, and beliefs
regarding causes of success (Dweck, 1986; Farr, Hofmann, & Ringenbach, 1993). Self-efficacy theory emphasizes the importance of beliefs
regarding task-specific capability (Bandura, 1997). Research conducted
with school children has demonstrated that these constructs can predict learner choices and learning. However, the few studies conducted
on goal orientation with adults have examined neither learner choices
(e.g., Brett & VandeWalle, 1999; VandeWalle, Brown, Cron, & Slocum,
1999) nor goal orientation and self-efficacy as simultaneous influences
on training outcomes despite suggestions to do so (Brett & Vdndewalle,
1999).
This research addresses which learners are most likely to learn from
learner controlled computer-based training and why. To answer these
questions, prior research on learning choices, goal orientation, and selfefficacy are reviewed to develop a theoretical model that will help identify the process by which learners gain knowledge. The research model
is an input-process-output model that considers learner choices to be
the primary mediator of individual difference effects on learning. In
other words, consistent with existing research on the learning process
(Ford e t al., 1998; Pintrich & de Groot, 1990), individual differences
are expected to predict behavior and cognition of learners during the
learning experience, and these behaviors and cognition arc cxpected to
be the most proximal predictors of learning. Figure 1 summarizes the
constructs used to operationalize the model in this study.
This study provides two contributions to existing research. The first
contribution is the introduction of individual differences and learning
theory to research on computer-based training. The model presented
provides a conceptual framework for research on computer-based training that is consistent with prior theory-driven research on individual differences and learning. This framework addresses not only which indi-

Learner Choices
Computer-based training often affords learners a high degree of control over their learning experience (Hall, 1997; Hannafin, 1984; Lawless
& Brown, 1997). An important finding in the educational literature is
that many students given control over their learning choose to terminate the experience before mastering the training task (Steinberg, 1989;
Tennyson, 1980). The possibility that some adult learners may make
choices that limit their learning is an important practical concern for
training designers and managers. If many employees make poor choices
in computer-based learning environments, then the current trend ofconverting instruction from the classroom to computers may reduce learning
outcomes.
Current theory and evidence regarding learning suggest a number
of behavior-focused principles regarding knowledge gain. Perhaps the
most important of these principles are practice and time on task. Based
on nearly 100years of research, learners who practice essential elements
of a task more than others gain more knowledge and skill (e.g., Goldstein, 1993; Thorndike, 1913). Similarly, based on research on the acquisition of expertise, learners who spend more time practicing learn
more than those who spend less time (Ericsson et al., 1993; Simon &
Chase, 1973).
Although high practice levels and time on task are generally agreed
to be essential to learning, they are seldom examined empirically as
choices made by learners. In most laboratory research on skill acquisition, the number of practice trials and time on task are held constant
(e.g., Ford et al., 1998; Kanfer & Ackerman, 1989; Kozlowski et al., in
press). In field research, instructor-led training has typically been the
focus of research, and in this type of training the instructor determines
the number and duration of practice activities (e.g., Martocchio, 1994;
Tannenbaum, Mathieu, Salas, & Cannon-Bowers, 1991). Thus, given the
increasing prevalence of learner controlled training programs, research

In addition to behavioral choices, cognitive efforts of learners should

of which is that learners must attend closely to the task while practicing.
In support of this perspective, resource models of human attention suggest that individuals who focus their attention on-task during practice
should be become more proficient than individuals who focus their attention off-task (Fisher & Ford, 1998; Kanfer & Ackerman, 1989). Thus,
the more learners engage in off-task attention, the less they are hypothesized to learn. This particular hypothesis has been supported in a number of laboratory studies, but still needs to be examined in the context of
organizationally sponsored training programs.

Individual Differences
In traditional instructor-led training, external influences may limit
the influence of individual differences on learning. For example, Schunk
(1991) suggests that the relationship between motivational constructs
and persistence is weak in many classroom settings because the teacher
determines how much time students spend on task. In computer-based
training, the learner generally does not experience the external pressures
of a live instructor and of peers completing the same activities. Thus, individual differences should be critical determinants of training effectiveness, in large part because of the influence they have over choices that
learners make. Goal orientation and self-efficacy are explored in the
next sections as motivational constructs with potential to predict learner
choices. Individual differences in age, education, and computer experience are also examined.
Goal orientations. Research has indicated that mastery and performance goal orientations affect how individuals approach difficult
learning tasks. High mastery-oriented individuals believe they can improve their abilities and show increased persistence in the face of difficulty. High performance-oriented individuals believe their abilities
are fixed and focus on demonstrating competence. As a consequence,
performance-oriented learners may withdrawal effort following negative feedback or avoid difficult tasks altogether (Ames, 1992; Duda &
Nicholls, 1992; Dweck, 1986). Although early research by Dweck and

In computer-based learning environments that offer options regarding time on task and opportunity for practice, the following hypotheses
are derived from goal orientation theory. Learners with higher mastery orientation are hypothesized to see practice activities as a learning
opportunity, and complete more of the available practice than learners
with lower mastery orientation. Moreover, learners with higher mastery
orientation will spend more time learning, examining material and practicing, than learners with lower mastery orientation. Meanwhile, learners with high performance orientation are expected to monitor their
progress by focusing attention on their performance levels, as indicated
by feedback, and on the implications of that feedback for their ability.
Consequently, learners with high performance orientation will worry
more about their performance, and focus more attention to topics such
as “How am I doing relative to others?” (Butler, 1993; Hofmann, 1993).
Such thoughts divert attention away from the task and toward the self;
the result is increased off-task attention that can interfere with learning.
Two recent laboratory studies have examined goal orientations in
situations where learners could choose some aspect of their practice
level. In the first study, Fisher and Ford (1998) assessed college students’ goal orientations and then had them learn a stock prediction task.
Results indicate that performance goal orientation predicted off-task attention (T = .34, p < .05), which was in turn negatively related to knowledge outcomes. However, neither mastery nor performance orientation
predicted time on task or practice. The failure of goal orientation to
predict these choices may have resulted from the limited duration of
the training and the limited number of practice opportunities available.
Most students completed training in less than 20 minutes ( M = 13.6,
SD = 4.48), and only one sample item was available as optional practice.

et al. (1998) had college students learn a radar simulation task. Results
showed that the number of times students practiced key skills predicted

Another possible explanation for the weak findings in these studies
is a failure to consider factors that moderate the relationship between
goal orientation and effort. Based on Dweck‘s original theory, one possible moderating condition is an individual’sperceived ability. Although
ignored by recent work in the organizational literature, Dweck (Elliott
& Dweck, 1988) and others (Ames, 1992) have hypothesized that the effects of performance goal orientation may differ depending on learners’
perceptions of their ability. According to these authors, individuals with
high performance orientation and high self-perceived ability may behave
just like individuals with high mastery orientation. Because these individuals are confident that they will succeed, they may take on and persist
through difficult tasks. Individuals with high performance orientation
and low self-perceived ability, on the other hand, should demonstrate
the classic avoidance pattern attributed to performance-oriented learners because, devoid of confidence that they have the ability to succeed,
they are likely to withdraw effort following negative feedback or avoid
difficult tasks altogether. Thus, in a difficult learning situation, performance orientation and confidence in ability may interact to determine
practice, time on task, and learning. Because this finding has not been
examined in the organizational literature and it has received only limited
support in the educational literature (Elliott & Dweck, 1988), it will be
explored here in the prediction of learning choices and learning. Learning self-efficacy will be examined in this study to capture individuals’
perceptions of domain-specific ability.
Learning sew-eficacy. Learning self-efficacy has been demonstrated
to influence how individuals approach learning. As defined by Bandura (1997), self-efficacy is a judgment of capability to organize and execute courses of action to attain particular types of performance. Schunk
(1989) suggests that the benefit of self-efficacy as referenced for learning a particular task is that it increases engagement. For example,
Bouffard-Bouchard (1990) found that students with high self-efficacy
worked more practice problems than students with low self-efficacy (for
reviews see Bandura, 1997; Pajares, 1996; Schunk, 1989). Research
examining self-efficacy and knowledge gain, or simiIar outcomes, has
found that pretraining self-efficacy measures positively predict learning
(e.g., Gist, Schwoerer, & Rosen, 1989; Martocchio & Weber, 1992).

Supporting the importance of self-efficacy in learner-controlled training, Warr and Bunce (1995) found a positive relationship between learning self-efficacyand knowledge score (T = .25, p < .01) in a study on selfpaced learning. They also found a negative relationship between learning self-efficacy and learning task anxiety ( T = -.%,p < .01). Thus, individuals with high learning self-efficacy are hypothesized to practice more
and spend more time learning than individuals with low self-efficacy.
In addition, high self-efficacy individuals are likely to experience less
off-task attention, generated by anxiety, than individuals with low selfefficacy.
Age, education, and computer experience. Age and education level
have been found to predict differences in learning-related beliefs and
outcomes across many settings (Colquitt, LePine, & Noe, 2000; Kubeck,
Delp, Haslett, & McDaniel, 1996; Schommer, 1998). Age may be
a particularly relevant influence on learner choices and outcomes in
computer-based learning environments. In general, older employees
have less interest in and worse attitudes toward computers than younger
employees (Comber, Colley, Hargreaves, & Dorn, 1997; Martocchio,
1993). Thus, increasing age may be associated with greater resistance to
the idea of a computer replacing a trainer; this resistance may manifest
in less practice and time on task.
Computer experience may also influence learner choices and knowledge gain from instruction delivered via computer. Learners with more
computer experience should be more comfortable navigating the course,
so they are expected to complete more activities and experience fewer
distracting thoughts than learners with less computer experience (e.g.,
Tsai, 1988). Experienced computer users are also predicted to require
less time to complete a computer-based training course.
Method

Sample
The sample consisted of 78 technical employees of a Fortune 500
manufacturing firm who completed a computer-delivered problemsolving course at the corporate training facility. Employees on the waiting list for the instructor-led version were offered the opportunity to take
the computer version. Volunteers varied considerably in age, with 2%
under 25,31% between 25 and 34,28% between 35 and 44,28% between
45 and 54, and the remaining 10% over 54 years of age. Volunteers were
well educated; the majority held college degrees (90%), and nearly half
of these individuals also attended some graduate school (38% of total
sample).

Learning Material
The course taught a standardized problem-solving process that is an
important and widely publicized component of the organization’s design
and manufacturing processes. Course material was converted from a
3-day instructor-led version of the course to be presented entirely via
computer. The course was organized into nine modules, each containing 3 to 10 sections. Navigation menus were created at the course level,
displaying module options, and at the module level, displaying section
options. Modules ranged in length from 15 to 52 pages or screens of information (A4= 33). An iconic interface was present on the screen at
all times so trainees could make decisions about where to go within the
course. The options available included “go to main menu,” “go to current module menu,” “go back a page,” and “go forward a page.” Other
features that could be accessed via icons were a course map (depicting the structure of the pages), a glossary (containing definitions of key
terms), and job aid diagrams.
Course materials included text, graphics, and interactive activities for
practice. Trainees were asked to complete activities and submit their responses to the company’s server via the corporate Intranet. Server-side
programming generated immediate feedback. There were 121 activities embedded in the course, at least four in each module. All modules
contained practice activities embedded in the main text and at least one
activity at the end that required practicing key tasks taught in that module. Learners were not required to complete any of these activities; they
could skip past them. Quizzes were also presented at the end of each
module. However, unlike the practice activities, quiz questions had to be
answered to complete a module and, ultimately, the course. It is worth
noting that the interactive nature of this course is consistent with recommendations for building effective computer-based training (e.g., Milheim, 1996). The high quality of this course was endorsed by an external
review group who granted it an award for quality as a technology-based
training tool (The Brandon Hall of Fame, http://www.brandon-hall.com
for award goals).

Learning seff-efficacy. Learning self-efficacy is the confidence learners have that they can learn the content of the course. A 3-item scale
(a= .75) was developed as per Ford et al. (1998) and other Likert measures of self-efficacy (Tannenbaum et al., 1991; Warr & Bunce, 1995).
A sample item is “I am confident that I can learn the material in this
course .”
Time on task. The computer recorded the time trainees started and
ended each module. Total time was calculated for each day by taking
the difference between the last time record (i.e., the last module started
or ended) and the first time record (i.e., the first module started). The
sum of the days’ times was used as the measure. The time on task
measure was overdispersed (Long, 1997). Square-root transformations
were conducted, but the correlations and regression weights were not
substantially affected. To facilitate interpretation of coefficients, raw
scores were used in all analyses reported in the paper.
Practice level. The computer also recorded the number of practice
activities trainees performed. Practice opportunities included answering questions, filling in forms, marking checklists, and entering text at
certain points during the course. There were 121 such activities in the
program, 47 (39%) presented throughout the modules and 74 (61%)
presented as cases at the end of the modules. Quiz questions were not
counted because all trainees completed them.
Off-task attention. Off-task attention is the extent to which trainees
thought about issues unrelated to course content. A 6-item measure
similar to Kanfer and Ackerman (1989) and Fisher and Ford (1998) was
used ( a = .73). A sample item is, “I let my mind wander while I was
learning the materials.”
Knowledge. Learning was assessed as a cognitive outcome (Kraiger,
Ford, & Salas, 1993) using both a multiple-choice and short answer
knowledge test. The multiple-choice test was a 25-item instrument developed by the company to assess verbal knowledge of key terms and
concepts. All but one item contained 4 response options; one item
had 5 options. A sample item is “A problem statement does which of the
following: (a) determines who belongs on the [problem-solving] team,
(b) identifies the root cause, (c) serves as a starting point for the problem
description, or (d) narrows the search for a simple, concise statement of
the object and defect.”
The short answer test was developed by the investigator in collaboration with subject matter experts to assess application knowledge (Bloom,
1956; Fisher & Ford, 1998). This test contained three questions asking

Discussion
The purpose of this study was to examine which employees learn
from computer-based training that allows a high degree of learner control. To bring theory to this question, the effects of learner choices on
learning and the effects of individual differences on learner choices were
examined. Results for learner choices support the importance of practice level and time on task for learning. Descriptive statistics reveal a
substantial range of practice and time on task, and because these differences were associated with differences in knowledge gain, learners who
elected to skip materials or move quickly reduced their knowledge gain.
Thus, the employees who learn most from this type of learning environment are those who complete more of the practice opportunities made
available and take more time to complete the experience. These results
suggest that the answer to the research question “Why do employees
learn?” is that they invested effort and time into the learning opportunity. Because individual difference prediction of practice and time on
task were weak, the answer to the question of “which employees” is less
clear. Implications of these findings for research on individual differences and learner choices are discussed below, followed by a discussion
of limitations and practical implications.

Individual Differences
This study addresses the call of VandeWalle et al. (1999) for more
research on goal orientation with adults and the suggestion by Brett
and VandeWalle (1999) to simultaneously examine goal orientations and
self-efficacy as predictors of training outcomes. The results suggest that
the relationships among goal orientation, practice, and learning may not
be as simple as current research suggests. This study found evidence

Learners who made the least use of practice were those with a high
performance orientation and low learning self-efficacy; learners with
high performance orientation and high self-efficacy made much more
extensive use of practice. This finding is largely consistent with Dweck’s
goal orientation theory. Dweck (1986) suggests that learners with high
performance orientation will avoid tasks they perceive as difficult but
persistent on tasks they are confident they can successfully complete;
thus, self-efficacy and practice should be positively correlated for performance-oriented learners.
The negative relationship between self-efficacy and practice for low
performance oriented learners is less clearly derived from Dweck’swork.
The negative relationship may result from 4 reduced sense of accountability for low performance oriented learners. Unlike those with high
performance orientation, learners with lo* performance orientation
should not be concerned about how others judge their ability and consequently their learning efforts. As a result, they may not feel accountable
for their learning choices. Without a sense of accountability to hold them
to continued practice, learners with high self-efficacy may feel confident
enough to skip practice opportunities. Those with low self-efficacy may
be willing to pursue additional practice in order to boost their knowledge and confidence. In other words, low performance-oriented learners who lack confidence in their ability to learn may not be afraid of being judged as incompetent for completing more of the available practice.
These results suggest that situations may arise when self-efficacy is negatively related to effort exerted to learn. Future research should explore
whether performance orientation and/or perceptions of accountability
cause self-efficacy to be negatively related to practice and learning in
situations when learners are provided control over their instruction.

always stimulate learners to complete more practice. The circumstances
that create this result should be of great interest to researchers and managers. In this particular study, subjects are adult learners trying to gain

told that this training was only their first introduction to the material;
they would have an opportunity to visit the course web site following
the session. In addition, the company sponsors on-site facilitation and
follow-up training on the problem-solving process. For most learners
this course was simply the beginning of efforts to learn the company’s

to examine the effects of basic personality characteristics, such as conscientiousness, and job attitudes, such as job involvement. Each construct has been positively linked with training outcomes in past research

choices. Finally, research should explore individual differences in capabilities for self-management. One suggestion in this regard is to examine self-regulatory skills that may influence how effectively learners
can control their own behavior and cognition in the face of competing
demands (Schunk & Zimmerman, 1997).
Learner Choices

This study extends prior research by examining actual measures of
learner choices, including practice level and time on task, in a field setting. Both practice and time are central to learning but difficult to assess
outside of the classroom. Consequently, this study is one of the first to
examine actual measures of practice and time on task within the context
of an organizationally sponsored training program.
Results suggest that, despite the appeal of computer-based training
as a way to make learning more efficient, employees may not use control over their learning wisely. Learners may skip practice that is critical for building understanding of the material. Learners may also move

spending extra time in the learning environment. Future research on attention in learner controlled environments is clearly warranted. One

as well as self-report measures. For example, learners could be videotaped and indicators of off-task attention, such as moving away from the
task, could be coded. Future research on learner choices could also expand the definition and operationalization to include choices regarding
how on-task attention is used (e.g., Fisher & Ford, 1998; Warr & Bunce,

Limitations and Implications
This study did not use a control group, so conclusions cannot be
drawn about the effectiveness of this particular course relative to other
instructional designs (ie., instructional methods) or formats (i.e., instructional media). However, the research question examined in this
study did not call for a manipulation of these factors. The purpose of
the study was to investigate the influence of individual differences and
learner choices as naturalistic phenomena in the context of learner controlled computer-based training.
Because the sample consisted of volunteers, restriction of range in
some predictors may have occurred. For example, trainees with little

training and serves as a caution to organizations that blindly follow the

often expected to complete training in between work activities at their
work site. This idea has been referred to as “desktop” training, and it
offers even more learner control than was afforded to participants in
this study. Learners in this study were brought to a central training
facility that was specificallydesigned to facilitate learning and offer some
protection from the immediate pressures of work. If some employees
terminate practice early in a protected learning environment, how much
will those same employees practice when they are expected to learn
completely on their own? Despite the high quality of the training and the
importance of its content to trainees, many learners in this study elected
to skip over practice opportunities and stay for half the time allotted.
Thus, simply providing access to computers with training activities does
not mean that trainees will use them to learn.
