Comparison of the influences of structured on-the-job training and
classroom training approaches on trainees’ motivation to learn
Wen-Rou Huang* and Ying-Ju Jao
Department of Business Administration, Feng Chia University, Taichung, Taiwan, R.O.C.
(Received 15 June 2015; accepted 16 September 2015)
Increasing trainees’ learning motivation is critical to help them perform well in the
training programme. This paper aims to examine the influences of the structured on-thejob training (S-OJT) and classroom training approaches on trainees’ learning motivation
and learning performance. An exploratory study was conducted with 90 students from a
vocational school located at the Yunlin County in the Midwest of Taiwan who enrolled
in a course entitled ‘hair coloring’. The training modules for S-OJT and classroom
training were developed and delivered in the training courses. The survey was administered prior to and after the training courses to assess the influences of the training
approaches. It was found that the trainees who received S-OJT generated higher learning
motivation and learning performance compared with those who received the classroom
training. Moreover, the trainees with lower initial learning motivation were motivated
more and generated higher learning performance after receiving S-OJT. This study
provides the potential theoretical and practical implications and can serve as reference
when choosing the training approach for a designed training programme.
Keywords: on-the-job training; structured on-the-job training; classroom training;
motivation to learn; self-efficacy

Introduction
Training evaluation refers to the process of systematically collecting the outcomes needed
to determine whether training is effective or not (Noe 2013). Irrespective of the purposes
of training programmes, traditionally, the essential issue of evaluation is to know whether
trainees have learnt the material designed in training and whether the trainees transfer
what they have learnt through the training programme to the real jobs (Campbell 1988;
Kraiger, Ford, and Salas 1993). Kirkpatrick’s four-level framework, most of the time, was
used as criteria to evaluate the efficiency and effectiveness of a training programme. These
four-level criteria include reactions, learning, behaviour transfer, and financial results.
In recent years, besides Kirkpatrick’s four-level framework, the affective outcomes
such as self-efficacy, motivation to learn, and attitudes have been discussed and proposed
to be used for training evaluation (Kraiger, Ford, and Salas 1993; Noe 2013). The
influence of training methods on self-efficacy, especially, has been studied by many
scholars. For example, Combs and Luthans (2007) stated that the trainees’ diversity
self-efficacy could be increased by the diversity training. Schowerer and coworkers
(2005) indicated that the general self-efficacy (GSE), specific self-efficacy (SSE), and
performance expectancy could be increased by training as well. Gist (1989) found that

Although many studies have been conducted to assess the effectiveness and efficacy of
different training methods on either learning performance, behaviour transfer or trainees’
self-efficacy, little is known about how S-OJT training and classroom training approaches
influence trainees’ motivation to learn. Since research has indicated that S-OJT is able to
generate higher self-efficacy than the classroom training method, the question whether the
trainees who receive S-OJT will generate higher learning motivation compared with those
who receive classroom training remains to be answered. The major purpose of this paper
aims to examine the influences of the S-OJT training and classroom training approaches on
trainees’ motivation to learn. The ultimate contribution of this study could be to serve as a
step stone for further study in understanding and comparing the influence of the different
training approaches on trainees’ learning motivation and, most importantly, applying the
knowledge into the training design to increase the training effectiveness.

Literature review
Motivation to learn
Motivation derives from the Latin word movere, meaning ‘to move’. Dowling and Sayles
(1978) referred motivation as ‘an inner desire to make an effort’, whereas Pinder (1998)
defined it as ‘the energy a person expends in relation to work’. Now, it is recognized as
‘the processes that account for an individual’s intensity, direction, and persistence of effort
toward attaining a goal’ (Kreitner and Kinicki 2010; Robbins and Judge 2013).
Motivation is a component of most human activity and combines with the ability to
produce behaviour and performance (Mitchell and Daniels 2003). It encompasses three
general psychological processes that account for arousal, intensity, and direction (Mitchell
and Daniels 2003). The arousal component results from a need or desire for some object
or state that is unfulfilled. Intensity component specifies how hard a person works to
perform a chosen behaviour, i.e. some needs are more important than others and some
goals are more difficult to attain than others (George and Jones 1999; Mitchell and
Daniels 2003). Directional component is similar to personal goals.
While general motivation is concerned with effort towards any goal, motivation to
learn has been defined by Noe and Schmitt (1986) as a specific desire on the part of the
trainees to learn the content of a training programme. It is the willingness to attend and
learn the material presented in a developmental programme (Cole, Feild, and Harris 2004;
Noe 1986) and it encompasses the desire to engage in training and development programmes, to learn the training content, to participate in training activities and to embrace
the training experience (Major, Turner, and Fletcher 2006). Similar to the construct of
motivation, motivation to learn varies across and within individuals and it could result in
effort and persistence towards training, which would impact the learning performance and
training outcomes. Hence, motivation to learn has been considered as an important
predictor to influence training effectiveness (Cannon-Bowers et al. 1995; Noe 2013).

Motivation to learn has also been considered as an affective outcome in training

learn can be changed through training and the trainees’ level of motivation to learn can
be either increased or decreased depending on the design of the training (Kraiger, Ford,
and Salas 1993; Noe 2013). Figure 1 shows the model that describes the relationships
among trainees’ characteristics, organizational/job characteristics, training programme
design, motivation to learn, and training outcomes (Cannon-Bowers et al. 1995; Noe

self-efficacy, and initial motivation to learn will be brought into the training programme.
The organizational and job characteristics such as organizational climate and policies are
important factors to determine the training objectives. All these variables will influence
the design of training programmes simultaneously, which reversely impact trainees’
motivation to learn, the willingness to attend and to participate in the training activities
in the training programme. The outcomes of the training programme, either affective
outcomes, learning performance, or return on investment (ROI), are directly affected by
the features of training design and the trainees’ motivation to learn.

Training approaches
Training approaches
Classroom training and S-OJT are two of the most popular training approaches used in
companies (Jacobs 2003). Instructor-led classroom training approach is one of the presentation methods in which trainees are passive recipients of information. Essentially, this
training method is created and guided by the company in order to develop the company
defined in a broad set of competencies or skills for positions or for the entire company
(Noe 2013). Lecture is frequently used in this method to deliver the training materials to a
group of trainees through spoken words in the classroom. To increase training effectiveness, the lecture is often supplemented with question-and-answer periods, role play,
discussion, case studies, video, workbooks, DVDs, and games (Yelon 1999; Noe 2013).
These methods allow trainees to participate more actively in the lecture, which facilitates
the learning and transfer of training. Specifically, several characteristics of classroom
training are summarized as follows:

Similar to the instructor-led classroom approach, S-OJT is created and guided by the
company. Unlike the instructor-led classroom approach, S-OJT belongs to hands-on
methods, where the trainee needs to be actively involved in learning (Noe 2013).
Essentially, S-OJT is ideal for developing specific skills and behaviours that occur on
the job and during everyday work. It was first proposed and defined as ‘the planned
process of developing competence on units of work by having an experienced employee
train a novice employee at the work setting or a location that closely resembles the work
setting’ by Jacobs and McGiffin in 1987 (Jacobs 2003, 28–29; Jacobs and McGiffin
1987). The fundamental design of the instructional events in S-OJT is mainly based on the
principles of the system theory and the social learning theory. S-OJT, thus, can be viewed
as a system that consists of training inputs, training processes, and training outcomes.
According to Jacobs, S-OJT stresses that people are able to learn skills and behaviours by
observing others and then modelling the behaviours perceived as effective (Jacobs 2003;
Noe 2013) besides emphasizing a one-on-one basis and training needs to occur in the
actual work setting (Jacobs 1992, 500). Specifically, several characteristics of S-OJT are
summarized as follows:
(1) One-on-one basis emphasizes the importance of the systematic instruction of
‘employees’ trained by experienced experts (Versloot, De Jong and Thijssen,
2001). Such design focuses on the interaction between the trainer and the trainee.
The trainer may call for the trainee to respond in a meaningful way to the model
that was presented and also provide them with feedback.
(2) Training modules are designed to guide trainers and trainees during the training
process. The modules can be presented in a variety of ways and include several
basic components such as training title, objectives, prerequisites, resources, content, events, performance tests, and feedback forms.
(3) Behaviours modelling emphasizes that people learn by observing other persons
whom they believe are credible and knowledgeable (Noe 2013). In other
words, learning through S-OJT comes from not only the process of observing
and seeing the consequences of the trainer or other trainees’ behaviour but also
directly experiencing the consequences of applying the learnt behaviour or
skill.
(4) A response is required from the trainees to the modelled behaviour, skills, and the
training content that were presented in a meaningful way.
(5) Practice involves learners’ trying out the observed behaviours and requires that
the trainers ascertain whether the trainees reproduce the behaviour observed in the
model.
(6) Feedback is the degree to which carrying out work activities generates direct and
clear information about trainees’ learning performance (Robbins and Judge 2013).
It provides the trainees with pertinent information about the accuracy and adequacy of their responses.
(7) Evaluation of performance is a summative judgement of the adequacy of the
trainee’s performance. The event ensures a trainee how he or she transfers the
learnt behaviour to the work.

Hypotheses
Figure 2 presents the relationships among S-OJT characteristics, learning processes, and
motivation to learn (Cannon-Bowers et al. 1995; Jacobs 2003; Mitchell and Daniels 2003;
Noe 2013). The model shows how the individual characteristics of the S-OJT approach
relates to each learning process of the social learning theory, which then initiates the
motivational psychological processes – arousal, intensity, and persistence – to further
influence the motivation to learn and the learning outcome.
Specifically, for S-OJT, the fundamental design of the instructional events is
mainly based on the principles of the social learning theory. According to the social
learning theory, four processes are involved in learning: attention, retention, motor
reproduction, and motivational processes (Noe 2013). Attention suggests that trainees
can learn only when they are aware of the important aspects of a model’s performance.
In order to attract trainees’ attention, most S-OJT programmes adopt one-on-one
instruction and training modules during delivery of the training. The one-on-one
instruction helps trainees focus on the important aspects of training activities and
the designs of the training module ensure that the trainees are aware of the specific
parts of the training contents (Jacobs 2003). Since the training module was designed
based on job demands and company’s needs, it ensures the training objectives are in
accordance with the organizations’ goals, which allows the trainees to realize that
there are subject matters being partially unfulfilled or below expectation. Such awareness can be deemed as the arousal component of the motivation to learn, which
actively initiates action in learning.
After preparing the trainees, the S-OJT trainer may ask the trainees to learn by
reading the training module and also by observing the modelled behaviours. Besides,
the trainer may ask them to respond in a meaningful way so that the trainees can
internalize the training content and be able to retain what they have learnt or observed
(Jacobs 2003). Trainees have to remember the behaviours or skills they observe from a
model and be able to recall them for the appropriate situation. This is the role of
retention. As to the process of motor reproduction, it involves trying out the observed

have learnt or observed and then get the feedback from the trainer. Through retention

process as they would like to be successful in the training programme so that they can
be successful in the future.

motivational processes result in a psychological force, which is called the motivation
to learn, and it does not matter what their initial level of motivation to learn is.
Motivation to learn produces attention to focus on particular issues, people and task
elements in the training process. Motivation to learn results in effort and persistence.
The trainees work harder when they are motivated and the motivation generates
more effort put in the learning process. In addition, the higher the motivation is, the
longer the trainees will sustain their effort to make task strategies and patterns of
behaviours to reach a particular goal (Mitchell and Daniels 2003). Compared to the
S-OJT approach, the instructor-led classroom training approach, most of the time,
uses lecture to communicate large amounts of information to a large group of
trainees without the behaviour modelling and actual job setting and opportunities
for the trainees to practice what they have learnt in the training programme. These
make it difficult for the trainees to retain and reproduce the behaviours or skills,
which might pose difficulties in initiating the motivational processes to generate the
motivation to learn in the learning processes. Although the classroom training
approach may offer video, workbooks, manuals, CD-ROMs, DVDs, and games to
facilitate trainees’ learning, the essence of this approach still belongs to one of the
presentation methods and the trainees who receive classroom training are used to be
passive recipients of information. Thus, based on the above discussion, the following
hypotheses are proposed:
H1: The trainees who received S-OJT will generate higher motivation to learn compared
with those who received the classroom training approach.
H2: The trainees with lower initial motivation to learn and receiving S-OJT will generate
higher motivation to learn compared with those who received the classroom training
approach.
H3: The trainees with lower initial motivation to learn and receiving S-OJT will generate
higher learning performance compared with those who received the classroom training approach.

Method
Participants and procedure
The participants of the study were 10th-grade students who enrolled in hair-colouring
courses in a vocational school located at Yunlin County in the Midwest of Taiwan. The
mean age of the participants was 16 years. The total number of 10th-grade students was
about 250. Ninety students from two out of five classes were randomly selected to
participate in the experiment. One of the two classes was randomly assigned to the
S-OJT and the other to the classroom training. All students provided valid data that
were used for statistical analysis.
The study was exploratory. To answer the research questions, a two-group pretest–
post-test design was used in this study. Specifically, the training course, entitled ‘hair
coloring’, was delivered to the vocational students to address the concepts, knowledge,
issues, skills, and techniques related to hair colouring. Two training approaches, i.e.
classroom training and S-OJT approaches, were used in the programme to deliver the
training contents to students. The classroom training approach was conducted through
lecturing with power point slides in conjunction with question-and-answer periods, role
play, and discussion. The S-OJT approach was carried out by using the training modules
to prepare the trainees in the learning process, and modelling the expected behaviours
followed by practice and feedback. The experiment began with a brief introduction about
the purpose of the study and the design of instrumentation in each class. Then, a survey
was administered prior to the training. Specifically, before training (Time 1, or T1), the
administrative assistant distributed and collected a survey, which helped gather the
information regarding learning motivation, learning performance, and demographic data.
The participants were allowed to complete the survey within approximately 20 minutes
and the questions raised by the participants were answered by the survey administrator
consistently across group administration. The participants then went through the training
process and the second survey was distributed to assess the results of post-training
learning motivation and performance at the end of the training (Time 2, or T2).

Measures
The study encompassed two measures, i.e. learning motivation and learning performance.
Learning motivation was measured by the instrument developed according to the
Motivated Strategies for Learning Questionnaire (MSLQ) (Pintrich and DeGroot 1990),
Patterns of Adaptive Learning Scales (PALS) (Midgley et al. 2000) and the items related
to ‘motivation to learn’ in the School Administrative Descriptive Survey (SADS) (Noe
and Schmitt 1986). The instrument contained 19 items that reduced to three factors:
‘intensity’, ‘direction’, and ‘persistency’. Participants responded to items such as ‘I will
review the learning contents repeatedly to help memorize them when learning’ (intensity),
‘I prefer class work that is challenging so I can learn new things’ (direction) and ‘I will
overcome the difficulties when facing them during learning’ (persistency) using a sevenpoint Likert scale ranging from 1 (not agree) to 7 (totally agree). The Cronbach’s alphas
for three individual factors, i.e. ‘intensity’, ‘direction’, and ‘persistency’, were 0.817,
0.848, and 0.612, respectively. The Cronbach’s alpha result for the whole learning
motivation scale was 0.924.

motivation was obtained and it was found that there are many correlations above 0.3,

Learning performance refers to the extent in which one can successfully perform the
skills of hair colouring after the training. Ten questions selected from the qualification
examination of Hair-colouring Technician for Class C Skills were used to evaluate the
trainees’ performance. It was measured at Time 1 and Time 2, and the score was
determined by the number of questions answered correctly. The more the questions one
can answer correctly, the higher the score or learning performance is.
The variables of individual demographic data were formulated according to the items
of background information designed by Cole, Field, and Harris (2004). The demographic
variables that were measured and controlled for statistics in the study include gender,
social status, career planning, and whether taking the courses for the Class C Skill Test of
hair colouring. All the questions were self-reported. Research has indicated that trainees’
personal characteristics such as attitudes and GSE can be brought into the training
programme to influence the learning outcomes. In order to know whether or not these
two groups are in homogeneity before training, the data regarding trainees’ pre-training
attitudes, GSE, initial motivation to learn and pre-training abilities were collected.
Attitudes were measured by the instrument developed according to Noe (1986) and Noe
and Schmitt (1986). GSE was measured by the instrument developed by Scherer and
colleagues (1982) and later modified in 1998 by Bosscher and Smit (Barnard 2005). The
Cronbach’s alpha results for the attitudes and GSE scale were 0.884 and 0.874,
respectively.

Data analysis
All data were analysed using the Statistical Package for Social Sciences (SPSS 17). The
paired comparison t-tests were used to assess the pre–post training changes in learning
motivation. The independent samples t-tests were used to examine the difference between
the mean scores of learning motivation or learning performance for the S-OJT training
group and the classroom training group.

Discussion
This study has attempted to compare the influence of S-OJT with that of classroom
training approaches on trainees’ motivation to learn. Note that, prior to the training, the
trainees in the two groups were similar in their motivation to learn, learning attitudes, and
level of GSE. This indicates that these two groups were homogeneous before receiving
training and the influence of the training approaches can then be properly examined.
Several in-depth interpretations of the results are presented as follows.
First, the results indicate that the level of motivation to learn can be influenced by
different training approaches. As found in this study, the level of trainees’ motivation to
learn increased after receiving their training programme, either S-OJT or classroom
training. Moreover, a higher level of motivation to learn was generated for the trainees
who received S-OJT compared with those who received classroom training. Since it has
been considered and argued that the motivation to learn can be changed and serve as one
of the training outcomes for training evaluation (Noe 2013; Kraiger, Ford, and Salas
1993), the finding of this study provides support to this concept.
Second, the study reasserts the relationships among self-efficacy, motivation and
performance. The role of self-efficacy in motivation and performance has been explored
since Bandura’s original publications (Schunk 1995). Self-efficacy refers to ‘people
judgments of their capabilities to organize and execute courses of action required to attain
designated types of performances’ (Bandura 1986, 391). Initial self-efficacy varies as a
function of abilities, attitudes and prior experience (Schunk 1995) and it can be changed
by various methods such as training, verbal persuasion, logical verification, behaviour
modelling, past accomplishments, and so on (Dunlap 2005; Wagner and Hollenback 2005;

Human Resource Development International

131

Noe 2013). Since the S-OJT approach emphasizes one-on-one interaction, behaviour
modelling, practice, and goal setting elements on the delivering process, it can raise and
heighten self-efficacy to improve skill development (Huang and Jacobs 2013). Selfefficacy along with situational factors such as rewards and feedback will initiate trainees’
motivational psychological processes to influence their learning strategies, effort level,
persistence, and the choices of goal setting (Schunk 1995). In other words, through the SOJT delivering process, trainees’ inner desire to make an effort or the so-called motivation
to learn will be activated and sustained till accomplishing the training activities (Noe and
Schmitt 1986; Kreitner and Kinicki 2010; Robbins and Judge 2013). Therefore, as it has
been shown that the trainees who received S-OJT could generate higher levels of SSE
compared with those who received the classroom training approach (Huang and Jacobs
2013), in this study, a higher level of motivation to learn was generated for trainees who
received S-OJT than those who received the classroom training approach, especially for
the trainees with a lower initial motivation to learn. These findings support the viewpoints
that self-efficacy can predict motivation and achievement across ability levels (Collins
1982; Cannon-Bowers et al. 1995; Noe 2013).
Third, higher learning performance was generated for the trainees who received S-OJT
compared with those who received the classroom training approach, which is in agreement with the previous finding (Jacobs 2003). As mentioned, motivation is a component
of most human activity and combines with the ability to produce behaviour and performance (Mitchell and Daniels 2003). The most common conceptualization of ability–
motivation interactions suggested by Vroom is that when motivation is low, both lowand high-ability individuals demonstrate a similar low level of performance (Kanfer and
Ackerman 1989). In contrast, when motivation is high, performance variability is due to
individual differences in ability. Since S-OJT can help trainees raise the SSE and also
initiate motivational generating processes, the level of trainees’ motivation to learn will be
increased, especially for the trainees with lower GSE (Huang and Jacobs 2013) and lower
initial motivation to learn. The higher the learning motivation is generated, the harder the
trainees in the S-OJT programme may work. Moreover, their motivation to learn produces
more effort and persistence put in the learning process compared with the trainees who
receive the classroom training approach. In addition, the trainees will sustain their effort
longer to make task strategies and patterns of behaviours to reach a particular business
goal (Mitchell and Daniels 2003) and also transfer what they have learnt to the workplace.
Although individual trainees have different ability levels, with high motivation to learn,
the high-ability trainees will generate a higher level of performance and the low-ability
trainees will also devote themselves to achieving a higher level of performance. Hence, as
indicated by the model shown in Figure 2 and the findings of many studies, trainees who
receive S-OJT will generate higher learning performance.
Implications
The results of this study contribute to research on training and learning motivation by
providing information about the influence of S-OJT and the classroom training approach
on trainees’ learning motivation. Moreover, this study reports the enhanced learning
performance after receiving S-OJT, which reinforces the findings from the previous
studies. The literatures have shown that the self-efficacy and learning motivation will
influence the learning performance. Since higher SSE is generated thanks to the structured
guidance on how to perform one’s tasks in the organization provided by S-OJT (Huang
and Jacobs 2009) and the learning motivation is increased because of the three

motivational processes in S-OJT (this study), it provides a certain extent of explanation as
to why the learning performance increases after receiving S-OJT. Human resource development (HRD) professionals, thus, should consider using the S-OJT approach for trainees,
especially for trainees with lower initial learning motivation in order for continuous
improvement in the employees’ training effectiveness and job performance.
In this study, training was delivered to the average 16-year-old students from the
vocational school. Unlike the ordinary 10th-grade students who study vocational skills
course, most of the students are required to study in school and work in the industry for
approximately 3 months in rotation throughout the entire study in order to earn the degree.
This makes the students similar to real workers in the industry. Although S-OJT was not
carried out in real work setting in this study, the findings should be able to be extended to
matured professionals working in industries.

Limitations
This study is not without limitations. Several limitations of this study are identified to help
guide future research. First, our sample was composed of 83 students from the vocational
school who enrolled in a course entitled ‘hair coloring’. The smaller sample size might
raise some concerns regarding the statistical analysis. For example, Gorsuch (1983, 332)
recommended a minimum sample size of 100 and a minimum sample size to item ratio of
at least 5:1 when conducting factor analysis where the ratio was approximately 4.4:1 in
this study. Increasing the sample size would provide sound statistical meaning of the
results. The second limitation could be translation of the questionnaire. Although a panel
of experts have reviewed the questionnaire, especially focusing on translation of the
questionnaire from English to Chinese, misinterpretation of some items in the questionnaire might happen.

Conclusion
In this study, the influence of S-OJT and classroom training approaches on trainees’
learning motivation and learning performance was examined. It was found that the level
of motivation to learn can be influenced by different training approaches. Moreover, the
trainees who received S-OJT generated higher learning motivation and learning performance compared with those who received the classroom training. In addition, the trainees
with lower initial learning motivation were motivated more and generated higher learning
performance after receiving S-OJT. The potential theoretical and practical implications
were provided and the findings of this study can serve as reference when choosing the
training approach for a designed training programme.
