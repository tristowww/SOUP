**1.** **Comparative Analysis of Training Delivery Methods (VR, LMS, Video):**

Based on your selected scenario and your personal experience with the in-class VR session, clearly analyze and compare the three training methods on the following criteria:

What scenario did you select?

- [**Realism and Fidelity** -- Which method most accurately represents real-world job conditions and task requirements?]{.mark}

  - **VR Training:** Highest realism and fidelity; immersive environment replicates real-world sensory and task environments accurately (e.g., Airport Ground Handler VR or OneBonsai Fire VR).

  - **LMS Training:** Moderate realism; limited interactivity reduces authenticity; good for theoretical understanding (IATA LMS or Fire Extinguisher LMS).

  - **Video Training:** Lower fidelity; passive observational learning; lacks interactivity and direct feedback on performance.

- [**Interactivity and Engagement** -- Reflecting on your VR experience, which method best supports active participation and engagement?]{.mark}

  - **VR Training:** Highly interactive and engaging; active involvement and immediate feedback on performance enhance learning and retention (personal VR experience).

  - **LMS Training:** Moderate interaction; allows some control and interaction through quizzes and modules but less immersive.

  - **Video Training:** Low interaction; purely passive learning; minimal engagement.

- [**Motivation and Learner Control** -- Which method likely enhances trainee motivation, sense of control, and involvement in the learning process? (Klein et al., 2006)]{.mark}

  - **VR Training:** Highest learner motivation and autonomy; interactive scenarios stimulate interest, self-efficacy, and involvement.

  - **LMS Training:** Moderate motivation; structured yet flexible; learner has control but limited active practice.

  - **Video Training:** Low motivation; lack of engagement and interactivity may limit learner control and interest.

- [**Transfer of Training** -- Drawing on course readings, which modality is likely to facilitate the strongest transfer of training? (Huang & Jao, 2016; Salas et al., 2007)]{.mark}

  - [**VR Training:** Strongest transfer (especially near transfer); realistic practice in authentic conditions leads directly to improved job performance.]{.mark}

  - [**LMS Training:** Moderate transfer; theory-based and structured instruction supports conceptual understanding, but weaker application to real-world tasks.]{.mark}

  - [**Video Training:** Weak transfer; limited to conceptual knowledge; passive viewing results in reduced skill retention and less effective application on the job.]{.mark}

**[Recommendation Examples for VR (Airport Ground Handler):]{.mark}**

- [**Recommendation 1:** Integrate structured performance feedback within VR scenarios (Huang & Jao, 2016).]{.mark}

  - [**Rationale:** Immediate, structured feedback enhances self-efficacy and improves transfer of training by clearly showing trainees their performance accuracy in realistic tasks.]{.mark}

- [**Recommendation 2:** Provide VR scenarios specifically designed for cross-training (Salas et al., 2007).]{.mark}

  - [**Rationale:** Cross-training in VR encourages development of shared mental models, improving team coordination and task adaptability in high-pressure scenarios.]{.mark}

**[Recommendation Examples for VR (Fire Safety):]{.mark}**

- [**Recommendation 1:** Incorporate realistic stressors into VR (e.g., dynamic fire scenarios or alarms).]{.mark}

  - [**Rationale:** Simulating stressors enhances trainees\' capability to perform under pressure, supporting improved skill retention and practical performance during real emergencies.]{.mark}

- [**Recommendation 2:** Pair VR training with immediate debriefing and reflection sessions (Salas et al., 2007).]{.mark}

  - [**Rationale:** Immediate guided reflection helps reinforce critical skills and lessons learned, solidifying trainee knowledge and enhancing transfer to real-life emergencies.]{.mark}
