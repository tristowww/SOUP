## 1 Preface

Specialty coffee (see definition in 4.1) acquires value from its attributes, which are either intrinsic (related to the material reality of a coffee) or extrinsic (related to the informational or symbolic attributes of a coffee). In the Coffee Value Assessment (CVA) paradigm, coffee evaluators assess a coffee's intrinsic attributes (its physical and sensory properties) during the physical, descriptive, and affective assessments. To build a holistic picture of a coffee's value, evaluators shall also identify and record an objective profile of coffee's informational or symbolic attributes, a process known as extrinsic assessment.

The extrinsic assessment is a descriptive activity, where an evaluator seeks to document as many details as possible, responding to questions like: "What information do I know about this coffee, beyond its intrinsic attributes?" Just as the descriptive assessment seeks to list significant sensory attributes of the coffee, the extrinsic assessment aims to list as many informational attributes as possible that may make the coffee valuable to buyers in the marketplace.

## 2 Scope

This standard has been designed for the extrinsic assessment of green coffee, in conjunction with the methods in other standards of this series (see section 3). The processes for selecting and training qualified assessors are outside the scope of this standard.

## 4 Terms and Definitions

### 4.1 General Terms

**Attribute(s).** A property that is characteristic of something; a quality or feature regarded as a characteristic or inherent part of a coffee. A product (or coffee) can be thought of as a collection of attributes. Well-defined attributes can be identified using a variety of methods.

**Assessor.** The person who compiles and organizes the extrinsic information about a coffee to complete the extrinsic assessment.

**Extrinsic attribute.** Extrinsic attributes are information or symbolic features of a coffee that are communicated throughout the value chain or supply system. Examples include a coffee's place of origin, the name of the producer, or any certifications it might carry.

**Specialty coffee.** A coffee or coffee experience that is recognized for its distinctive attributes and, because of these attributes, has significant extra value in the marketplace.

### 4.2 Extrinsic Attributes Terms

The following definitions are offered as a non-exhaustive list of extrinsic assessment terms that are not self-explanatory.

**Farming.** Planting, tending, and harvesting coffee fruits from coffee trees.

**Region.** The coffee-producing region within a country, or other sub-national geographical information, such as state, province, municipality, village, etc.

**Species.** The species of the *Coffea* genus. Examples: *Coffea arabica*, *Coffea canephora*.

**Variety or blend of varieties.** The specific variety within a *Coffea* species, or a known blend of varieties. Examples: Castillo, SL28.

**Processing.** Preparing green coffee beans for stable transportation and storage. Processing may be used as a tool to impact a coffee's sensory attributes and, hence, to differentiate coffee.

**Wet Mill/Processing Station.** The company or location where coffee is transformed into a stable, dry form, such as parchment or dry cherry.

**Dry Mill.** The company or location where the coffee is hulled and/or the green coffee lot is prepared for sale.

**Process Type.** The main category of the processing method (washed, natural, etc.).

**Process Description.** A detailed description of the process, beyond the process type.

**Trading.** The activities including both "exporting" (arranging a coffee's departure from its country of origin, including coordinating logistics and absorbing financial risk) and "importing" (receiving coffee in its destination country, including coordinating logistics and absorbing financial risk).

**Grade.** A quality grade assigned to a lot of coffee under national or international standards for green coffee.

**ICO Number.** The lot number used by the exporter under the International Coffee Organization's (ICO) conventions.

**Farm gate price.** The price received by the producer for a specific lot of coffee.

**Certification.** An attestation from a third party that certain qualifications have been met using standards and independent verification of compliance with the practices and norms stipulated by the specific standard.

## 5 The Extrinsic Assessment

### 5.1 Overview

A complex product like coffee has many potential extrinsic attributes. During an extrinsic assessment, evaluators record these attributes using designated checkboxes and supplementary notes in the Extrinsic Assessment Form (see section 7). The form includes a selection of widely recognized and value-generating attributes as checkboxes, rather than as an exhaustive list. To capture more detailed information, assessors should add their own freely elicited attributes. This standard outlines the categories and attributes included as checkboxes in the extrinsic assessment and provides a list of additional potential attributes that assessors may add as freely elicited notes.

If an attribute appears in one of the checkboxes, it does not necessarily mean that it is more important or more valuable than attributes that are not listed. Rather, these attributes appear because they have been demonstrated to generate value and are recognizable to assessors working anywhere in the coffee system.

### 5.2 Procedure to Complete the Extrinsic Assessment

To reduce bias, any assessment of extrinsic information shall be kept wholly separate from the sensory assessments. Only after all assessments have been completed should the separate results be compiled into a single dossier. Sometimes the assessments may be performed by different people in different roles. For example, a quality control specialist may complete the descriptive assessment, a buyer the affective assessment, and a sales representative the extrinsic assessment. The latter would here be called the "assessor" (see 4.1). The assessor should search for information from different sources, for example, in the sample label, offer sheet, etc. The assessor may choose to request information from suppliers, in order to complete the profile if some information is lacking, though a single coffee will likely never receive a mark in every single box.

Whether an assessor uses paper forms (see section 7) or software to record the extrinsic information, the procedure is the same. The information available about a coffee is organized in five categories: Farming, Processing, Trading, Certifications, and Other. Each category includes a list of attributes. The assessor shall check the attributes to indicate the information they possess about the coffee. For example, if the farm gate price is known, the corresponding box under the "Trading" category shall be checked. Similarly, if the assessor knows a coffee to be a honey process, they are aware of the processing method and therefore shall check the "Processing Type" box. However, as the processing method is neither "Washed" nor "Natural," the assessor shall check "Other."

Next, the relevant information in each category shall be recorded. To our first example above: if the farm gate price is not relevant to the assessor, it does not need to be recorded, even if known. Conversely, if the processing type in the second example is relevant, the assessor should not only tick "Other," but should also write "Honey" under "Relevant Processing Information."

### 5.3 Farming Information

In the extrinsic assessment, the "Farming" category encompasses the following list of extrinsic attributes:

- Country
- Region (which may include sub-national geographical information, such as state, province, municipality, village, etc.)
- Name of farm or co-op
- Name of producer(s)
- Species
- Variety or blend of varieties
- Harvest date/year
- Other

The assessor should populate the "Relevant Farming Information" category with similar or related information as freely elicited descriptors:

- Other geographical information, which may include:
  - Altitude
  - Latitude
  - Longitude
  - Farm map
- Other botanical, cultivation, or picking information, which may include:
  - Pruning
  - Fertilizing
  - Shading
  - Other growing conditions
  - Harvesting approach

### 5.4 Processing Information

In the "Processing" category, assessors shall record the following attributes, when known or present:

- Name of Processor(s):
  - Wet Mill/Processing Station
  - Dry Mill
  - Other (which could include the name of the lead processors if key individuals are known)
- Process Type:
  - Washed
  - Natural
  - Other
- Process Description
- Decaffeinated

With the broad range of names and descriptions used in processing, the "Other" section for "Process Type" could include a wide range of information. Basic descriptive information about the process type refers to the last layer of the fruit left on the seed by the end of the drying process:

- Fruit-dried (dry process, natural)
- Mucilage-dried (honey, semi-dry)
- Parchment-dried (washed, wet process, semi-washed, demucilaged)
- Seed-dried (wet hulled)

Additional specific information may also be documented in this section, including:

- Name or type of process applied, as identified by the processor
- Duration of fermentation process(es)
- Temperature or humidity documented (or water activity)
- Use of any fermentation aids
- Use of any flavoring aids

A succinct description of a coffee's processing is sufficient to tick the "Processing Description" checkbox. As an example, even a short summary such as "the ripe coffee cherries are pulped, fermented for about 12 hours, and then dried mechanically" could count as a valid processing description. However, more detail is usually valuable. For example, the type of fermentation, as described by the processor, may be a relevant extrinsic attribute for assessors. Additionally, assessors may note if starter cultures, fermentation adjuncts, or flavorings of any kind have been added during the processing, as described by the processor.

Assessors may note the type of decaffeination process, such as water, CO2, methylene chloride, or ethyl acetate methods, as a freely elicited attribute.

### 5.5 Trading Information

In the "Trading" category, assessors shall record any grades the coffee might qualify for, such as:

- Grade: size grade (AA, Supremo, etc.), or other grade (EP, SHG, SHB, etc.)

As well as other trading-related information, such as:

- ICO Number
- Name of importer
- Name of exporter
- Farm gate price
- Lot size
- Other

Assessors may include under "Relevant Trading Information" information a coffee may accrue during the trading process, like:

- Trading model, for example "direct trade," "spot purchase," etc.
- Free on Board (FOB) price
- Price premium
- Traceability information
- Payment terms
- Duration of relationship between buyer and seller

### 5.6 Certification Information

Assessors shall record the certifications the coffee might possess, such as:

- 4C
- Fair trade (including Fairtrade)
- Organic
- Rainforest Alliance
- Food safety
- Other

Other information that may be captured here, as freely elicited descriptors, includes:

- Additional detail about certifications held (i.e., certificate number, issue or expiry dates, verification links, etc.).
- Other third-party certification schemes like SMBC Bird Friendly or Regenerative Organic.
- Second-party verification schemes, developed by companies rather than in collectives (for example, Nespresso AAA or Starbucks C.A.F.E. Practices).
- Geographical Indication, an intellectual property right used on products that have characteristics attributable to a specific geographical origin (for example, Cafe de Colombia, Cafe Veracruz, Cafe Genuino Antigua).

### 5.7 Other

The above checklist is not meant to be exhaustive. Any other extrinsic attribute, including more personal information about the farm or producer that might add value to the coffee in the supply chain, should be listed, to help in the final assessment of a coffee's total value. Examples of this could include miscellaneous attributes, such as the farm story, the coffee producer's gender/youth status, and the coffee's social/environmental impact. A common attribute in this category is the following:

- Awards. This could include direct or indirect links to awards, e.g., Cup of Excellence winner lot, previous Cup of Excellence award-winning farm, coffee used by barista champion, SCA Sustainability Award winner, etc.

## 6 Recording the Assessment Results

The use of a cupping app or cupping platform, specifically designed for the Coffee Value Assessment data structure, is recommended. In cases where the use of a CVA cupping platform is not practical or is unavailable, tasters should use the form in section 7.

## 7 Appendix (Informative): Extrinsic Form

[Extrinsic Assessment Form - see original PDF]

The Extrinsic Form is a one-page, repeatable assessment sheet with the following structure. Each sample receives its own block containing:

**Header fields:** Name, Date, Purpose, Sample No.

**Farming section:** Checkboxes for Country, Region, Name of Farm or Co-op, Name of Producer(s), Species, Variety or Varieties, Harvest Date/Year, and Other. Adjacent free-text area for "Relevant Farming Information."

**Processing section:** Checkboxes for Name of Processor(s) (with sub-items: Wet Mill/Station, Dry Mill, Other), Process Type (with sub-items: Washed, Natural, Other), Decaffeinated, and Process Description. Adjacent free-text area for "Relevant Processing Information."

**Trading section:** Checkboxes for Grade, ICO Number, Name of Importer, Name of Exporter, Farm Gate Price, Lot Size, and Other. Adjacent free-text area for "Relevant Trading Information."

**Certifications section:** Checkboxes for 4C, Fair trade, Organic, Rainforest Alliance, Food Safety, and Other. Adjacent free-text area for "Relevant Certification Information."

**Other section:** Checkbox for Awards. Adjacent free-text area for "Other Relevant Information."

The form in the original PDF includes two sample blocks per page, allowing assessment of two coffee samples on a single printed sheet.
