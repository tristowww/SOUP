01
On-Brand
Woven Threads

Each flavor descriptor becomes a thread flowing across the canvas — English in blue, Spanish in red. Where terms share meaning, golden connection lines weave the strands together. Where they don't, the threads pass untouched. The textile metaphor mirrors Loom's namesake: stories woven into a tapestry of cooperation across the supply chain.

Data: Scrape ~50 US roaster websites (bag descriptions, offering pages) + green coffee importer lot listings (Royal Coffee, Cafe Imports, Genuine Origin). Spanish terms from cooperative sites in Colombia, Guatemala, Honduras.
02
Exploratory
Force Network

Every descriptor is a node in a live physics simulation. Node size reflects how often the term appears. English and Spanish terms naturally cluster apart because they share few connections. Hover any node to see its cross-lingual links light up — the connected terms glow while everything else dims. Push nodes around with your cursor to feel the structure.

Data: Frequency counts from scraped roaster/importer text (EN) and Cup of Excellence jury notes + cooperative descriptions (ES). Cross-lingual links validated via bilingual cupping forms and SCA lexicon mapping.
03
Structural
Radial Bridge

English terms are arranged on the left arc, Spanish on the right. Arcs across the center connect concepts that share meaning — green for strong alignment, gold for partial, dashed for weak. The empty space between unconnected terms is as revealing as the connections. Terms like "Complex" and "Juicy" reach out and find nothing on the other side.

Data: Alignment scoring via multilingual embedding models (e.g., LABSE or multilingual SBERT) applied to scraped descriptor corpora. Manual validation with bilingual Q-graders.
04
Narrative
Supply Chain Flow

Tracks how a single coffee's description transforms as it moves through the supply chain. At origin, a producer calls it "dulce, suave, buen cuerpo." By the time a US roaster prints the bag, it's "caramel, honey, velvety body, bright and juicy." Each handoff adds, translates, or replaces language — the flavor doesn't change, but the story does.

Data: Trace the same lots through exporter catalogs (e.g., Ally Coffee, Volcafe), importer offerings (Royal, Cafe Imports), and final roaster bag copy. Cup of Excellence lots are ideal — documented from farm through auction.
05
Quantitative
Dot Frequency

Each dot represents approximately 50 uses of a descriptor across the scraped corpus. Blue dots for English roaster language, red for Spanish producer language. The rows where only one color appears — "Bright" with 468 EN dots and zero ES, or "Sweet/Dulce" with 724 ES dots and zero EN — are the vocabulary gaps made physical.

Data: NLP frequency extraction (tokenize + custom NER) from scraped roaster bag copy (~3,000 offerings) and Spanish-language cooperative/exporter sites (~800 descriptions).
06
Contrast
Valence Divergence

The same word carries opposite emotional weight in different contexts. "Fermented" extends right (positive) when a US roaster describes a natural-process coffee, but extends left (negative) in Colombian quality control where it signals a processing defect. "Bright" is the highest praise on a US cupping table and means "sour" to a retail consumer. These valence flips are where miscommunication lives.

Data: Sentiment/valence scoring from SCA cupping form annotations, US specialty reviews (e.g., Coffee Review), Colombian Q-grader notes, and consumer review platforms (Amazon, trade show surveys).
07
Rank Shift
Bump Chart

Same terms, different priorities. "Chocolate" holds the #1 spot in English but drops to #5 for producers. "Dulce" (sweet) is the #1 producer descriptor but doesn't even appear in the English specialty vocabulary. Lines that cross dramatically — like "Clean" jumping from #6 in EN to #4 in ES — reveal where values align despite different words. Dashed lines trailing off mark terms that exist in only one language.

Data: Ranked frequency lists from both corpora. EN rankings from roaster/importer scrapes. ES rankings from Cup of Excellence (cupofexcellence.org) jury forms, Federación Nacional de Cafeteros resources, and cooperative marketing materials.
08
Correlation
Scatter Bubble

X-axis plots English frequency, Y-axis plots Spanish frequency. A diagonal line marks where a term would sit if used equally in both languages. Terms near the diagonal — "Chocolate," "Clean/Limpio" — are shared concepts. Terms stuck on the X-axis ("Bright," "Complex") exist only in roaster English. Terms on the Y-axis ("Dulce," "Cuerpo") belong to producers. The empty upper-right quadrant — no term is high-frequency in both — is the visual thesis.

Data: Paired frequency counts requiring bilingual concordance. Best source: Cup of Excellence dual-language jury sheets. Supplemented by scraping the same lot descriptions from Spanish-language exporter sites and their English-language importer counterparts.
09
Density
Category Heatmap

Zooms out from individual terms to semantic categories: sweet, fruit, mouthfeel, aroma, abstract, earth/agricultural. Cell darkness shows how much each category dominates a vocabulary. Spanish is overwhelmingly sweet and mouthfeel-focused — producers describe coffee through body and sweetness. English spreads across fruit, aroma, and abstract categories — roasters prize complexity and specificity.

Data: Category tagging via SCA Flavor Wheel taxonomy + custom NER pipeline. Map each scraped descriptor to one of 6-8 categories. Agricultural terms (yield, ripeness) require scraping cooperative field reports and agronomist communications.
10
Proportion
Waffle Grid

Two 100-square grids — each square represents 1% of the vocabulary's total descriptor usage. The English grid is a patchwork of many colors: fruit, aroma, sweet, mouthfeel, abstract all compete for space. The Spanish grid is dominated by two large blocks — sweet and mouthfeel take up over half the grid. This proportional view makes the structural difference visceral.

Data: Same frequency corpus as Dot Frequency, aggregated by category. Proportions calculated as category_total / corpus_total for each language independently.
11
Overlap
Vocabulary Venn

The simplest, most striking summary. Two overlapping circles: 10 terms unique to English (berry, citrus, bright, complex, juicy...), 8 terms unique to Spanish (dulce, cuerpo, suave, panela, rendimiento...), and just 6 shared pairs in the center. That narrow overlap is the entire common ground between two languages describing the same cup of coffee.

Data: Deduplicated term lists from both corpora, cross-referenced via bilingual embedding similarity + manual validation. Shared terms require alignment scores above a threshold (e.g., cosine similarity > 0.7).
12
Distribution
Beeswarm

Each circle is a flavor descriptor placed along a frequency axis. English terms float above the center line, Spanish below. Circle size reflects how often the term appears. The physical separation between the two swarms — and the different shapes they form — shows how the two vocabularies have different density profiles. English clusters tightly in the 200–600 range with many mid-frequency terms. Spanish has a few dominant terms and a long tail.

Data: Full frequency distributions from both scraped corpora. Beeswarm positioning uses force-directed collision avoidance. Best visualized after scraping 50+ roasters (EN) and 30+ cooperatives or CoE archives (ES).
