Lost in Translation

The coffee in your cup was described in at least three different languages before it reached you. Somewhere along the way, the producer's voice was replaced by the roaster's vocabulary.

The bag says "blueberry, meyer lemon, and honey." The farmer said "dulce."

Specialty coffee uses one of the most detailed sensory vocabularies of any consumer product. A single bag might describe its contents using terms like bright citrus acidity, stone fruit, and syrupy body, language drawn from decades of cupping science, largely codified through the SCA Flavor Wheel. These terms are precise, internally consistent, and widely understood by the roasting and buying community that created them.

But those protocols were developed in English, by cuppers and roasters in consuming countries. When a Colombian farmer describes the same coffee, the language looks different. A producer might say dulce (sweet), cuerpo (body), or suave (smooth). The gap between these descriptions is not a failure of translation. It is a structural difference in how each side of the supply chain talks about quality.

This project applies NLP and cross-lingual analysis to that gap: scraping tasting note language from US roasters and Latin American producers, extracting actual flavor descriptor phrases (filtering out marketing copy, farm names, and negated terms), counting frequencies within those phrases, and mapping where meaning aligns, diverges, or inverts.

Background

A worker-owned roastery asks "who benefits?" about every decision: sourcing, wages, community investment. This project applies that question to language. Who benefits from the way specialty coffee describes flavor, and whose vocabulary gets overwritten along the way?

Methodology

English tasting notes were extracted from product descriptions scraped via the Shopify JSON API across US specialty roasters. Rather than counting terms across entire product pages, the pipeline first isolates actual tasting note phrases using four strategies: structured HTML elements with note-related classes, regex patterns matching "Notes: X, Y, Z" formats, detection of comma-separated flavor lists, and sentence-level filtering for contexts containing signal words and known flavor vocabulary. Negated terms are excluded. Capitalized mid-sentence terms that likely represent proper nouns are skipped. Spanish descriptors were scraped from Latin American cooperative websites and filtered using adapted patterns ("notas de," "sabor a," "perfil de taza").

Language across the supply chain

The same coffee passes through distinct vocabularies at each stage. Roasters lean on precise sensory descriptors; importers bridge origin and market language; institutions codify standards and definitions; producers describe what they grow in their own terms. The grid below shows how the top terms shift at each link in the chain.

Reading this grid: Each column shows the top terms extracted from that source type's content. Terms that appear in multiple columns represent shared vocabulary; terms unique to one column reveal where language diverges.

How description changes across the supply chain

One coffee, four descriptions
Producer: "Dulce, suave, buen cuerpo" → Exporter: "Sweet, full body, smooth" → Importer: "Honey sweetness, heavy body, silky mouthfeel" → Roaster: "Caramel and honey, syrupy body, bright and juicy with notes of blueberry"

At each handoff the description is rewritten. No one is being dishonest; each person describes what they taste using available tools. But the cumulative effect is that the producer's voice is overwritten entirely by the time the coffee reaches a consumer.

Valence inversions

Some terms carry opposite connotations depending on context. Fermented signals desirable complexity in US specialty but marks a defect in Colombian quality control. Bright is high praise on a US cupping table, cautious for Latin American producers, and "sour" to retail consumers. Earthy is a defect flag in US specialty but a defining terroir characteristic for Sumatran wet-hulled coffees. When the same word registers as praise in one context and failure in another, the issue extends beyond translation into whose value system the vocabulary encodes.

Implications for equity

Price is tightly coupled to description. A lot marketed as complex acidity, stone fruit, and floral notes commands a different price than dulce y suave, even for the same coffee. The English vocabulary does not just describe flavor; it assigns economic value. There is room to build on the SCA Flavor Wheel by incorporating producer-originated descriptors, preserving terms like panela and suave on labels (the way wine preserves terroir and cru), and involving producers in their own coffee's narrative.

How we built this

This analysis is built on real data, not hypotheticals. Here is what the pipeline does, step by step:

1. Gather source data. We pull tasting notes from four source types across the coffee supply chain: US specialty roasters (Shopify product API), green coffee importers like Ally Coffee and Cafe Imports (HTML scraping of offering lists), coffee institutions like the National Coffee Association and SCA (web content plus local SCA standards documents including the Descriptive Assessment protocol, Coffee Value Assessment framework, and Attributes Framework whitepaper), and Latin American producer and cooperative sites (Shopify endpoints and direct HTML scraping).

2. Extract actual flavor language. Raw product pages contain marketing copy, farm names, altitude numbers, and processing details alongside flavor descriptors. The pipeline filters down to actual tasting note phrases using pattern matching, flavor vocabulary detection, and negation filtering. Terms like "not bitter" are excluded. Proper nouns and brand names are skipped.

3. Count what is distinctive. Rather than simple word counts, we use TF-IDF (term frequency-inverse document frequency) to surface terms that are characteristic of each language's vocabulary, not just frequent. This separates the signal — the words each tradition actually relies on — from the noise.

4. Align across languages. To compare English and Spanish terms, we use multilingual sentence embeddings — a model trained to place words with similar meanings close together regardless of language. This lets us measure how well "honey" and "miel" align without relying on a dictionary.

5. Discover topic structures. NMF (non-negative matrix factorization) topic modeling groups terms into clusters for each language independently. This reveals whether the two vocabularies organize flavor into the same categories or fundamentally different ones.

6. Score valence flips. Some terms carry opposite connotations in different contexts. The pipeline scores each term's positive or negative framing within its surrounding text to detect where meaning inverts across the supply chain.

All of the data you see in the tables and visualization above was generated by this pipeline. The code, data, and methodology are open source.
