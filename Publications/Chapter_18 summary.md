## Document Summary

This chapter presents a theory-grounded framework for leveraging AI to enhance workplace accessibility and inclusion. It identifies five structural barriers that limit access to work for marginalized groups, then proposes AI-based solutions organized around five framework pillars: **Autonomy, Individualization, Representation, Anonymity, and Belonging**. The framework shifts organizations from reactive accommodation to proactive, AI-enabled inclusive design.

## Framework Background

### Overview of Access to Work Issues

The concept of **access to work** includes the ability of all individuals to obtain and maintain successful employment through full participation, as desired, in the workplace (Fain, 2006; Iwarsson & Stahl, 2003). This involves the structural, organizational, and societal barriers that may hinder individuals from entering, remaining, and thriving in the workforce. Factors that can promote access to work include physical accessibility, availability of necessary resources, equitable opportunities, and supportive, inclusive work environments.

A more traditional focus for workplace design and structure is on majority populations, creating structural barriers that systematically disadvantage marginalized individuals, as seen through the emphasis on diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives recently (Park et al., 2023; Roberson, 2019). Policies, procedures, and environments resulting from a more traditional focus may fail to accommodate the diverse needs of employees from underrepresented populations, specifically those with disabilities, chronic illnesses, neurodivergence, or marginalized identities based on race, ethnicity, religion, gender identity, sexual orientation, socioeconomic status, and age.

Emerging artificial intelligence (AI) technologies, including adaptive machine learning, personalized workflows and development opportunities, and user-friendly interfaces, are creating more unique and revolutionary possibilities for inclusivity (Adolph et al., 2025; Thompson et al., 2023; Touzet, 2023).

The presented framework focuses on innovative AI applications to facilitate a more inclusive workplace with subsequent outcomes, such as improved organizational culture, reduced turnover, and less emotional labor for underrepresented groups. Rather than trying to adapt existing systems and practices to include marginalized groups retroactively, AI technology can create more authentically inclusive and supportive environments within an organizational structure proactively (Robert et al., 2020; Shams et al., 2025; Zallio et al., 2025). This approach presents a **shift from reactive accommodation to proactive inclusion**, allowing for increased access to work from the ground up and from the individual to organizational levels.

## Structural Problems in Organizations

Despite widespread organizational emphasis, efforts, and investment in DEI recently, numerous, often unintended structural barriers persist that systematically limit workplace accessibility and career advancement for marginalized groups.

### Surface-Level Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Practices

Drawing upon **Impression Management Theory** (Goffman, 1949), many organizations may engage in superficial actions designed to promote positive perceptions rather than enact meaningful change:

- **Diversity washing** \-- Organizations publicly display an emphasis on diversity while failing to address fundamental issues surrounding inequality (Baker et al., 2024; Vertovec, 2012)

- **Performative allyship** \-- Public demonstrations of support without substantive action (Noon, 2018), such as displaying people of color or with disabilities on marketing material to appear diverse without actually providing support

- **Tokenism** \-- Highlighting a few underrepresented individuals while failing to deal with potential organizational lack of diversity (Kanter, 1977)

These forms of surface-level initiatives treat diversity as a means of compliance instead of a more strategic organizational effort.

### Post-COVID-19 Return-to-Office Challenges

The **Job Demands-Resources Model** (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007) helps explain how post-pandemic transitions have created disparate impacts on underrepresented populations. The shift to remote work temporarily reduced some barriers but return-to-office mandates have reintroduced inequalities in access to work.

Remote work can benefit diverse employees by:

- Reducing commute barriers and lowering living costs

- Increasing flexibility for those with caregiving responsibilities

- Decreasing exposure to microaggressions and workplace bias for individuals who may not wish to disclose an underrepresented or disadvantaged population status (Allen et al., 2021)

The shift back to in-person work shows an increase in **emotional labor demands** for minorities, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and neurodivergent employees, who now must navigate environments that have unaddressed inclusion challenges (Grandey, 2000).

**Code-switching requirements** are increased for racial, ethnic, and gender minorities, who must adapt their communication, appearance, and behavior to fit norms, requiring emotional labor that detracts from job performance and contributes to burnout (Clair et al., 2019; McCluney et al., 2021).

**Masking** for neurodivergent individuals has also intensified as such individuals return to work environments that do not accommodate their sensory, social, or cognitive needs, leading to decreased performance and increased turnover (Hartman & Hartman, 2024; Pryke-Hobbes et al., 2023).

### Bias in Hiring and Selection Processes

**Implicit Bias Theory** (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995) demonstrates how unconscious stereotypes influence hiring despite organizational diversity efforts. The overemphasis on traditional criteria \-- prestigious degrees, specific experience, and cultural fit \-- systematically excludes diverse talent pools who may have relevant skills from non-traditional backgrounds (Shukla, 2022; Uhlmann & Cohen, 2005).

Bias also exists in AI selection tools, creating potentially unintended **algorithmic discrimination** (Shams et al., 2025; Thompson et al., 2023). Practices such as **resume whitening**, where candidates modify names to appear more ethnically \"white,\" highlight perceived discrimination in hiring (Bertrand & Mullainathan, 2004; Kang et al., 2016).

### Failed DEI Initiative Implementation

Building on **Organizational Change Theory** (Lewin, 1947), many DEI efforts fail due to ineffective execution and follow-through rather than poor intentions. Leadership often demonstrates a lack of sustained behavioral change despite public commitments to DEI (Dobbin & Kalev, 2017). This reactive and singular approach results in temporary box-ticking diversity that fails to lead to systemic organizational change (Roberson et al., 2024).

### Unsafe Expression and Communication Barriers

**Psychological Safety Theory** (Edmondson, 1999) emphasizes the importance of employees feeling that their organization provides a safe culture and environment to voice concerns and authentically express themselves without fear of negative consequences. Many organizations lack processes for employees to report bias, discrimination, or exclusionary practices safely and anonymously.

The absence of confidential feedback channels prevents organizations from identifying patterns of exclusion and microaggressions that prevent workplace inclusion (Basford et al., 2014; Junca-Silva & Ferreira, 2025; Sue et al., 2007).

### Implications of Structural Problems

These structural problems impact underrepresented groups differentially, creating consequences beyond the individual-level experiences to impact organizational-level performance and effectiveness:

**Individual level:**

- Decline in trust among diverse employees

- Higher turnover, absenteeism, and disengagement (Dobbin & Kalev, 2017; Mannix & Neale, 2005)

- Significant emotional demands from code-switching, identity concealment, and navigating microaggressions (Grandey & Gabriel, 2015; Hewlin, 2003)

**Organizational level:**

- Difficulty attracting and keeping diverse talent

- Reinforcement of existing biases; reduced innovation and competitive advantage

- Failure to meet the needs of diverse markets and stakeholders (Alshemmari & Monawer, 2024; Ostergaard & Timmermans, 2023)

- Wasted resources, sunk costs, and legal and reputational risks

## Proposed Framework

These structural problems can be addressed by leveraging five specific needs and resources that promote organizational allyship, drawing on **Optimal Distinctiveness Theory** (Brewer, 1991). This theory emphasizes that individuals strive to balance two fundamental needs: the need to belong and feel part of a group, and the need to maintain a sense of individuality through their unique identity.

The framework addresses these critical organizational needs by proposing targeted AI interventions across **five core pillars**: Autonomy, Individualization, Representation, Anonymity, and Belonging. **Belonging** emerges as the overarching cultural outcome of successfully implementing these pillars.

## Pillar 1: Autonomy

Autonomy represents the fundamental capacity for employees to manage their professional development, career pathways, and daily work tasks effectively. Drawing on **Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan, 1985), this pillar emphasizes intrinsic motivation through competence, autonomy, and relatedness (Ryan & Deci, 2024). **Social Capital Theory** (Lin, 1999) highlights the importance of access to resources and opportunities through social networks and relationships.

### AI-Guided Learning and Career Progression

User-friendly and widely accessible AI applications (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) can be an effective means to employee-driven individualized career pathing, upskilling, and learning opportunities aligned with both individual goals and organizational needs.

This approach emphasizes **growth over set selection criteria**, supporting equitable access to skill development. **Adaptive growth paths** implement AI to match employees\' desired career trajectories based on industry trends and evolving employee skills, incorporating **Universal Design for Learning** principles (Rose & Meyer, 2002).

**Current applications:** Gloat (2015) recommends tailored learning and growth paths; Degreed (2012) curates learning journeys tied to career aspirations.

> **Individual impact:** Equitable access to growth opportunities aligned with strengths and interests.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Higher retention, engagement, and a more skill-diverse workforce (Carroll, 1991).

### Automated Task Matching for On-the-Job Training

AI systems can be designed to assign meaningful tasks based on employee skills and growth potential, replacing traditional approaches that often result in a mismatch between skills and job duties (Phillips, 1998; Shibly, 2019). **Growth-first matching** implements AI technology to reassign tasks dynamically and in real time as employees gain new knowledge, skills, and abilities.

**Current applications:** Monday.com Ltd. (2012) assigns tasks aligned with employee skills; Asana Inc. (2008) tracks completion and suggests future opportunities.

> **Individual impact:** Confidence and skill-building through fair opportunities.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Better talent usage, stronger team performance, and a more effective merit-based culture.

### AI-Powered Hiring Insights Looking at Transferable Skills and Interests

Implementing AI to evaluate transferable skills shifts from a selection focus on rigid qualifications to demonstrated potential. AI can create **anonymous profiles** from job applicants\' resumes, reducing resume whitening practices and reducing opportunity for bias (Jooss et al., 2024; Lin, 1999).

**Current applications:** Pymetrics (2013) uses neuroscience-based assessments; Eightfold AI (2016) analyzes transferable skills and matches candidates with roles.

> **Individual impact:** Increased access to opportunities based on demonstrated skills.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Expanded applicant pools, reduced bias, and a more inclusive workforce.

## Pillar 2: Individualization

Individualization deals with fostering inclusivity through individualized work structures and equitable accommodations that adapt to individual needs and preferences. By applying AI technology to create adaptive systems, organizations can move past standardization and create environments where all employees can fully participate.

### Adaptive Work Environments

AI can optimize both physical and virtual work environments to meet individual sensory, cognitive, and physical needs. **AI-enhanced biometric spaces** use biometric feedback to adjust lighting, temperature, noise levels, or offer stretching or posture suggestions, drawing on Person-Environment Fit Theory (Edwards et al., 1998).

**Current applications:** Humanscale (n.d.) uses predictive AI for personalized ergonomic setups; Microsoft Viva (2021) integrates AI to optimize digital workspace setups.

> **Individual impact:** Enhanced comfort and reduced stress.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Engagement, inclusivity, and reduced absenteeism.

### AI-Assisted Workflow Personalization

Existing AI and machine learning algorithms can predict and identify individual employees\' work habits and styles to personalize workflows, balance workloads, and optimize productivity. This is particularly beneficial for neurodivergent individuals or those who process information differently (Branicki et al., 2024; Rollnik-Sadowska & Grabinska, 2024).

**Current applications:** Clockwise Inc. (2016) optimizes schedules to create focused time blocks; Otter.ai Inc. (2016) automates meeting notes and task management.

> **Individual impact:** Reduced cognitive load and stress; personalized support to increase retention.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Higher productivity, improved satisfaction, and reduced turnover.

### AI-Driven Flexible Work Arrangements

Using AI applications to enable hybrid and remote work tailored to individual schedules and team dynamics ensures equal participation. Rooted in **Telework Equity Theory** (Golden & Eddleston, 2020), this approach addresses how traditional return-to-office policies can disproportionately disadvantage underrepresented groups.

Other innovative approaches include AI-driven platforms that make remote collaboration seamless, **virtual reality workspaces** adjustable to each employee\'s preferred layout, and **sensory-adaptive workspaces** that accommodate sensory sensitivities (Botha et al., 2024; Singer, 1999).

**Current applications:** VirBELA (2012) offers customizable VR workspaces; Slack (2009) facilitates asynchronous communication.

> **Individual impact:** Flexibility to balance personal and professional priorities.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Enhanced retention and inclusive collaboration.

## Pillar 3: Representation

Ensuring equitable visibility and inclusion in leadership, opportunities, and decision-making is crucial for creating truly diverse organizations.

### AI-Facilitated Mentorship Matching

AI can assess employees\' skills, interests, and career aspirations to create highly personalized mentorship matches that evolve over time. This builds on **Mentoring Theory** (Kram, 1988).

**Current applications:** Together Mentoring (2018) uses AI-driven algorithms; Ten Thousand Coffees (2014) facilitates structured networking.

### AI Translation and Captioning

Language accessibility is critical in global and multilingual teams. AI-powered translation and captioning tools ensure full participation by providing real-time language support, including sign language interpretation. This aligns with **Accessible Technology Theory** (Emiliani, 2006).

**Current applications:** Google Translate (2006) provides real-time translations; Otter.ai (2016) offers AI-powered transcription.

### Transparent AI-Dashboards for Diversity and Intersectionality Metrics

AI-powered dashboards that monitor diversity across multiple dimensions provide organizations with actionable insights, aligning with **Diversity Management Theory** (Thomas & Ely, 1996).

Beyond simple demographic tracking, future AI dashboards could incorporate **real-time sentiment analysis** to assess employee perceptions of inclusion and belonging.

**Current applications:** Syndio (2016) helps organizations analyze pay equity; Tableau (2003) enables customizable data visualization.

## Pillar 4: Anonymity

In the workplace, psychological safety is essential for fostering an inclusive environment in which all employees can contribute authentically without fear of bias or retaliation (Edmondson, 1999).

### Avatar-Based Video Meetings and Interviews

AI-powered avatar systems in virtual interactions represent a significant advance in reducing appearance-based biases. They provide a more engaging alternative to cameras-off policies while maintaining anonymity, aligning with **Implicit Bias Theory** (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995).

One potential extension is **identity-free hiring simulations**, which remove all identifying characteristics and present only task performance data.

**Current applications:** Gather Town (2020); avatar technologies in Zoom (2022) and Microsoft Teams (Mesh avatars, 2022).

### AI-Feedback Channels and Employee Input Platforms

Anonymous feedback mechanisms powered by AI play a crucial role in identifying and addressing systemic workplace issues, aligning with **Minority Stress Theory** (Meyer, 2003).

Beyond identifying recurring concerns, AI feedback systems can proactively suggest intervention strategies, shifting DEI efforts from reactive to proactive.

**Current applications:** AllVoices (2017) enables anonymous reporting; CultureAmp (2009) uses surveys to highlight inclusion barriers.

### AI-Moderated Communication for Bias

Real-time AI monitoring of workplace communications analyzes video calls or online chats for microaggressions and biased language, aligning with **Intergroup Contact Theory** (Allport, 1954).

**Current applications:** Textio (2014) flags and corrects biased language in written communication.

> **Individual impact:** Fosters confidence in authentic participation.
>
> **Organizational impact:** Strengthened team cohesion, enhanced trust, and a more innovative and inclusive workplace.

## Discussion

### Role of Industrial-Organizational Psychologists

I-O psychologists play a central role in translating this framework into practice, acting as scientist-practitioners who combine empirical research with practical application (Banks et al., 2016; Rynes et al., 2018). Their expertise allows organizations to:

- **Diagnose** structural barriers such as biased hiring practices, inequitable access to development, and exclusionary work environments

- **Design and implement** data-driven interventions grounded in psychological theory and organizational science

- **Train** leaders and employees to work effectively with AI tools, ensuring technology supplements human judgment rather than replacing it

### Ethical Considerations

**Transparency and fairness:** AI must be designed to avoid reinforcing existing inequities and should include clear guidelines for ethical use (Taddeo & Floridi, 2018).

**Privacy and data protection:** Especially important for employees whose data may be particularly sensitive, such as those with disabilities or health conditions (Ienca & Andorno, 2017).

**Human judgment preservation:** AI should supplement, rather than replace, human judgment (Searle, 1980).

**Inclusive governance:** AI tools must be developed and governed inclusively (Taddeo & Floridi, 2018), involving diverse perspectives in design and testing phases.

## Conclusion

AI can be leveraged to remove organizational structural barriers and allow employees to have access to meaningful employment without being limited by outdated systems susceptible to bias. The framework integrates four pillars:

- **Autonomy** \-- AI-guided learning and task-matching systems empower employees to drive their own growth

- **Individualization** \-- Adaptive work environments and personalized workflows create flexible, accessible spaces

- **Representation** \-- AI-driven mentorship matching and transparent diversity dashboards help ensure equal access to leadership pathways

- **Anonymity** \-- Avatar-based interactions, identity-free hiring, and confidential feedback channels protect psychological safety

Rooted in theory and ethical principles, this framework emphasizes designing AI systems and tools that focus on human needs rather than forcing individuals to adapt to the existing system. By adopting this holistic, ethically guided approach, organizations can act as allies by moving past expecting marginalized groups to adapt and instead build systems that proactively support all employees.
