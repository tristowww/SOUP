## Framework Background

This section provides the theoretical foundation for understanding access to work within the context of artificial intelligence applications. We begin by examining the core concepts and then explore how AI technologies intersect with workplace accessibility.

### Overview of Access to Work Issues

The concept of access to work includes the ability of all individuals to obtain and maintain successful employment through full participation, as desired, in the workplace (Fain, 2006; Iwarsson & Stahl, 2003). This involves the structural, organizational, and societal barriers that may hinder individuals from entering, remaining, and thriving in the workforce. Factors that can promote access to work, considering these barriers, include physical accessibility, availability of necessary resources, equitable opportunities, and supportive, inclusive work environments.

A more traditional focus for workplace design and structure is on majority populations, creating structural barriers that systematically disadvantage marginalized individuals, as seen through the emphasis on diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives recently (Park et al., 2023; Roberson, 2019). Policies, procedures, and environments resulting from a more traditional focus may fail to accommodate the diverse needs of employees from underrepresented populations, specifically those with disabilities, chronic illnesses, neurodivergence, or marginalized identities based on race, ethnicity, religion, gender identity, sexual orientation, socioeconomic status, and age. It is relevant to address workplace accessibility issues that stem from decisions focusing on majority populations, ensuring they are included in organizational considerations proactively.

Current technology and its more universal and user-friendly access present opportunities specifically in the artificial intelligence space to eliminate structural organizational barriers, creating more inclusive workplaces (Fain, 2006; Iwarsson & Stahl, 2003). Emerging artificial intelligence (AI) technologies, including adaptive machine learning, personalized workflows and development opportunities, and user-friendly interfaces, are creating more unique and revolutionary possibilities for inclusivity (Adolph et al., 2025; Thompson et al., 2023; Touzet, 2023).

The presented framework focuses on innovative AI applications to facilitate a more inclusive workplace with subsequent outcomes, such as improved organizational culture, reduced turnover, and less emotional labor for underrepresented groups. Rather than trying to adapt existing systems and practices to include marginalized groups retroactively, AI technology can create more authentically inclusive and supportive environments within an organizational structure proactively (Robert et al., 2020; Shams et al., 2025; Zallio et al., 2025). This approach presents a shift from reactive accommodation to proactive inclusion, allowing for increased access to work from the ground up and from the individual to organizational levels.

## Structural Problems in Organizations

Despite widespread organizational emphasis, efforts, and investment in DEI recently, numerous, often unintended structural barriers persist that systematically limit workplace accessibility and career advancement for marginalized groups. These efforts often fail, limiting true allyship and access to work for the populations they aim to support. While AI offers promising tools to help address these barriers, such as providing more personalized workflows, without proactive integration, these technologies can fail to reduce structural barriers and even reinforce inequalities. Understanding the theoretical backing and drawing from theory-driven approaches, the need to understand structural problems in organizations should not only include adapting policies or practices but also leveraging AI solutions intentionally to create true access to work and more inclusive workplaces.

### Surface-Level Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Practices

Drawing upon Impression Management Theory (Goffman, 1949), many organizations may engage in superficial actions designed to promote positive perceptions rather than enact meaningful change. Diversity washing represents one means through which this can manifest, where organizations publicly display an emphasis on diversity while failing to address fundamental issues surrounding inequality (Baker et al., 2024; Vertovec, 2012). Performative allyship from organizations may also be characterized by public demonstrations of support without substantive action (Noon, 2018), such displaying people of color or with disabilities on marketing material to appear diverse without actually providing support or making efforts to reach those populations in recruiting and selection (Dobbin & Kalev, 2017; Georgiadou et al., 2019). This type of tokenism highlights a few underrepresented individuals while failing to deal with potential organizational lack of diversity (Kanter, 1977). These forms of surface-level initiatives treat diversity as a means of compliance instead of a more strategic organizational effort.

### Post-COVID-19 Return-to-Office Challenges

The Job Demands-Resources Model (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007) helps explain how post-pandemic transitions have created disparate impacts on underrepresented populations. The shift to remote work temporarily reduced some barriers but return-to-office mandates have reintroduced inequalities in access to work.

Recent presidential action mandating a return to in-person work for federal employees in the United States has prompted other workplaces to follow. This move highlights significant challenges related to accessibility in the transition from remote to in-person work (The White House, 2025). Remote work can benefit diverse employees by reducing commute barriers, allowing individuals to lower living costs and live farther from work, increasing flexibility for those with caregiving responsibilities, and decreasing exposure to microaggressions and workplace bias for individuals who may not wish to disclose an underrepresented or disadvantaged population status (Allen et al., 2021).

The shift back to in-person work shows an increase in emotional labor demands for minorities, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and neurodivergent employees, who now must navigate environments that have unaddressed inclusion challenges (Grandey, 2000). These employees face increased pressure to manage emotions, mask in various ways, provide diversity education to coworkers, and serve as representatives of their groups. Additionally, code-switching requirements are increased for racial, ethnic, and gender minorities, who must adapt their communication, appearance, and behavior to fit norms, requiring emotional labor that detracts from job performance and contributes to burnout (Clair et al., 2019; McCluney et al., 2021).

Masking for neurodivergent individuals has also intensified as such individuals return to work environments that do not accommodate their sensory, social, or cognitive needs, leading to decreased performance and increased turnover (Hartman & Hartman, 2024; Pryke-Hobbes et al., 2023). Challenges faced by transitioning LGBTQ+ individuals are also relevant to the return-to-office mandate, forcing these individuals to navigate decisions about disclosure and potential negative perceptions or harassment. Additionally, barriers for pregnant women and working parents are exacerbated when trying to balance caregiving and return to work responsibilities, potentially leading to negative perceptions about their commitment to work and ability to perform their job (Allen et al., 2021).

### Bias in Hiring and Selection Processes

Implicit Bias Theory (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995) demonstrates how unconscious stereotypes influence hiring despite organizational diversity efforts. These biases may disadvantage job applicants from underrepresented populations.

The overemphasis on traditional criteria, such as prestigious degrees, specific experience, and cultural fit, systematically excludes diverse talent pools who may have relevant skills from non-traditional backgrounds (Shukla, 2022; Uhlmann & Cohen, 2005). These criteria often reflect differential opportunities instead of actual applicant capabilities.

Bias also exists in AI selection tools, creating potentially unintended algorithmic discrimination (Shams et al., 2025; Thompson et al., 2023). AI systems trained on historical data often perpetuate existing biases despite being perceived as more objective than human raters or decision-makers. Practices such as resume whitening, where candidates modify names to appear more ethnically "white," highlight perceived discrimination in hiring (Bertrand & Mullainathan, 2004; Kang et al., 2016). Despite efforts toward equal opportunity, many hiring processes remain biased against marginalized applicants.

### Failed Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Initiative Implementation

Building on Organizational Change Theory (Lewin, 1947), many DEI efforts fail due to ineffective execution and follow-through rather than poor intentions. Leadership often demonstrates a lack of sustained behavioral change despite public commitments to DEI (Dobbin & Kalev, 2017). Without consistent accountability and ongoing measurement, DEI initiatives may become disconnected from day-to-day decisions and organizational practices.

Organizations frequently implement interventions in isolation, such as single training sessions or policy updates, without integrating diversity principles and policies into core business processes like performance management, succession planning, recruitment and selection, and resource allocation (Georgiadou et al., 2019). This reactive and singular approach results in temporary box-ticking diversity that fails to lead to systemic organizational change (Roberson et al., 2024). The lack of subsequent assessment of program effectiveness in the long term prevents organizations from identifying gaps and making directed efforts at improving diversity systemically, leading to potential perceptions of inauthenticity from employees about leadership and organizational diversity efforts.

### Unsafe Expression and Communication Barriers

Psychological Safety Theory (Edmondson, 1999) emphasizes the importance of employees feeling that their organization provides a safe culture and environment to voice concerns and authentically express themselves without fear of negative consequences. Many organizations lack processes for employees to report bias, discrimination, or exclusionary practices safely and anonymously. Traditional reporting structures often require formal complaints through human resources (HR) or systems that may perpetuate the problems being reported.

The absence of confidential feedback channels prevents organizations from identifying patterns of exclusion and microaggressions that prevent workplace inclusion (Basford et al., 2014; Junca-Silva & Ferreira, 2025; Sue et al., 2007). Additionally, many organizations may fail to protect employees who report discrimination, leading to career retaliation from leadership. Communication barriers can compound these issues because employees from different cultural backgrounds or with differential communication needs may not understand reporting procedures or may lack confidence that their concerns will be heard. The lack of multilingual support and accessible communication processes can further discourage reporting among diverse employees, further facilitating exclusionary environments.

### Implications of Structural Problems

These structural problems impact underrepresented groups differentially, creating consequences beyond the individual-level experiences to impact organizational-level performance and effectiveness. The effects of exclusionary workplace environments, even as an unintended result of structural problems, compromise both individual and organizational-level goals. When diversity efforts remain surface-level without addressing deeper structural issues, trust among diverse employees can decline, resulting in higher turnover, absenteeism, and disengagement (Dobbin & Kalev, 2017; Mannix & Neale, 2005). The emotional demands on underrepresented employees can be significant because they need to code-switch, hide parts of their identities, and navigate frequent microaggressions, all of which can affect mental and physical health (Grandey & Gabriel, 2015; Hewlin, 2003).

At an organizational level, these problems make it difficult to attract and keep diverse talent. Biased hiring practices may contribute to limited opportunities for candidates from underrepresented backgrounds, while a lack of safe communication channels prevents employees from reporting exclusionary behaviors, perpetuating biased practices (Allen et al., 2021; Bertrand & Mullainathan, 2004). As a result, organizations with less diverse workforces often continue to reinforce existing biases, struggle to innovate to create a competitive advantage, and fail to meet the needs of diverse markets and stakeholders (Alshemmari & Monawer, 2024; Ostergaard & Timmermans, 2023). The gap between stated values and actual practices can lead to wasted resources or sunk costs, and legal and reputational risks. Without addressing these structural barriers directly, organizations risk losing diverse talent to build a more inclusive organizational culture.

Overcoming these challenges requires moving beyond performative actions and committing to real structural change. This framework draws on evidence-based theories to propose AI-based solutions organized around four core elements or pillars within the framework: Autonomy, Individualization, Representation, and Anonymity. The theoretical foundation of the framework ensures that suggested AI applications are grounded in scientific understanding of human behavior and organizational dynamics. ## The AIRA Framework

### Proposed Framework

These structural problems can be addressed by leveraging five specific needs and resources that promote organizational allyship, drawing on Optimal Distinctiveness Theory (Brewer, 1991). This theory emphasizes that individuals strive to balance two fundamental needs: the need to belong and feel part of a group, and the need to maintain a sense of individuality through their unique identity. In organizational contexts, this means employees feel most engaged via autonomy and individualization when they are included as valued members of the group while also being recognized for their unique identities, experiences, and contributions.

The framework addresses these critical organizational needs by proposing targeted AI interventions across five core pillars: Autonomy, Individualization, Representation, Anonymity, and Belonging. Belonging emerges as the overarching cultural outcome of successfully implementing these pillars, fostering stronger connections, psychological safety, and shared commitment across diverse employee groups. By focusing on these concepts, organizations can proactively remove barriers and create environments where all employees feel both connected to the organization and authentically themselves. ## Autonomy

Autonomy represents the fundamental capacity for employees to manage their professional development, career pathways, and daily work tasks effectively. Drawing on Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985), this pillar emphasizes intrinsic motivation through competence, autonomy, and relatedness (Ryan & Deci, 2024). Social Capital Theory (Lin, 1999) also highlights the importance of access to resources and opportunities through social networks and relationships, while leveraging more skill based-hiring supports evaluating individuals based on demonstrated capabilities rather than traditional credentials (Uhlmann & Cohen, 2005). This ultimately highlights a more humanistic and equitable move from a traditional emphasis on selection to a more individualistic and independent focus on employee training and development. These opportunities are accessible to employees by leveraging available AI technology for upskilling and career progression to facilitate this shift (Dachner et al., 2021; Jaiswal et al., 2021).

### Artificial Intelligence-Guided Learning and Career Progression

User-friendly and widely accessible AI applications (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) can be an effective means to employee-driven individualized career pathing, upskilling, and learning opportunities that are both aligned with individual goals and organizational needs. These easily accessible and usable AI chat tools can be implemented or developed by organizations to facilitate this individualized learning. Specifically, organizations can train employees on how to use these applications to initiate self-driven learning and development opportunities. For example, employees wanting to develop data analytic skills could be taught to prompt AI tools to develop a learning plan from their current understanding of data analytics to meet their end learning goal. This applicant emphasizes growth over set selection criteria, supporting equitable access to skill development and professional development. Creating a shift in organizational thinking from finding the "best employee" through selection to focusing on minimally acceptable qualifications for the job to allow more opportunities for growth and learning within a role (Bucher et al., 2024; Mao, 2025). Equitable access to learning promotes inclusion and helps reduce bias inherent in selection by ensuring that all employees, regardless of background, can have access to high-quality job opportunities.

Creating adaptive growth paths that implement AI to match employees' desired career trajectories based on industry trends and evolving employee skills could provide individualized suggestions for employee development opportunities to grow into leadership or specialized roles and increase their earning potential. Personalized learning management systems can further be leveraged to customize content, pace, and delivery method to meet diverse learning needs, incorporating Universal Design for Learning principles to support employees with disabilities or those balancing caregiving or other external responsibilities (Rose & Meyer, 2002).

Additionally, AI chatbots and information-seeking tools could be implemented to enable employees to access policies, procedures, and training opportunities on their schedules, particularly benefiting individuals with caregiving responsibilities or secondary jobs. Fortunately, many of these internal tools exist in larger organizations and could be easily used for individualized knowledge acquisition and learning to facilitate employee onboarding, training, and continued information search autonomy. This allows employees to access these tools on their schedule and discretion as opposed to a traditional reliance on human sources of information or a lag in email communication.

Current similar applications include platforms like Gloat (2015), which recommends tailored learning and growth paths using employee skill profiles and goals, and Degreed (2012), which curates learning journeys tied to career aspirations and organizational priorities. These systems and further proposed AI-based applications facilitate employee autonomy, addressing superficial DEI practices by embedding DEI principles into individualized development and supporting employee development and growth rather than short-term compliance. For individuals, these systems create equitable access to growth opportunities aligned with their strengths and interests. Individuals from underrepresented backgrounds can particularly benefit from increased access to work through development and career progression opportunities (Nixon et al., 2024; Oladele & Anector, 2025). For organizations, these approaches contribute to higher retention, engagement, and a more skill-diverse workforce (Carroll, 1991).

### Automated Task Matching for On-the-Job Training

AI systems can be effectively prompted and designed to assign meaningful tasks based on employee skills and growth potential. This can replace a more traditional perspective of an employee applying for a job and assuming tasks based on perceived skill match. This approach can often be misled by the job posting or leave the employee with a mismatch between skills and job duties, leading to potentially lower job performance, not reflecting an employee's true capabilities (Phillips, 1998; Shibly, 2019). The proposed AI system could reduce this barrier and subsequent issue by taking a more objective approach to assigning tasks and thus promoting equitable and fair skill development and on-the-job learning. This approach reduces bias in task distribution and ensures fair distribution of high-value or important assignments, addressing systemic inequities that have historically favored certain groups or those with privileged network access.

Also, growth-first matching could result from implementing AI technology to reassign tasks dynamically and in real time as employees gain new knowledge, skills, and abilities, to help employees develop capabilities and prepare them for future roles or upskilling opportunities (Jaiswal et al., 2021). Such proposed tools could use current predictive analytic methods to proactively identify when employees are ready for more challenging or advanced assignments aligned with both individual goals and organizational goals, creating a more strategic approach to employee development.

Modern related applications include platforms like Monday.com Ltd. (2012), which assigns tasks aligned with employee skills and growth objectives, and Asana Inc. (2008), which tracks completion and suggests future opportunities based on task performance. These systems address bias by providing equitable access to developmental tasks and include growth into everyday operations for widespread inclusion and equity. For individuals, this promotes confidence and skill-building through fair opportunities. For organizations, it results in better employee talent usage, stronger team performance, and a more effective merit-based culture in which advancement is tied to demonstrated development rather than organizational relationships or interpersonal preferences.

### Artificial Intelligence-Powered Hiring Insights Looking at Transferable Skills and Interests

Implementing AI to create systems that evaluate transferable skills could help shift from a selection focus on rigid qualifications to demonstrated potential for training to learn those skills, expanding access to employment for more diverse candidates. By emphasizing capabilities gained through non-traditional paths, organizations support greater equity and inclusion.

Additionally, to reduce resume whitening practices and create a reduced opportunity for bias by creating an anonymous profile from a job applicant's resume. This can be done more consistently across candidates with AI tool prompting and intentional human oversight. Thus, emphasizing actual abilities over traditional resume credentials and reducing unconscious bias toward names, certain educational institutions, or companies, and gaps in employment. Such tools could then further match applicants internally to opportunities based on skills, and potential, reducing reliance on informal networks and promoting equitable access to work (Jooss et al., 2024; Lin, 1999).

Several current applications that apply adjacent concepts include Pymetrics (2013), which uses neuroscience-based assessments to evaluate soft skills and potential while minimizing bias, and Eightfold AI (2016), which analyzes transferable skills and then matches candidates with roles. These AI tools address potentially unintended organizational issues caused by an overemphasis on selection focused on potential and reduce reliance on traditional criteria that often disadvantage marginalized groups (Bertrand & Mullainathan, 2004; Fabris et al., 2025). These applicants can also support underrepresented groups navigating post-COVID-19 workplace transitions and ever-changing labor markets and demands. For individuals, this approach increases access to opportunities based on demonstrated skills rather than specific criteria. For organizations, it expands applicant pools, reduces bias, and supports a more inclusive workforce able to meet the changing nature of work.

## Individualization

Individualization deals with fostering inclusivity through individualized work structures and equitable accommodations that adapt to individual needs and preferences. This pillar includes flexible and remote work structures that provide employees with agency over their workspace, work environment, and workflow. Applying AI technology to create adaptive systems within work, organizations can move past more standardization in work and create environments where all employees can fully participate and feel comfortable.

### Adaptive Work Environments

AI can be implemented to optimize both physical and virtual work environments to meet individual sensory, cognitive, and physical needs, ensuring accessibility and comfort. This application of AI can support employees in having equal access and ability to work without having to repeatedly advocate for accommodations, thus addressing structural barriers that often require individuals to self-advocate and disclose sensitive information, additionally benefiting all employees and their preferences in doing so.

Creating AI-enhanced biometric spaces represents an innovative solution to leveraging AI for greater access to work, where biometric feedback (e.g., heart rate, focus levels, body temperature, body position, movement) is used to adjust lighting, temperature, noise levels, or simply offer stretching or posture suggestions to optimize comfort and employee well-being. This means of predictive ergonomic optimization can use AI to analyze biometric data and workspace usage patterns to proactively recommend ergonomic adjustments and prevent discomfort or injury, drawing on Person-Environment Fit Theory (Edwards et al., 1998).

Another application of AI can be to create organization-wide inclusive design guidelines by assessing compliance with best practices and accessibility standards by utilizing summarization capabilities to provide a universally understandable and accessible set of guidelines. This can ensure consistent and inclusive workspace design throughout the organization, even for organizations across locations. These AI-generated guidelines make accessibility a core organizational practice rather than an afterthought once an employee who needs such accommodations is hired, thus aligning with Disability-Inclusive Workplace Practices (Stone & Colella, 1996).

Some modern and related applications include Humanscale (n.d.), which now uses predictive AI to recommend personalized ergonomic setups, and Microsoft Viva from Microsoft Corp. (2021), which integrates AI to optimize digital workspace setups and support diverse user needs. These systems address post-COVID-19 challenges by reducing the need for masking, code-switching, and emotional labor. They also counter bias in workplace design by ensuring accessibility is part of the organization, and employees do not need to advocate or disclose a disability status. For individuals, this results in enhanced comfort and reduced stress; for organizations, it can lead to engagement, inclusivity, and reduced absenteeism.

### Artificial Intelligence-Assisted Workflow Personalization

Existing AI and machine learning algorithms can be used to predict and identify individual employees' work habits and styles to personalize workflows, balance workloads, and optimize productivity. This approach can use employee data probably already collected to recognize patterns in timing, organization, communication style preferences, or active hours to predict when an employee is most productive. This application is particularly beneficial for neurodivergent individuals or those who process and understand information and organize work differently than the standard workflows typically allowed or defaulted to (Branicki et al., 2024; Rollnik-Sadowska & Grabinska, 2024). Individualizing work allows organizations to create equal access to work and opportunities for all employees to succeed.

For example, an AI-based work planning application could dynamically restructure work assignments to allow for differential information processing and workflow preferences, benefiting all employees. This application particularly makes sense because there is typically not always one correct way to complete many work assignments and tasks. This allows AI assistants, which are more easily accessible for workplace use, to assist with task allocation and scheduling.

Some similar current applications include Clockwise Inc. (2016), which optimizes schedules to create focused time blocks for improved workflow, and Otter.ai Inc. (2016), which automates meeting notes and task management, helping employees streamline workflows while reducing cognitive effort. These applications could help address post-COVID-19 challenges by supporting all types of employees who may struggle in that transition and the ever-changing nature of work. For individuals, this results in reduced cognitive load and stress, and for those with disabilities, chronic illnesses, neurodivergence, or mental health conditions, it offers personalized support to increase employee retention (Cleveland et al., 1997). For organizations, higher productivity, improved employee satisfaction, and reduced turnover and absenteeism can result from a more inclusive culture.

### Artificial Intelligence-Driven Flexible Work Arrangements

Another innovative solution is using AI applications to enable hybrid and remote work that is more tailored to individual schedules and team dynamics, ensuring equal participation and collaboration regardless of pre-existing conditions, health situations, or other circumstances. This flexibility helps employees stay effective in their work even when unexpected personal needs arise and makes it easier for team members to adjust and support one another. Rooted in Telework Equity Theory (Golden & Eddleston, 2020), this approach also addresses how traditional return-to-office policies can disproportionately disadvantage underrepresented groups.

An effective application of AI to leverage these advantages is through a hybrid collaboration optimization system to facilitate team and co-working productivity across different work structures (in-person, remote, or hybrid) and locations. AI can analyze data such as team schedules, individual work patterns, communication preferences, and project timelines and deadlines to recommend the most inclusive and efficient ways for teams to collaborate. By continuously processing and updating these data, AI can also automatically coordinate meetings, adjust workflows, and suggest optimal times and formats (avatar-based chat, email, video, in-person) for collaboration that support both individual needs and overall team goals. This flexibility allows for coordination and inclusion in a consistent manner for both team and collaborative work, even in changing work environments and individual situations.

Other innovative ways to promote access to work include AI-driven platforms that make remote collaboration seamless and virtual reality workspaces that can adjust to each employee's preferred layout, accessibility settings, and communication style. Sensory-adaptive workspaces can use AI to accommodate sensory sensitivities, different communication preferences, and diverse information processing, helping to reduce the need for masking (Botha et al., 2024; Singer, 1999).

Some applications that maintain similar goals for collaborative work include VirBELA (2012), which offers customizable VR workspaces for immersive collaboration, and Slack (2009), which facilitates asynchronous communication that can be tailored to specified to individual and team preferences. These systems address post-COVID-19 challenges by supporting employees managing caregiving responsibilities, chronic health conditions, or other personal constraints. This more dynamic form of collaboration can also help to counter rigid return-to-office policies that can differentially impact certain groups. For individuals, these options provide flexibility to balance personal and professional priorities without fear of repercussions. For organizations, flexible models promote enhanced retention and inclusive collaboration.

## Representation

Ensuring equitable visibility and inclusion in leadership, opportunities, and decision-making is crucial for creating truly diverse organizations. Traditional approaches to decision-making based on subjective notions of fit can perpetuate bias and maintain existing power structures. Modern AI technologies and advanced interfaces might offer potential solutions for unifying immersive workspaces that cater to a broader set of employee needs, while reducing some of the emotional labor and isolation frequently experienced by underrepresented groups in traditional work settings.

### Artificial Intelligence-Facilitated Mentorship Matching

Mentorship matching supported by AI technology has the potential to revolutionize career development opportunities, particularly for underrepresented professionals who have historically faced barriers in accessing meaningful mentorship opportunities. AI can potentially assess employees' skills, interests, and career aspirations to create highly personalized mentorship matches that evolve over time. By thoughtfully connecting employees with mentors who understand both their career aspirations and lived experiences, AI-powered matching systems create pathways for authentic professional relationships (Luo et al., 2025). This approach builds on Mentoring Theory (Kram, 1988), which emphasizes how meaningful mentor-mentee relationships foster professional growth and development.

Future AI systems could be designed to evaluate the intersection of employees' identities, experiences, and professional goals to facilitate meaningful mentor pairings, leveraging deep learning models to identify alignment in professional trajectories and leadership development needs (Davis et al., 2022). This nuanced approach acknowledges the complexity of individual journeys while providing targeted guidance and support that helps break down barriers to social capital for underrepresented groups. Current platforms like Together Mentoring (2018), which uses AI-driven algorithms to match employees based on career goals and expertise, and Ten Thousand Coffees (2014), which facilitates structured networking for career growth, demonstrate how AI-driven matching can create impactful mentorship connections.

As these technologies evolve, mentorship ecosystems will become more active, enabling professionals to connect with different mentors at various career stages. Future AI systems could incorporate career trajectory analysis to recommend mentorship adjustments over time, ensuring that employees receive guidance tailored to their evolving expertise and leadership potential. AI could also enhance the tracking of mentorship progress, ensuring that relationships remain beneficial and adaptable (Choudhary et al., 2024). This flexibility allows employees to refine their career trajectories with guidance that aligns with their evolving professional needs.

### Artificial Intelligence Translation and Captioning

Language accessibility represents a critical component of workplace inclusion, particularly in global and multilingual teams. AI-powered translation and captioning tools ensure full participation by providing real-time language support, including sign language interpretation. This technology aligns with Accessible Technology Theory (Emiliani, 2006), which emphasizes the importance of removing communication barriers in professional settings.

Future AI translation systems could be developed to integrate cultural nuances, idioms, and context to improve the quality and inclusivity of translations (Gernsbacher, 2015). Additionally, AI-powered tools could allow for localized dialect adaptation, recognizing regional linguistic variations and workplace-specific terminology to enhance communication fluidity. This sophisticated approach helps reduce code-switching requirements and supports authentic expression across linguistic and cultural boundaries. AI-powered sign language interpretation enhances accessibility in virtual meetings and presentations, ensuring that deaf and hard-of-hearing employees can engage fully in professional discussions (Ranathunga et al., 2023).

Current platforms like Google Translate (2006), which provides text and voice translations in real time across multiple languages, and Otter.ai (2016), which offers AI-powered transcription and captioning services, demonstrate the potential for real-time language support in professional settings. Still, future AI-driven systems could further refine translations by incorporating domain-specific jargon, industry terminologies, and cultural context to ensure greater accuracy and natural communication. Future developments will likely introduce instant multilingual collaboration tools with advanced voice synthesis capabilities, enabling a seamless communication experience across cultures and abilities (Anik et al., 2025). These enhancements will foster greater inclusivity, ensuring that language barriers do not hinder professional advancement or workplace engagement.

### Transparent Artificial Intelligence-Dashboards for Diversity and Intersectionality Metrics

Transparent AI dashboards for diversity and intersectionality metrics play a crucial role in accountability efforts. AI-powered dashboards that monitor diversity across multiple dimensions -- including race, gender, disability, and their intersections -- provide organizations with actionable insights for improving representation. This approach aligns with Diversity Management Theory (Thomas & Ely, 1996), which emphasizes the importance of systematic approaches to fostering inclusive organizations.

Future AI systems could be designed to integrate diversity data with employee feedback and organizational metrics to provide detailed intersectional insights, using predictive analytics to model career progression disparities, representation gaps in leadership, and hidden biases in promotion trends. These tools could also generate tailored recommendations for equitable succession planning, ensuring that leadership pipelines reflect diverse representation and inclusive advancement opportunities. Platforms like Syndio (2016), which helps organizations analyze pay equity and representation across workforce demographics, and Tableau (2003), which enables customizable data visualization to track workforce diversity trends, currently demonstrate how organizations can visualize and track diversity metrics effectively. These tools enable real-time monitoring of representation trends, identification of systemic barriers to advancement, assessment of program effectiveness, and development of data-driven DEI strategies.

Expanding beyond simple demographic tracking, future AI dashboards could incorporate real-time sentiment analysis to assess employee perceptions of inclusion and belonging, enabling leadership to implement targeted interventions that address barriers to equity in hiring, team dynamics, and leadership representation. This evolution allows organizations to address representation challenges not only at a structural level but also from an experiential perspective, fostering a deeper understanding of workplace inclusion.

By using these AI-driven tools, companies can ensure that diversity initiatives are data-driven, accountable, and aligned with employee experiences. Integrating AI-driven DEI reporting into broader corporate strategy could enable organizations to track inclusion outcomes alongside financial and operational performance, ensuring that representation efforts remain a core business priority. As AI technology continues to refine these capabilities, organizations will have more precise methods for addressing representation challenges and fostering sustainable inclusion at every level.

## Anonymity

In the workplace, psychological safety is essential for fostering an inclusive environment in which all employees can contribute authentically without fear of bias or retaliation (Edmondson, 1999). Traditional workplace practices, such as mandatory camera-on policies in virtual meetings or blanket return-to-office mandates, can disproportionately impact certain groups, including transgender individuals, pregnant workers, and neurodivergent employees who often engage in masking or code-switching behaviors. This additional emotional labor creates inequitable burdens on underrepresented populations. Modern AI-driven solutions can leverage anonymization technologies and protected feedback channels to address these challenges and facilitate employees' right to anonymity, reducing forced disclosure of specific statuses.

### Avatar-Based Video Meetings and Interviews

The integration of AI-powered avatar systems in virtual interactions represents a significant advance in reducing appearance-based biases during meetings and hiring processes. These systems provide a more engaging alternative to cameras-off policies or voice-only interviews while maintaining anonymity. They help combat discrimination based on race, gender, age, or disability by ensuring evaluations focus on competency over visual identity (Trifilo & Blau, 2024). This approach aligns with Implicit Bias Theory, which highlights how unconscious biases shape workplace interactions and decisions (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995).

For transgender employees, pregnant workers, and neurodivergent individuals, AI-driven avatar-based interactions reduce pressure to conform to normative expectations. Transgender employees, for example, may feel scrutinized over their appearance or voice, while pregnant workers often face assumptions about commitment or productivity. Neurodivergent individuals who mask to meet neurotypical norms also experience heightened emotional labor. The ability to present oneself through a customizable digital avatar allows individuals to engage without these stressors, fostering psychological ease and enabling focus on performance.

This integration also addresses return-to-office policies that disproportionately affect those who have benefited from remote work flexibility. Caregivers, people with chronic illnesses, and neurodivergent employees often experience greater barriers in traditional office environments. AI-facilitated hybrid collaboration models help ensure remote workers can participate on equal footing with in-office colleagues.

One potential extension is identity-free hiring simulations, which remove all identifying characteristics and present only task performance data. This ensures hiring decisions are made purely on demonstrated capabilities rather than demographic factors that might activate bias. As technology evolves, fully anonymized recruitment pipelines will become more common, supporting merit-based evaluations. Current platforms like Gather Town (2020), and avatar technologies in Zoom (2022) and Microsoft Teams (Mesh avatars) (2022), illustrate the feasibility of these approaches (Zhao et al., 2024). However, future iterations will include even more sophisticated anonymization features.

Unlike traditional "cameras-off" interactions, which can feel impersonal, AI avatars incorporate facial expressions and gestures, maintaining nonverbal communication cues and creating more natural, engaging dynamics. This helps reduce social detachment and ensures biases tied to appearance do not influence evaluations (Salvetti & Bertagni, 2024).

### Artificial Intelligence-Feedback Channels and Employee Input Platforms

Anonymous feedback mechanisms powered by AI play a crucial role in identifying and addressing systemic workplace issues. These platforms protect psychological safety while generating actionable insights for organizational improvement. According to Psychological Safety Theory (Edmondson, 1999), employees must feel safe expressing concerns without fear of negative consequences. AI-powered feedback systems allow reporting of biases, microaggressions, and exclusionary practices while maintaining confidentiality.

The trend-based feedback analysis capabilities of these platforms help predict areas of exclusion or inequity by identifying patterns in employee input. This approach particularly benefits marginalized groups, aligning with Minority Stress Theory (Meyer, 2003), which recognizes the unique stressors underrepresented individuals face in the workplace. Current applications like AllVoices (2017), which enables anonymous reporting of harassment or discrimination, and CultureAmp (2009), which uses surveys to highlight inclusion barriers, demonstrate the effectiveness of these tools.

Beyond identifying recurring concerns, AI feedback systems can proactively suggest intervention strategies. For example, if frequent reports highlight gender-based microaggressions, the system could recommend targeted training or policy updates. By shifting DEI efforts from reactive to proactive, these platforms help address systemic inequities before they escalate.

Organizations using these systems can build inclusive cultures that attract diverse talent and foster innovation through cognitive diversity (Cox & Blake, 1991). Fully anonymous feedback channels create transparency and accountability. Employees are more likely to voice concerns when assured their identities are protected, increasing detection of issues before they escalate. Leadership that acts on these insights demonstrates a commitment to inclusion, reinforcing trust between employees and management.

### Artificial Intelligence-Moderated Communication for Bias

Real-time AI monitoring of workplace communications is a powerful tool for promoting inclusive interactions and reducing harmful behaviors. These systems analyze video calls or online chats for microaggressions and biased language, providing immediate feedback to promote respectful, inclusive dialogue. This aligns with Intergroup Contact Theory (Allport, 1954), emphasizing the value of positive interactions in reducing prejudice and fostering inclusion.

Modern AI systems can suggest real-time inclusivity prompts, providing language corrections or intervention strategies during meetings to guide participants toward more inclusive communication. This reduces the burden on marginalized individuals to self-advocate or address problematic behavior directly.

Additionally, intelligent meeting assistants can detect interruptions, ensure equal participation, and amplify underrepresented voices. By ensuring that all team members are heard, these systems foster inclusive collaboration and reduce emotional labor for those who often navigate challenging communication dynamics.

Current platforms like Textio (2014), which flags and corrects biased language in written communication, demonstrate the feasibility of AI-driven monitoring, although future developments will likely see integrated AI moderators capable of managing live spoken interactions in virtual environments. Organizations adopting these tools promote a culture of respect and inclusion, supporting psychological safety and reducing exposure to microaggressions. For individuals, this fosters confidence in authentic participation. For organizations, it strengthens team cohesion, enhances trust, and contributes to a more innovative and inclusive workplace.

## Belonging

### Discussion

Intentional consideration of the four pillars of the proposed framework creates an organizational foundation that enables and considers employee autonomy, individualization, representation, and the need for anonymity at times. This enables employees from both majority and underrepresented backgrounds to feel valued, seen, and empowered. By addressing the aforementioned structural barriers in addition to framework considerations, these efforts work in conjunction to foster authentic and equitable opportunities for access to work and employment experience. This foundation enables a broader organizational move toward ethically guided and intentionally implemented AI practices to remove structural barriers organizations may currently face, causing differential access to employment.

### Role of Industrial-Organizational Psychologists

Industrial-Organizational psychologists play a central role in translating this framework into practice, acting as scientist-practitioners who combine empirical research with practical application (Banks et al., 2016; Rynes et al., 2018). Their expertise allows organizations to diagnose these structural barriers, such as biased hiring practices, inequitable access to development, and exclusionary work environments, that may hinder full participation in work from underrepresented groups.

Beyond assessment, I-O psychologists are trained to design and implement data-driven interventions grounded in psychological theory and organizational science. Additionally, I-O psychologists are qualified to train leaders and employees to work effectively with AI tools, ensuring technology supplements human judgment rather than replacing it. This human-centered approach enables trust and supports long-term behavioral change across the organization.

### Ethical Considerations

Transparency and fairness are critical when implementing AI tools for more inclusive workplaces. AI must be designed to avoid reinforcing existing inequities that may be predisposed to have and should include clear guidelines for ethical use (Taddeo & Floridi, 2018). Establishing oversight systems allows organizations to continuously monitor AI decisions or outputs for potential biases and unintended consequences, ensuring alignment with organizational values of fairness and equity (West, 2018). Additionally, ethical use of AI tools implies that organizations are accountable for keeping systems adaptable and regularly updated to reflect evolving organizational contexts and AI standards and legislation.

Privacy and data protection are also important in the use of AI, especially for employees whose data may be particularly sensitive, such as those with disabilities or health conditions (Ienca & Andorno, 2017). Data governance policies and strong security protocols should be used to protect personal data and ensure employee trust in such tools. Also, providing employees with transparent, user-friendly privacy controls empowers them to decide how their data is used and shared, enhancing autonomy and psychological safety.

While AI offers significant potential to enhance inclusivity, it is vital to preserve human skills and critical judgment (Searle, 1980). Having AI supplement, rather than replace human judgment, ensures that human insight and decision-making is central to organizational processes. Training and resources that support effective human-AI collaboration can allow employees to develop confidence and skills in utilizing these tools.

To ensure AI tools are equitably used across employees, they must be developed and governed inclusively (Taddeo & Floridi, 2018). This requires involving diverse perspectives in the design and testing phases, rigorously evaluating for unintended consequences or outcomes, and implementing continuous feedback and improvement processes. Including marginalized voices in creating AI tools helps prevent perpetuating biases and supports the development of accessible and equitable tools. In the future, global coalitions may also create more universal ethical AI guidelines to promote fairness and equity across different cultures and organizations.

## Future Directions

## Conclusion

AI can be leveraged to remove organizational structural barriers and allow employees to have access to meaningful employment without being limited by outdated systems that are susceptible to bias. By rethinking processes from selection to training, AI can create new pathways to equitable, meaningful work. Also, tools like adaptive work environments, AI-driven mentorship, and anonymized feedback help create an inclusive organization, removing the burden on individuals to advocate for themselves. This allows organizations to implement AI tools in a more proactive and sustainable means as opposed to a traditional reactive manner.

Creating workplaces with built-in access to work requires an approach that integrates the four pillars of the proposed framework: Autonomy, Individualization, Representation, and Anonymity by leveraging modern AI technology. This enables organizations to move beyond surface-level diversity and make structural, lasting changes to attract and retain diverse talent and promote real inclusion.

AI-guided learning and task-matching systems support Autonomy, empowering employees to drive their own growth and access opportunities traditionally only available to select groups. Individualization strategies, such as adaptive work environments and personalized workflows, create flexible, accessible spaces where all employees can perform at their best without needing to self-advocate constantly.

Representation-focused tools, including AI-driven mentorship matching and transparent diversity dashboards, help ensure equal access to leadership pathways and decision-making, breaking down barriers to advancement. Meanwhile, anonymity-focused solutions like avatar-based interactions, identity-free hiring, and confidential feedback channels protect psychological safety and reduce the emotional labor required from underrepresented employees.

Rooted in theory and ethical principles, this framework emphasizes designing AI systems and tools that focus on human needs rather than forcing individuals to adapt to the existing system. It also highlights the important role of I-O psychologists in diagnosing barriers, creating evidence-based interventions, and guiding intentional AI integration.

By adopting this holistic, ethically guided approach, organizations can act as allies by moving past expecting marginalized groups to adapt and instead build systems that proactively support all employees. This shift requires organizational leadership commitment, continuous evaluation, and investment in training and infrastructure. When fully utilized, AI-based solutions to reducing barriers to work can empower individuals, foster inclusion, and position organizations for greater innovation, resilience, and provide equal access to work for collective goal achievement.
