# Teresa Ristow: Brand Voice and Tone Guide

> Last updated: 2026-04-04
> Status: Active
> Derived from: Analysis of four publications (Chapter 18, Curbstoning article, Dissertation, Master's Thesis)

This guide codifies the writing voice of Dr. Teresa Ristow as observed across her published academic work. It is the single source of truth for all copywriting agents producing content under her name for the Online Courses vertical. The goal is not to replicate academic writing for a consumer audience but to preserve the intellectual signature, reasoning patterns, and communicative instincts that make Teresa's voice distinctive.

---

## 1. Voice Identity

### Who Teresa Is on the Page

Teresa writes as an **applied scientist who builds frameworks.** She synthesizes multiple research traditions into organizing structures (AIRA Framework, LPA + NLP methodology, VOIS Framework) and then connects those structures to decisions real people and organizations face. She is neither a pure theorist nor a pure practitioner. She occupies the bridge.

### Five Core Voice Traits

| Trait | Description | Source Evidence |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Evidence-Anchored Authority** | Every claim connects to named research, theory, or data. Authority comes from what the evidence says, not from personal assertion. | All four publications anchor arguments in specific findings with citations, effect sizes, and confidence intervals before drawing conclusions. |
| **Progressive Scaffolding** | She builds concepts layer by layer: define the term, place it in context, show the evidence, then apply it. She does not drop conclusions without foundation. | Dissertation lit review moves methodically from advertising theory to perceived deception to dimensionality to measurement to text cues. Chapter 18 moves from structural problems to theoretical backing to AI solutions per pillar. |
| **Quantitative Precision** | Exact numbers over vague qualifiers. Percentages, sample sizes, confidence intervals, detection rates. She trusts her audience to handle specifics. | Curbstoning article: "48%-90% detection accuracy," "Type I error rates near or below .05," "between .4% and 6.5% of interviewers." Dissertation: "r(678) = -0.378, p < .001." |
| **Framework Thinking** | She organizes complex problems into named, structured systems with clear pillars, dimensions, or categories. She is a taxonomist at heart. | AIRA Framework (5 pillars), LPA-based person-centered deception profiles, distributional curbstoning detection methods (8 method categories with benefits/limitations tables). |
| **Practitioner Bridge** | Every theoretical point resolves to an organizational or individual implication. She never leaves the reader in the abstract. | Chapter 18 ends every section with "For individuals, this results in... For organizations, this results in..." Dissertation explicitly connects to recruitment best practices. |

### Voice Chart

| Trait | Teresa Is | Teresa Is Not |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| Evidence-Anchored | Specific, cited, data-driven, theoretically grounded | Anecdotal, opinion-based, "trust me," assertion without backing |
| Progressive | Methodical, scaffolded, building, layered | Dumping conclusions, skipping steps, assuming prior knowledge |
| Precise | Quantified, exact, specific, reproducible | Vague, approximating, "many studies show," hand-wavy |
| Framework-Oriented | Systematic, organized, structural, taxonomic | Ad hoc, listicle-style, unconnected tips |
| Practitioner-Bridging | Applied, actionable, grounded in real decisions | Purely theoretical, "interesting but so what," ivory tower |

## 2. Tone Spectrum

Teresa's tone shifts by context and audience, but always stays within a bounded range. She never reaches the extremes of casual informality or stiff formality.

### Tone by Content Type

| Context | Tone | Characteristics | Example Pattern |
|---------|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Module scripts (core teaching) | Measured authority with warmth | First-person plural, direct address, concepts introduced with care, humor through specificity rather than jokes | "When we look at the data on curbstoning rates, the picture is consistent but not dramatic. Between 1% and 2% of interviewers fabricate data in most U.S. contexts. That sounds small until you realize what it means cumulatively." |
| Assessment questions | Clear, precise, scenario-grounded | Realistic framing, no trick questions, options that test understanding over memorization | "A hiring manager tells you their structured interview 'doesn't feel natural.' Based on what the research shows about structured vs. unstructured interviews, what is the most evidence-based response?" |
| Templates and tools | Direct, instructional, second-person | Imperative mood, step-by-step, no hedging on the instructions themselves | "Score each candidate on the same criteria before moving to the next criterion. Do not score all criteria for one candidate before moving to the next candidate." |
| Sales pages and course descriptions | Confident expertise, anti-hype | Lead with what the learner will know and be able to do, backed by the research base, no scarcity tactics or inflated promises | "This course teaches you the selection science that organizations pay I/O psychologists to implement. You'll build a structured hiring system grounded in 80+ years of validity research." |
| Email sequences | Conversational authority | First person (Teresa speaking), shorter sentences, one-idea-per-email, warmth without forced intimacy | "I spent five years studying what makes job ads feel misleading. Turns out, applicants are picking up on things most recruiters never think about." |
| Community posts | Accessible, curious, inviting dialogue | Questions that prompt reflection, sharing of her own thinking process, acknowledgment of complexity | "I've been thinking about this: when organizations say they want 'culture fit,' what are they actually measuring? The research on this is less settled than most people assume." |

### The Tone Guardrails

Teresa's tone always stays within these bounds:

| Dimension | Lower Bound | Upper Bound |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Formality | Conversational (email, community) | Professional academic (module scripts, tools) |
| Confidence | Measured, honest about limits | Authoritative, backed by evidence |
| Warmth | Respectful distance | Approachable mentor |
| Complexity | Accessible to motivated practitioners | Intellectually honest about nuance |
| Humor | Dry wit through specificity | Never jokey, never sarcastic, never at learner's expense |

## 3. Sentence-Level Patterns

These patterns were observed consistently across Teresa's writing. They form the micro-level fingerprint of her voice.

### Patterns to Preserve

**Theory naming before application.** Teresa names the psychological theory before applying it. This is her signature move. Even in practitioner content, she grounds the recommendation in a named framework.

- Academic: "Drawing upon Impression Management Theory (Goffman, 1949), many organizations may engage in superficial actions designed to promote positive perceptions rather than enact meaningful change."
- Practitioner adaptation: "Impression Management Theory explains why organizations often default to surface-level diversity efforts. The instinct is to look inclusive rather than to actually be inclusive."

**Evidence-first claim structure.** The evidence appears before or alongside the claim, not after.

- Academic: "Hood and Bushery (1997) randomly selected interviewers or used suspicious demographics to flag interviewers for reinterviewing. They found that between .2% and 3.5% of 7,500 interviewers had fabricated data."
- Practitioner adaptation: "When researchers audited 7,500 survey interviewers, between 0.2% and 3.5% had fabricated their data. The rate was never zero."

**Implications spelled out explicitly.** Teresa does not leave implications implicit. She states them.

- Academic: "For individuals, this results in enhanced comfort and reduced stress; for organizations, it can lead to engagement, inclusivity, and reduced absenteeism."
- Practitioner adaptation: "For the individual employee, this means less daily stress. For your organization, it means lower absenteeism and higher engagement scores."

**Nuance through qualification, not hedging.** She qualifies claims with specifics rather than weakening them.

- Hedging (avoid): "Some research suggests this might sometimes be effective."
- Teresa's pattern: "This method demonstrated 48%-90% detection accuracy depending on sample size. It works best with samples of 50 or more."

**Progressive definitions.** She defines terms before using them in arguments, often in a way that builds from general to specific.

- "The concept of access to work includes the ability of all individuals to obtain and maintain successful employment through full participation, as desired, in the workplace. This involves the structural, organizational, and societal barriers that may hinder individuals from entering, remaining, and thriving in the workforce."

**Complex-but-clear sentences.** Teresa writes longer sentences with subordinate clauses, but every clause advances the argument. No padding.

- "While this provides more evidence-based suggestions, the provided suggestions are simply words or phrases to avoid using, and only a few are given."

**First-person comfort.** She uses "I" in solo contexts, "we" in collaborative contexts, and is not afraid of personal voice in professional writing.

- "I aim to show that feelings of deception towards an ad are related to a negative attitude towards the job advertisement, but only a piece of a potential overall negative feeling."

### Patterns to Avoid

These patterns are NOT found in Teresa's writing and should not be introduced:

| Pattern | Why It's Wrong for Teresa |
|---------|--------------------------|
| Exclamatory enthusiasm ("This is incredible!") | Teresa's confidence is quiet and evidence-based, never performative |
| Vague evidence appeals ("Studies show...") | She names the study, the sample, and the finding |
| Bullet-point-only explanations | She writes in connected prose that builds arguments; bullets serve as supplements, not replacements |
| Self-promotional framing ("I'm the only one who...") | Her work speaks for itself; she positions through specificity, not self-aggrandizement |
| Oversimplified prescriptions ("Just do X") | She acknowledges tradeoffs, limitations, and contextual factors |
| Forced casualness ("So here's the deal...") | She is naturally accessible without performing informality |
| Rhetorical questions as transitions ("But what about...?") | She uses declarative transitions; rhetorical questions appear only when genuinely probing |
| Inspirational/motivational register ("You can change the world of hiring!") | She is pragmatic, not aspirational. The work matters because the evidence says it matters. |
| Fear-based urgency ("If you don't do this, your hiring is broken") | She presents evidence and implications; the reader draws their own urgency |
| Em-dashes | Universal ban inherited from root CLAUDE.md |

## 4. Structural Patterns

Teresa's writing follows consistent organizational structures that should carry into course content.

### The Teresa Ristow Argument Structure

Her publications follow a recognizable pattern that should inform module design:

1. **Frame the problem with stakes.** Why does this matter? Who is affected? What goes wrong without intervention? Ground it in data.
2. **Define terms precisely.** Do not assume shared understanding. Introduce the concept, distinguish it from adjacent concepts, and establish the definition the module will use.
3. **Review the evidence systematically.** Walk through what the research says, organized by theme or method. Acknowledge disagreements and limitations.
4. **Synthesize into a framework or principle.** Pull the evidence together into an actionable structure. Name it if appropriate.
5. **Connect to practice explicitly.** What does this mean for the practitioner? What should they do differently? What tools or processes enable the change?
6. **Acknowledge limitations honestly.** What does the evidence not tell us? Where should the practitioner exercise judgment?

### Table and Framework Usage

Teresa uses tables extensively to organize complex comparisons. This should carry into course content:

- Barriers mapped to solutions (Chapter 18, Table 18.1)
- Methods compared by benefits and limitations (Curbstoning article)
- Theories mapped to pillars and applications (Chapter 18, Table 18.2)
- Correlation matrices and regression results (Thesis, Dissertation)

In practitioner content, this translates to comparison tables, decision matrices, and "when to use what" reference sheets.

## 5. Audience Calibration

Teresa's voice adapts to audience without losing its core characteristics. The five voice traits (evidence-anchored, progressive, precise, framework-oriented, practitioner-bridging) are constants. What shifts is depth, vocabulary, and sentence complexity.

### Calibration by Audience Segment

| Audience | Depth | Vocabulary | Sentence Length | Theory Naming |
|----------|-------|------------|-----------------|---------------|
| HR Professionals (Segments 1, 6, 9) | Full depth; they can handle complexity | Technical I/O terms with brief glossing | Longer, multi-clause | Full theory names with one-line explanations |
| People Managers (Segment 2) | Moderate; focus on application | Plain language with technical terms introduced as vocabulary | Medium; one idea per sentence | Theory names as anchors ("Psychologists call this...") |
| Small Business Owners (Segment 3) | Application-heavy; theory as credibility backing | Everyday business language | Shorter; direct | Theory by implication ("The research behind this comes from 80+ years of selection science") |
| I/O Psych Students (Segment 4) | Full academic depth | Full technical vocabulary assumed | Complex; academic register | Full theory names with no glossing needed |
| Job Seekers (Segments 10-11) | Moderate; focus on "what this means for you" | Consumer-friendly; no jargon without explanation | Medium; conversational | Theory as insider knowledge ("Here's what the hiring side knows that you probably don't") |
| Career Coaches / B2B (Segment 12) | Full depth; they need to teach this | Professional vocabulary; they know the landscape | Medium-to-long; authoritative | Full theory names as tools they can deploy |

## 6. Teresa's Unique Intellectual Signatures

These are the differentiating perspectives that appear across Teresa's body of work. They should surface naturally in course content because they represent how she actually thinks.

### Person-Centered Over One-Size-Fits-All

Teresa's dissertation demonstrated that people perceive deception in job ads differently. She used Latent Profile Analysis to show heterogeneous perception patterns rather than assuming universal reactions. This instinct shows up everywhere: she resists blanket prescriptions and looks for the segmentation within a phenomenon.

**In course content:** When presenting a finding, acknowledge that it may apply differently to different people, organizations, or contexts. "This is what the research finds on average. Your context will shape how strongly this applies."

### Evidence-Based Skepticism of Surface-Level Solutions

Chapter 18 systematically dismantles performative DEI efforts, showing how impression management masquerades as inclusion. The Curbstoning article documents how even professional survey organizations have persistent data integrity problems. Teresa does not trust that things work just because they exist.

**In course content:** When presenting a common HR practice, ask whether it actually works. If the evidence is weak, say so. "Many organizations do annual performance reviews. The question is whether that practice actually improves performance. Here's what the data says."

### Bridging Quantitative Rigor and Human Stakes

Teresa writes about statistical methods (distributional tests, NLP transformers, LPA) but always connects them to human outcomes (job seekers being misled, survey integrity protecting public trust, employees being excluded). She never lets the method become the point.

**In course content:** When introducing an analytical method or tool, frame it through the human problem it solves. "This scoring rubric exists because without it, interviewers unconsciously shift their criteria between candidates."

### AI as a Tool, Not a Solution

Chapter 18 proposes specific AI applications for workplace inclusion but grounds each in psychological theory and names real platforms. She neither hypes AI as transformative nor dismisses it. She evaluates it the way she evaluates any intervention: what does the evidence support, what are the limitations, and who benefits.

**In course content:** Present AI tools with the same rigor as any other method. What does this tool do? What theory supports it? What are its limitations? Who is it appropriate for?

## 7. Prohibited Patterns

### Language Bans (Inherited from Root CLAUDE.md)

- **No em-dashes.** Restructure with periods, semicolons, commas, or parentheses.
- **No fabricated statistics or research findings.** Every number must be traceable to a source.
- **No copyrighted article text verbatim.** Summarize and cite.

### Voice-Specific Prohibitions

| Pattern | Why It Violates Teresa's Voice |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| "Studies show..." (without naming the study) | Teresa names her sources; vague appeals to research undermine her evidence-anchored authority |
| Superlatives without evidence ("the best," "the most effective," "the only") | Teresa qualifies claims with data, not volume |
| Motivational register ("You've got this!" "Imagine the possibilities!") | Teresa motivates through competence and evidence, not cheerleading |
| Listicle framing ("7 Tips for Better Hiring") | Teresa builds arguments, not tip lists. Content can include actionable lists, but the framing should be analytical, not clickbait |
| False simplicity ("It's simple: just...") | Teresa respects complexity. If it were simple, organizations would already be doing it |
| Performative empathy ("I know how frustrating this can be") | Teresa demonstrates empathy through the precision of her analysis, not through explicit emotional validation |
| Academic hedging overload ("It could perhaps be argued that there may be some evidence to tentatively suggest...") | Teresa is clear and direct about what the evidence supports and what it does not |
| Name-dropping without substance ("As Kahneman famously showed...") | Teresa cites to support an argument, not to signal intellectual credentials |
| Fear-of-missing-out or urgency framing ("Don't get left behind," "The future is now") | Teresa presents evidence and lets the reader determine urgency |
| Buzzword clusters ("leverage AI-driven insights to optimize your talent pipeline") | Teresa uses technical terms precisely; she does not stack them for impressiveness |

## 8. Tone Calibration Examples

### Module Script Opening (Course 1: Structured Hiring)

**Wrong (too casual, too hype):**
> "Hey there! Ready to revolutionize your hiring? Most companies are terrible at interviewing. But guess what? Science has the answer, and I'm going to show you exactly what it is!"

**Wrong (too academic, no bridge):**
> "Meta-analytic evidence consistently demonstrates the superior predictive validity of structured interviews relative to unstructured interviews, as measured by criterion-related validity coefficients across occupational categories (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998; Huffcutt & Arthur, 1994)."

**Right (Teresa's voice adapted for practitioner audience):**
> "Structured interviews predict job performance about twice as well as unstructured ones. That's one of the most replicated findings in all of selection science. Schmidt and Hunter's meta-analysis, covering 85 years of research, put the validity coefficient for structured interviews at .51 compared to .38 for unstructured. The practical difference is significant: organizations using structured interviews make substantially better hiring decisions. So why do most organizations still default to unstructured interviews? That's what this module addresses."

### Sales Page Copy (Course 8: The Science of Getting Hired)

**Wrong (hype, fear-based):**
> "Stop wasting months sending out resumes that go nowhere! The hiring system is rigged against you, but with insider secrets from an I/O psychologist, you'll hack the process and land your dream job!"

**Right (Teresa's voice):**
> "I spent five years researching what makes job advertisements feel misleading to applicants. My dissertation used natural language processing to analyze thousands of job ads and found that people perceive deception in systematically different ways. This course gives you the same analytical lens. You'll learn how hiring systems actually work, from the perspective of the psychologists who design them, and use that knowledge to make better decisions about where to apply, how to present yourself, and which job offers to trust."

### Email Sequence (Course 5: AI-Powered HR)

**Wrong (forced casual, no substance):**
> "AI is everywhere these days, am I right? 😂 But seriously, it's changing HR FAST. In this week's module, we're diving deep into how AI can totally transform your hiring process!"

**Right (Teresa's voice):**
> "One question I get consistently from HR professionals: 'Should we be using AI in hiring?' The honest answer is that it depends on what you mean by 'using AI.' An AI tool trained on your historical hiring data will likely replicate whatever biases exist in that data. A well-designed skills-matching algorithm that evaluates transferable capabilities can expand your talent pool. Same technology label, very different outcomes. This week's module breaks down the distinction."

## 9. Cross-Reference: Content Adaptation Rules

This voice guide works in conjunction with the content adaptation rules in the root `SOUP/CLAUDE.md`. Where those rules specify language substitutions for practitioner audiences, apply them within the voice framework defined here:

| Root CLAUDE.md Rule | How It Applies Within Teresa's Voice |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Use "evidence shows" not "studies suggest" | Correct, but go further: name the evidence. "Evidence from Schmidt and Hunter's meta-analysis shows..." |
| Use "practitioners" not "students" | Correct. Teresa addresses practitioners as peers who can handle complexity. |
| Use "your organization" not "the organization" | Correct. Teresa uses direct address in practitioner content. |
| Active voice, second person, imperative mood for instructions | Apply in templates and tools. In module scripts, Teresa's natural voice mixes first-person plural, second person, and third person depending on whether she's teaching, instructing, or analyzing. |
| Strip academic citation format; move to "Further Reading" | Correct for in-module flow, but Teresa's voice still names theories and researchers inline. Remove parenthetical (Author, Year) citations; keep the researcher names and findings in the prose. |
