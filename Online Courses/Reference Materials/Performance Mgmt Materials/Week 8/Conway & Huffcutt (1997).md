The purpose of this investigation was to examine the psychometric properties
(interrater reliabilities within source and correlations between sources) of subordinate, supervisor, peer, and self-ratings of job performance. Different job types and
dimension types were compared. Using meta-analytic methodology, we found that
subordinates showed the lowest mean reliability (.30) and supervisors showed the
highest (.50), with peers in between (.37). Mean correlations between sources were
low for subordinateratings (.22 with supervisor, .22 with peer, and .14 with self-ratings) and for self-ratings (.22 with supervisor and .19 with peer ratings). The mean
supervisor-peer correlation was higher at .34. Both reliabilities and correlations
between sources tended to be higher for nonmanagerial and lower complexity jobs.
Comparisons of between-source correlations with within-source reliabilities indicated that, with some qualifications, the different sources had somewhat different
perspectiveson performance. Dimension reliabilitiesdiffered somewhat for interpersonal and cognitive dimensions.
Multisource performance rating systems have become increasingly popular in
recent years. Common sources include supervisors, peers, and even the workers

themselves (self-ratings).In the case of management personnel, ratings are increasingly obtained from subordinates (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). Ratings from
multiple sources including subordinates are typically made on dimensions such as
interpersonal skills, problem solving, effort, and overall performance. The ratings
are then used for purposes such as validation research (e.g., Atwater & Yammarino,
1992; Fleenor, 1996), personal development (e.g., 360" feedback; London &
Smither, 1995; Tornow, 1993), and basic research (e.g., Hater & Bass, 1988).
Increased use of multiple sources (especially nontraditional sources such as
subordinates) makes it importantto understand the psychometricproperties of these
sources' ratings. In particular, it is important to examine the reliability of ratings
within each source (i.e., interrater reliability). Such information informsresearchers
as to the level of confidence that they can have in the ratings. More important, it
can help to identify the appropriate number of raters needed to reach a desired level
of reliability (e.g., .70; Nunnally, 1978). For example, a survey by London and
Smither (1995) found that four to six subordinates and about the same number of
peers are typically employed in multisource feedback systems. It would be useful
to know whether these numbers provide adequate reliability.
In addition to improving the quality of rating systems, information on
interrater reliability can also be useful for research. For example, this information can be used to correct validity coefficients for criterion unreliability
(Viswesvaran, Ones, & Schmidt, 1996). And, basic research (e.g., leadership
research using subordinate ratings) can benefit from the correction of observed
relations for unreliability.
Moreover, it is importantto examine correlations between pairs of rating sources
in order to determine whether these sources conkibute unique perspectives on
performance. It is generally assumed that multiple sources should be included in a
rating system because they have relatively unique perspectives (i.e., that they cover
different parts of the criterion domain; Tornow, 1993). If this assumption is false,
then multiple sources merely represent an increase in the number of raters rather
than truly different perspectives.
The purpose of this study was to examine the psychometric properties of
multisource ratings. This was done by conducting a meta-analysis of subordinate,
supervisor, and peer interrater reliabilities and of correlations between all pairs of
sources (including self-ratings).Ultimately, the most important thing is that multisource ratings are valid measures of performance, and that they are useful for
predicting important criteria and stimulatingpersonal growth. Each source we study
has shown some evidence of validity, which we review in the Discussion. It is
therefore important to examine reliabilities (which are useful for maximizing the
existing validity) and between-source correlations (which are useful for evaluating
redundancy). Previous research and current hypotheses are discussed later.

INTERRATER RELIABILITIESOF SUBORDINATE,
SUPERVISOR, AND PEER RATINGS
Rating Source
First, we examined interrater reliabilities of the various sources, including subordinates, supervisors, and peers. Two previous large-scale studies have examined
the interrater reliability of job performance ratings. Viswesvaran et al. (1996)
meta-analytically summarized supervisor and peer reliabilities. For overall performance ratings (either a single rating or a composite)they found mean single rater
reliabilities of .52 for supervisors and .42 for peers. Rothstein (1990) studied a
sample of 9,975 ratees from a variety of organizations. All ratees were evaluated
by their supervisor and a second rater. The rating form included a "duty" criterion
and an "ability" criterion. Interrater reliabilities were .48 and .52 for the duty and
ability criteria, respectively.
However, there are several limitations in the current literature on interrater
reliability. The first and most important limitation is that there has been no
meta-analysis of subordinate ratings. Bernardin, Dahmus, and Redmon (1993)
stated that despite the increased popularity of using subordinatesas raters, empirical
research has lagged behind. As suggested by Viswesvaran et al. (1996), it is
desirable to know how subordinate reliabilities compare with those of supervisors
and peers. A second limitation is that the peer estimate from Viswesvaran et al.
(1996) was based on only nine coefficients, mainly because they included only
published studies. Including unpublished studies should provide a larger pool of
coefficients and a stronger comparison of supervisor and peer reliabilities. A third
limitation is that there has been no assessment of whether interrater reliability is
different for mechanical composite ratings of overall performance (i.e., summed or
averaged dimensional ratings) than it is for single overall ratings or for individual
dimensional ratings. Psychometric theory would predict that a composite measure
would have higher reliability, but this prediction has not been tested empirically.
This meta-analysis addressed these limitations. We first looked at subordinate
reliabilities, which were hypothesized to be lower than reliabilities for either
supervisors or peers. Managers may behave differently toward different subordinates (London & Smither, 1995;Van Velsor & Leslie, 1991a), leading to relatively
dissimilar evaluations from multiple subordinates. Peer reliabilities were examined
using a relatively large number of coefficients. We expected that the difference
between supervisor and peer reliabilities reported by Viswesvamn et al. (1996)
would be replicated. For all three sources we hypothesized the highest reliability
for mechanical composite ratings of overall performance as compared to a single
overall performance rating or to ratings on individual dimensions.

Job Type
In addition, we examined the moderating influence of job type on interrater
reliability. It is possible that reliability may be higher for some jobs than for others,
and these differences have not been investigated in the literature. For example,
managerial jobs may be inherently difficult for supervisors and peers to observe
and rate for several reasons. A relatively high proportion of the work is mental in
nature and not easily observed. The work also tends to be discontinuous in nature
(Borman & Brush, 1993), which may make it harder to observe coherent segments
of behavior. Finally, managers tend to spend considerably more time with subordinates than with supervisors or peers. We therefore hypothesized lower supervisor
and peer reliabilities for managerial than for nonmanagerial jobs. No hypothesis
was offered for subordinate reliabilities because nonmanagerial jobs do not typically have subordinates.
Relatedly, interrater reliability could vary according to the general complexity
of the job. Complexity refers to the information-processing demands of a job
(Hunter, Schmidt, & Judiesch, 1990). It is likely that jobs with greater informationprocessing demands (i.e., higher complexity) would be less observable and therefore harder to rate. Accordingly, we hypothesized that reliabilities would be higher
for less complex jobs. This comparison is not completely independent of the
manager versus nonmanager comparison because managerial jobs tend to be
relatively complex.
Dimension Type
Finally, we compared reliabilities for different types of performance dimensions.
Viswesvaran et al. (1996) analyzed the reliabilitiesof 10dimensionsfor supervisors
and peers, and found some evidence that interpersonal and communication competence were rated less reliably by supervisors than were other dimensions. No such
analysis has been done for subordinates. To maximize the number of coefficients
of each type we used two fairly general dimension categories. The first category
included interpersonal behavior dimensions such as relations with coworkers and
communication skill. The second category included cognitive dimensions (work
that is primarily mental) such as problem solving and judgment. (Some dimensions
did not fall in either category.) The two categories were compared with each other
and with the results for all dimensions. For supervisors we hypothesized lower
reliabilities for interpersonal dimensions (consistent with Viswesvaran et al., 1996).
Because Murphy and Cleveland (1995) conjectured that subordinates and peers
have more opportunity than supervisors to observe interpersonal behavior, we
hypothesized relatively high interpersonal reliabilities for these sources. Because
cognitive dimensions are necessarily difficult to observe, we hypothesized relatively low cognitive dimension reliabilities for all sources.

CORRELATIONS BETWEEN RATING SOURCES

Rating Sources
Along with interrater reliabilities within sources, we examined correlations among
the four main sources of ratings: subordinates, supervisors, peers, and the ratees
themselves. Harris and Schaubroeck (1988) conducted a meta-analysis of correlations between supervisor, peer, and self-ratings. They found moderately high
agreement between supervisors and peers, but relatively low agreement between
these two sources and self-ratings. Mean correlations (corrected for unreliability
using coefficient alphas for each source) were .62 for supervisor-peer, .35 for
supervisor-self, and .36 for peer-self correlations. The uncorrected values were
.48, .22, and .24, respectively.
The current literature on correlationsbetween sources has important limitations.
First, as was the case with reliabilities, subordinates have not been studied metaanalytically.It would be helpful to know how independentsubordinatesare of other
sources (i.e., the extent to which they tend to cover unique criterion variance). To
the extent that subordinates are redundant, it may be problematic to include them
because there is the potential for negative reactions from both the managers and the
subordinate raters (Carroll & Schneier, 1982).
A second limitation of the current literature is that no meta-analytic study has
compared between-source correlations directly to within-source reliabilities. Although existing between-source correlations suggest some distinctivenessbetween
sources,examiningthe distinctivenessof perspectives requires assessing agreement
between sources relative to agreement within the sources (Van Velsor & Leslie,
1991a). Sources can be said to have distinct perspectives on performance if
agreement within sources is higher than agreement between sources. For example,
two subordinate raters, or two peer raters, should show higher agreement than
should a subordinate and a peer rater.
These limitations were addressed by this study. Consistent with Harris and
Schaubroeck (1988), we hypothesized relatively high correlations between supervisors and peers, and low correlations between self-ratings and supervisors and
peers. We also hypothesized that subordinates would show low correlations with
all other sources. This is because subordinates are likely to observe a relatively
small (and different) portion of their managers' job performance, relative to the
amount of observation by supervisors or peers (Borman, 1991; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). We also expected that correlations between sources would be lower
than correlations within sources (i.e., interrater reliabilities). In other words, we
hypothesized that each source would have a relatively unique perspective on
performance.

Job Type
We additionally examined the moderating influence of job type on correlations
between sources. First, we compared managerial and nonmanagerial jobs. Harris
and Schaubroeck (1988) compared two job categories, one including managerial
and professional jobs and the other including blue collar and service jobs. The
general finding of lower correlationsfor managerial/professionaljobs suggests that
managerial jobs may tend to show lower relations between sources. It is desirable
to increase the precision of these findings by separating managerial jobs from
others. Second, we compared jobs of different complexity levels.
Hypotheses were similar to those for reliabilities. We hypothesized the lowest
correlations for managerial jobs due to the relatively unobservable nature of such
jobs, and to the fact that different sources are probably exposed to different aspects
of performance (relative to nonmanagerial jobs). For example, some of Borman
and Brush's (1993) managerial "mega-dimensions" such as planning and organizing may be more observable to superiors (who likely must approve managers'
plans), whereas others such as maintaining good working relationshipsmay be more
observable to peers and subordinates (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). We also
hypothesized lower correlations for higher complexity jobs because manageriaYprofessional and technical work is probably less observable (and harder to rate)
than lower complexity work.

Dimension Type
We compared between-source correlations for the categories of rating dimensions
described earlier. Specifically, for each pair of sources we computed the mean
between-source correlation for (a) interpersonal dimensions, (b) cognitive dimensions, and (c) all dimensions. Murphy and Cleveland (1995) argued that workers'
interpersonal interactions may differ across sources. We therefore expected relatively low correlations for interpersonal dimensions as compared to correlations
averaged across all dimensions. Similarly low correlations were expected for
cognitive dimensions because cognitive work is necessarily difficult to observe,
rendering it difficult to rate. Self-ratings were not included in dimension type
comparisons. This is because expected differences were due to differences in
observation, and self-raters are able to observe all of their own work, even on
cognitive dimensions.
In summary, the present meta-analysis included (a) interraterreliabilities (within
sources) for subordinate, peer, and supervisor ratings and (b) correlations between
sources, including self-ratings. Job type and dimension type were investigated as
potential moderators.

METHOD
Literature Search

We conducted an extensive literature search to identify studies with performance rating information. Bibliographies of appropriate reviews were inspected,
such as Viswesvaran (1996), Harris and Schaubroeck (1988), Kane and Lawler
(1978), Bass (1981), and Landy and Farr (1983). Computerized searches were
done using PSYCHLIT and Dissertation Abstracts International. Vendors
and/or authors of all multirater feedback instruments described in Van Velsor
and Leslie (1991b) were contacted in an attempt to identify unpublished studies.
Other researchers and consulting firms believed to be involved in interrater
reliability or multirater feedback research were also contacted. Finally, a manual
search was conducted of the following journals from January of 1950 until June
of 1996: Academy of Management Journal, Journal of Applied Psychology,
Journal of Occupational and Organizational Psychology, Journal of Vocational Behavior, Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, and
Personnel Psychology.

Decision Rules
The following rules were used to decide which studies and which study coefficients
to include in the meta-analysis.
1. Only studies providing interrater correlation coefficients (Pearson product-moment, Spearman rank-order, or intraclass correlations) for on-the-job performance ratings by supervisors,peers, subordinates,andlor the ratees (self-ratings)
were included. If F ratios, etas, or epsilons were presented, intraclass correlations
were computed from these indices.
2. Coefficients based on ratings from supervisors two or more levels above the
rake were excluded because they are likely to have less opportunity to observe
performance than immediate supervisors.
3. Coefficients based on ratings collected for administrative decision making
(e.g., salary or promotion decisions) were excluded. We originally intended to
examine purpose for rating as a potential moderator but found too few coefficients
based on administrative ratings.
4. If multiple coefficients for a sample were presented based on different rating
formats, the coefficients were averaged across formats.
5. Studies of college teaching performance were excluded because peers and
supervisors typically have little opportunity to observe other teachers first-hand.

6. If a coefficient was based on the aggregate of more than one rater's ratings,
it was converted to the reliability of a single rater as described later under Analysis
Strategy. Therefore if a coefficient was based on an aggregate but the number of
raters aggregated was not specified, the coefficient was excluded.
7. Within-source reliabilities were recorded separately for each source, and
between-source correlations were recorded separately for each pair of sources. A
sample could therefore conceivably have provided coefficients of each type.
8. Coefficients based on subordinate ratings of leader behavior were included,
because such ratings are often almost indistinguishablefrom explicit performance
ratings. Leader behavior measures typically include a series of behavioral statements evaluated on Likert-type scales (e.g., a 5-point scale ranging from always to
never, used with the Leader Behavior Description Questionnaire; Stogdill &
Shartle, 1955). Performance feedback instruments generally follow the same type
of format (Van Velsor & Leslie, 1991b). Only two types of coefficientshad enough
leader behavior studies to compare with performance studies (subordinate reliabilities, with 14 leader behavior and 12 performance studies, and subordinate-self
correlations, with 6 and 9 studies, respectively; a few studies could not be classified). Both types of coefficients showed similar means for leader behavior and
performance, supporting the inclusion of leader behavior.
9. Coefficients for three types of performance ratings were recorded separately.
The first type included overall ratings (i.e., single, overall performance ratings). The
second type included composite measures (statistical combinations of dimension
ratings). The third type included ratings of performance on individual dimensions.
Individual dimensions were further broken down into interpersonal or cognitive
dimensions. The three types of ratings (overall, composite, and dimensional) were
recorded separately, so a particular study could potentially have had each type. In
fact, it was rare for a study to have more than one type (e.g., only five studies had
reliabilities for a composite and for the dimensions that made up the composite).
All three types of ratings were included in analyses to test the hypothesis that
composite ratings would be most reliable. The separate dimension types were
included in analyses to test for differences between interpersonal and cognitive
dimensions. To test all other hypotheses, coefficients for all types of ratings for a
given sample were averaged. Other strategies for selecting a single coefficient for
each sample were tried (e.g., using only dimensional ratings) and produced very
similar results.
10. Peer reliabilitiesfor 28 separatesamples were recorded from Project A, with
a total sample of over 10,000 (Campbell, 1986; Pulakos & Borman, 1986).
Examination of these reliabilities showed that they were significantly 07 < .05)
lower than other peer reliabilities (the means differed by .lo). We decided that
including all 28 samples would inordinately influence our results. Instead, one
sample was randomly selected for inclusion and the rest were excluded.

The final dataset included 281 coefficients from 177 samples, with a total sample
size of 28,999. As expected, some samples provided more than one coefficient. Of
the 281 coefficients, 204 (73%) were published in journals or books, 8 (3%) were

papers, technical reports, dissertations). The 159 references from which our coefficients were drawn are listed in the reference section, identified with asterisks.
In terms of the types of coefficients, 123 were within-source reliabilities and
158 were between-source correlations (44% and 56% of the total, respectively).
Further breakdowns of within-source reliabilities and between-source correlations
are shown in Tables 2,3,4, and 5.

sample had been taken. As mentioned earlier, some samples provided more than
one type. The total numbers of overall, composite, and dimensional within-source

For each study the following variables were recorded: the coefficient(s), sample
size, rating source (or pair of sources for between-source correlations), type of
rating (overall, composite, or dimensional), whether ratees were managers or not,
and job complexity.Military officers were grouped with managers because officers

supervisors, and lower level administrative jobs. Low-complexity jobs, given a

categorized (e.g., based on a mix of different jobs). We attempted to code several
other variables (e.g., length of rater-ratee acquaintance, research versus administrative rating purpose) but either the information was rarely available or there was
very little variability among studies.

Analysis Strategy

Analyses were conducted separately for each source and for each pair of sources.
Hunter and Schmidt's (1990) procedures were used to compute observed means and
variances of coefficients. In all analyses, cases were weighted by sample size. Eighty
percent confidence intervals and credibility intervals were computed for each type
of coefficien using the procedures of Viswesvaran et al. (1996). A confidence
interval indicates the precision of the mean of a distribution. There is an 80% chance
that the true mean falls within this interval. A credibility interval, recognizing that
there may be some true variation in coefficients from one study to the next (not due
to sampling error), indicates the amount of true variation. For example, 80% of true
peer reliabilities would fall within the credibility interval for peers.
Before conducting the analyses, all coefficients were adjusted to the reliability
of a single rater. Sometimes a reference reported a reliability adjusted using the
Spearman-Brown prophecy formula to represent the reliability of the mean across
raters. We merely reversed this correction whenever appropriate, so all reliabilities
would be comparable. When between-source correlationswere based on aggregates
of multiple raters, the correlations were corrected to represent a single rater from
each source. This was done using Nunnally's (1978) formula 7-2 (p. 238).
To estimate the distinctiveness of perspectives, the mean correlation for each
pair of sources was first compared to the within-source reliabilities for the two
sources involved. Then, we corrected correlations between sources using the mean
within-source reliabilities for the two sources involved. Self-other correlations
were corrected using only the other sources' reliabilities. If correlations between
sources were equal to reliabilities then the corrected correlations would equal 1.00,
indicating a lack of distinctiveness.To the extent that between-source correlations
were lower than reliabilities, the corrected correlations would be less than 1.00,
indicatingdistinct perspectives (i.e., more agreement within than between sources).

DISCUSSION
Interrater Reliabilities of Subordinate, Supervisor,
and Peer Ratings
Reliabilities have implications for increasing the quality of rating systems, and for
correcting observed relations (e.g., validity coefficients).No source had particularly
high reliability for a single rater and managerial jobs in particular showed low
reliabilities. Fortunately, multiple raters are often available (at least for peers and

346

subordinates). This means the quality of ratings can be improved, providing higher
reliabilities for each source than the reliabilities presented here.
Increasing reliability is useful because it increases validity. Therefore, reliability
information is only useful to the extent that the measures in question possess some
degree of validity (which can then be raised by increasing the reliability). Research has
demonstrated considerable evidence of validity for supervisors (Bornmer, Johnson,
Rich, Podsakoff, & MacKenzie, 1995; Heneman, 1986; Viswesvaran, 1996)and peers
(Kane & Lawler, 1978; Norton, 1992; Viswesvaran, 1996) in the form of moderate
correlations with objective criteriasuch as productivity, salary change, and promotions.
Relatively little evidence of this type exists for subordinatesbut there are some positive
findings (e.g., Van Velsor & Leslie, 1991b).Further, some evidence supports the utility
of subordinate feedback ratings for improving ratee performance (Atwater, Roush, &
Fischthal, 1995; Hazucha, Hezlett, & Schneider, 1993; Srnither, Wohlers, & London,
1995).Given this evidence for the three sources, it is important to determine the number
of raters that will achieve high reliability, thereby maximizing validity. Single-rater
reliabilities from this study can be adjusted with the Spearman-Brown formula to
determine how many raters from a source are needed to achieve a desired level of
reliability (e.g., .70 or .80).
As mentioned earlier, London and Smither (1995) reported that four to six
subordinates and peers are typically used in multisource feedback. Applying the
Spearman-Brown formula to our mean reliabilities showed that four subordinates
would produce a reliability of only .63. Six subordinates would yield a reliability
above .70 (actually .72). Numbers of raters to reach higher reliabilities appear in
Table 6. For example, reaching a reliability of 3 0 requires 10 subordinates.

A second factor to consider (in addition to the number of raters) when attempting
to maximize reliability is whether to use composite, overall, or dimensional ratings.
Compositeratings were generally found to maximize reliability. This is not relevant
to developmentalfeedbackprograms because the emphasis is on specificbehavioral
dimensions, so dimensionratings are required. When a global criterion is used (e.g.,
for test validation), a composite is likely to be preferable to an overall rating from
a standpoint of reliability.
Results of this study have implications for correction of observed relations (e.g.,
validity coefficients) for criterion unreliability. Reliabilities can be used to correct
correlation coefficients from individual studies, and also to construct artifact
distributions for meta-analysis. The subordinate reliabilities reported here provide
the first meta-analytic values that can be used for this purpose. Subordinate
corrections can be useful when subordinate ratings are used as validation criteria
and also when used in leader behavior research.
These results also strengthen the conclusions of Viswesvaran et al. (1996)
regarding supervisor reliability corrections in meta-analysis of test validities.
Supervisor reliability values of .60 (Hunter & Hunter, 1984) and .80 (Hartigan &
Wigdor, 1989) have been used for this purpose. These results confirmed Viswesvaran et al.'s (1996) conclusion that both of the values cited earlier are likely to
have resulted in underestimates of validity. This study adds to Viswesvaran et al.
(1996) by providing separate values for different job types. Lower values should
be used for managerial jobs (.44 for supervisor and .36 for peer ratings). Supervisor
correction values for nonmanagerial jobs depended on complexity, with lower
reliabilities for higher complexity jobs.
A theoretical issue raised by these results is the reason for supervisors' higher
reliability as compared to subordinates and peers. Possibly this was because it is
part of the supervisor's job to pay attention to and evaluate subordinates' performance. Peers may not attend to performance as closely because it is generally not
part of their job to do so, and they may have less investment in their peers'
performance than do supervisors. Subordinates, on the other hand, probably do pay
close attention to the behavior of their superior. However, superiors may behave

differently toward different subordinates (London & Smither, 1995; Van Velsor &
Leslie, 1991a). The low subordinate reliabilities may reflect these differences in
treatment from the same superior. Alternatively, Yammarino and Dubinsky (1994)
presented some evidence that subordinate ratings are based on implicit theories and
have little to do with the superior's actual behavior. Further research should
compare these explanations.
Dimension type results have both theoretical and practical implications. Theoretical implicationsconcern the portions of job performance observed and attended
to by different sources. Findings supported the idea that interpersonal behaviors are
relatively observable to subordinates. It is also likely that subordinates attend
closely to their supervisors' interpersonal behaviors. The relatively low interpersonal reliabilities for peers were unexpected. It may be that peers do not place
particularly high weight on their coworkers' interpersonal behavior. This goes
against conventional wisdom somewhat, and it may be that peers are more concerned with noninterpersonal dimensions of their coworkers' performance such as
productivity and effort. Also unexpected were the relatively high cognitive dimension reliabilities for supervisors and peers. Although cognitive work is unobservable, the results often may not be. The relatively high reliabilities may reflect
supervisor and peer access to the results of problem solving, judgment, and other
cognitive behaviors (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995).
A practical implication of dimension type results concerns the weight given to
different sources in multisourcefeedback. Although subordinates generally showed
the lowest reliabilities, reliabilities for interpersonal dimensions were comparable
to those for peer ratings. This suggests that relatively more emphasis should be
given to subordinateresults for interpersonal ratings than for other dimension types.
Both theoretical and practical dimension type issues can be explored further using
more specific dimension categories. For example, Borman and Brush's (1993)
taxonomy for managerial performance dimensions (e.g., planning and organizing,
technical proficiency, decision makinglproblem solving, etc.) could be useful for
such explorations.

Correlations Between Sources
Correlations between sources were examined to help understand the value of
including multiple sources in a rating system. Although the sources we studied have
all shown evidence of validity (validity evidencefor subordinates,supervisors, and
peers was reviewed earlier, and evidence for self-ratings is reviewed later), multisource rating systems depend on their providing incremental validity. This can only
happen if the sources show relatively low correlations and therefore unique perspectives (i.e., if the sources are not redundant). The hypothesis that subordinates
would show low correlations with others was supported. More important, when

MULTI-SOURCE PERFORMANCE RATINGS

349

compared to within-source reliabilities subordinates showed a relatively unique
perspective. Our results suggest that it is important to include subordinatesbecause
their ratings are not redundant with other sources. Excluding subordinates would
likely result in deficient performance information, and the unique contribution of
subordinates may outweigh the potential problems caused by discomfort with
upward feedback.
Self-ratings also showed low correlations (and a unique perspective), consistent
with Harris and Schaubroeck (1988). Self-subordinate correlations were particularly low. Our results therefore provide additional support for Harris and Schaubroeck's (1988) conclusion that self-ratings are affected by egocentric biases. Harris
and Schaubroeck's (1988) meta-analysis showed self-ratings to be higher, on
average, than supervisor or peer ratings. This suggests that the self-other disagreement is due in part to range restriction caused by inflated self-ratings. But they also
found that correcting for range restriction did not erase the low agreement. Harris
and Schaubroeck (1988) therefore hypothesized other biases for self-ratings such
as a self-esteem effect. Those with high self-esteem may inflate their self-ratings
whereas those with low self-esteem may not. In fact, some ratees may even give
themselves overly low ratings (e.g., Atwater & Yarnmarino, 1992, found that a
significant number of ratees underrated themselves in comparison to ratings they
received from others). This type of bias in the self-ratings would cause low
agreement as found in this study.
We should note that self-ratings may not be completely at fault for the low
agreement. To some extent self-ratings may measure something unique and valid.
This notion is supported by Mabe and West's (1982) meta-analysis of correlations
between self-ratings and other measures such as tests and objective performance.
Also, Fleenor (1996) found that somewhat different assessment center dimensions
showed validity against self-ratings as compared to supervisor ratings.
To the extent that self-ratings are unique and valid, they can be a useful addition
(e.g., for a test validation study). To the extent that self-ratings suffer from
egocentric biases this contribution is lessened. Regardless of the reason for the
self-other disagreement, an implication of the disagreementis that individuals will
often either overrate or underrate themselves as compared to their subordinates,
supervisors, andlor peers. London and Smither (1995) summarized evidence and
theory supporting the idea that disagreement (characterized as low self-awareness)
leads to bad outcomes such as career failure.
Feedback from the other sources may help to avoid these bad outcomes by
increasing self-awarenessand performance in weak areas. Although not completely
consistent, some evidence supports a positive effect of feedback on self-awareness
(London & Smither, 1995). As noted earlier, there is also evidence that feedback
increases performance (e.g., Atwater et al., 1995). It would be interesting to
calculate self-other correlations following feedback to see if these correlations are
higher than before feedback.

Correlations for supervisor, peer, and self-ratings were moderated by job type,
as was the case with interrater reliabilities.Managerial jobs showed lower correlations than did nonmanagerial jobs, and there was evidence that higher complexity
was associated with lower between-source correlations. An implication of these
results is that researchers should expect lower agreement between sources when
studying managerial or higher complexity jobs.
Only supervisor and peer perspectives for nonmanagerial jobs, and for low- and
medium-complexity jobs, showed little distinctiveness. For these job types the
corrected between-source correlations were fairly close to 1.00 (all .85 or higher).
The evidence suggests that supervisor and peer perspectives were reasonably
distinct for managerial jobs and for high-complexity jobs. However, the usefulness
of including both supervisors and peers for nonmanagerial jobs or for low- or
medium-complexity jobs is less clear. Although more raters may increase reliability, it should not be assumed that substantially unique portions of criterion variance
will be covered by the different sources.
The overall findings regarding job type have implications for future research on
reliability and correlations between sources. Job type was found to be an important
moderator. In particular, there were clear mean differencesbetween managerial and
nonmanagerial jobs. Further, smaller credibility intervals for managers for virtually
all types of coefficients indicate less true variation than for nonrnanagers. One
implication of these findings is that future searches for moderators are more likely
to yield positive results for nonmanagerial jobs, because there is more unexplained
(true) variance in coefficients.
A second implication is that more fine-grained analyses of job types are likely
to be fruitful for nonmanagerial jobs. Only one narrowly defined type of job
(managers) was examined here. The fact that only managers showed relatively little
true variation suggests that other more narrowly defined job types may also show
little variation. That is, specific job type may be an important moderator among
nonmanagerial jobs, even within complexity levels. Holland's taxonomy of types
(e.g., Holland, 1996) could provide a useful way to classify jobs for this purpose.
For example, Holland's artistic (e.g., interior designer) and social (e.g., counselor;
clergy member) types are probably similar in complexity, but artisticjobs may show
higher interrater reliabilities because these jobs tend to have observable outcomes
(and this should make performance easier to rate).
Dimension type results showed some evidence of higher between-source correlations for interpersonal dimensions. This may indicate that interpersonal behaviors
are relatively observableto all sources. In contrast,task-oriented behaviors and their
results are probably more observable to supervisorsand peers than to subordinates.
One implication is that researchers should expect somewhat lower between-source
correlations for noninterpersonal performance dimensions. A second implication
is that different rating sources are more likely to show different results (e.g.,
different test validities) for noninterpersonal criteria. Future research could focus

on more specific categories of behavior, such as Borman and Brush's (1993)
taxonomy of managerial megadimensions.
Summary of Contributions

This study makes severalcontributionsto the existing literature. First, psychometric
properties of subordinateratings were examined meta-analytically for the first time.
Results have implicationsfor the number of subordinatesthat provide ratings (more
are required than for other sources to achieve comparablereliability), for correcting
observed relations, and for the incremental value of including the subordinate
perspective in a rating system.
A second contribution is the comparison of ratings for managerial and nonmanagerial jobs. With the popularity of multirater systems for managers it is useful to
know that reliabilities are relatively low (compared to reliabilitiesfor nonmanagers)
as are between-source correlations. When dealing with managerial jobs, different
reliability values should be used to determine the number of raters needed, and to
correct correlations for unreliability.
A third contribution is the comparisons among dimension types. Results can
help in understanding the observability of different types of behaviors for different
sources. Further, multisource feedback depends on dimension information so it is
useful to know about psychometric properties of dimension types.
A fourth contribution is a strengthening of Viswesvaran et al.'s (1996) finding
that peers had lower reliabilities than did supervisors. The overall supervisor and
peer results here are fairly consistentwith those of Viswesvaran et al. In part because
of the relatively small number of peer coefficients they analyzed, Viswesvaran et
al. equivocated somewhat in stating that peers showed lower reliabilities than
supervisors. This study, being based on substantially larger numbers of coefficients
for each source, provides stronger evidence of this difference.
A fifth contribution is the finding that composite rating measures showed higher
reliabilities. Composite measures and single overall or dimensional measures had
not been compared previously using meta-analysis.
A sixth contributionis that distinctivenessof rating sources was tested by a direct
comparison of between-source correlations and within-source reliabilities.Individual studies have done this in the past but this study represents the first meta-analytic
comparison.
In summary, these results provide information about how to improve the quality
of rating systems, and also provide a greater understanding of the different perspectives on performance. The relatively low reliabilities (especially for managerial
jobs) can be improved with multiple raters and composite ratings. The perspectives
of the different sources were fairly distinct. With the possible exception of supervisors and peers for some job types, the inclusion of multiple rating sources was
supported.
