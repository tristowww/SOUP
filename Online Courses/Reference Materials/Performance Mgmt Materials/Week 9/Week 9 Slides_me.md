## Interrater Reliability and Interrater Agreement {#slide-15}

/

15

::: {.smartart .hList1 layout="hList1"}
**Interrater Agreement**

- High agreement means that raters are giving the same or very similar ratings to the same individuals, implying consensus in evaluation
- Focuses on how similar the ratings are between raters

**Interrater Reliability**

- Measures the consistency of ratings across different raters
- High reliability means that different raters give consistent ratings over time, but it doesn't necessarily mean they rate the exact same way
:::

## Interrater Reliability and Interrater Agreement {#slide-16}

- Reliability without Agreement:  Raters may consistently give high ratings, but their ratings might not match (i.e., one rater consistently gives scores of 4/5, while another consistently gives 3/5).
- Agreement without Reliability:  Raters might agree on ratings, but those ratings could be influenced by systematic bias (e.g., both raters give overly generous scores, making the results unreliable).

## Measuring Interrater Agreement {#slide-21}

21

::: {.smartart .vList2 layout="vList2"}
**2 types:**

- ICC (2): the average of multiple raters
- ICC (1): single ratings from multiple raters

**Exact matching of ratings btw different raters**

- 1. Cohen's Kappa (for categorical or ordinal data)
- Measures agreement among more than 2 raters for continuous data -- how well raters assign similar scores to the same individuals
- 2. Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC):

**Measures agreement among more than 2 raters for continuous data -- how well raters assign similar scores to the same individuals**

- 2 types:

**1. Cohen's Kappa (for categorical or ordinal data)**

- Ranges from -1 to 1
- Measures agreement btw two raters, correcting for the agreement that could happen by chance
:::

- Interrater correlations are different than reliability
