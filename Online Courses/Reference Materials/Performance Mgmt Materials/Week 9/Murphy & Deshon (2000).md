INTERRATER CORRELATIONS DO NOT ESTIMATE
THE RELIABILITY OF JOB PERFORMANCE RATINGS
KEVIN R. MURPHY
Colorado State University
RICHARD DE SHON
Michigan State University

Interrater correlations are widely interpreted as estimates of the reliability of supervisoly performance ratings, and are frequently used
to correct the correlations between ratings and other measures (e.g.,
test scores) for attenuation. These interrater correlations do provide
some useful information, but they are not reliability coefficients. There
is clear evidence of systematic rater effects in performance appraisal,
and variance associated with raters is not a source of random measurement error. We use generalizability theoly to show why rater variance
is not properly interpreted as measurement error, and show how such
systematic rater effects can influence both reliability estimates and validity coefficients. We show conditions under which interrater correlations can either overestimate or underestimate reliability coefficients,
and discuss reasons other than random measurement error for low interrater correlations.

Measurement error can seriously obscure the effects of treatments
and interventions, and attenuate the correlations between measures.
Corrections for attenuation have long been available, and are widely
used in applied research. However, although the idea of correcting for
the known effects of measurement error is simple and uncontroversial,
the operational process of correcting for attenuation can be a difficult
and confusing exercise (Cronbach, Gleser, Nanda, & Rajaratnam, 1972;
DeShon, 1998; Lumsden, 1976). A number of methods exist for estimating the reliability of most tests or measures, and choice of the appropriate reliability estimate is often a complex and important matter (Schmidt
& Hunter, 1996).
The choice of methods for estimating reliability is especially important when correcting for measurement error in ratings of job performance. These ratings are usually obtained from a single supervisor,who

Assumptions Underlyingthe Use of Interrater Reliability Measures

The argument that interrater correlations provide reasonable estimates of the amount of measurement error in ratings is based on a number of assumptions about the processes involved when evaluating the
performance of particular individuals. In particular, the use of interrater correlations as reliability estimates is based on three key assumptions: (a) that similarly situated raters in organizations can be considered
to function as alternate forms of the same measurement instrument, (b)
that agreement between raters reflects true score variance in ratings, and
(c) that disagreement between raters is best conceptualized as measurement error (i.e., variance that is unrelated to true performance).
Raters are equivalent to alternateforms. Schmidt and Hunter (1996)
are quite explicit in making the assumption that each “rater is analogous
to a different form of the rating instrument” (p. 209). In another paper,
Viswesvaranet al. (1996) argue that the question of interest in evaluating
the reliability of performance ratings is whether “the same ratings will
be obtained if a different but equally knowledgeable judge rated the
same employee” (p. 565). If raters are viewed as alternate forms of a
measurement instrument, the correlation between these alternate forms
should provide an estimate of reliability.
There are several reasons to believe that raters in organizations cannot be treated as interchangeable forms of a rating instrument. First,
Viswesvaran et al.’s (1996) term “equally knowledgeable judge” is surprisingly restrictive. In most organizations, different raters observe different behaviors, and have different responsibilities when completing
performaw ratings (Borman, 1974; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). In-

As will be detailed below, the assumption that agreement between
raters is necessarily due ratees’ true performance levels of ratings is not
well supported. Rather, there are a number of reasons why different
raters might be expected to agree in their evaluations that have little to
do with the influence (if any) of ratee performance on ratings.
Rater effects are a source of random measurement error. Schmidt and
Hunter (1996) note that “supervisorsare quite idiosyncraticin their evaluations of workers. This idiosyncrasy is the largest known source of error
of measurement in using ratings to evaluate performance” (p. 210). In a
similar vein, they note that “the specific error for each rater is that rater’s
idiosyncraticperceptions of employee job performance. This specific error is known to be very large in ratings of job performance” (p. 209).
Schmidt and Hunter (1996), Viswemaran et al. (1996), and others
who interpret interrater correlations as reliability estimates treat rater
effects as sources of random error in ratings, but do so with no clear
justification. Their assumption that rater effects are a source of random error makes sense in terms of a classical model of reliability, in
which observed scores are partitioned into true scores and errors (Lord
& Novick, 1968), but it is not a reasonable assumption from the perspective of more comprehensiveapproaches to understanding the psychometric characteristics of ratings, such as generalizability theory (Cronbach
et al., 1972). Generalizabilitytheory suggests that variance due to raters
is probably not “true score” (although as will be shown below, it is probably not orthogonal to ratees’ performance levels), but that it is also not
necessarily a source of random measurement error. Decisions about
how to interpret and deal with rater effects in performance appraisal
depend on the substantive nature of these effects and on the generalizations one wished to make on the basis of ratings.

rater effects that have been examined in the voluminous literature on
performance appraisal do not exhibit the known characteristics of random measurement error. According to classical reliability theory (see
Lord & Novick, 1968) measurement error has three defining characteristics: (a) measurement errors have a mean of zero, (b) measurement
error is uncorrelated with true scores, and (c) measurement errors on
two tests or measures are uncorrelated. The assumption that measurement errors have a mean of zero is almost certainlywrong; performance

Generalizability analyses can be very useful for sorting out ratee effects, rater effects, interactions, and so forth, and their implications for
various generalizations one might want to make about ratings. However,
the factorial designs that characterize much of the current research on
the psychometric characteristics of performance ratings (especially ratings collected in multisource rating systems) do not allow one to disentangle a potentially critical confound in interpreting performance rating
data-that is, the fact a variety of contextual factors that are known to
influence performance ratings. Raters are, almost by definition, nested
within rating contexts (e.g., their position in the organization, their relationships with ratees, the political factors impinging on their ratings,
etc.), and in principle, it may be impossible to completely separate rater
effects from the effects of the context in which the rater operates. Although we will use the term “rater effect” in later sections of this paper, it
is important to recognize that what is sometimes treated as idiosyncratic
rater variance in ratings is more likely to be a combination of the effects
of rater characteristics and the effects of the context in which the rater
operates. Both characteristics of the rater and characteristicsof the rating context are likely to substantially affect interrater correlations, and
the effects of these factors is not at all what one would expect if rater
effects were treated simply as a source of random measurement error.
The data collection designs in the great majority of the studies reviewed by Viswesvaran et al. (1996) and by Rothstein (1990) substantially
limit our ability to evaluate systematic rater effects. Typically, ratings are
collected from a single supervisor (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995), creating
a research design in which ratees are nested within raters. This makes
it very difficult to sort out rater and ratee effects, and it impossible to
sort out rater effects from rater by ratee interactions. Several recent

rater effects are among the most important sources of variance in ratings. There is not yet a sufficient database to support a meta-analysis of

Rater and Rating Context Effects That Influence InterraterAgreement

The classical true score model of reliability suggests that correlations
between ratings obtained from two separate raters can be explained fully
by the influence of true scores and that disagreements among raters in
their relative evaluations of a set of ratees are explained fully by the influence of random measurement error. Several decades of research on
performance appraisal suggest that a more complex set of factors (several of which are likely to represent systematic rater effects) influence
the correlations among ratings.
Why raters might agree in their evaluations. There is considerable evidence that supervisory ratings provide some valid information about the
performance of those being evaluated (Milkovich & Wigdor, 1991; Murphy & Cleveland, 1991, 1995). One implication of this research is that
agreements between raters should indeed reflect, in part, the influence
of “true score” variance. Unfortunately, there are also a number of factors that might lead two raters to agree in their performance ratings,
even if their is no “true score” variance in the ratings that either of them
provide. These factors can be grouped into four categories: (a) shared
goals and biases, (b) shared perceptions of the organization and of appraisal systems, (c) shared frames of reference, and (d) shared relationships with ratees. As these titles imply, similarities in factors other than
ratee performance can lead raters to agree. As these titles further imply,
dissimilarities in each of these factors can become sources of disagreement between raters (e.g., raters who are pursuing different goals might
provide different performance ratings).
One possible explanation for artifactual agreement between raters
is that they may share biases in favor of or against a particular class
of ratees (e.g., ratings might be influenced by ratee gender, ethnicity,
age, attractiveness, etc.). Research suggests that the influence of each of
these biases, considered in isolation, is likely to be small (Kraiger & Ford,
1985; McEvoy & Cascio, 1989). Nevertheless, the cumulative effects of

Influence of Systematic Rater Effects on the Reliability of Ratings

One of the main points in this presentation is that the variance due
to raters should not be thought of as error without careful consideration.
Classic reliability theory does not provide a mechanism for considering
this issue but generalizability theory was developed specifically to disentangle the various sources of variance in observed scores and encourage
a more rigorous approach to defining error variance. In terms of a generalizability theory design, there are four sources of potential variance
in a simple rater x ratee design: ratee variance, rater variance, ratee x
rater interaction variance, and error variance. The interaction variance
is confounded with the error variance in this design and therefore cannot
be estimated separately. The rater variance and the error variance can
be separated in this design and that leads to the question of whether the
rater variance should be lumped together with the error variance when
estimating the generalizability coefficient (equivalent to the reliability
coefficient in this design) or whether rater effects should be considered
as variance that is neither true score nor error.
In generalizability theory, the issue of whether the rater variance
should be treated as error is resolved by determining whether the purpose of measurement is to make relative or absolute decisions about
individuals. Some decisions that are about individuals are made with
reference to an absolute standard, as in criterion referenced measurement. For example, if you wish to determine whether a specific student
has mastered a skill (e.g., sight-reading of music), this evaluation must

What Do Interrater Correlations Tell Us?

As highlighted above, classic reliability theory is based upon the very
restrictive assumption that raters are parallel assessments of the ratee. Clearly, this assumption is rarely met, even as an approximation,
in actual rating situations. As a result, the correlation between ratings
from different supervisors is not interpretable as a reliability coefficient.
However, this correlation does provide information that can be useful.
To understand what information is provided by interrater correlations, it
is useful to approach the correlation between ratings made by different
raters from a generalizability theory perspective.

sampling perspective (Cronbach, Rajaratnam, & Gleser, 1965). For

ratings. The suggestion that interrater correlations could provide better
estimates is conceptually appealing, but it is not consistent with a large
body of research on performance evaluation in organizations. Studies of
internal consistency, test-retest correlations, and interrater correlations
all provide useful information about the likely levels of reliability in performance rating, and it is likely that the accumulated evidence from a
variety of sources cited herein allows us to make reasonable statements
about the upper and lower bounds of reliability. However, the ultimate
solution to the problem of accurately estimating the psychometric qualities of performance ratings will require researchers to continue to examine the more critical (and more complex) question of the construct
validity of performance ratings. A number of authors (e.g., Harris &
Schaubroeck, 1988; Rothstein, 1990; Viswesvaran et al., 1996) have provided critical information about the extent to which raters agree in their
relative evaluations of subordinates, but there is still a good deal to learn
about why raters agree, why they disagree, and what this tells us about
the meaning of performance ratings.
Why are interrater correlationsso low? Previous reviews of interrater
correlations (e.g., Rothstein, 1990; Viswesvaran et al., 1996) have provided little explanation for why correlations between ratings are so low.
Rather, the low correlation among ratings has been taken as evidence of
massive random error, and this error, in turn, explains why the correlations are low. If low interrater correlations does not mean that ratings
are necessarily saturated with random measurement error, the persistent
inability of raters to agree in their relative evaluations of ratees becomes
a phenomenon worthy of study. In particular, it is important to ask the
question of why the correlations among performance ratings are so uniformly low.
Cleveland and Murphy’s (1993) review of the role of rater goals
in performance appraisal (see also Murphy & Cleveland, 1995) suggests one explanation for low interrater correlations: because raters are
nested within rating contexts, it seems reasonable to expect that rater
effects are more likely contribute to disagreement more than to agreement. That is, raters differ in terms of their position in the organization,
their relationships with ratees, their goals when ratings, and so forth.

are normally collected in settings where range restriction is ubiquitous,

Suppose, for example, that an organization used a performance appraisal form with a 9-point rating scale. If ratings were normally distributed throughout the entire scale, they would have a mean of about 5
and a standard deviation of about 1.5. It is more likely, however, that ratings will be concentrated at the top end of the scale (Bretz et al., 1992).

only a small part of the rating scale, the effects of range restriction on
interrater correlations can be staggering.

Interrater correlations contain useful information, but they do not
provide an estimate of the reliability of supervisory ratings of job performance. These correlations can either overestimate or underestimate
reliability, and there is no justification for correcting for attenuation on
the basis of average interrater correlations. The psychometric model
that assigns all variance to true scores and random measurement error,
and that assumes that the only difference between a rating and true performance is that random error, is not a reasonable one for analyzing performance ratings, and the interpretation of interrater correlations that
flow from that model are also not reasonable.
Generalizability theory was developed to allow one to estimate the
dependability of ratings obtained in contexts where there might be multiple systematicsources of variance in ratings. The accumulated body of
research on performance appraisal suggests that there are indeed many
different sources of variance in ratings, and that “true score” variance
may represent only a small proportion of the systematicvariability in job
performance ratings. Factors other than true scores can influence the
correlations among ratings and can also influence the correlations between ratings and other measures (e.g., scores on ability tests). Generalizability theory provides methods for analyzing ratings of this sort, and
this model does not require you to assume (incorrectly) that true performance and random measurement error are the only sources of variance
in rating. The parallel test model, which requires this assumption, is
simply not applicable to supervisory performance ratings.
The common finding that interrater correlations are low, even when
raters have similar positions in the organization, has typically been taken
to indicate the massive effects of random measurement error in rating,
and has been put forth as a justification for substantial corrections for attenuation in studies that use ratings as criteria. The analyses presented
here suggest that interrater correlations can substantiallyunderestimate
reliability and that corrections based on interrater correlations may spuriously inflate our estimates of the correlation between ratings and other
measures. It also suggests that factors other than measurement error explain low interrater correlations. However, these analyses also show that
interrater correlations are not always and everywhere overestimates of
reliability. Rather, they are one piece of the puzzle in understanding
the psychometric characteristics of job performance ratings. However,
