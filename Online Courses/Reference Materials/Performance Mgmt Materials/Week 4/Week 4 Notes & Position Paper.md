\"Halo error\" is when our overall impression of someone influences how we rate their specific qualities. For example, if we think someone is generally good, we might rate them highly across all areas, even if they aren\'t actually that great in every single area.

- **Within-rater ratings**: This is when the same person is rating someone across different criteria or at different times. The statement says that these ratings are \"increased by halo\", meaning that if a rater has a positive overall view of someone, they are likely to give them high ratings in many areas, possibly ignoring some of their weaknesses.

- **Between-rater ratings**: This is when different people rate someone. The statement says that these ratings are \"reduced by halo error\", implying that when different people rate an individual, the influence of the halo error is less because different raters may have different overall impressions, balancing out any overly positive or negative ratings one rater might give.

In simpler terms, the statement is saying that when one person does all the ratings, their general impression of someone can significantly influence the ratings, potentially making them inaccurately high. But when different people are giving the ratings, this mistake is less likely to happen, making the ratings more balanced and accurate.
