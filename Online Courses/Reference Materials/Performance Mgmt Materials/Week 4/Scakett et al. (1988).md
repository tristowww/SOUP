In this study we proposed that understanding of interrelations among criterion measures can be
improved by differentiating between measures of typical and maximum job performance. We offered
denning characteristics of typical and maximum performance measures and requirements for a fair
examination of relations between the two. Typical and maximum performance measures of the
speed and accuracy with which supermarket cashiers process items were obtained from two large
samples (JVs = 635 and 735) of cashiers from 12 supermarket chains. Relatively low correlations
between typical and maximum performance measures were found. We discuss the implications of
these findings for the use of maximum performance measures as criteria in the validation of selection
systems and for the understanding of the impact of ability and motivation on job performance.

The "criterion problem" continues to be a major issue facing
I/O psychology. Considerable attention is devoted to a wide variety of issues regarding the conceptual appropriateness and
empirical characteristics of various approaches to criterion
measurement. A number of typologies exist for categorizing criteria. For example, Smith (1976) offered a three-dimensional
typology, categorizing criteria on a short term-long term dimension, a general-specific dimension, and a proximity to organizational goals dimension (i.e., behavior vs. results vs. effectiveness).
There is considerable research on the convergence of measures from different parts of the typologies. Some of this research involves a direct comparison that is based on obtaining
multiple criteria for the same individuals. For example, a recent
meta-analysis by Heneman (1986) examined the relation between supervisory ratings of performance and results-oriented
measures of performance—essentially a comparison of subjective versus objective criterion measures. Heneman reported a
mean correlation, corrected for error of measurement, of .27
between subjective and objective performance measures. Other
research involves an indirect comparison, which shows that the
relation between predictors and criteria differs across samples
using different types of criteria. Ghiselli's (1966) classic examination of test validities exemplifies this approach: Validity studies were categorized as using either training criteria or job proficiency criteria. Higher validity coefficients were obtained using training criteria.
In this article we contend that an understanding of criteria
can be increased through the consideration of a dimension
along which criteria can be categorized that has been largely
neglected up to this point, namely, the categorization of criteria
as measures of typical versus maximum performance. This ter-

minology is routinely used when dealing with predictors: The
terms typical and maximum performance are commonly used
to differentiate between ability and personality measures (e.g.,
Cronbach, 1960). However, when applied to criteria, the basic
question of the relation between typical and maximum performance on a job has not received much attention.
The distinction between typical and maximum performance
does surface in Kane's (1982) work on performance distribution assessment. Kane noted that some individuals may consistently perform at the same level, whereas other individuals' performance may be more variable. Recognizing that two individuals may have the same modal performance but differ in
maximum performance, or that two individuals may have the
same level of maximum performance but differ in typical performance, Kane's approach asked raters to indicate the frequency with which an individual performs at each of a number
of specified performance levels. Although dealing conceptually
with the typical versus maximum performance distinction,
Kane's work dealt with judgments about performance and did
not involve an explicit examination of the relations between
typical and maximum performance.
Some clarification is in order as to the defining characteristics
of typical and maximum performance measures. Three characteristics are proposed as necessary for a measure to be categorized as "maximum performance." First, there must be explicit
awareness that one is being evaluated. Second, there must be
awareness of and acceptance of implicit or explicit instructions
to maximize effort. Note that when the term maximum performance is used to refer to predictors, this acceptance of instructions is commonly assumed: We assume that a person's score
on a cognitive ability test reflects the person's best effort. Third,
performance must be measured over a short enough time duration that the performer's attention remains focused on the accepted goal of maximum performance. For example, although
a college professor realizes that his or her performance will be
evaluated by students at the end of the term, the time duration

Typical Performance Measures
Different cash register systems record different employee performance indices. Two measures common to many systems are items per
minute, a measure of speed, and voids per shift, a measure of accuracy.
Mean items per minute and mean voids per shift were computed for
four consecutive I-week periods. For the new-employee sample, these
were the first 4 weeks on the job after training. Coefficient alpha was
computed across the 4 weeks for each of these measures, and both
proved to be highly reliable. Coefficients for the speed measure were .95
for the new-employee sample and .86 for the current-employee sample.
Coefficients for the accuracy measure were .84 for the new-employee
sample and .86 for the current-employee sample. The four weekly measures were then summed to form the typical performance measures of
speed and accuracy. Note that these measures were obtained unobtrusively; none of the chains provided performance feedback to employees

Standard shopping carts containing 25 specified items were prepared.
Employees were told that they would be timed and errors would be recorded while they processed these carts. They were told that this was

Method
Samples
We obtained data from two distinct groups of employees. The first,
labeled the new-employee sample, consisted of 635 new hires from 9
supermarket chains. The second, labeled the current-employee sample,
consisted of 735 employees from 9 supermarket chains with at least 6
months experience. A total of 12 chains participated in the study: 6
contributed to both the new-employee and current-employee samples,
3 each contributed solely to one sample.

Supervisory Rating Measures
Supervisors rated employees using graphic rating scales on three dimensions: register operations, knowledge and judgment, and overall
performance. These rating scales were not designed with a comparison
with the typical and maximum performance measures in mind; separate ratings of speed and accuracy were not obtained, but rather a general rating of register operations. Intercorrelations among these ratings
averaged .78. Thus, the attempt to obtain a subjective measure of one
specific job component, namely, register operations, was not successful.
As with many rating systems, raters did not differentiate clearly among
job dimensions. Nonetheless, we examined the correlations between supervisory ratings of register operations and typical and maximum performance measures for exploratory purposes.

Discussion
The primary finding that we report here is that carefully developed, highly reliable measures of typical and maximum performance obtained from two relatively large samples show only
a modest correlation (.16 in one sample and .36 in the other)
with one another. The issue of the relation between typical and
maximum performance measures has not been examined in
any detail in the job performance literature; our intent is to
argue for the value of this distinction in increasing our understanding of criterion measurement. Clearly, at this point only a
single aspect, albeit a critical one, of a relatively simple, repetitive job has been examined. We make no assertions that a similar pattern of results would result if other jobs were examined.
We do argue that increased knowledge of the relation between
typical and maximum performance is important in a number
of ways.
First, it is relatively common to use maximum performance
measures such as work sample tests as criteria in validation
studies. This is appropriate if the intent of the selection system
is the prediction of maximum performance; however, if the intent is the prediction of long-term typical performance, the use
of maximum performance criteria implicitly assumes that
maximum performance measures are interchangeable with typical performance measures, or at least that validity coefficients
obtained using maximum performance measures can be generalized to the prediction of long-term typical performance. The
observed low correlations between typical and maximum performance obtained in this study do not preclude the finding of
similar predictor-criterion relations for both types of criterion
measures; however, differences in predictor-criterion relations
would not be surprising in light of these results.
Second, the possibility that different predictors will prove
useful for the prediction of typical and maximum performance
merits consideration. As noted earlier, it is reasonable to view
the maximum performance measure as highly ability loaded,
whereas motivational factors play a larger role in long-term typical performance. Differential value of ability and dispositional
variables in predicting typical versus maximum performance
can be hypothesized.
Third, the interpretation of results of common comparisons,
such as those between subjective and objective performance
measures, training and on-the-job measures, and long-term and

short-term measures, can be aided by ensuring that the comparison is not confounded with the typical versus maximum performance distinction. For example, viewing a correlation between training grades and supervisory ratings as providing information on the relation between subjective and objective
performance measures should be done with caution, because
the two measures also differ on the typical versus maximum
dimension.
Some comment is in order on the finding that maximum performance was more highly correlated with supervisory ratings
than was typical performance. Our expectation had been that
supervisory ratings reflect performance over an extended period of time and thus should reflect typical performance.
Knowledgeable industry representatives tell us that our findings
should not have been unexpected. Store managers' attention is
divided among many departments; they attend to the checkstand primarily during peak periods. During peak periods,
managers may help out as baggers and, in this context, are likely
to observe cashiers. Their primary concern regarding cashiers
is whether the cashier can "turn it on" during peak periods;
thus, their evaluation of cashier performance reflects maximum
performance more than typical performance.
We encourage the investigation of the issue of the relation
between typical and maximum performance measures in other
jobs and also encourage moving beyond comparing criterion
measures to examining the predictability of typical and maximum performance measures. It seems likely that as job complexity increases, the role of ability as a determinant of typical
performance will increase. Thus, the correlation between typical and maximum performance may be substantially higher in
more complex jobs than in the current study. Additional data
on this as yet unexamined issue in criterion measurement is
clearly needed.
