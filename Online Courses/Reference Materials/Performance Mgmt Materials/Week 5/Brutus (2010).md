Performance ratings have long dominated research on performance appraisal yet very little is
known about the usage of narrative comments despite the fact that they are a common
methodology to assess performance in organizations. Using theoretical streams from the ﬁelds
of industrial–organizational psychology, communication, and human resource management,
this paper explores the implications of using narrative comments for the various stakeholders
in the performance appraisal process. The psychological processes underlying the production of
narrative comments are explored. The paper also discusses the processes related to the
reception and the impact of receiving performance information in this format for evaluatees.

1. Introduction
A cursory look at performance appraisal research is sufﬁcient to discover the overwhelming attention given to quantitative
approaches (Pearce & Porter, 1986). Indeed, most of what is known about the process of evaluating individual performance in
organizations hinges on the use of numerical ratings, a proven yet relatively narrow operationalization of this process. In
organizational practice, however, performance appraisal systems are not really limited to rating scales. These systems also include
a qualitative component in the form of narrative comments (e.g., Rose & Walsh, 2004; Smither & Walker, 2004; Timmreck &
Bracken, 1995; Woods, Sciarini, & Breiter, 1998). While it is difﬁcult to pinpoint the prototypical performance appraisal process, it is
fair to assume that a majority of them combine quantitative and qualitative formats. That is, most individuals going through the
task of evaluating others are presented with a rating scale and with an opportunity to write comments about the target of their
evaluations. Conversely, those being evaluated are also presented with quantitative data (often at an aggregate level) and with
comments that have been written about them.
Even so, the role of narrative comments in performance appraisal has rarely been the subject of systematic investigation and
only very recently has some researchers begun to focus on this element. For example, Gillipsie, Rose, and Robinson (2006) used
experts to code the clarity of comments provided as part of a 360-degree feedback exercise. The results of this study indicate
signiﬁcant variation in the clarity of comments across sources: comments provided by supervisors and subordinates were
signiﬁcantly clearer than comments from peers. In a longitudinal study on the impact of comments, Smither and Walker (2004)
also uncovered a weak but signiﬁcant link between the characteristics of comments provided by direct reports and the subsequent

Communication theorist Marshall McLuhan argued that each medium, independent of the content it mediates, has its own
intrinsic effects and that all media have characteristics that engage users and recipients in different and unique ways (McLuhan,
1962). For the evaluator, the format sets the parameters within which the information is collected and there are profound
differences between agreeing to a performance-related statement by ticking a box and actually writing a statement about
someone's performance. In his seminal work on self-report measures, Schwarz (1999) brought to light the sensitivity of
respondents to subtle variations in question formats. Structural elements of closed-ended questions provide strong cues that not
only inﬂuence the intended meaning of a question but also remind respondents of alternatives that they may not have considered
otherwise. While Schwarz's work underscores the potency of ﬁne variations within closed-ended questions, the present paper
focuses mainly on variation across formats (i.e., quantitative and qualitative). The current paper also frames these effects within
the unique context of performance appraisal. In appraisal situations, format structure has implications beyond its inﬂuence on
evaluators.
The evaluative information offered by different formats also has signiﬁcant implications for the evaluatee, the receiver of the
performance information. For example, obtaining a below average rating on a leadership scale is certainly distinct from reading:
“Bob is rarely willing to accept our input as to the best strategy to take. In the weekly meetings, he never seriously considers our
suggestions about budget allocation. Most of us don't even prepare our numbers anymore”. It is posed that a variation in evaluation
methodology shapes, in very signiﬁcant ways, how performance information is collected, presented and, very likely, interpreted.
The aim of this paper is to shed light on the psychological processes related to evaluating others with narrative comments and
to receiving these comments as part of a performance evaluation process that is used for individual development. This paper has
the following construction: First, I provide some background as to the neglected role of narrative comments in the performance
appraisal literature. Second, the implications of using narrative comments, from the point of view of the evaluator, are probed.
Third, the inﬂuence of format is scrutinized from the perspective of the feedback recipient. In these sections, a series of testable
propositions are offered to help structure the paper and stimulate future research. Fourth, and ﬁnally, some implications and broad
research questions on the use of narrative comments in performance appraisals are considered.
2. Performance appraisal research: brief history and context
The use of rating scales in performance evaluations is deeply rooted in the history of personnel psychology (Landy & Farr, 1980).
As long ago as the early 1900s, performance ratings were used in organizations and investigated by behavioral scientists (Aiken,
1996; Austin & Villanova, 1992). Today, the literature on performance evaluations focuses almost exclusively on performance
ratings and it is fair to say that, in contemporary research in human resource management and industrial–organizational
psychology, performance evaluation has become synonymous with performance ratings. This focus is best illustrated in the
seminal book by Murphy and Cleveland (1995) on performance appraisal in which the evaluation process is labelled “rating” and
evaluators “raters.” That no other evaluation format other than ratings is considered or even acknowledged by these authors is
telling.
An appreciation of narrative comments as an evaluation method is also absent from the most popular undergraduate human
resource management texts. Paradoxically, the format of performance evaluations has long dominated research in this area.
However, this line of investigation has focused exclusively on variations within rating format (e.g., Tziner & Kopelman, 2002). In
fact, in performance appraisal research, the term format typically refers to variations in graphic rating scales (e.g., BARS, BOS) and
attention on rating format has been so exclusive that researchers in the area have repeatedly called for a broader approach to the
study of performance appraisal (Ilgen, Barnes-Farrell, & McKellin, 1993; Landy & Farr, 1980; Sulsky & Keown, 1998).
To be fair, the relative neglect of narrative comments is not unique to the area of performance appraisal. Survey research as a
whole has also long ignored their role as an alternative or a complement to closed-ended questions and this, despite the frequent
use of open-ended questions in practice (Converse, 1987; Krosnick, 1999). However, an element that may have exacerbated the
neglect of comments in performance appraisal is the utilitarian aim of performance appraisal systems and the fact that they are
developed, ﬁrst and foremost, to support salary or bonus administration and promotion. According to a study by Bretz, Milkovich,
and Read (1989) over 90% of Fortune 100 ﬁrms use performance appraisals for administrative purposes. Under an administrative
paradigm, a performance appraisal system is used to justify organizational decision making, a task best handled by numbers. In
addition, the dependence on quantitative approaches is reinforced by legal requirements overseeing performance appraisal
(Bernardin & Beatty, 1984; Field & Holley, 1982; Malos, 1998). Taken within a broader context, the imposition of structure to
support individual decision making is ubiquitous in contemporary organizations. Heath, Larrick, and Klayman (1998) labelled
cognitive repairs those organizational practices designed to counter cognitive shortcomings of individuals and enhance human
judgement and reasoning. Like the structured selection interview (Schuler, 1993), rating-based performance appraisal processes
are designed for the purpose of measurement precision. A last, but rarely noted, element that favors the use of quantitative
appraisal systems is that they also translate into substantial economies for raters (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). The most obvious of
these economies is time or the relative efﬁciency with which evaluations can be completed. An expedient completion of

Only very recently has some research started to emerge on the role of qualitative formats in performance appraisal. This shift in
focus may be attributed to three factors. The ﬁrst one is logistical in nature and pertains to the fact that technological advances now
facilitate the processing of qualitative information. Technology has not only made it easier than ever to gather narrative comments
and do so anonymously but it has also facilitated the analysis, aggregation, and reporting of qualitative information (e.g., Geer,
1991; Jackson & Trochim, 2002; Krosnick, 1999; Mossholder, Settoon, Harris, & Armenakis, 1995; Poncheri, Lindberg, Thomson, &
Surface, 2008). For example, Christianson DeMay, Chandonnet, Rasinowich, and Fenlason (2006) used a statistical software
package to automatically code over 3000 performance-related comments according to a predetermined competency framework. A
process that would have taken weeks to do manually took these researchers only a few hours. The second factor is measurement
based. Most researchers acknowledge that there is a limit to how well performance ratings can capture individual performance; a
limit that is unlikely to be pushed back with alternative rating formats (Bretz, Milkovich, & Read, 1992; Feldman, 1986; Lance &
Woehr, 1986; Sulsky & Keown, 1998). Individual performance is deeply embedded within work contexts (Ferris, Munyon, Basik, &
Buckley, 2008; Judge & Ferris, 1993) and, because of their qualitative nature, narrative comments are better equipped than
standardized ratings to capture this context. To use Johns' (2006) journalistic analogy to depict organizational context, narrative
comments offer the what, where, when, and why of an evaluation. Interest in narrative comments follows recent calls for more
contextual approaches to organizational research (Cappelli & Scherer, 1991; Johns, 2006). The third reason for the accrued interest
in narrative comments is because they seem to alter how performance information is perceived by the receiver. As feedback
material, narrative comments do have a distinct ﬂavour than ratings. Dalessio (1998) argues that they provide additional
information that helps feedback recipients interpret the ratings. Some evidence does suggest that recipients pay more attention to
words than they do numbers. Ferstl and Bruskiewicz (2000) surveyed executives after they had received feedback reports
consisting of quantitative evaluations (i.e., averaged ratings by competency, results by item, distributions, etc.) and anonymous
narrative comments. These executives reported attending to narrative comments more than they did to the quantitative in the
feedback reports. As a result, researchers and practitioners are beginning to view narrative comments as valuable complements to
ratings in developmental processes.
3. Proposed framework
In this paper, I propose a theoretical framework, shown in Fig. 1 that outlines the main characteristics of narrative comments
found in performance appraisal. This framework describes the various processes underlying how these comments are generated by
evaluators. In turn, it also delineates the many purported consequences of narrative comments for evaluators and evaluatees.
4. Characteristics of comments
In discussing variations in feedback format it is important to tease out the format of feedback from its content. In performance
evaluation, multiple sentences can be articulated around a single performance-related theme (e.g., “Bob needs to work harder on
his people skills. He is impolite and really rubs people the wrong way”). Conversely, a single sentence can also contain multiple
themes (e.g., “Bob is very persuasive and committed but he lacks vision and the ability to deal with higher management”). Thus,
the speciﬁcation of a unit of analysis for feedback content is needed to isolate its effect from those related to format. In a
performance appraisal context, the performance referents, or themes, contained in comments represent such a unit of analysis
(Smither & Walker, 2004). In line with the principles of qualitative methodology and content analysis (e.g., Crano & Brewer, 1986),

each meaningful and distinguishable performance-related theme found in narrative comments can be considered a separate
entity, labelled narrative unit for the purpose of this paper. Each narrative unit is equivalent to that found in a well-designed
quantitative item and its accompanying rating scale. It is posed that narrative units will vary along four dimensions: valence,
performance domain coverage, speciﬁcity, and the provision of suggestion for development. In addition, the number or amount of
narrative units present in a feedback message represents another important dimension of this message, one that will be considered
in this paper. In the following sections, I describe these dimensions in detail.

4.1. Valence of narrative units
By most accounts, the valence of performance information represents its most important characteristic (e.g., Audia & Locke,
2003). Valence refers to the extent that the evaluative connotation of the information is positive or negative (Korsgaard, Meglino, &
Lester, 1997). By design, ratings convey valence through quantitative values that represent the frequency or the evaluation of a
speciﬁc behavior. Narrative units also convey valence, albeit in a less explicit and direct manner. The valence of a comment can be
expressed via adjectives (e.g., great, poor, etc.) or with sentences containing evaluative undertones such as, “She is unable to follow
the marketing plan set up last year.”

4.2. Domain coverage of narrative units
Individual performance is a multidimensional construct (e.g., Campbell, McCloy, Oppler, & Sager, 1993). Ratings are typically
designed to ensure that they capture areas of the performance domain accurately (Bretz et al., 1992). In operational terms, the task
of rating requires an evaluator to read a statement that captures some aspect of performance and to provide a judgement about a
speciﬁc target using a Likert scale (Kaiser & Kaplan, 2005). Performance items and their accompanying rating scales represent thin
slices of the performance domain. In contrast with ratings, narrative units vary in terms of the area(s) of the performance domain
covered by the comment. Evaluators may choose to concentrate on particular areas at the expense of others. It is important to
stress the fact that some degree of structure, in the form of prompts, is often provided to guide the production of narrative
comments towards speciﬁc areas of the performance domain. Kulesa and Bishop (2006) describe open-ended questions used in
surveys along a general-speciﬁc axis. General prompts simply set the stage and offer very little guidance for the respondent
(e.g., “Please comment on X's performance at work”). Speciﬁc prompts direct the evaluator to provide a narrower set of
information. As such, they can be structured to encourage the evaluator to center exclusively on the most salient performance
issues of the target or on speciﬁc performance dimensions. For example, the following prompts have been used in recent research
efforts: “Please comment on the one area that this person needs to work on” (Gillipsie et al., 2006), or “Please print any comments
that you would like to share concerning your manager's leadership skills” (Smither & Walker, 2004). However, even with such
prompts, narrative comments provide evaluators much more ﬂexibility than ratings in terms of what area of the performance
domain is covered.

4.3. Speciﬁcity of narrative units
A unique characteristic of narrative comments is the degree of precision and detail that they can offer. Comments can be very
brief and express a general evaluative message (e.g., “Bob is a poor manager”), or they can be very speciﬁc and provide much detail
as to the perceived performance of the evaluatee (e.g., “Bob is rarely willing to accept our input as to the best strategy to take. In the
weekly meetings, he never seriously considers our suggestions about budget allocation. Most of us don't even prepare our numbers
anymore”). Most research on comments have articulated the speciﬁcity of comments around their focus on behaviour, as opposed
to traits (Gillipsie et al., 2006; Smither & Walker, 2004). However, many feedback models use a more elaborate operationalization
of speciﬁcity by underscoring the need to frame the behavioral information with additional information. For example, the Center
for Creative Leadership feedback model, called ‘Situation-Behavior-Impact’ (SIB), argues that the description of the behavior
should be preceded by a description of the situation(s) in which the behavior has occurred and followed by the consequences of
this action (Kirkland & Manoogian, 1998). While research on the topic indicates that a majority of comments generated in
performance evaluation exercises tend to be quite speciﬁc, much variation has also been observed (Gillipsie et al., 2006; Rose,
Farrell, & Robinson, 2004; Smither & Walker, 2004).

4.4. Suggestions for development in narrative units
A unique characteristic of some narrative comments is that they often include suggestions for development. These prescriptive
elements of comments arise when evaluators suggest developmental strategies to the evaluatee (Kaufman & Agnew, 2006; Rose &
Robinson, 2006). In their analyses of the content of over 500 comments, Gillipsie et al. (2006) reported that nearly a third of them
contained speciﬁc recommendations for the evaluatees. These suggestions are often naturally offered by evaluators but they can
also be prompted. Kulesa and Bishop (2006) describe prompts for open-ended answers used in survey research along a prescription–description axis (e.g., “Please provide suggestions as to how this person can improve”).

4.5. Amount of narrative units
In addition to the discrete characteristics of narrative units, the number of units contained within a feedback message is an
important characteristic of this message. Because narrative comments are essentially unbound, evaluators can theoretically generate
as many or as few comments as they want. Also, when multiple evaluators are involved in assessing a single target, the number of
comments contained in the feedback message is bound to increase. Moore-Miller, O'Farrell, and Christianson DeMay (2006) found that
evaluators provided an average of 3 narrative units per evaluations. In a multisource feedback context, Smither and Walker (2004)
reported that supervisors received an average of 7 narrative units from their subordinates. Based on the standard deviation reported by
these researchers, over 30% of supervisors received at least 13 units of narrative feedback in a single evaluation.
5. Narrative comments from the perspective of the evaluator: two different languages
Narrative comments represent a much more expansive format of appraisal than ratings. In addition to conveying valence, they
provide an almost inﬁnite range of options to evaluators in formulating their message. In many ways, performance ratings, with their
very tight structure, and narrative comments, with their inherent expansiveness, represent two very different language forms. Like
languages, each format provides a unique system of symbols and rules used to communicate ideas, opinions, and experiences to others.
The work of Daft and Wiginton (1979) on the use of language in organizations proposes a continuum of language forms that ranges
from ambiguous or “high variety languages” (e.g., music, painting) to speciﬁc or “low variety languages” (e.g., mathematics). High
variety languages are equipped to communicate a broader range of ideas and emotions, while low variety languages express a more
narrow range of ideas but convey them with greater precision. On this continuum of language variety, Daft and Wiginton (1979) place
verbal expression, the type of language tapped by narrative comments, higher than ratings and Likert scales.
Because qualitative and quantitative formats typically coexist in performance appraisal, evaluators are asked to speak both
languages. After providing a series of ratings related to a target's performance, they are subsequently given the opportunity to
provide comments to justify or clarify their quantitative assessment. Thus, as an evaluator shifts from yielding ratings to producing
comments, there is a loosening of the structure imposed on their evaluations. In the next few sections, the manner by which the
production of narrative comments unfolds for the evaluator is outlined. More speciﬁcally, I address the cognitive, motivational, and
normative underpinnings of this process.
5.1. Cognitive processes in performance appraisal
The most recent advances in performance appraisal research have been attributed to the social cognitive perspective (DeNisi &
Williams, 1988a,b; Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). The manner by which information is retrieved and integrated to form an overall
judgement is particularly relevant to our discussion as these processes coincide with the introduction of format in the evaluation
process. Research in social cognition has established that two separate cognitive processes come into play when individuals tap
into their memory and integrate information to evaluate someone (Hastie & Park, 1986). Online processing occurs when
performance information is acquired and integrated to form an overall impression of a target. Evaluators can rely on this
continuously updated and readily available impression to evaluate the target. Memory-based processing, on the other hand, takes
place when evaluators have to draw on past episodes to render their evaluation (Bargh & Thein, 1985; Hastie & Park, 1986; Srull &
Wyer, 1989). This process is less efﬁcient and accurate than online processing as it relies heavily on long-term memory and the
evaluation of idiosyncratic episodes about the target (DeNisi, Robbins, & Cafferty, 1989; DeNisi & Peters, 1996; DeNisi & Williams,
1988a,b). The next section will explore how these cognitive processes play out in the production of comments.
5.1.1. Inﬂuence of cognitive processes on narrative units
The evaluation format will have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the cognitive processes underlying the evaluation process. The use of
ratings is analogous to a matching task, one for which the evaluator is asked to ﬁnd an appropriate equivalent for his or her
valuation of the target's performance (i.e., the rating level of a performance item). Borman (1991) portrayed ratings as mechanisms
that help evaluators conduct “an organized and efﬁcient search for rate performance-related behavior” (p. 289). According to
Borman, this format allows the efﬁcient and effective processing of performance information by offering a clear presentation of
standards. In their theory of ratings, Wherry and Bartlett (1982) call attention to the function of the ‘rating stimulus’ in facilitating
recall and centering raters' attention on desired behaviors. In contrast, the structure provided by narrative comments is much less
directive and provides little guidance to the evaluator.
Another characteristic of comments that hinges on cognition is the need to communicate one's evaluation in writing. Research
in cognitive psychology has shown that writing is among the most demanding of cognitive activities (e.g., Fitzer, 2003; Kellog,
1994). The most widely cited and inﬂuential model of writing, by Hayes and Flower (1980), describes writing as a dynamic and
complex process that draws from deep-seated knowledge. Putting one's thought into words is a difﬁcult task, one that requires
precision and speciﬁc information. Finally, the opportunity to provide suggestions for development also increases the cognitive
demands of evaluators, albeit one of a different sort. This element is not evaluative in nature. Rather, it requires a problem-solving
approach to performance: evaluators are looking for a solution to the performance weaknesses diagnosed. It requires asking
oneself, “What could this individual do to improve?” In sum, it posed that the demands related to the lack of directive prompts, the
need to convey the evaluation in writing, and the possibility of providing suggestions for improvement will enhance the role
played by memory-based processing in the production of narrative comments.

Proposition 1. In comparison with ratings, the production of narrative comments will require more cognitive effort.
5.1.2. Cognitive implications of combining words and comments
As stated in the Introduction, most performance appraisal systems are based on hybrid systems that use a combination of
ratings and narrative comments. When used together, a certain consistency is expected between the valence of the information
conveyed via the two formats. A preliminary phase of the writing process consists of planning what one wants to communicate
(Hayes & Flower, 1980). When faced with the task of writing about others, an evaluator will consider the ratings that were made
and will produce narrative comments that have a certain level of consistency with ratings. Empirical research on narrative
comments provides some evidence of this uniformity. Smither and Walker (2004) analyzed the content of over 1000 comments
yielded as part of an upward feedback process for bank managers and found the valence of these comments, coded by two
independent experts, was signiﬁcantly related with averaged performance ratings.
Proposition 2. There will be congruence between the valence of ratings and that of comments.
5.2. Motivational processes in performance appraisal
While the cognitive factors underlying the use of comments are very important, an exploration of feedback formats would be
remiss not to address the volitional component aspect of performance appraisal. There is a very strong motivational component to
performance evaluations and much research has explored the underlying factors and consequences of rater motivation (Harris,
1994; Longnecker, Sims, & Gioia, 1987). According to Murphy and Cleveland (1995): “Raters do not fail to yield accurate ratings
because they are incapable of accuracy but rather because they are unwilling to rate accurately” (p. 265). The prospect of having to
deal with criticism, ill-feelings, and other social costs related to the evaluatees' reactions can be quite uncomfortable for evaluators
(Smith, Harrington, & Houghton, 2000). These costs led Pearce and Porter (1986) to describe performance appraisal as “one of the
most emotionally charged activities in business life” (p. 212). Discomfort with evaluations has been found to lead to a great
reluctance to engage in these duties (Bernardin & Villanova, 2005; Harris, 1994; Villanova, Bernardin, Dahmus, & Sims, 1993). In
this section, I look at the inﬂuence of motivation on the production of narrative comments.
5.2.1. Inﬂuence of motivational processes on narrative units
The motivation of evaluators is likely to have a great inﬂuence on their production of comments. First, volitional elements will
have a direct inﬂuence on the number of comments generated. Motivated evaluators will write more […]. Secondly, it is also posed
that volitional elements will inﬂuence the characteristics of the comments. As stated in the previous section, evaluating others is a
difﬁcult task and, with ratings, the only option available to those who are less motivated is to adjust rating levels, at or above those
which were originally intended (Jawahar, & Williams, 1997; Rynes, Colbert, & Brown, 2002). The production of comments, in
contrast, allows for a much broader array of adjustments. A simple one is to provide comments that lack speciﬁcity. The scant
research on the speciﬁcity of comments in performance appraisal has uncovered substantial variation in the speciﬁcity of
comments generated by evaluators (Gillipsie et al., 2006; Smither & Walker, 2004). Research on feedback has shown that, in an
attempt to make the feedback more acceptable to recipients, managers often deliver negative feedback in more ambiguous terms
(Baron, 1993; Fisher, 1979). Another strategy that could raise the comfort of evaluators would be to provide suggestions for
improvement. These suggestions could be seen as attempts to help the evaluators and have the effect of counterbalancing the
negative evaluations.
Proposition 3. Motivated evaluators will generate a greater amount of comments as well as comments that are more speciﬁc and
contain more suggestions for development than evaluators who are less motivated.
5.2.2. Motivational implications of combining words and comments
In the section on cognition, it was proposed that most evaluators would seek to use the two formats in a fairly consistent
manner. However, when considering motivational effects, evaluators may willingly introduce divergence between the information
found in the two formats. Discrepancies between ratings and comments may arise when the two formats serve different purposes.
In many situations ratings are fed into administrative decisions while narrative comments are used for developmental purposes.
Evaluators are quite sensitive as to the purpose of evaluations (Jawahar & Williams, 1997) and they may use comments to ‘speak’ to
the evaluatee while using comments to ‘speak’ to decision makers. In these situations, comments may be used to mitigate and
nuance the feedback message, especially when ratings are negative.
Proposition 4. The congruence between comments and ratings will be inﬂuenced by the purpose of the appraisal whereas less
congruence will be present when the evaluation is used for decision-making purposes.

The idea that organizations can be characterized by their feedback-giving norms was initially introduced by Herold and Parsons

and Kacmar (2001) conﬁrmed the fact that feedback environments could be used to differentiate between organizations and, since

then, many researchers have pursued this line of research (Levy & Williams, 2004; London & Smither, 2002). The feedback norms
present in organizations probably exert a powerful inﬂuence on the amount and type of narrative comments generated by
evaluators. Indeed, one simple reason for which supervisors give feedback is that they simply consider it part of their job and are
expected to do so (Larson, 1984). Norms related to informal feedback-seeking and feedback-sending behaviours are also likely to
inﬂuence the use of narrative comments. Informal feedback represents a useful mechanism for individual self-regulation (Ashford
& Cummings, 1983; Fedor, Rensvold, & Adams, 1992; Miller & Jablin, 1991) and evaluators operating in organizations where
feedback-seeking and feedback-sending behaviours are common will probably be more inclined to yield comments.
Proposition 5. The main characteristics of narrative comments (i.e., valence, domain speciﬁcity, and suggestion for development) will
be inﬂuenced by the norms of the organization.
5.4. Consequences of writing narrative comments: the case of the motivated evaluator
Thus far, the focus of the paper has been exclusively on the factors underlying the production of narrative comments. I now turn
to the consequences, for the provider of these comments, of engaging in this task. As described previously, writing comments is an
involved task and it is likely to have consequences for the evaluator, especially for the motivated one. At the low end of the
motivational spectrum, providing comments or ratings is unlikely to have much of an impact on the evaluator because the
apprehensive or indifferent evaluator will only engage in the process superﬁcially. For the motivated evaluator, however, writing
about other's performance can have implications for other feedback-related behaviors. Steelman, Levy, and Snell (2004)
emphasized the role of “the contextual aspects of day-to-day supervisor–subordinate and coworker–coworker feedback processes
rather than the formal appraisal session” (p. 166). This broader perspective on the feedback process recognizes that formal
performance evaluations represent only a single point of contact between an evaluator and an evaluatee. The formal message
delivered by the ratings or narrative comments is part of an ongoing and informal exchange that many argue is more important
than the formal feedback episode itself (Ashford & Cummings, 1983; Fedor, 1991; Herold & Parsons, 1985; London, 1997).
An interesting corollary of the formulation of narrative comments is that generating comments may inﬂuence the subsequent
behaviors of evaluators. For example, research on eye witness testimony has found that the quality of testimonies improve when
witnesses are given the opportunity to put down in writing their accounts of the event (Magner, Markham, & Barnett, 1996).
Similarly, in performance appraisal, writing comments about someone's performance is likely to inﬂuence how one gives feedback
in other settings. Smither and Walker (2004) point out that “narrative comments are likely to share some similarities with verbal,
face-to-face feedback” (p. 580). In their work on diary keeping, DeNisi and Peters (1996) also alluded to the fact that an enhanced
appraisal process could have a positive impact on day-to-day feedback behaviors. Research in cognitive psychology has long
recognized the strong relationship between writing and speech. Writing enables one to structure one's thoughts and provides a
conceptual model for speech (Olson, 1996). An evaluator who has thought through the formulation of written comments for a coworker is probably better able to follow-up and to provide feedback in person to that individual. The relevance of this spillover
effect is not negligible as the one aspect of performance appraisal that brings the most discomfort to evaluators is the ensuing faceto-face discussion with the evaluatee (Barnes-Farrell, 2001; Bernardin & Villanova, 2005; Larson, 1984). Naturally, the advantage of
such a transfer is contingent upon the appropriateness of the original behavior. It will be positive only if the evaluator generates
narrative comments of quality. Under these conditions, it is posed that the experience of generating such narrative comments leads
to better quality face-to-face feedback exchanges.
Proposition 6. The generation of narrative comments will be related to comfort with subsequent in-person feedback-giving situations.
6. Narrative comments from the perspective of the evaluatee
In this section, I look at the implications of using comments for the evaluatee or the receiver of the information. Most of the
literature on how evaluatees respond to performance information rests on the perception of performance appraisal systems as
resource allocation mechanisms (i.e., for administrative use) and, more speciﬁcally, on perceptions of their accuracy, justice, and
fairness (Gilliland & Langdon, 1998; Keeping & Levy, 2000; Smither 1998). In a developmental context, however, acceptability,
rather than accuracy, may well be the most important criterion. In these situations, the process aims to develop self-awareness and
persuade individuals to increase their effort, change the direction of their effort, persist in trying to reach their goal, increase their
knowledge or skills, or some combination of these (Kanfer, 1990). The present section focuses on the manner by which narrative
comments play out in the eye of the feedback receiver.
Although the importance of feedback format for recipients has not received much attention in organizational sciences, it has
been a topic of interest in other areas of social science. Cognitive psychologists and linguists have long established the complex
interplay that exists between the function of words and the function of numbers in communication (e.g., Chomsky, 1986). In
applied cognitive psychology, for example, research has contrasted the effects of communicating information with natural
language as opposed to quantitative formats. The spheres within which this question has been addressed are varied, ranging from
the deﬁnition of standards of proof for juries (Kagehiro, 1990), to the labelling of additives in food products (Wogalter, Kalsher, &
Litynski, 1996), and the description of house attributes in real-estate transactions (Lindberg & Gorling, 1991). This body of research
yielded equivocal results. Some researchers claim that there is no substitute for the precision of numbers while others argue that
natural language is more comprehensible and thus, more useful (Moxey & Sanford, 2000; Newstead, Pollard, & Riezebos, 1987;

S. Brutus / Human Resource Management Review 20 (2010) 144–157

151

Sanford, Fay, Stewart, & Moxley, 2002). A similar debate is also found in educational psychology where the application of grades as
a comparative tool (an administrative use) is often at odds with their use as an educational, formative instrument (Butler, 1987;
Butler & Nisan, 1986). On one hand, grading systems are used to assess the learning achieved by pupils and, thus, requires
precision. On the other hand, the information conveyed needs to be straightforward enough to be understood by the students (and
their parents) to help them monitor and evaluate their learning (Cureton, 1971). In sum, the tension between precision in
assessment and communication proﬁciency is certainly not unique to the performance appraisal area.
6.1. Understanding two different languages
Regardless of how it is communicated, feedback information is personal and meaningful for recipients. Anecdotal accounts,
however, indicate that narrative comments elicit especially strong reactions from recipients (e.g., Dalessio,1998). Ferstl and Bruskiewicz
(2000) found that, when provided with both quantitative and qualitative feedback, evaluatees pay more attention to the latter. In his
seminal study on managers, Mintzberg (1973) argued that, although most have access to a variety of information, they often prefer more
natural means of communications. In research on organizational survey reports, evidence also suggests that managers are more
interested in employee comments than in quantitative results (Patton, 2002). Indeed, there is something powerful about reading text
that has been written about you. In the following paragraphs, I describe the implications of receiving feedback via these two formats.
Media richness theory is used as a theoretical framework to explain differences in recipients' reactions to format variations.
The leading theory of communication in organizations, media richness theory (Daft & Lengel, 1984), provides some grounding
for the issue of feedback format variations and its impact on recipients. This theory has been used in recruiting research, for
example, to explain organizational image formation (Allen, VanScooter, & Otondo, 2004; Cable & Yu, 2006) and in strategic
management to describe knowledge transfer across organizations (Gupta & Govindarajan, 2000; Mohr & Spekman, 1994).
According to the theory, media richness is deﬁned, in part, by the extent to which a medium conveys a sense of personal focus
(Russ, Daft, & Lengel, 1990). Narrative comments have personal intent, they are about you and written for you. This characteristic is
not found with ratings that are generic in nature and designed to describe broad behavioral episodes. An evaluatee who receives
the information is aware that the feedback has been written speciﬁcally for him or her and this makes narrative comments much
richer, as a medium, than ratings.
Daft and Lengel (1984) inform us that the appropriateness of communication media is contingent on the routineness of the
information to convey and that lean media are most effective for routine messages because these are accompanied with
established frames of reference. As a result, their interpretation is simple, rational, and high in objectivity. Conversely, rich media
are better adapted to the communication of nonroutine events (Daft & Lengel, 1986). According to Lengel and Daft (1988),
“nonroutine communications have greater potential for misunderstanding, and are often characterized by time pressure,
ambiguity and surprise […] personal feelings and subjective beliefs may inﬂuence message interpretation” (p. 226). Because
performance feedback is so sensitive and such a scarce resource in organizations (Ashford, Blatt, & VandeWalle, 2003), theory
would suggest that rich media are better for communicating it. In marketing research, much research focuses on the best way to
use numbers for pricing purpose (e.g., Boyes, Lynch, & Mounts, 2007; Gijsbrechts, 1993). In the management literature, there is
substantial anecdotal evidence that hints at the preference of words over number in feedback messages (Antonioni, 1996;
Waldman, Atwater, & Antonioni, 1998). Atwater and Brett (2006) recently offered empirical support for notion that feedback
recipients react more favourably to comments than ratings.
Proposition 7. Narrative comments convey more personal focus than ratings and, as a result, will lead to stronger reactions from
recipients.
6.2. The inﬂuence of format on feedback recipients
According to feedback intervention theory (FIT), feedback interventions command and receive substantial attention from
recipients and, as a result, variations in feedback cues have signiﬁcant consequences for the behavioral responses of recipients. The
elaboration likelihood model of persuasion (Petty & Cacioppo, 1986) also posits that, for issues that are deemed personally
relevant, individuals engage in very elaborate and conscious processing. In these situations, the content of the message is carefully
scrutinized, and persuasion only occurs with arguments of quality. In formal feedback situation, elaboration likelihood is likely to
be high and recipients will actively scrutinize the message qualities and its relevance for them (Audia & Locke, 2003). In this
section, I describe how the basic characteristics of narrative comments impact the subsequent behavior of recipients.
Without a doubt, the effects of negative feedback on behavior are more complex than those of positive feedback because of the
consequences that it has for the recipient's self-concept. Negative feedback triggers defence mechanisms (Baron, 1993) and is often
accompanied with affective reactions that distort the self-regulatory process and effective behavioral adjustments (Kluger &
DeNisi, 1996). As with ratings, the valence of comments will be important for evaluatees. However, because comments also vary in
speciﬁcity, they will inﬂuence the self-regulatory process in distinct fashion. The effectiveness of feedback interventions is
optimized when feedback is precise. A narrative unit that is discriminating in terms of the behaviors, the tasks, the context in
question, or any of their combinations (e.g., “He disciplined Joe last month when he arrived late. It was about time”) is more likely
to enable the self-regulatory process than a vague statement. A speciﬁc feedback message feeds into the goal-setting process by
enabling the deﬁnition of objectives (Klein, Whitener, & Ilgen, 1990, Mento, Steel, & Karren, 1987; Tubbs, 1986). The scant research
on the impact of the speciﬁcity of narrative comments on subsequent behavior has yielded mixed results. Moore-Miller et al.

(2006) did uncover a positive relationship between the speciﬁcity of comments provided by supervisors and subsequent
performance improvements. Smither and Walker (2004), on the other hand, did not ﬁnd a direct effect for the speciﬁcity of
comments generated by subordinates (in terms of task or behaviour) on subsequent performance.
Proposition 8. The positive inﬂuence of narrative units on subsequent behavior will be moderated by their level of speciﬁcity such that
their inﬂuence will be greater when speciﬁc.
In addition to conveying valence, some research also indicates that feedback format can have a normative connotation.
Feedback that evokes comparisons with others directs attention to the self and prevents the appropriate use of the feedback
(Kluger & DeNisi, 1996). Smither, Wohlers, and London (1995) found that those who receive normative feedback perceived it as
less useful, are less willing to discuss it with others, and are less satisﬁed with the feedback process than those who receive
individualized feedback. Numbers allow easy comparisons with others and are often systematically included in the feedback via
the use of normative data (e.g., standard deviation, percentile, etc). As a result, quantitative feedback, even when devoid of explicit
normative data, has been found to evoke comparison with others. This association can even be found in young children. Research
conducted in educational psychology found that grades drew pupils' attention to social comparisons (Nicholls, 1979, 1983). In an
empirical demonstration of this effect in the classroom, Butler & Nisan (1986) and Butler (1987) found that numerical grading
systems, in contrast to narrative comments, did invoke an ego-involved focus in ﬁfth and sixth graders that led to a decline in
performance and in interest in the task. Recently, Atwater and Brett (2006) tested this effect in an experimental ﬁeld study. While
their investigation failed to support the hypotheses that narrative feedback leads to more positive reactions and behavioral change
than quantitative feedback, it represents a ﬁrst step in uncovering the normative connotations of feedback format.
Proposition 9. Narrative units, in general, will be perceived as less normative and will lead to less social comparison than ratings.
An additional characteristic of comments that will inﬂuence behavioural change is the provision of suggestions. Performance
improvement as a result of feedback is contingent upon the appropriateness of the recipient's response and feedback information
translating into appropriate developmental strategies (Ilgen, Fisher, & Taylor,1979). It is one thing to be told (and to accept) that you are a
poor leader, it is quite another to take the appropriate steps to enhance this aspect of your performance. Wood and Locke (1990) make the
distinction between working harder and working smarter, as a result of feedback. In the presence of prescriptive information, recipients
are more likely to adjust their strategies accordingly and work smarter (Audia & Locke, 2003). At the very least, these suggestions inform
the evaluatee as to possible developmental opportunities. Burke and Gaylord (2005) reported that 44% of managers participating in a
multisource feedback program used the information derived from comments in drafting their individual development plans.
Proposition 10. Comments that include prescriptive information will have a greater impact on behavior change than simple comments
that are only descriptive.
6.3. An ecological perspective of narrative comments
Although the inﬂuence of discrete narrative units on recipients allows for a better understanding of the inﬂuence of format on
feedback process, such an approach lacks ecological validity. In actual performance appraisal situations, narrative comments are
probably not composed of single units but of multiple ones. These units are likely to vary in characteristics (i.e., valence, domain
coverage, speciﬁcity, and suggestions for development), especially when the evaluations of many evaluators are solicited. Also, as
stated earlier, comments typically accompany ratings in the evaluation process. The combination of the two formats in a feedback
message is very likely to have an inﬂuence on the interpretation of comments. In the following section, I describe how variation
between multiple comments and how the combination of comments with ratings may inﬂuence the recipient.
6.3.1. Feedback messages containing multiple narrative units
As stated in the Introduction, most feedback messages contain more than one narrative unit. Smither and Walker (2004)
reported that supervisors received an average of 7 narrative units from their subordinates and close to a third of them receiving at
least 13 units. While the quantity of comments received by an evaluatee is contingent on a variety of factors, it is safe to assume that
the typical feedback message received by an evaluatee is composed of (a) multiple comments (i.e., narrative units) of (b) varying
characteristics (i.e., valence, domain characteristic, speciﬁcity, and suggestion for development). Because comments cannot easily
be aggregated and need to be conveyed serially, disagreement and conﬂict across the various comments will be quite salient for the
recipient. Thus, the accumulation of multiple narrative units within a feedback episode will considerably raise the complexity of
the message. This complexity will be exacerbated in the case of multiple evaluators for which a fair degree of contradiction is often
present (Atwater & Yammarino, 1997; Atwater, Ostroff, Yammarino, & Fleenor, 1998). These contradictions can take various forms.
For example, narrative units targeting the same behavioral domain can be opposite in terms of valence. Also, various units can put
the emphasis on different domains. In these situations, narrative comments will present a greater amount of less organized
information for the recipient to process. Many authors have suggested that incoherent arguments generate negative affect on the
part of evaluatees, which spills over into the evaluation of the information, decreasing the likelihood of persuasion (Chaiken &
Eagly, 1976, 1983;Eagly, 1974). Ilgen et al. (1979) argued that feedback, if too abundant, may have detrimental effects on an
individual. In a study on call center employees, Salvati, Gosselin, Morin, and Morin (2004) found that feedback overload led to

S. Brutus / Human Resource Management Review 20 (2010) 144–157

153

increased stress level and a reduced desire to respond to the feedback. Thus, to the extent that this information is convergent, the
message will be understood and internalized better. However, this effect will decrease as the feedback becomes more divergent.
Proposition 11. The relation of narrative comments on behavior change on a particular domain will be moderated by the variability in
the characteristics of comments such that a weaker relationship will be observed when variability is high.
6.3.2. Feedback messages containing comments and ratings
Another form of variation that recipients will need to resolve is the one found across formats. A dual-format feedback message
combines both precision and richness. Research in decision making, and more speciﬁcally on probability expression, indicates that
there is great variability in the perceived values assigned to words as opposed to numbers (Budescu, Weinberg, & Wallsten, 1988).
Ratings convey a level of precision that will often be at odds with the message conveyed by narrative comments and this, even
when a discrepancy was not intended by the evaluator. In reconciling this discrepancy, the richness of comments is likely to
overshadow that of ratings (see Proposition 7) resulting in more attention being given to words. However, the impact of words
may be mitigated by various factors such as the convergence of the comments and the normative information provided with
ratings (i.e., average of multiple evaluators). Moreover, both formats are inherently confounded by the purpose of the appraisal. As
stated in the Introduction, ratings typically feed into administrative decisions while comments are mostly used for a
developmental use. When administrative decisions are attached to the feedback, the focus of recipients will be drawn toward
ratings because it is this information that will most matter for them.
Proposition 12. The inﬂuence of comments on the reactions of recipients will be moderated by the purpose of the evaluation such that
stronger reactions will arise from ratings when it is used for decision making.
6.4. Reciprocating the feedback received
As stated before, performance evaluation can be construed as an ongoing communication between the evaluator and the
evaluatee and the formal evaluation (via ratings or comments) is but one episode of this continuous exchange. In communication
theory, the principle of reciprocity states that, in relationships where it is in the interest of each to establish good relationships,
individuals will reciprocate each others' communication behaviors (McCrosky & Richmond, 2000). This effect has been found in
speech behavior (Giles, Mulac, Bradac, & Johnson, 1987). Social exchange theory posits that individuals give beneﬁts to others in
expectation of receiving beneﬁts back from them (Blau, 1964). A reciprocation effect could be at play with the use of narrative
comments. In this instance, the work put into the generation of comments is accompanied with expectations that the recipient will
take the feedback seriously and will respond accordingly. Conversely, evaluatees that receive lengthy narrative evaluations may
recognize the efforts deployed by the evaluators and may be more inclined to respond in behavioral terms, either by instigating
behavior changes or engaging in a discussion around the feedback. This reciprocating effect is likely to be moderated by the
characteristics of the comments in that a desire for further discussion by the part of the evaluatee is likely to ensue when the
feedback is negative, vague and not accompanied with suggestions. These characteristics increase uncertainty, a key factor for a
proactive search for feedback (Ashford & Tsui, 1991; Fedor et al., 1992).
Proposition 13. The relationship between the amount of narrative feedback received and the desire to engage in subsequent discussions
with the feedback provider will be moderated by valence, speciﬁcity, and the provision of suggestions such that more discussion will
ensue when the feedback is negative, less speciﬁc, and devoid of suggestions.
7. Discussion
In this paper the argument was made that extending the format of evaluations along a qualitative axis literally transforms the role of
the evaluator and that of the feedback recipient. The shift from numbers to words has multiple effects on the process, some clearly
beneﬁcial, such as an increase in the involvement of the evaluator and a richer feedback message for the evaluatee. On the other hand, it
also increases the demands for users. For a feedback provider, producing narrative comments differs drastically from using a rating
instrument. One can think of an evaluator as an artist: ratings represent painting-by-numbers while narrative comments offer a white
canvas. As such, most of the differences between the two formats hinge upon the increased freedom bestowed upon the evaluator.
Taking the recipient's perspective, reading comments about oneself also differs substantially from interpreting quantitative results. The
feedback message becomes much more personal as it is tailored to the evaluatee. In the spirit of stimulating research on this topic, a
series of speciﬁc research propositions using various theoretical and empirical elements were proposed. However, as stated by an
anonymous reviewer, the list of potential issues raised by performance evaluation format is virtually endless and the various links
proposed in this paper represent only the tip of the iceberg. In the following few paragraphs, I offer two broad and exploratory research
directions that could further enhance the understanding of feedback format effects.
7.1. What is the current state of practice in the use of comments in organizations and how does it map onto research?
This paper is based on the very fundamental assumption that narrative comments play an important role in organizations'
evaluation practices. While I am conﬁdent that few would dispute the fact that comments are commonly used, a clearer picture of

154

S. Brutus / Human Resource Management Review 20 (2010) 144–157

exactly how they are relied upon is needed. A thorough survey of the many structural alternatives that are currently used in
organizations would help in moving a research agenda on narrative comments forward. For example, the order effects outlined in
this paper assumed that ratings precede narrative comments. There may be interesting implications in inverting this order. Conger
and Toegel (2003) proposed that comments should be used before ratings in order to counter fatigue effects and decrease the
priming that occurs from using rating ﬁrst. There is also some evidence that having evaluators write comments ﬁrst enables the
production of ratings. DeNisi et al. have found positive inﬂuences of writing a diary on the processing of performance information
and ratings (DeNisi et el., 1989; DeNisi & Peters, 1996; Varma, DeNisi, & Peters, 1996). Schmitt and Kunce (2002) also demonstrated
that having respondents elaborate in writing on their answers to multiple-choice items positively inﬂuenced the quality of these
answers.
Research is also needed to validate some of the common prescriptions related to feedback-giving. For example, some literature
on feedback-giving argues that a valuable feedback message also requires contextual information and an explanation of the
outcomes of behaviour (e.g., situation-behavior-impact; Kirkland & Manoogian, 1998). At this stage, the distinct contribution of the
contextual framing of the behaviour and of its outcome has not been validated empirically. Research is needed on how to best
structure the feedback message to improve the quality of these narratives.
The close link between the management of comments and technological advances is another reason for gaining a better
understanding of current practice. Technology constantly expands the capacity of information management systems. As noted by
an anonymous reviewer, text analysis software can been used to uncover common themes across comments. The scant research
that was conducted on this procedure has already shown promise (Christianson DeMay et al., 2006). Interestingly, this form of
aggregation alters the essential nature of narrative comments and, in fact, makes them espouse certain characteristics of ratings
(e.g., less personal). The implications of these permutations for recipients need to be explored further.
7.2. What are some of the determinants that underlie the production and acceptance of evaluative comments?
As a result of their heightened cognitive and volitional components, a wealth of individual determinants probably drives how
comments are generated and also perceived and/or accepted. The most obvious one is the ability to write (for the evaluator) and
read (for the evaluatee). Writing ability rests on a vast and complex set of hierarchical knowledge (Hayes & Flower, 1980) and a
good writer will possess an undeniable advantage when engaging in this task. Speciﬁcally, a good grasp of vocabulary, the ability to
use a wide range of words in writing and syntax, and the ability to structure meaning in a complex manner within sentences will
enable writers in communicating their evaluations. Similarly, the appropriate literacy levels would also enable the proper
interpretation of the nuances of the written word. Secondly, personality traits are likely to underlie one's motivation to write about
others. Such traits have been found to play in a role in ratings. For example, conscientiousness, a personality trait that has been
linked to certain characteristics of ratings (Bernardin, Cooke, Ross & Villanova, 2000) is likely to also inﬂuence the generation of
comments. On a related point, the provision of written comments aimed at fostering someone else's development can easily be
construed as a prosocial behavior. The many individual and organizational determinants of organizational citizenship behaviours
may well predict the extent to which evaluators engage fully in the task (e.g., Kidder & McLean Parks, 2001). On the receiving end,
one could assume that personality characteristics, such as tolerance for ambiguity, already found to be related to the reception and
acceptance of feedback in general (e.g., Bennett, Herold, & Ashford, 1990; Herold & Fedor, 1998) would also be linked to the
reception of narrative comments. A better understanding of these determinants is important to optimize the use of narrative
comments in practice.
8. Conclusion
For over two decades, researchers have called for a moratorium on format research in performance appraisal. It is a widely held
belief that greater advances in performance appraisal research lies in understanding the process underlying evaluations rather
than in their format. The present paper argues that format, deﬁned along a qualitative–quantitative axis, probably matters a great
deal because it is so intimately linked fundamental elements of the evaluation and communication of performance. Narrative
evaluations remove much of the structure found in traditional evaluation systems and profoundly alter the way performance
information is collected and presented to recipients. As evidenced by the numerous questions raised in this paper, research on
narrative comments needs to catch up with the signiﬁcant role that they play in organizational practice.
