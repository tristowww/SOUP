- Summary: This article explores the influence of managers\' implicit person theory (IPT) on the procedural justice of performance appraisals. - Summary: The article addresses halo error in performance ratings, where the overall impression of an employee influences the assessment of specific traits or behaviors. The authors offer an in-depth analysis of this bias and its implications in the evaluation process.

Implicit person theory (IPT) refers to an individual\'s beliefs about the malleability of personal attributes, including intelligence, personality traits, and abilities. It underscores the deep-seated assumptions people hold about whether human characteristics are fixed or changeable. This concept is especially pivotal in the contexts of interpersonal evaluations, judgments, and behaviors, including in educational and organizational settings.

There are generally two types of IPTs:

**1. Entity Theory:**

- **Belief:** Personal attributes, such as intelligence and personality, are fixed and unchangeable.

- **Impact:** Individuals with this belief are more likely to make stable trait inferences about others, leading to stereotyping and quick judgments that are resistant to change.

**2. Incremental Theory:**

- **Belief:** Personal attributes are malleable and can be developed or changed over time with effort, learning, and experience.

- **Impact:** Individuals holding this belief are more open to changing their evaluations of others and are less likely to rely on stereotyping.

**Implications in Performance Appraisal:**

In the Context of Heslin & VandeWalle (2011):

- **Manager\'s IPT:** The IPT of managers can influence their judgment and evaluation during performance appraisals. Managers with an entity theory might be rigid in their evaluations, while those with an incremental theory may be more flexible and considerate of employees' potential for growth and development.

In Practice:

- **Bias & Fairness:** Managers with a strong entity theory may make judgments that are more resistant to change, potentially leading to biases. On the contrary, managers with an incremental theory might be more amenable to updating their evaluations based on new information, fostering a perception of fairness.

- **Development & Feedback:** The IPT also affects how managers deliver feedback and support employee development. Those with an incremental theory are likely to provide constructive feedback aimed at development, while entity theorists might focus on evaluative feedback highlighting fixed traits.

- **Employee Reactions:** Employees\' IPTs can also influence how they receive feedback and their motivation to improve. Incremental theorists are more likely to embrace feedback as an opportunity for growth, while entity theorists might view feedback as a judgment of their fixed abilities.

Implicit person theory, therefore, is a critical aspect to consider in understanding, evaluating, and enhancing the processes and outcomes of performance appraisal and employee development in organizational settings. It shapes the dynamics of feedback, evaluation, development, and the overall effectiveness of performance management systems.
