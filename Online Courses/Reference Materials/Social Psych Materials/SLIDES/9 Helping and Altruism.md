## Types of Prosocial Behavior {#slide-4}

-   Prosocial behavior  is a broad construct that includes any act that helps others regardless of motives.

<!-- -->

-  i.e., Altruism always involves prosocial behavior, but not all prosocial behavior is altruistic.

- Altruism  - performing a behavior that is designed to increase another person's welfare, and particularly those actions that do not seem to provide a direct reward to the person performing them.

- Egoism  - performing a prosocial act primarily out of self-interest or to avoid punishment (e.g., for recognition, money, reputation, etc.)

<!-- -->

-  When social psychologists examine prosocial behavior, it is often focused on the behavioral outcome (someone was helped)

## Evolutionary Explanations for Prosocial Behaviors {#slide-34}

- Evolutionary psychology explores how prosocial tendencies could have been naturally selected for by increasing one's likelihood of having their genes with those tendencies passed on

- There have been three evolutionary explanations offered for altruism:

<!-- -->

- Kin Selection : Favoring those who have your genes

- Reciprocity : Favoring non-kin by rules of reciprocity

- Social Rewards/Reputation:  Providing social benefits

34

![](ppt/media/image21.png "Google Shape;493;p61")

## Kin Selection {#slide-35}

- Evolutionary explanations suggest that helping may be passed on genetically 

<!-- -->

- Innate tendencies of helping will be those that benefit survival of one's genes (and thereby passing along that innate helping tendency)

- One demonstration of this principle is  "kin selection" : natural selection of people who have a tendency to help genetically related others because that tendency was carried to subsequent generations

- When we observe present-day animals behaving altruistically toward kin we are observing animals that are executing---mechanically---programs for altruism toward animals who are related

## Studying Reciprocity  {#slide-39}

- Prisoner's dilemma:  A common context for interaction between two people is asking those people to decide whether to "cooperate" or "not cooperate"
- 

<!-- -->

- If both cooperate: Good outcome for both
- If Person A cooperates but not Person B: Person A is exploited, and Person B does well at no cost
- If Person A does not cooperates but Person B does: Person A is does well at no cost, and Person B is exploited best because you do minimal effort but get some reward)
- If both Person A and Person B choose not to cooperate, outcome is worse than both cooperating, but better than being exploited

## Studying Reciprocity {#slide-40}

- In the prisoner's dilemma context

- "Not cooperating" is the best strategy for a single turn

- However, the best long-term strategy for maximizing one's outcomes is a strategy of reciprocity

- When this context is played in real-life, people tend to be cooperative, even when playing for a single round

- Why are people cooperative, when the best outcome for them is to not cooperate?

- Research finds that our cooperative nature may come from the best long-term strategy in this context

40

Even with total strangers and when the optimal immediate monetary strategy is to not cooperate, people are still generally cooperative

## Reciprocity {#slide-43}

-   Competition was held where computer scientists created programs to play the prisoner's dilemma against one another based on a strategy
-   The best performing strategy was Tit-for-tat : start out cooperating and then reciprocate the last thing the opponent did to you---nicer and promotes cooperation, more nice- never the first to defect
-  Tit-for-tat has three properties that underlie people's behavior universally

<!-- -->

- Cooperative:  Never attacks first
- Forgiving:  If the opponent cooperated on the previous turn, then cooperate on the next
- Fair:  if the opponent did not cooperate on the last turn then do not cooperate on the next

<!-- -->

-  Most of the winning strategies were initially cooperative strategies
-  These results suggest an evolutionary explanation for the origin of cooperation - it is what was most successful and likely to improve evolutionary fitness

## Regional Differences in Helping {#slide-54}

- Survey research indicates that people in rural areas report higher levels of empathic concern (Smith, 2015)

- A review of 35 studies containing over 14,000 participants ( Steblay , 1987) 

<!-- -->

- Studies were conducted to examine differences in rural vs metropolitan  settings
- All studies had participants from either a rural or metropolitan setting put in situation where they could help another person (e.g., simple favor, helping someone injured)

- Strangers are significantly more likely to be helped in rural communities than in metropolitan areas (effect size ( r)  = .29 . 

-  For people who moved (e.g. rural to big-city), the area they currently lived in mattered the most

54

The meta-analyses show that across most definitions of helping, people from rural areas tend to help at higher rates than metropolitan  citizens
