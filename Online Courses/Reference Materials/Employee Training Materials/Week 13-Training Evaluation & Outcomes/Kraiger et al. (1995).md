Measuring Knowledge Organization as a
Method for Assessing Learning during Training
KURT KRAIGER,1 University orColorado at Denver, Denver, Colorado, and EDUARDO SALAS and
JANIS A. CANNON-BOWERS, Naval Air Warfare Center, Orlando, Florida

This paper applies the conceptual work of K. Kraiger, J. K. Ford, and E. Salas
(1993) to the evaluation of two training programs. A method known as structural
assessment (SA) was described and adapted for use in the evaluation of a training
program for computer programming and a PC-based simulation of a naval decision-making task. SA represents and evaluates pairwise judgments of relatedness
of concepts drawn from the training content domain. In the first study, SA scores
of students (determined by similarity to an expert solution) were significantly
higher after training than before but did not predict performance on a take-home
exam 12 weeks later. In the second study, we manipulated training content by
providing half the students with the goals and objectives of the transfer task (an
advance organizer) before training and providing the other half with the same
information after training. As hypothesized, SA scores were higher for those receiving the organizers before training; SA scores were also more strongly related to
performance on the criterion task for this group. Implications of the results for
training evaluation are discussed.

INTRODUCTION
The objectives of the present studies were to
apply and test a cognitively based measure of
learning during training: the structural assessment of knowledge. This effort differs from traditional training research in that the focus is on
evaluation, not training, and the evaluation
measure is theoretically based, drawn from a
classification scheme of learning objectives derived by Kraiger, Ford, and Salas (1993). It differs from previous research on structural assessment in that the present studies apply structural
assessment procedures to training, not educational settings. Although training and education
are similar contexts, they are not identical. In

Kirkpatrick (1987) recommended measuring

Kraiger et ai. (1993) conducted a thorough
review of theoretical work from numerous psychological disciplines to derive a preliminary
classification scheme for assessing learning outcomes. Specifically, learning may be evident
from the achievement of outcomes in any of
three domains: cognitive, skill-based, or affective. Within each domain are separate learning
categories (e.g., the cognitive domain was proposed to be composed of verbal knowledge,
knowledge organization, and cognitive strategies). For each subdomain, Kraiger et ai. recommended new procedures or measures to infer
learning during training.
This construct-oriented approach to developing evaluation measures has several advantages
over the typical evaluation studies that use convenient criteria. First, it forces training researchers to be explicit not only about the intended learning objectives but also about the
appropriate
training interventions and evaluation criteria. Second, a construct-oriented
approach offers the potential for validating evaluation criteria. The use of construct-based

evaluation criteria enables researchers to specify a nomological network that includes testable
hypotheses.
To date. few have attempted to develop and
validate conceptually based evaluation measures such as those proposed by Kraiger et ai.
(1993). Accordingly, the primary objective of the
current research was to apply and test a measure of cognitive learning outcomes suggested
by Kraiger et ai. Consistent with a construct validation approach, we first describe the measure
within a theoretical context and then propose
testable hypotheses.
Measurement

of Knowledge Organization

Traditionally knowledge acquisition has been
assessed via achievement tests administered at
the end of training; prototypical exams are in
multiple-choice or true-false format and assess
recall or recognition of previously learned verbal
data (Goldsmith and Kraiger, in press). Such
criteria may not be appropriate for training programs that set higher-order learning objectives.
One higher-order learning outcome is the organization of knowledge. Research and theory
suggest that knowledge organization is distinct
from the amount of declarative knowledge and
is a good predictor of subsequent task performance. Specifically, research in cognitive and
instructional psychology has shown that measures of knowledge organization
are only
marginally related to measures of declarative
knowledge (e.g., Trigwell and Sleet, 1990) and
account for significant variance in achievement
beyond that accounted for by recognition tests
(e.g., Sumfleth, 1988).
Learning theorists and researchers studying
expert/novice differences have long argued that
the organization of task-specific information is
critical for effective task performance. For example, McKeithen, Reitman, Reuter, and Hirtle
(1981) showed that expert computer programmers organized their knowledge of programming languages along semantic dimensions,
whereas novice programmers organized the language along syntactic lines. Similarly, Lesgold
et al. (1989) found that organization of radiology

knowledge predicted residents' performance at
diagnosing X rays. Research in instructional
psychology has also found that the similarity between students' knowledge organization and
that of content experts is a good predictor of
classroom exam performance (e.g., Goldsmith,
Johnson, and Acton, 1991).
Assessing knowledge organization. The method
used in these studies to measure knowledge organization is called structural assessment (SA).
As described by Goldsmith and Kraiger (in
press), SA consists of four steps: defining a referent structure for a training domain, eliciting
trainees' judgments of relatedness among key
concepts, deriving representations
of trainees'
knowledge given these judgments, and evaluating the quality of the derived representation. Because these steps are described in detail
elsewhere (Goldsmith and Kraiger, in press;
Kraiger and Salas, 1993), they are only summarized here.
A referent structure is defined through examination of the training content, or focused interviews with trainers or subject matter experts. A
set of 15 to 30 concepts should be defined to
represent critical concepts in the training domain. Generally the greater the number of defined concepts, the better the resulting representations (Goldsmith et aI., 1991).

Research to date has indicated that SA is a
potentially valid method for measuring knowledge organization. However, additional research
is necessary to provide construct-oriented evidence of the validity of the procedure to assess
learning during training. Learning during training implies at least two conditions: (a) that
scores on the measure change in predicted ways
as a result of instruction,
and (b) that the
method is as valid in training contexts as it appears to be in educational contexts. Education
and training differ principally in application or
end states. The objective of education is often
the acquisition (or organization) of knowledge in
and of itself. Thus exam scores are meaningful
criteria for educational studies (e.g., Goldsmith
et aI., 1991). The objective of training is typically
to prepare participants for performance in a
subsequent domain. Thus the application of
knowledge (via performance on a transfer task)
is a more meaningful criterion.
The following studies were conducted to provide construct-oriented evidence of the validity
of applying the SA procedure to training. We
first conducted a pilot study to gain experience
in applying the methodology to training. In the
second study, specific hypotheses were offered
about the relationship of the SA approach to
pre- and posttraining events.

Discussion
The pilot study was conducted to provide ex-

perience for the researchers in applying the
methodology to training.
Evidence for the validity of the structural assessment method was mixed. SA scores (i.e.,
Pathfinder C scores) were significantly higher after training than they were before, but they did
not predict transfer to a posttraining task. That
SA scores were significantly greater after training than before indicates that trainees organized
their task-relevant knowledge in a manner significantly more similar to the target structure as
a result of training. Given that computer programming often requires a conceptual understanding of principles and practices in a particular environment (i.e., development of a mental
model), this evidence suggests that SA may be
an appropriate evaluation tool in this domain.
Goldsmi th et al. (1991) reported similar success
in using Pathfinder to assess learning in a statistics/programming domain.
The absence of a relationship between SA
scores and performance on the transfer task was
unexpected because prior research has shown
that subjects' ability to construct meaningful
mental models is a better predictor of transfer of
training than is their recall of declarative knowledge of the task (e.g., Brown, Kane, and Echols,
1986). However, the transfer task in this study
required not only knowledge of the training content (SPSSX programming) but also consider-

that subjects' perceptions

of the competencies

required for success on the final projects differed
significantly from their understanding
of the
SPSS knowledge developed through training. A
better test of the predictive validity of SA scores
may require a transfer task that is more directly
relevant to the training content. (For example in
this study, a structural assessment of subjects'
understanding of multiple regression may have
been a more valid predictor of performance on a
later multiple regression programming problem
than would an assessment of knowledge of
SPSSx.) This may explain why the posttest multiple-choice test predicted transfer performance
whereas SA did not. Although the content of the
multiple-choice test was limited to questions
about SPSSx, tests such as these and the transfer
problems are prone to contamination from testtaking skills or general intelligence (Gagne and
Beard, 1978). Thus the predictive validity of the
multiple-choice test could have been attributable to shared method variance or a spurious
relationship involving intelligence, effects that
are less likely to occur with the SA method.
To further evaluate the construct validity of
the SA approach, we conducted a second study,
which differed from the pilot study in three
ways. First, we collected data from a larger sample to increase the power of the statistical tests.
Second, whereas the pilot study suggested that
SA was a sensitive measure of learning over an

manipulation. Closeness scores were positively
correlated with total scores on the simulation
for the entire sample (r = 0.32), though the relationship was not significant. When the same
correlation was examined for the experimental
conditions, a different pattern emerged. C scores
were strongly correlated for subjects in the before condition (r = 0.63, p < 0.005) but uncorrelated for subjects in the after condition (r =
-0.11, ns). A similar pattern of results was
found for correlations on raw ratings between
subjects and averaged referent ratings (r = 0.64
vs. r = -0.13) and for C scores and simulation
criteria Qther than total score (e.g., percentage of
targets correctly engaged). Thus although closeness scores were not significantly correlated

with performance on the transfer task for the
entire sample, they were significantly correlated
with task performance for subjects who received
advance organizers prior to training. Advance
organizers moderated the predictive validity of
SA scores for transfer task performance.
Discussion
These results provided additional constructoriented evidence for the validity of the structural assessment of knowledge acquisition. The
pilot study and results of other studies have
shown that Pathfinder scores change as a function of instruction (Acton, 1991; Kraiger and
Salas, 1993). The results again show that test
scores increase as a function of instruction, but
they also reveal that SA scores are sensitive to
the specific effects of an instructional intervention designed to affect knowledge organization.
When information about the task goals and
training objectives was provided prior to presentation of the training material, subjects
developed more meaningful organizations of
training content, as opposed to subjects who received the same content but without the advance organizers.
These results also provided evidence of the
predictive validity of the SA approach. In contrast to the pilot study, in which SA scores did
not predict transfer, this study used a transfer
task that was specifically related to the assessed
knowledge domain. More important, the results
of the pilot study provided evidence of discriminant validity: It is not measures of all types of
knowledge organization that predict transfer,
only measures of those that are meaningfully
constructed (by learners). When subjects were
not given advance organizers prior to instruction, they were able to organize information, but
not in a way that was meaningful for task performance. For those subjects given advance organizers, the better the quality of their knowledge structures (assessed by the similarity of
their Pathfinder representations to the referent
structure), the better their task performance.
There were several qualifiers to this study.
First, whereas the criterion domain of the second

GENERAL DISCUSSION
Summary of Construct-Oriented
of Validity

Evidence

Campbell (1976) argued that construct validity can never be shown in a single study but
must be accumulated across multiple studies using multiple strategies, including content validity, convergent validity, discriminant validity,
predictive validity, and experimental evidence
in which the intended measure is used as either
an independent or a dependent variable. The results of both studies can be summarized wi thin
this framework.
Content validity results from careful test construction. The SA approach is content valid because the concepts tested are directly relevant to
the training content, and trainees' organization
of training content is an intended outcome of
training. In both studies, the SA approach used
terms that were derived directly from the training content. In general, the SA approach will be
content valid whenever concepts are based on
examination of training materials, interviews
with subject matter experts, or both and whenever a principal objective of training is to alter
trainees' knowledge structures.

FACTORS

Convergent validity is assessed through the
degree to which two measures of the same construct similarly rank trainees. Convergent validity was not assessed in the present investigations, but prior research comparing structural
assessment with multidimensional scaling solutions has generally found significant relationships (e.g., Cooke and McDonald, 1987). Alternatively, SA solutions using Pathfinder could be
compared with other elicitation techniques,
such as free sorts (e.g., Champagne, Klopfer, Desena, and Squires, 1981) or with measures of
other learning constructs more conceptually
aligned with knowledge organization
(e.g.,
schema theory). More research of this nature is
needed.
Discriminant validity is evident when two
measures of different latent constructs are unrelated to each other. In the pilot study the SA
approach yielded scores that were uncorrelated
with multiple-choice test scores, an assessment
of trainees' declarative knowledge. This outcome is consistent with other results using Pathfinder (Kraiger and Salas, 1993) and other studies comparing various knowledge organization
and declarative knowledge measures (Trigwell
and Sleet, 1990). The implication of these results
is that the SA approach assesses a construct different from declarative knowledge and may be
used in conjunction with other measures to assess a broader range of learning outcomes.
Predictive validity is evident when scores on a
measure predict some meaningful criterion. Because the purpose of training is usually to improve posttraining
performance, transfer of
training is a meaningful criterion for evaluating
the validity of a learning outcome measure. In
the pilot study SA scores were positively correlated with scores on a posttraining measure, although the relationship was nonsignificant. In
the primary study the transfer task was directly
related to training content. In this experiment
SA scores were significantly correlated with task
performance, provided subjects were informed
of the goals and objectives of the task prior to
training. These results provide preliminary evidence of the predictive validity of C scores.

MEASURING KNOWLEDGE ORGANIZATION

Evidence of predictive validity is important
for two reasons. First, training evaluation offers
a unique opportunity to evaluate the construct
validity of measures because of the close link
between the attributes being assessed and the
requirements for success in the posttraining environment (Kraiger, 1994). Second, predictive
validity may hold practical value, given that organizations are justifiably concerned about the
impact of training yet often do not provide opportunities for on-the-job measures after training. Evidence of the predictive validity of a posttraining measure in one setting may be used to
argue for the practical impact of the training in
other settings where on-the-job behavior or performance cannot be assessed.
In future studies, it would be valuable to vary
the time interval between posttraining and criterion measurement
and also to look more
closely at the relationship between model content and criterion performance. For example, in
a team training setting, structural assessment
can be applied to both team and task understanding (Cannon-Bowers, Salas, and Converse,
1993). Then, during task performance, both
team processes and task functioning could be assessed and the predictive validity of the two assessments could be compared.
It is also important in future applications of
the structural assessment approach that the
training objectives target knowledge organization as a meaningful learning outcome (rather
than declarative knowledge, skill development,
etc.). Two training interventions likely to improve participants' knowledge organization are
cognitive apprenticeships (e.g., Collins, Brown,
and Newman, 1989) and concept learning (Tennyson and Cocchiarella, 1986). For example, the
principal objective of the latter approach is to
aid learners in developing prototypes, generalizing existing knowledge to new information,
and discriminating relevant concepts from irrelevant ones.
SUMMARY
The purpose of these studies was to apply and
test a cognitively based measure of learning dur-

December 1995-815

ing training. The structural assessment method,
tested in these studies, was designed to test the
learning construct of knowledge organization.
Both the construct and the measure were proposed by Kraiger et al. (1993). Thus the methodology and results of this research provide preliminary support for the typology of learning
outcomes and measures proposed by Kraiger et
al. (1993). When trainees learn, they are usually
expected to do more than accumulate declarative knowledge of the training content. They typically organize that information in a meaningful
way, then compile knowledge in forms that enable high performance on tasks. Structural assessment provides an alternative method of
evaluating learning, one that targets a higher
level of knowledge acquisition.

Across studies, the results provide prelimi.
nary support for the structural assessment approach. Although additional research is necessary, this is one of the first investigations to have
tested true cognitively based learning measures
in a training context. The results are encouraging in that we may have tools that allow us to
assess, examine, and understand trainees' organization of knowledge. The science and practice
of training may further evolve as we begin to
understand how training events affect structural
development and how such organization affects
on-the-job transfer and application. We hope
that this study encourages future work that will
lead to better training design and evaluation.
