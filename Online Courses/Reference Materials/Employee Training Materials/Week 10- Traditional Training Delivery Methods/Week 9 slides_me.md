## Klein et al., (2006) {#slide-15}

Motivation to learn and course outcomes: The impact of delivery mode, learning goal orientation, and perceived barriers and enablers

This study goes beyond delivery methods to examine how  motivation, learning goal orientation, perceived barriers, and enablers  interact and influence learning outcomes in training programs.

Key Takeaways

- Motivation is the Engine:   Motivation to learn  is a  critical predictor  of course outcomes regardless of the training delivery mode (e.g., in-person classroom vs. online learning modules). Highly motivated individuals are more likely to actively engage with the training content, persevere through challenges, and ultimately achieve better learning outcomes.
- Learning Goal Orientation Matters:  The study differentiates between two types of learning goal orientations:

<!-- -->

- Mastery Goal Orientation:  Individuals with this orientation are focused on  learning, improvement, and mastering new skills . They tend to view challenges as opportunities for growth and actively seek feedback to improve.
- Performance Goal Orientation:  Individuals with this orientation are primarily concerned with  demonstrating competence and looking good . They might avoid challenges and be less receptive to feedback for fear of appearing inadequate.

## Salas et al., (2007) {#slide-16}

Testing three team training strategies in intact team: A meta-analysis

This meta-analysis examines the effectiveness of three common team training strategies in enhancing team performance.

Key Takeaways:   Three Training Approaches for Stronger Teams

The study compared the effectiveness of three team training strategies:

- Coordination Training:  Focuses on developing  shared mental models  and task coordination skills. This might involve simulations or exercises that require team members to anticipate each other\'s actions and work together seamlessly towards a common goal.
- Communication Training:  Aims to improve team communication skills and information sharing. This could involve training on active listening, clear and concise communication, and conflict resolution techniques.
- Team Process Training:  Develops team problem-solving, conflict resolution, and decision-making skills. This type of training might involve role-playing scenarios that challenge the team to work together effectively to overcome obstacles.

## Brown (2001) {#slide-17}

Using computers to deliver training: Which employees learn and why? 

This study explores factors influencing employee learning outcomes in computer-based training (CDT) programs.

Key Takeaways: Tailoring CDT for Individual Needs

- Learning Styles and Preferences:  Not all employees learn equally well through computer-based training. Some might prefer interactive exercises and simulations, while others might learn best through video lectures or narrated presentations. Organizations should consider incorporating a variety of delivery methods within CDT programs to cater to diverse learning styles.
- Self-Efficacy and Motivation:  Employees with higher  self-efficacy  (belief in their ability to learn) and  intrinsic motivation  (desire to learn for personal growth) tend to have better learning outcomes in CDT programs.  Training designers can promote self-efficacy and motivation by:
- \* \*\*Setting Clear Learning Objectives:\*\*   Ensure trainees understand the goals of the training and how it will benefit them in their work.
- \* \*\*Providing Opportunities for Mastery Experiences:\*\*   Incorporate activities and exercises that allow trainees to experience success and build confidence in their newly acquired skills.
- \* \*\*Offering Personalized Learning Paths:\*\*  Consider offering adaptive learning modules that adjust to the individual\'s pace and learning needs. 
- \* \*\*Integrating Gamification Elements:\*\*   Utilize elements of gameplay
