## Person Analysis  {#slide-7}

A gap analysis can be used to determine what is responsible for differences between employee's current and expected performance 

An employee's "readiness for training" refers to whether employees have the personal characteristic (ability, attitudes, beliefs, and motivation) necessary to learn  whether    employees have the personal characteristics (ability, attitudes, beliefs, and motivation) necessary to learn program content and apply it on the job, and whether the work environment will facilitate learning and not interfere with performance

Interviews can be used to assess these factors 

## Factors that Inhibit Learning {#slide-8}

Person characteristic- employee knowledge, skill, ability, and attitude

Literacy audit can be used to assess

Includes cognitive ability, readability, and self-efficacy 

Cognitive ability includes verbal comprehension, quantitative ability, and reasoning ability 

Importance for this aspect increases as job difficulty increases

Input relates to the instructions that tell employees what, how, and when to perform; can also refer to the resources that the employees are given to help them perform (can relate to equipment, time, or budget)

Situational constraints include lack of proper tools and equipment, materials and supplies, budgetary support, and time. 

Social support refers to managers' and peers' willingness to provide feedback and reinforcement 

## Factors that Inhibit Learning Cont. {#slide-9}

Issues with performance standards can arise when expectations are made unclear

Consequences refer to the type of incentives that employees receive for performing well 

Can provide incentives to attend training 

Feedback refers to the information that employees receive while they are performing

Important to provide employees with regular feedback

## Root Cause Analysis  {#slide-11}

A root cause analysis refers to the process of determining whether training is the most likely solution to a performance problem or gap 

If employees lack the knowledge and skills to perform a job and the other factors are satisfactory, training is needed

If employees have the knowledge and skills to perform but input, output consequences, and/or feed-back are in adequate, training may not be the solution

## Competency Modeling  {#slide-13}

Competencies are sets of skills, knowledge, abilities, and personal characteristics that enable employees to perform their jobs successfully 

Determines what competencies are necessary to complete a job 

## Job Analysis and Competency Modeling {#slide-14}

Job analysis is more work and task focused (what is accomplished), whereas competency modeling is worker-focused (how objectives are met or how work is  accomplished) 

Competency models provide descriptions of competencies that are common for an entire occupational group level of jobs, or an entire organization 

The competencies generated are more general and believed to have great application to a wider variety of purposes (i.e. selection, training, employee development, and performance management 

Job analysis describes what is different across jobs, occupational groups, or organization levels and generations specific KSAOs of a particular jobs 

It is used to generate specific requirements to be used for employee selection

## Experiment  {#slide-22}

Study looked at how different (within-person) factors ability impacts people's ratings on their training rating emphasis including

Breadth (variety of tasks)

Activity level (amount of times task performed),

Self efficacy (belief they can complete job)

cognitive ability impacts people's ratings on their training rating emphasis 

Participants: (AGE) mechanics in the U.S. Air Force. AGE mechanics use powered and nonpowered equipment to repair the various aircraft used by the U.S. Air Force

Mechanics were sent a task inventory to complete 8 and 12 months after graduating from the formal training

## Results  {#slide-24}

There were differences in task-level experiences across individuals when tenure was held constant and that these differences were related to recommended training-emphasis rating

Those who performed more tasks during the first 12 months of the job (high breadth) rated formal training as more important than those with less breadth 

The Findings highlight the importance of studying task-level experience factors for their impact on task analysis ratings, such as training emphasis

There was much variability in the task-level experiences of incumbents with the same job tenure and that this variability was predictive of task ratings of recommended training emphasis

Changes in task experience and individual characteristics are systematically related to within-person changes in one type of task rating

Research is needed that identifies when task analysis ratings stabilize and how person, task, or job factors affect the length of time it takes for these ratings to stabilize

## Sahinidis  &  Bouris  (2008) {#slide-25}

Organizational commitment (pool & pool 2007): the relative strength of an individual's identification and involvement in a particular organization

Training should have a positive impact on motivation and employee commitment 

Training is defined in this study "as the planned intervention that is designed to enhance the determinants of individual job performance"

A shortfall in performance can be due to employees not feeling motivated anymore to apply their skills, they may be afraid in doing so, or they may believe that there may be a conflict of interest with the organization, when a change is about to take place, which training should identify and address

## Hypotheses {#slide-26}

There is a positive relationship between employee perceived training effectiveness and job satisfaction

There is a positive relationship between employee perceived training effectiveness and motivation

There is a positive relationship between employee perceived training effectiveness and employee commitment
