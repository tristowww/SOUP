The mediating role of training utility in the relationship between
training needs assessment and organizational effectiveness
W. van Eerdea*, K.C. Simon Tanga and Graeme Talbotb
a

Technische Universiteit Eindhoven, Eindhoven, the Netherlands; bNew Zealand Road Transport
and Logistics Industry Training Organization, New Zealand
In a survey among respondents from 96 organizations, we found that the utility of
training partially mediated the relationship between Training Needs Assessment
(TNA) comprehensiveness and the effectiveness of the organization. We did not find an
effect of training quantity on training utility and organization effectiveness. Both TNA
comprehensiveness and training quantity were found to be unrelated to organizational
size. The results support the importance of conducting TNA in practice.
Keywords: training needs assessment; training needs analysis; training utility;
organizational effectiveness

Introduction
Training is the process of making proficient through instruction and hands-on practice that
is expected to be used in the performance of assigned duties (Goldstein 1993). Research on
employee training has stressed the impact of training on different types of outcomes. A
popular distinction in what training may contribute is made in the four-level model of
Kirkpatrick (1998). This model distinguishes; (1) reaction criteria that represent attitudinal
and affective responses to the training; (2) learning criteria, learning outcomes of the
training without reference to the performance for which the training was meant; (3)
behavioural criteria, actual on-the-job performance; and (4) results criteria, distal and
macro criteria that relate to productivity and profits.
In this study, we aim to provide further insight into the effect of training programmes,
in aggregate, at the results level. Previous studies found positive relations between training
efforts and the development of industries (Colarelli and Montei 1996); employee wages
(Lynch 1994); employee turnover (Huselid 1995); and on productivity and company
performance (Huselid 1995; Kitching and Blackburn 2002). We extend this research by
examining how training needs assessment and training utility relate to organizational
effectiveness.
Training can be beneficial to organizations but, in practice, obstacles in the
implementation may lead to diminished outcomes. Although investment in training might
be a wise decision, it may not necessarily lead to improved productivity. For example, in a
survey on the impact of human capital investment on business productivity, Lynch and

Black (1995) found that the number of employees trained did not have a substantial impact
on productivity. There may be many reasons to engage in training programmes, and not all
may be expected to lead to improvement of organizational output.
This study investigated whether the extent to which organizations assess training needs
before they engage in training programmes may affect outcomes for the organizations. Needs
assessment (or needs analysis) is ‘the process of determining the organization’s training needs
and seeks to answer the question of whether the organization’s needs, objectives, and
problems can be met or addressed by training’ (Arthur, Bennett, Edens and Bell 2003, p. 235).
The idea behind training needs assessment is that only when there is a match between training
needs and the content of training, beneficial outcomes to organizational performance can be
realized. This match is shown when the utility of the training is judged favourably. In this
article, we report an empirical test of how training needs assessment affects organizational
effectiveness through the effect of the assessment on training utility. We also examined the
relation between training quantity, utility and effectiveness, which implies that investing more
in training generally will lead to higher effectiveness through training utility.
Furthermore, we took into consideration the size of the organization, because some
studies, for example Lynch and Black (1995), found that it was an important factor
contributing to the investment in training and the professionalism of Human Resource
(HR) practices.
Training and organizational effectiveness
Overall, we expected training to have a positive effect on organizational effectiveness
because raising the level of and range of workers’ competencies would imply a broader
repertoire of behaviour that may be used in the organization to improve performance.
A substantial number of studies indicate a positive relationship between HR interventions
and organization level performance (Holzer, Block, Cheatham and Knott, 1993; Huselid
1995; Blundell, Dearden, Meghir and Sianesi 1999; Bartel 2000; Gmür and Schwerdt
2005; Wright, Gardner, Moynihan and Allen 2005). The above studies indicate that the
effects of training on organization productivity and profitability are less well researched
than those examining the effect of training on trainees’ attributes (such as wages or
mobility). A meta-analysis conducted by Gmür and Schwerdt (2005) included samples
from the US, Europe and East Asia and found an average effect of training on
organizational success of r ¼ .14. It should be noted that the confidence interval included
the zero (ranging from 2 .05 to .32), which means that there may not be an effect, or that it
may even be slightly negative. As most research was cross-sectional, no causal order
between the variables can be established. This means that alternative explanations for this
relationship cannot be ruled out, such as effective organizations tend to invest more in HR
as a consequence of their performance, or that other variables may influence both training
and effectiveness, or that the relation may only exist as an implicit theory in the heads of
the respondents (Wright et al. 2005). Overall, it is plausible that training affects
performance, but it may not be the case in every instance. In the following sections we will
discuss reasons why training may not yield the desired effect, and introduce training needs
assessment and training utility as concepts that may explain the effect of training on
organizational effectiveness further.
Training needs assessment (TNA)
One of the reasons why training does not deliver the desired effect may be that it does not
suit the needs of the organization. Although this seems a straightforward and simple idea,

not much evidence is present in the literature to support it. Arthur et al. (2003) reported
that only a few studies on training included a needs assessment (only 6% of all the data
points in their meta-analysis). The studies that were conducted on needs assessment
revealed positive effect sizes (ds ranging from .28 to 1.93). However, these results do not
relate to organizational effectiveness, but rather to criteria related to reactions, learning
and behaviour of individuals in training. In this study, we consider whether the presence of
training programme needs assessment, – and more specifically to what extent the
assessment is pursued in a comprehensive manner – is related to the overall effectiveness
of the organization.
Traditionally, the ‘felt’ needs approach has been applied which simply asks employees
to list the training they desire (Holton, Bates and Naquin 2000). Although these ‘felt’
training needs might boost morale, it is unlikely that they yield results at the business level
because employees may report the training they want and it may differ from the training
they really need. Guthrie and Schwoerer (1994, 1996) pointed out that self-assessed
training needs can be systematically affected by factors other than actual need. For
example, they found that respondents in later career stages reported lower training selfefficacy, lower training utility and less need for management training.
Although self-assessment of training needs involves the risk of not identifying the
actual need, it should be noted that the assessment by others does not guarantee that actual
needs are identified better. Chiu, Thompson, Mak, and Lo’s (1999) conclusion of a review
of the literature was that 87% of the needs assessments were initiated by trainers, which
may signify a supply-led approach to TNA. Also, other considerations may play a role in
the choice for a specific training programme, for example based on subsidies given by
governments, the compliance with a particular qualification framework, or legislative
requirements. These reasons may differ from the true business needs of the employer.
A systematic approach to TNA includes at least the determination of job requirements,
required skill levels and the current skill levels of the employees involved. Many case
studies on TNA have been documented that demonstrate this (Taylor, O’Driscoll and
Binning 1998). TNA models have not seen significant changes over the past 30 to 40 years,
in terms of theory as well as practice (Goldstein 1993). Several TNA models have been
presented in the literature (McGehee and Thayer 1961; Mager and Pipe 1984; Rossett
1987; Rummler and Brache 1995; Taylor et al. 1998; Leigh, Watkins, Platt and Kaufman
2000). In the following we describe the Organization-Task-Person (OTP) framework as a
typical approach to TNA.
Organization-Task-Person framework
The OTP three-level conception of needs assessment (McGehee and Thayer 1961) is
considered the core framework for needs assessment in the academic literature (Holton
et al. 2000), and most of the models developed since have been based on this three-level
framework. The organizational analysis consists of considering how personnel training
can help to attain organizational goals and where in the organization training is needed.
The task analysis focuses on what the employee must learn in order to perform well in the
job and, thus, what the training should cover, and the person analysis identifies who, in
the organization, will need training and for what purpose.
A conscious and structured approach to training needs would in all likelihood help to
increase the selection of training solutions that most closely relate to the organizational
needs. It is likely that this will affect training utility. In the next section we will elaborate
on the utility of training for organizations.

Training utility
Only if the relevant training needs are identified and addressed will training lead to the
desired results. It is likely that some organizations attempt to solve their human performance
problems through training without determining whether training was the answer to
their problems in the first place. In extreme cases inadequate or incomplete training is even
likely to promote performance deficiencies (McGehee and Thayer 1961; Goldstein 1993).
Training should only be used as a solution to human resource problems when the cause of
these problems is a lack of competencies (Mager and Pipe 1984). After all, performance
improvement may lie in other factors. Rummler and Brache (1995) suggested that 80%
of the performance improvement opportunities reside in managing performance, by
providing the adequate conditions in terms of input and output, feedback, performance
consequences and so on. They suggested that only 15% to 20% of performance may be
ascribed to the competency area. A good TNA should highlight the issues that need to
be addressed through training and distinguish these competency issues from the other
factors affecting performance.
We consider the perceived utility of training, as judged by those in charge of training,
such as (HR) managers, to be the most important factor to affect organizational
effectiveness. Only if training satisfies training needs can it be considered useful, and it is
suggested that only when it is useful, will effectiveness result.
Utility may be viewed from the perspective of the trainee and from the organization.
Alliger, Tannenbaum, Bennett, Traver and Shotland (1997) distinguish between
‘affective’ and ‘utility’ judgements of training satisfaction. Affective judgements measure
the extent to which the trainee ‘liked’ the different aspects of training. Utility judgments,
on the other hand, reflect the extent to which trainees can apply the training subjects in
their work. Alliger et al. (1997) showed that these utility judgements of trainees show
modest but significant relationships to immediate learning and transfer.
Here, we take the perspective of the organization and consider the utility of training as
perceived by those responsible for training programmes as a mediating variable, both
between quantity of training and effectiveness, and between TNA comprehensiveness and
effectiveness. This is expressed in the following hypotheses:
Hypothesis 1a: The utility of training mediates the relation between training quantity and
organizational effectiveness.
Hypothesis 1b: The utility of training mediates the relation between the comprehensiveness of TNA and organizational effectiveness.
Of course there may be other factors that affect training outcomes in organizations.
Here, we consider the size of the organization as an important influence.
Organization size
Investment in training and training needs assessment may depend on the circumstances in
which an organization operates. The availability of more resources increases the flexibility
of larger organizations to take their staff off the job to undertake training. Moreover, large
organizations tend to have HR departments and in-house trainers that are more involved
with developing their human resources (Matlay and Addis 2002). Smaller family
businesses, such as taxi companies, may conduct little formal evaluation of training needs.
On the other hand, managers may still be as familiar, or even more familiar, with workers’
training needs because in small organizations, top management is likely to have more

day-to-day contact with workers.1 Thus, training needs may be assessed informally.
However, we were interested in the effects of a more formal, systematic approach to TNA.
Smaller organizations tend not to have human resource development planning in place.
Hence, their training decisions may be more ad hoc and reaction driven (Johnston and
Loader 2003). Previous studies indeed showed that large establishments provide more
training than their smaller counterparts (Baron, Black and Loewenstein 1987; Billet 2001).
Although the relationship between size and training quantity was not found consistently
(Colarelli and Montei 1996), we think that the size of the organization is likely to influence
the extent to which training is provided because of the availability of resources and a more
formalized HR approach.
Also, we expect organizational size to be related to the comprehensiveness of TNA,
due to the more professional approach to HR of larger organizations. Research involving
1000 US based organizations, Saari, Johnson, McLaughlin and Zimerle (1988) found a
positive relationship between organization size and the use of needs assessment. Based
upon these ideas, we tested the following hypotheses related to organizational size:
Hypothesis 2:

Organizational size is positively related to training quantity.

Hypothesis 3:

Organizational size is positively associated with TNA
comprehensiveness.

Overall, our hypotheses can be framed in a model, shown in Figure 1. It can be seen that
training is hypothesized to affect organizational effectiveness indirectly through training
utility. The utility of the training is determined by training quantity (hypothesis 1a) and
by the comprehensiveness of TNA (hypothesis 1b). Furthermore, we expect that
organizational size has an impact on both training quantity (hypothesis 2) and the
comprehensiveness of TNA (hypothesis 3).
Method
Research setting, procedure and sample
The ‘New Zealand Road Transport and Logistics Industry Training Organization’ (NZRT
and LITO)2 is the organization that instigated this research. It is one of 46 Industry
Training Organizations in New Zealand, each of which was created by their industries to
work with them to develop national qualifications and training arrangements that meet
the needs of those industries. However, the ITO itself does not deliver training. In this
particular industry, all organizations are included that provide logistical services.

This industry comprises all sectors of commercial road transport industry (trucking, bus,
taxi and courier), stevedoring and ports, freight forwarding, distribution and warehousing.
This study was conducted to gain insight into training and organizational
effectiveness, and to obtain information from the employers that was used to build a
web-based tool for use by them to assess their training needs. Further information on the
tool may be obtained from the authors.
A questionnaire designed to ascertain information to test the hypotheses was carefully
constructed in cooperation with the ITO General Manager. In addition to the key data sought
from the survey, additional topics were included to establish motivation to train, perceived
barriers to provision of training, the role of NZQA qualifications and the need for a TNA
tool. A pilot of the proposed questionnaire was administered to a selection of the ITO staff to
validate structure and content and to verify that responses would provide the feedback
required. Following minor amendments, the survey was deployed in the form of a webbased questionnaire, because it was determined that this was the most suitable medium to
reach large numbers of participants within a short time period. It was also a cost-effective
way to manage the limited budget (Dillman and Bowker 2001). Organizations were sent an
email that explained the purpose of the survey and the benefits that their participation would
provide. The email included a link to the questionnaire.
The sample of organizations invited to participate in the survey was drawn from the
UBD New Zealand business directory, the Road Transport Forum (RTF) directory, and
the internal database of NZRT and LITO called TITAN (which contains the details of
employers and trainees that are currently registered for training supported by the ITO).
The initial contacts for this survey were the organization managers and/or human resource
managers, if there were any in the organization. In order to obtain a good level of response
a friendly reminder was mailed to those addresses that had not responded after two weeks.
The survey was distributed across a sample of 398 organizations, and 96 responses were
received which was considered reasonable in light of the timing of the survey, which
occurred during a peak in the business cycle.
Measures
Background variables. We requested, for background analysis, variables such as the
nature of the enterprise (e.g. privately held or partnership); number of years operating;
sector in the industry; and region in which the organization was located.
Organizational size. The size was measured by establishing the number of full-time
employees (FTE). This number was transformed into the natural logarithmic because the
number of employees was not normally distributed in this sample.
Training quantity. Training quantity was determined by the sum of the number of hours a
new employee was required to spend in training, and the hours of training that an average
employee spends in training each year (Kunneman, Key and Sleezer 2000). The
correlation between these number of hours was r ¼ .34 ( p , . 001).
TNA comprehensiveness. We measured the comprehensiveness of the TNA undertaken in
the organization prior to decisions made on training in the past. Based on the OTP-model,
we created a 7-item scale with a 5-point response scale ranging from ‘definitely not’ to
‘definitely’ to assess the use of TNA on each of the three levels (see Appendix).

Perceived utility of training programmes. Training utility was measured with a 6-item
scale, a combination of Ford and Noe’s (1987) scale and items from Morgan and Casper’s
(2000) scale. The items were: ‘The employees have been able to apply what they have
learned in training to the job’; ‘The time spent away from the job to attend training
has been worthwhile’; ‘Most of the material in the training programmes the employees
have attended has been relevant to skills I hoped they would develop’; ‘ I am satisfied with
the match of training programme content with my idea of what should be taught’; ‘ I am
satisfied with the training programme’s emphasis on most important knowledge and skill
requirements’; and ‘The training programme prepared our employees to perform their
current job tasks more effectively’. Response labels ranged from strongly disagree (1) to
strongly agree (5).
Organizational effectiveness. Seven items were drawn from Delaney and Huselid’s (1996)
measures of perceived effectiveness relative to other players in the market. The
respondents were requested to compare their organization to other similar organizations on
the following seven outcomes: quality of services; the ability to attract essential
employees; the satisfaction of customers or clients; relations among employees in general;
growth in sales; profitability; and market share. The answer format ranged from 1 (much
worse) to 5 (much better).
Results
The respondents consisted of CEO/ GMs (40.6%); Owners 17.7%; Line Managers 17.7%;
Supervisors 8.3%; Human Resource Managers 6.3%; and other 9.4%. There was a
reasonable distribution over the regions of the country: Northern 11.5%, Auckland 21.9%,
Central 37.5%, Canterbury/Westland 18.8% and Otago Southland 10.4%. Table 1
provides the sectors in which the organizations are active. Table 2 shows the descriptive
statistics of and correlations between the variables in the questionnaire. The average size
of the organizations in the sample was 66 full-time employees, but overall, the
organizations were rather small (median ¼ 18). The average hours spent on the induction
and annual training was 88. It can be seen that the organizations considered their TNA
rather comprehensive and judged the utility of training rather favourably overall. The
same can be said for their rating of effectiveness of the organization relative to others.

concluded that TNA comprehensiveness and training quantity were neither linearly nor
nonlinearly related to organizational size.
Discussion
This study provides insight on training at the macro level of training programmes in
organizations. We did not find that the hours of training were related to organizational
effectiveness, although other studies found this relation, or that the hours of training were
related to the utility of the training. However, the attention devoted to training in another
sense, i.e. the comprehensiveness of the needs assessment was significantly and positively
related to organizational effectiveness. This relation was partly mediated by the perceived
utility of training. In other words, the alignment of the training programme with the
organization’s training needs influences effectiveness, confirming earlier models and
results (McGehee and Thayer 1961; Mager and Pipe 1984; Goldstein 1993). Only a partial
mediation of utility was found in the relation between TNA and effectiveness; TNA

contributed directly to effectiveness in this sample. We did not find any (linear or
nonlinear) relation between the size of an organization and the extent to which the
organization invested in training hours, or on how comprehensive the TNA in
the organization was. We were able to show these effects in an industry that is not
characterized by highly educated employees. Nevertheless, comprehensive TNA and the
utility of the programmes were associated with better organizational effectiveness.
In addition, the study’s contribution lies in the sample of organizations, ranging from
small and medium entrepreneurial businesses to large-scale organizations in an industry that
employs persons from many different educational backgrounds. It also adds information
about New Zealand to the studies on training that have for most part been conducted in the US.
Notwithstanding the insight, several limitations of our study can be pointed out. First,
we were required to rely on perceptual measures of organizational effectiveness as we are
dealing with privately held establishments. Although financial measures are more
desirable, perceptual measures of effectiveness are regularly used and have proven to be
reliable measures (Venkatraman and Ramanujam 1987; Dollinger and Golden 1992;
Powell 1992).
Second, this study only examined cross-sectional differences between organizations.
In order to gain a clearer understanding of the relationships between TNA, training utility
and organizational effectiveness, a longitudinal analysis is required. Even though we
suggested that TNA and improved perceived utility of training initiatives lead to better
effectiveness, it is reasonable to assume that there is a lagged effect, as it would take
months or even years before the results of training start to take effect. Also, reverse
causation or spurious relations with third variables cannot be ruled out and it remains
possible that the relations mirror the image of the respondents, rather than portray the real
situation. The response rate was not very high, but there appeared to be sufficient variance
in the variables studied.
Third, the study was limited to information from a single source for all variables in the
model tested, and mono-method bias may have inflated correlations.
Finally, this study included training quantity, TNA comprehensiveness and training utility
as predictors of effectiveness. Other aspects of TNA, such as the sources consulted (trainee
and HR manager), or the nature of the TNA employed (e.g. organization analysis, task
analysis, person analysis, performance analysis) might have been interesting additional
variables. It may also be interesting to add other variables related to training, transfer climate
in particular. The exclusion of this variable was a conscious decision in the light of managing
the length of the questionnaire, but it may be a relevant addition in future research.
Implications for practice appear to be that the use of TNA is indeed helpful.
A systematic analysis of needs before making decisions about training appears to pay off.
In other words, it is worth the effort to consider the question why training is needed, what
should be the content of the training to fulfil this need and to whom it should be provided.
If the tools are available, this systematic analysis does not have to take much time and
effort. In light of changes that organizations make, for example, the increased use of
teams, this needs analysis should be a recurring process in which decisions are continually
optimized. Ultimately this should lead to the effective use of resources available for
human resource practices.

Training needs analysis comprehensiveness scale
1. Before considering training we carefully assessed if the problem could have been somewhere
else (such as the work environment, the tools, reward systems).
2. The job description of the employee’s job was reviewed prior to deciding on the training
programme to be undertaken by the employee.
3. Training was consciously linked to the company’s strategic plans.
4. We decided on the desired competency level we expected an employee to reach after
training.
5. We determined the knowledge, skills and attitudes an employee must have in order to
perform the job successfully.
6. The job related competency requirements were ranked according to their importance.
7. The current skill/competency level of employees was assessed prior to deciding on the
training programme to be undertaken by the employee.
