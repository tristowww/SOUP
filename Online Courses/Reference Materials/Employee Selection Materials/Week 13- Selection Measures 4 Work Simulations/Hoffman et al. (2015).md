This study uses meta-analysis and a qualitative review of exercise descriptions to evaluate the content,
criterion-related, construct, and incremental validity of 5 commonly used types of assessment center
(AC) exercises. First, we present a meta-analysis of the relationship between 5 types of AC exercises with
(a) the other exercise types, (b) the 5-factor model of personality, (c) general mental ability (GMA), and
(d) relevant criterion variables. All 5 types of exercises were significantly related to criterion variables
(␳ ⫽ .16 –.19). The nomological network analyses suggested that the exercises tend to be modestly
associated with GMA, Extraversion and, to a lesser extent, Openness to Experience but largely unrelated
to Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Emotional Stability. Finally, despite sparse reporting in
primary studies, a content analysis of exercise descriptions yielded some evidence of complexity,
ambiguity, interpersonal interaction, and fidelity but not necessarily interdependence.

Despite much discussion of AC exercises, there has been little
empirical research, and conflicting perspectives on whether exercises reflect a meaningful way to analyze, interpret, and report AC
data persist (Lance, 2008; Jackson, Lance, & Hoffman, 2012). In
addition, whereas voluminous research has focused on the role of
exercises in AC construct validity and the magnitude of exercise
effects, surprisingly little research has attended to even basic
questions of other aspects of the validity of AC exercises (Howard,
2008; Rupp et al., 2008). Further, when exercise information is
reported, the inclusion is often incidental and not the primary focus
of analysis, resulting in a fragmented literature base. Accordingly,
a systematic review has the potential to bring considerable clarity
to this defining feature of the AC method.
This study provides meta-analytic summaries of the relationships
between five types of exercises commonly used in ACs and (a) the
other types of AC exercises, (b) criterion variables, and (c) the
five-factor model (FFM) of personality and general mental ability
(GMA). Finally, we supplement these analyses by introducing a
taxonomy that specifies five characteristics of AC exercises and
indicators associated with each characteristic, and use this framework
to code the characteristics of the exercises previously reported in this
literature.

Managerial Simulation Exercises
The observation of assessee behavior in moderate- to highfidelity behavioral simulations distinguishes ACs from other approaches commonly used to inform staffing decisions (Highhouse,
2001; International Task Force on Assessment Center Guidelines,
2009). ACs are most commonly targeted toward managers
(Spychalski, Quiñones, Gaugler, & Pohley, 1997) and, accordingly, are designed to simulate tasks pertinent to the managerial
role. Although not exclusively used in the context of ACs, managerial simulation exercises are commonly used in ACs. AC exercises are characterized by substantial variability in form and
function. However, on the basis of Thornton’s (1992) taxonomy of
exercises, six broad types of exercises are commonly used when
reporting and describing AC exercises: in-basket (IB), cooperative
leaderless group discussion (LGD), competitive LGD, role-play
(RP), oral presentation (OP), and case analysis (CA) exercises.
Despite considerable variability in exercises across local AC
administrations, past research has found this framework useful to
conceptualize and categorize AC exercises (cf. Bowler & Woehr,
2006; Connelly, Ones, Ramesh, & Goff, 2008; Lievens, Chasteen,
Day, & Christiansen, 2006). Theoretically, the situation simulated
in each type of exercise is proposed to activate specific traits and,
thus, elicit behaviors pertinent to performance in that context for
individuals with higher levels of the activated trait (Lievens et al.,
2006). To the extent that the exercise simulates important job
tasks, effective performance in the situation depicted in each
exercise is conceptualized as an indicator of candidate ability to
effectively complete positional requirements (Hemphill, 1959;
Jackson, 2012), organizational roles (Mintzberg, 1973), or tasks
(The Occupational Information Network [OⴱNet]; U.S. Department of Labor, Employment and Training Administration, 2008;
Dierdorff, Rubin, & Morgeson, 2009) associated with managerial
work. Below, we briefly describe each of the exercise types before
turning our attention to a discussion on their validity.

In-Basket Exercise
The IB exercise simulates “the paperwork that arrives in the
mailbox or on the desk of the typical manager” (Lievens et al.,
2006, p. 258). The assessee is presented with a packet of information that they must read, prioritize, and address to resolve issues

facing the organization (Thornton, 1992). It has been estimated
that as much of two thirds of managers’ time is spent dealing with
information (Hales, 1986), and managerial work is characterized
by high levels of complexity (Dierdorff et al., 2009). The IB is
designed to simulate this contextual characteristic common to
managerial work.

Leaderless Group Discussion
In LGD exercises, participants are given problems to resolve in
a set period of time and are required to work in an unstructured
group to resolve these problems (Bass, 1954; Lievens et al., 2006).
Usually, the group consists of peers required to work together by
sharing and discussing information to come to a decision. In
addition to AC-specific applications, LGDs are also used in other
settings, such as: classroom settings to demonstrate group processes and leader emergence, organizational settings as a teambuilding tool, and research settings to observe leader emergence
(Bass, 1954; Judge & Ilies, 2002).
Thornton (1992) distinguishes between cooperative and competitive LGDs. Consistent with past reviews (Bowler & Woehr,
2006), we were unable to examine whether the LGD was cooperative or competitive because of an insufficient number of studies
providing the information needed to make this determination.
Accordingly, both forms of LGDs were collapsed into a single
exercise type. However, in light of Lievens et al.’s (2006) findings
that subject matter experts (SMEs) evaluated both forms of LGD
as activating the same traits, this should not pose a substantial
limitation to the present results.

Role-Play
In RP exercises (also referred to as “interview simulations”),
participants typically have a one-on-one conversation with a role
player. The nature of the conversation varies, but often the role
player plays the part of a subordinate or customer, and the assesse
must conduct the meeting directed at resolving the issue (Thornton, 1992). In many cases, the situation involves effectively resolving personally challenging or volatile issues (e.g., delivering
negative feedback to an employee). Of course, interpersonal tasks
and, particularly, managing employees are defining features of
managerial work (Dierdorff et al., 2009). By eliciting behaviors
associated with resolving work problems through one-on-one
meetings, often with direct reports, RPs simulate an important
aspect of managerial work.

Case Analysis and Oral Presentation
We discuss the CA and OP together for two reasons. First, there is
less research investigating these exercises relative to LGDs, IBs, and
RPs. Second, the type of stimulus materials and the content of the
participant’s response is often quite similar across these exercises. In
both the CA and OP, the participant typically reads written material
“that describes an organizational problem and is then asked to prepare
a set of recommendations about it for higher management” (Thornton,
1992, p. 71). Thus, both the OP and the CA simulate common
elements in descriptions of managerial work such as time spent
engaging in processing information, strategic planning, and problem
solving (Mumford, Campion, & Morgeson, 2007). The key difference

The Validity of Assessment Center Exercises
Although voluminous AC research has discussed the magnitude,
meaning, and implications of the ubiquitous “exercise effect”
(Jackson, Lance, & Hoffman, 2012; Lance, 2008; Sackett & Dreher, 1982), scarce research has attended to the psychometric properties of AC exercises. Howard (2008) argues that by focusing
almost exclusively on the debate between the variance explained
by dimensions and exercises, AC research has neglected other
critical questions. It is ironic that in an era in which AC research
has heavily focused on exercise effects (Lance, 2012), very little
research has actually examined evidence of their psychometric
soundness (Howard, 2008; Lance, 2008; Lievens, 2009; Thornton
& Rupp, 2004). The importance of more fully considering the
psychometric properties of AC exercises is amplified by the recent
proposition that AC exercise variance reflects a substantively
meaningful and performance relevant information (Lance, 2008).
The first three sets of analyses in the present study focus on the
criterion-related validity of exercise scores. Whereas substantial research has attended to the criterion-related validity and nomological
network of dimensions and OARs (Arthur et al., 2003; Collins et al.,
2003; Dilchert & Ones, 2009; Gaugler et al., 1987; Meriac et al.,
2008), relatively limited attention has focused on the criterion-related
validity of AC exercises. Historically, exercises have been viewed as
passive measurement vehicles (Lance, 2012) and an inappropriate
scoring and interpretative framework (Arthur, Day, & Woehr, 2008;
Arthur & Villado, 2008; Rupp et al., 2008). Whereas closer inspection
of exercises has frequently been encouraged, surprisingly little research has done so (Howard, 2008; Lance, 2008).
In response to this deficiency, we provide an analysis of the
criterion-related validity of the set of five AC exercise types using a
meta-analytic regression framework. As described above, the frequently documented low levels of convergence across the five types
of AC exercises is consistent with the idea that these exercise types
are designed to reflect unique situations that simulate different managerial roles or tasks (Hoffman & Meade, 2012; Howard, 2008;
Lievens et al, 2009; Neidig & Neidig, 1984) and therefore elicit
distinct behaviors germane to managerial performance (Lievens, Tett,
& Schleicher, 2009). On the basis of theoretical and empirical evidence that the different exercise types capture unique situations relevant to the criterion domain, we expect each exercise type to explain
unique variance in criterion variables. Finally, we are unaware of any
past research examining the incremental validity of AC exercises
beyond GMA and the FFM. Therefore, an additional purpose is to
determine the extent to which the different types of exercises explain
unique variance over and above established predictors.

Nomological Network of Exercises
Next, conceptual ambiguity in exercise scores is a proposed hindrance to their interpretation (Arthur et al., 2008). As noted by
Spector, Schneider, Vance, and Hezlett (2000), “Research is needed
that explores the relations of AC ratings with constructs measured by

3

other sources so that we can better understand what constructs they
represent” (p. 1467). The nomological network of dimensions
(Dilchert & Ones, 2009; Goffin, Rothstein, & Johnston, 1996; Shore,
Thornton, & Shore, 1990) and, to a lesser extent, OARs (Collins et al.,
2003) have occasionally been examined by AC research. Yet, this
research has rarely been extended to the examination of exercises.
Consistent with the methods proposed by Cronbach and Meehl
(1955), we examined the overlap among exercise types and constructs
measured using other methods (e.g., paper-and-pencil measures) to
facilitate inferences as to the convergent and discriminant validity of
different exercise types.
A recent meta-analysis (Meriac et al., 2014) used socioanalytic
theory (SAT; J. Hogan & Holland, 2003; R. Hogan & Kaiser,
2005; R. Hogan & Shelton, 1998) as a framework to investigate
the nomological network of AC dimensions on the basis of their
correlations with the FFM and GMA. SAT proposes that two broad
motive patterns govern interpersonal interactions in organizations:
“Behavior designed to get along with other members of the group
and behaviors to get ahead or achieve status vis-à-vis other members of the group” (J. Hogan & Holland, 2003, p. 100). Previous
research mapped the FFM onto getting along and getting ahead
and documented expected patterns of relationships between these
traits and on-the-job behaviors (Bartram, 2005; Chiaburu, Oh,
Berry, Li, & Gardner, 2011; J. Hogan & Holland, 2003). Getting
ahead is a broad cluster of traits that predicts the proclivity to “take
initiative, seek responsibility, compete, and try to be recognized”
(J. Hogan & Holland, 2003, p. 101). Extraversion and Openness
are indicative of an orientation toward getting ahead (J. Hogan &
Holland, 2003) or agency, the related hierarchical factor from
Digman’s (1997) model. In contrast, individuals higher in getting
along are “evaluated by others as good team players, organizational citizens, and service providers” (J. Hogan & Holland, 2003,
p. 101). In FFM terms, Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and
Emotional Stability are conceptualized as indicators of getting
along or communality, as termed in Digman’s (1997)framework.
On the basis of SAT, Meriac et al. (2014) proposed that dimensions would differentially relate to GMA and personality indicators of getting along and getting ahead. Consistent with this expectation, dimensions showed significant correlations with GMA
and personality indicators of getting ahead (Extraversion and, to a
lesser extent, Openness to Experience), as predicted. Proposed
correlations with personality indicators of getting along (Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability), on the other
hand, were not substantiated. One potential implication of this
pattern of results is that ACs may not effectively measure and
predict behaviors indicative of getting along (Meriac et al., 2014).
Meriac et al. (2014) acknowledged that, given concerns regarding the construct-related validity of dimensions (Lance, 2008),
different results might have emerged had exercise scores been the
focus of analysis or that certain types of exercises might better
predict these behaviors. Although Lievens et al. (2006) used SMEs
to link the FFM and exercises, and these linkages do suggest that
some of the AC exercises should be more likely to elicit behaviors
indicative of getting along, these linkages have rarely been empirically scrutinized. Thus, this study seeks to solidify the nomological network of AC exercise by determining whether, on average,
the observed correlations are consistent with experts’ evaluations
of each exercise’s activating potential. Specifically, drawing from
trait activation theory (Lievens et al., 2006; Tett & Guterman,

Each type of exercise typically (but not necessarily) begins with

Meriac et al., 2008), leader effectiveness (Hoffman, Woehr,

interaction and to the focus on solving complex, multifaceted, and
often data-based problems. Consistent with this rationale, Lievens,
De Fruyt, and Van Dam, (2001) found that assessors’ notes in
written exercises primarily contained behaviors relevant to GMA.
Accordingly, we expect GMA to relate to performance across all
exercises and to relate particularly strongly to performance in IB
and CA exercises.

Hypothesis 2: GMA will be more strongly related to IB and

By presenting the candidate with an interpersonally oriented
leadership situation in which the participant is evaluated on the
basis of exerting influence over others, interpersonal exercises are
expected to activate behaviors that correspond to agency or getting
ahead, such as Extraversion and Openness to Experience. These
interpersonally oriented leadership roles typically require an individual to take charge in the form of offering negative feedback and
goal setting (the RP), having one’s suggestions adopted (the LGD),
and delivering a persuasive presentation (the OP), all behaviors
consistent with Digman’s (1997) interpretation of Extraversion
and Openness as indicative of a broader tendency toward agency
and achievement striving. In addition, performance in the interpersonal exercises also requires the cognitive flexibility to be
comfortable in complex and novel interpersonal situations described in DeYoung, Peterson, and Higgins’s (2002) plasticity
interpretation of Extraversion and Openness. We would expect
traits associated with plasticity to be associated with performance

activating potential for behaviors related to Extraversion and
Openness (Lievens et al., 2006). Thus, we expect the RP, LGD,
and OP to demonstrate positive associations with Extraversion and

simulation of a status-striving situation in which participants typically advocate a position and is commonly used as an indicator of
leader emergence (Judge, Bono, Ilies, & Gerhardt, 2002), we

Leader consideration and sensitivity behaviors are a key aspect
of implicit leadership theories (Epitropaki & Martin, 2005; Lord,
De Vader, & Alliger, 1986). Similarly, past studies show that
Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and to a lesser extent, Emotional Stability, correlate with relational and prosocial behaviors
on the job, such as leader consideration behaviors (DeRue, Nahrgang, Wellman, & Humphrey, 2011), transformational leadership
(Judge & Piccolo, 2004), leader effectiveness (Judge et al., 2002),
and more relational forms of citizenship behaviors (Chiaburu et al.,
2011). On this basis, we expect leadership simulations predicated
on interpersonal interactions to activate traits indicative of these
behaviors.
Although we expect getting ahead to predict performance in the
interpersonal simulations, we also expect FFM analogues of getting along to predict behavior in situations with high demands for
cooperative problem-solving, in this case, the RP and LGD. The
OP is a situation demanding one-way, rather than two-way communication, potentially limiting the demands for cooperation. In
contrast, the LGD and RP are assumed to be relatively interdependent situations, often scored on the basis of using cooperation,
negotiation, and compromise to solve problems. In support of this
suggestion, Conscientiousness, Emotional Stability, and Agreeableness tended to be evaluated as having high activating potential
in RPs and LGD by SMEs (Lievens et al., 2006).
Hypothesis 5: Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Emotional Stability will be more strongly related to performance in
the RP and LGD relative to the IB, CA, and OP.

Taxonomy of Assessment Center Exercise
Characteristics
Although these criterion-related validity and nomological network analyses will be instructive in evaluating the constructrelated validity of AC exercises, they are limited to describing only
the most popular types of exercises as they have commonly been
categorized in empirical and conceptual research over the past 30
years (Bowler & Woehr, 2006; Thornton & Byham, 1982). Although AC research has commonly grouped exercises based on
these types, there is also variability within these types. Recent
research has begun to examine more specific characteristics of AC
exercises (Lance, 2008; Lievens, 2009; Lievens, De Corte, &
Westerveld, 2012; Lievens, Tett, et al., 2009; Schollaert &
Lievens, 2012). This research has advanced AC research and
practice by directing attention to this neglected aspect of the AC
process. Yet, this research has tended to focus on relatively narrow, isolated exercise characteristics in single AC administrations
and has rarely considered the broader situational constructs by
which to group exercise characteristics.

informing the candidate of dimensions before the AC were conceptualized as situational factors that would reduce ambiguity and,

Next, we distinguish two features of social context, interpersonal demands and interdependence. Social context encompasses
interpersonal interactions and social characteristics (e.g., social
density, norms, and conflict) that affect role performance (Dierdorff et al., 2012; Johns, 2006; Strong, Jeanneret, McPhail, Blakey,
& D’Egidio, 1999), and can vary in intensity from the mere
presence of others to strong interpersonal influence (Dierdorff et
al., 2012). Dierdorff et al. (2009) argued that social context represents the dominant characteristic of managerial work. The simulation of interpersonal interactions is a defining feature of the AC
method, and exercises that require interpersonal behaviors are
expected to capture key social skills. For our purposes, interpersonal demands refer to any situation that requires participants to
interact with others (e.g., delivering a presentation, having a conversation with a customer, and delivering negative feedback).
Exceeding mere interpersonal interaction, interdependence is a
feature of social context that requires individuals to take on reciprocal, connected roles to meet their obligations (Dierdorff et al.,
2012). The distinction between interpersonal interactions and interdependence may seem subtle; interdependent contexts inherently entail social demands but also require candidates to collaborate with others and demonstrate collective responsibility (e.g.,
helping a coworker complete a task; Dierdorff et al., 2012). In our
analysis of AC characteristics, situations that prime conflict rather
than cooperative behaviors were coded as lower in interdependence. Additionally, consistent with Fiedler’s (1971) classic least
preferred coworker contingency model of leadership, position
power was coded as reducing the requirement to cooperate. On
other hand, situations that required interacting with peers and
working cooperatively (e.g., reaching consensus) were coded as
high in interdependence.
The final contextual characteristic, fidelity, is unique in that it is
not relevant to describing work context per se. However, fidelity,
or the degree of similarity between the simulated situation and that
of the focal context, is a key characteristic of assessment measures
and thus, is relevant to this framework (Ployhart, Schneider, &
Schmitt, 2006). We coded fidelity as, overall, the extent to which
the exercises clearly simulated tasks of relevance to the target job
context. For instance, exercises based on a job analysis and exercises simulating the same industry, occupation, and organization
were conceptualized as having higher fidelity to the target role.
Fidelity has been the subject of much discussion and multiple
frameworks in recent years, including multifaceted conceptualizations (Lievens & Patterson, 2011; Thornton & Kedharnath, 2013).
Ployhart et al. (2006) distinguished between physical and psychological fidelity, with psychological fidelity reflecting a match between the psychological situation of the test and the criterion
domain and physical fidelity reflecting the match between the
physical behaviors required to complete the test and those required

for effective performance in the criterion domain. In recognition of
more multifaceted conceptualizations of fidelity, we also attempted to code physical and psychological fidelity.

Method
Literature Search
Both computer and manual searches were conducted for empirical studies, including data relevant to the criterion-related validity
and nomological network of the five types of AC exercises. We
searched online databases (Web of Science, Web of Knowledge,
PsycINFO, and Business Source Complete) using the keywords
assessment center, assessment center exercise, in-basket, in-tray,
leaderless group discussion, role-play, case analysis, problem
analysis, written analysis, analytical problem, customer meeting,
presentation, and competitive allocation exercise. Manual searches
of the reference list of past reviews (e.g., Bass, 1954; Lievens,
Tett, et al., 2009; Schippmann, Prien, & Katz, 1990) were also
conducted to locate additional studies. We also examined the
Society for Industrial and Organizational Psychology Conference
Program from 2006 to 2011 and requested papers from authors
presenting AC research. This initial search yielded 203 published
and 46 unpublished studies.
Studies were included in the meta-analysis if correlations were
reported between performance in IB, LGD, RP, OP, or CA exercises and (a) criterion variables, including job performance, manager potential, and training performance; (b) GMA; and/or (c) a
personality construct. Forty-nine published studies and nine unpublished studies met the inclusion criteria (k ⫽ 69, N ⫽ 13,290).
The studies and the data drawn from each study are reported in the
Appendix.

Coding of Studies
Three coders coded the studies, and all data points were coded
by at least two independent coders. Coders recorded the exercise
type, sample size, scale reliabilities, and correlations between
relevant variables. Each simulation exercise in the included studies
was coded as belonging to one of the six types of exercises
identified by Thornton (1992) and used in prior reviews (Bowler &
Woehr, 2006; Lievens et al., 2006). The coders used the exercise
descriptions provided by Lievens et al. (2006) to code simulation
exercises as cooperative LGD, competitive LGD, RP, IB, CA, or
OP. Unfortunately, LGDs were rarely distinguished on the basis of
content; thus, and consistent with past reviews (Bowler & Woehr,
2006), we collapsed cooperative and competitive LGDs into a
single exercise type.
Consistent with reviews by Meriac and colleagues (2008, 2014),
personality constructs were coded as Neuroticism, Extraversion,
Openness to Experience, Agreeableness, or Conscientiousness,
using Barrick and Mount’s (1991) coding scheme. Criterion measures were coded as ratings of job performance or other criterion
measures (external ratings of potential, salary or promotion rate).
Second, we coded whether the exercise was part of an AC. For
instance, stand-alone LGDs or case analyses were coded as not
being part of a formal AC process. Finally, we recorded whether
the exercise explicitly included dimensions in the scoring or were

high agreement (86%). Disagreements were resolved via a consensus discussion. The final list of studies and the sample size,

Meta-Analysis Method
We used an adapted version of SAS PROC MEANS (Arthur,
Bennett, & Huffcutt, 2001) to conduct the primary analyses. The
effect sizes were individually weighted by the sample size and
corrected for attenuation due to predictor and criterion unreliability
(Hunter & Schmidt, 2004; Raju, Burke, Normand, & Langlois,
1991). To maintain independence of data, whenever an individual
study contained two or more of the same bivariate relationships
(e.g., the relationship between an LGD exercises and two measures
of GMA), the corresponding correlations were averaged. Accordingly, each individual sample contributed only one data point per
relationship. We also report the 80% credibility interval around the
estimated mean population relationship, 95% confidence intervals
around the average sample correlation, and the Q statistic. When
interpreting the 80% credibility interval, 90% of the estimated true
validities are above the lower bound 80% credibility value. A
confidence interval built around the average correlation indicates
the consistency of the estimate. The Q statistic approximates a ␹2
distribution and gives an indication of the degree of homogeneity
in population relationship. A significant Q statistic suggests that
the observed effects are sufficiently heterogeneous to indicate the
presence of moderators.
When reliability information was not reported, an artifact distribution was constructed. When a study did not report reliability
for performance, personality, or GMA, the average internal consistency reliability reported in the primary studies was used. Unfortunately, reliability information was rarely reported for the
exercises. When reliability information was not provided for AC
exercises, we used the meta-analytic values of the correlations
among postexercise dimension ratings within each type of exercise
provided by Bowler and Woehr (2006), corrected using the
Spearman–Brown prophecy formula to account for the number of
indicators. This yielded an internal consistency estimate for overall
scores on each exercise type. In essence, this approach acknowledges the high intercorrelation among dimensions by treating each
dimension rating from a given exercise as an indicator and aggregates these to form a single reliability estimate for the overall
exercise score.
Next, given that ACs are an expensive tool, participation in ACs
is often reserved for relatively high-potential or effective managers, and candidates are often prescreened into the AC process on
the basis of less expensive measures, such as personality and
GMA. Thus, and consistent with recent AC meta-analyses (Arthur
et al., 2003; Meriac et al., 2008), we also corrected the relationship
between ACs and outcomes for predictor range restriction on the
basis of the range restriction estimate provided by Gaugler et al.
(1987).
To examine the incremental validity of exercises, we constructed a partially complete 12 ⫻ 12 correlation matrix that
included the correlations between each exercise type with (a) the
other exercises, (b) FFM personality dimensions, (c) GMA, and (d)
criterion variables. This matrix was partially complete because we
did not record the correlations between the FFM, GMA, and

Taxonomy of Exercise Characteristics
We based the five exercise characteristics and their definitions
(see Table 1) on recent research investigating the context of
managerial work (Dierdorff & Ellington, 2008; Dierdorff et al.,
2009) and AC research on exercise characteristics (Lievens, 2009;
Lievens, Dilchert, et al., 2009). We developed indicators of each
characteristic based on the aforementioned resources and deductively on the basis of our collective experience researching and
administering ACs (see Table 1). To ensure the content validity of
the exercise characteristics and associated indicators, two SMEs
and leading AC scholar-practitioners reviewed the content of the
taxonomy. 1 These experts agreed that the taxonomy adequately
described key features of AC exercises but signaled a potential
deficiency in the absence of technology from the taxonomy. On
this basis, we added two indicators of technology to the complexity
characteristic.
We next gathered all available exercise information from the
primary studies. In total, 174 different exercises were reported.
Three coders coded descriptions of each of the 174 exercises by
rating each indicator and by providing an overall rating of each of
the five exercise characteristics. For every exercise, at least two
coders indicated whether each indicator was present in the exercise, not present in the exercise, or whether there was insufficient
information to make this determination. The coders reported that
the taxonomy captured the content of the exercise descriptions and
did not note any deficiencies. The average percent agreement
across the indicators was satisfactory (77%). Coders were also
asked to provide an overall rating for “On the basis of the definition, indicators, and your understanding of the construct, to what
extent is this characteristic present in the exercise?” using a scale
ranging from 1 (Not at all) to 5 (To a very great extent). However,
the coders were unable to reliably rate the five overall exercise
characteristics (mean interrater correlation ⫽ .50). In light of this
unreliability, our analysis focuses on the indicators only; we report
the overall scores for the sake of comprehensiveness.

in all five exercises and was the strongly personality correlate of

Hypothesis 4 was partially supported on the based on the relatively

notably, the OP correlated similarly with Extraversion.
Finally, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Neuroticism

not evidence notable correlations with the LGD or RP. Accordingly, Hypothesis 5 was rejected. Notably, although not hypothesized, Conscientiousness was the strongest personality correlate of
the IB (␳ ⫽ .15). Together, these results implicate a nomological
network of AC exercises consisting of GMA, Extraversion, and to
a lesser extent, Openness to Experience and potentially Conscientiousness but not Agreeableness or Emotional Stability.

Taxonomy of Exercise Characteristics
The third overarching question concerned the characteristics of
AC exercises, and we responded to this question by using the
taxonomy presented in Table 1 to code characteristics of the 174
exercises described in the primary studies. As reflected by the
frequency with which coders endorsed “Unable to Code,” there
was often insufficient information to describe the exercise. However, some discernable trends did emerge. First, some evidence for
complexity was found. For instance, 18% of the exercises reported
high information content, requiring candidates to synthesize an
average of 16 unique sources of information. In addition, on the
basis of available information, there was evidence of a lack of

structure, with the exercises offering multiple approaches to complete the task and participants being unlikely to be asked direct
questions via a response questionnaire.
Next, interpersonal interaction frequently characterized the simulated tasks. Although there was evidence for interpersonal interaction in the exercise descriptions, there was less evidence for
interdependent situations simulated in the exercises. The demand
for cooperation, a defining aspect of interdependence, was present
in less than one quarter of the exercises. Similarly, exercises were
rarely reported to have simulated interactions involving customers,
peers, or across functional organizational units, all of which are
key interdependent situations that necessitate a collaborative approach to problem-solving.
Finally, physical and psychological fidelity was relatively high
on the basis of available information. That is, a substantial proportion of the exercises simulated situations that incumbents
would be required to perform on the job and occurred in a similar
industry and/or occupation. It is surprising to note that an equal
proportion of exercises were based on a job analysis as those that
were not based on a job analysis. This stemmed from exercises that
assessed student samples or managers from different jobs or occupations, negating the possibility for a formal, job-specific, job
analysis. Despite this unexpected finding, overall, there was evidence of fidelity across many of the exercises.

Discussion
Exercises introduce substantial variance to AC ratings (Lance,
Lambert, et al., 2004; Lievens & Conway, 2001; Sackett & Dreher,
1982), and the need to better understand their functioning has been
acknowledged for decades (Lance, 2008; Lievens, Dilchert, et al.,
2009; Neidig & Neidig, 1984; Thornton, 1992; Rupp et al., 2008;

Sackett & Dreher, 1982). In response, this study provides a metaanalysis of the criterion-related validity and nomological network
of five types of simulation exercises commonly used in ACs, a
taxonomy of exercise characteristics, and a preliminary description
of key situational characteristics of AC exercises. On the basis of
these analyses, we respond to the three overarching questions
posed in the introduction. First, we use the meta-analytic evidence
to evaluate the criterion-related validity of exercises in the context
of the ongoing debate between dimension and exercise-based
approaches to design, scoring, and interpretation of ACs. Second,
we discuss the nomological network underlying the exercise types
relative to the network underlying dimensions. Third, we highlight
trends in our analysis of exercise characteristics. Together, these
insights frame a discussion of the role of simulation exercises in
AC research and practice.

First, the majority of the studies included in this review were
based on ACs that were explicitly designed to be scored on the
basis of dimensions. Thus, the weaker effects might have occurred
because these ACs were not designed to be scored around exercises. Related to this possibility, collapsing across all dimensions,
as is the case with exercise-based scoring (Lance, Foster, et al.,
2004), might result in the loss of important information about
differential weighting of different dimensions in different exercises. For instance, in consensus or mechanical scoring, problemsolving skills often receive the highest weight in IBs, whereas
dimensions such as customer service are more ancillary. Reconceptualizing overall exercise performance as dimensional performance that is specific to the exercise might show stronger correlations than those reported here (Hoffman & Meade, 2012; Putka
& Hoffman, 2013).
As suggested by an anonymous reviewer, the observed differences might also be due to differences in the studies sampled
across our review and parallel reviews (Arthur et al., 2003; Gaugler et al., 1987; Meriac et al., 2008). We agree with this possibility, as only one study was in common between the present
review and that by Meriac et al. (2008). Thus, we encourage
research directly comparing exercises and dimensions in the same
study, rather than taking an either-or approach to interpreting AC
ratings. This can be accomplished tasking a multifaceted approach
to AC ratings, such as using overall dimension and overall exercise
scores or possibly dimensions within exercises in AC research.
Next, exercise ratings at least partly include assessor variance
(e.g., Putka & Hoffman, 2013). Confounding idiosyncratic assessor bias with exercise scores might also account for the stronger
exercise effects in the structure of ACs but weaker correlations
with the external criterion variables observed here. Related to this
explanation, it could be that the exercises were valid because they
included measures of dimensions, as most of the exercise scores
were based on ACs in which dimensions were explicitly assessed.
However, it is possible that aggregating to the exercise level
introduces various sources of error (e.g., assessor effects), thereby
reducing criterion-related validity. Finally, criterion measures are
typically structured around dimensions (Borman & Brush, 1993;
Smith, Organ, & Near, 1983). Thus, using exercises, rather than
dimensions, may actually reduce the fidelity of AC scores to
common criterion measures.
Regardless of the reason for the weaker criterion-related validity
of exercises, using exercises rather than dimensions or OAR resulted in up to almost a 50% reduction in criterion-related validity,
at least based on a comparison with past reviews. This large-scale
comparison is important in light of recent arguments to remove
dimensions from the design and interpretation of ACs (Jackson,
2012; Lance, 2012). Specifically, based largely on the results of
internal structure research showing that exercises explain more
variance than dimensions (Lance, Lambert, et al., 2004), recent AC
research has argued that traditional dimension-based design, scoring, and interpretation should be replaced by a focus on exercises
in the design and scoring of ACs (Jackson, Stillman, & Atkins,
2005; Jackson, Stillman, & Englert, 2010; Lance, 2008). Collectively, the present findings suggest that these calls are premature
and that focusing on exercises at the expense of dimensions would

be to the detriment of the predictive efficacy of the method, at least
based on available data. As we outline in the implications, the
present findings, in conjunction with recent research, point to a
multifaceted perspective that considers both dimensions and exercises in the design, interpretation, and scoring of ACs (Hoffman,
2012; Hoffman, Melchers, Blair, Kleinmann, & Ladd, 2011).

AC exercises. First, the correlations with GMA and especially the
FFM were notably weak and, thus, show that exercises are not
redundant with the FFM and GMA. However, in nomological
network analyses, the pattern can be just as informative as the
magnitude, and the pattern of results yielded partial support for the
proposed nomological network of the five exercise types.
As expected, GMA was related to each of the five types of AC
exercises and particularly the IB. These findings are quite consistent with Meriac et al.’s (2014) findings that GMA was related to
all seven dimensions from Arthur et al.’s (2003) framework but
especially problem-solving– oriented dimensions. As such, both
dimensions and exercises received similar levels of nomological
network support on the basis of their overlap with GMA.
On the basis of Hogan’s SAT (Hogan & Shelton, 1998), we
expected the LGD, RP, and OP to correlate more strongly with
Extraversion and Openness than the CA and IB. Consistent with
this expectation, either Openness or Extraversion was the strongest
personality correlate of performance in each of the three interpersonal exercises, providing some evidence that these exercises
simulate the status striving behaviors associated with the drive to
get ahead. In contrast, Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and
Emotional Stability were consistently unrelated to the five exercise
types, including the three interpersonally oriented exercise types.
Together, these analyses provided some support for the differential
nomological network of the exercise types. But the more pronounced trend was that, to the extent that exercises correlated with
the FFM, they tended to overlap more with the FFM indicators that
capture an interpersonal-style oriented toward getting ahead rather
than getting along.
It is interesting that Meriac et al.’s (2014) meta-analysis of
dimensions drew very similar conclusions. AC dimensions tended
to correlate with GMA, Extraversion, and to a lesser extent,
Openness, but they showed almost no correlation with Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability. On one hand,
the correspondence between these large-scale reviews is encouraging for the use of exercise scores. Specifically, the similarity in
nomological networks responds to the suggestion to take an external approach to construct validation (Rupp et al., 2008; Spector
et al., 2000) and in doing so, further refutes the idea that exercises
are a meaningless unit of analysis (Arthur et al., 2008). Instead, at
least on the basis of correlations with the FFM and GMA, exercises seem to share a similar nomological network as dimensions.
Yet, these findings also suggest a considerably less nuanced nomological network of both dimensions and exercises than that
proposed by SMEs (Lievens et al., 2006). Accordingly, the presumed nomological network of both exercises and dimensions
should be further scrutinized and potentially reconciled on the
basis of the empirical nomological network.

correlations with FFM indicators of getting along, across largescale reviews of the nomological network of exercises and dimen-

average AC does not correlate with known personality predictors
of prorelational orientation and prosocial behaviors, such as Conscientiousness and Agreeableness (Bartram, 2005; Chiaburu et al.,

commonly linked to leader consideration behaviors (DeRue et al.,
2011) and organizational citizenship behaviors (Chiaburu et al.,
2011), these findings point to a potential deficiency in the method.
Indeed, a recent review supported leader consideration as the most
important behavioral predictor of effective leadership (DeRue et
al., 2011) and one that is proposed to be increasing in importance
in the modern world of work (Dierdorff et al., 2009). The weak
associations between Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability and ACs might reflect the idea that ACs are akin to
measures of maximum performance, in that the exercises are brief
in duration, are explicitly evaluated, and assessees are aware of
and accept implicit instructions to do their best (Sackett, 2007). It
is possible that in this type of situation assessees will exert high
levels of effort, behave ethically, and treat others with dignity and
respect, reducing range and, thus, the potential to derive meaningful evaluations of these dimensions.
In response to the third overarching question, we created a
taxonomy of exercise characteristics and used this taxonomy to
provide a preliminary analysis of 174 different exercises that were
used in the meta-analysis. Consistent with Arthur et al.’s (2003)
observation regarding dimensions, exercise information is drastically underreported. Indeed, the vast majority of indicators could
not be coded because of insufficient information, and available
information was insufficient for two trained coders to provide
reliable overall ratings of the five exercise characteristics. Typically, brief or incidental descriptions of exercises were provided,
with little discussion of the specific situational and task characteristics. Accordingly, we urge AC researchers to provide a more
thorough description of exercise characteristics. One approach is to
incorporate the framework developed here as an appendix in AC
studies to better describe the characteristics of AC exercises.
Although a useful first step to organize emerging research on
exercise characteristics, the taxonomy forwarded here is only a
first step and must be further scrutinized and revised via continued
empirical study.
Despite sparse reporting in the primary studies, some meaningful trends emerged. First, across the various exercise types, evidence of fidelity, complexity, and ambiguity (low structure) was
observed. The finding of high levels of fidelity is encouraging,
given that fidelity is a frequently listed strength of the AC method
(Lievens & Schollaert, 2011; Thornton & Kedharnath, 2013).
Although job analysis was commonly reported, many of the same
exercises were administered to managers from different organizational levels and organizations, meaning that the exercises were
not necessarily based on a formal job analysis. Thus, although
high, the level of fidelity might be quite variable across exercise
administrations, and we encourage continued analysis of exercise
fidelity. The evidence for complexity is consistent with the cognitive demands facing managers and with our findings that GMA
is among the strongest predictors of performance in AC exercises.
Similarly, the evidence for unstructured and ambiguous situations
supports that this critical aspect of managerial work (Mintzberg,
1973) is simulated to some extent in AC exercises.

Next, evidence for interpersonal interaction in the three interpersonal exercises is consistent with the central role that interpersonal interactions play in modern managerial work (Dierdorff et
al., 2009). However, less evidence was found for interdependence.
That is, although interpersonal in nature, few situations required
working cooperatively to complete goals. Instead, interpersonal
simulations commonly imbue the role player with position power,
which reduces the demands for interdependence. This might be
one potential explanation for the weak association between Conscientiousness and Agreeableness and the exercises: These exercises do not simulate interdependent contexts and the associated
“getting along” behaviors.
Although some evidence for interdependence stemmed from the
instructions to reach consensus and peer-based interactions in
LGDs, anecdotally, many LGDs unfold with the majority of the
time spent debating the focal issue and a relatively small amount
of time at the end spent hurriedly devising an agreed-upon decision. In other words, although instructions require consensus, it is
possible that because a later meeting is not possible and the
decision has no real world impact, consensus can be easily
reached, thus precluding the detection of cooperative behaviors.
This explanation is consistent with the finding that Extraversion
tends to be the strongest predictor of leadership in LGDs and of
interpersonal and drive dimensions (Meriac et al., 2014), findings
stemming from other literatures that that Extraversion predicts
leader emergence but not necessarily leader effectiveness (Judge et
al., 2002), and that more destructive manifestations of Extraversion, such as narcissism, predict performance in LGDs (Brunell et
al., 2008).
Given the increasing importance of interdependence in work
settings (Wood, Hoffman, & Twenge, 2012) and the increasing
importance of relationship building for managers in particular
(Gentry, Harris, Baker, & Leslie, 2008), the minimal evidence for
interdependence in the exercise types is troubling. As levels of
management have decreased and organizations have moved to
team-based structures, the development of long-term and productive working relationships without position power is an increasingly important part of work (Wood et al., 2012). One potential
implication of these findings is that AC exercises explicitly designed to better capture cooperative and interdependent situations
might help to elicit these behaviors. Accordingly, we encourage
future research to focus specifically on whether simulation exercises and AC dimensions are valid predictors of this type of
behaviors and if not, how they might be redesigned to do so more
effectively.

Implications and Directions for Future Research
This review provides the most comprehensive analysis to date of
the psychometric properties of exercise types and, in doing so,
offers several implications for AC research and practice. First, by
providing construct and criterion-related validity for different
types of AC exercises, the findings codify exercises as a meaningful unit of analysis for AC research. This insight is important as
it provides a key piece of evidence underscoring the controversial
exercise effect. Yet, consistent with the minimal reporting of
exercise characteristics found here, exercises are rarely explicitly
examined in AC research. Thus, the primary implication of supporting the validity of exercises is that practitioners should con-

Despite the key role that exercises play, it is important to note
that the criterion-related validity of exercises in this review was
lower than parallel reviews of dimensions (Arthur et al., 2003;
Meriac et al., 2008). From this perspective, it is critical that
research and practice continue to rely on dimensions in AC design
and interpretation. However, although it might be tempting to
dismiss exercise-based interpretations as inappropriate, our results
suggest this would be premature. Along with recent primary data
showing that that both exercises and dimensions account for
unique criterion variance (Hoffman, Melchers, et al., 2011;
Lievens, Dilchert, & Ones, 2009), the present meta-analysis underscores exercises as substantively meaningful and performance
relevant. Whereas AC research and practice has traditionally focused on dimensions, it is important to also consider exercises in
design and interpretation.
Accordingly, we recommend that future research and practice
take a multifaceted perspective on AC performance (Hoffman,
2012), where dimensions, exercises, and their interactions are
considered in AC design, scoring, and interpretation. For instance,
overall dimension scores, overall exercise scores, and performance
on dimensions within exercises (Hoffman & Meade, 2012; Putka
& Hoffman, 2013) can be a useful strategy to capture insights
about dimension- and exercise-based performance. Likewise, AC
designers should devote greater attention to the situational characteristics underlying AC exercises and their effectiveness in simulating key characteristics of the focal job (Lievens, Tett, &
Schleicher, 2009). The taxonomy provided here can be used to
guide AC design.

Limitations
As always, the contributions of this study must be viewed in
light of some limitations. First, we were only able to classify
exercises based on five broad types of exercises. As noted by
Lievens, Tett, and Schleicher (2009), the vast majority of research
has conceptualized exercises based on the type of situation the
exercise presents, and reviews have used Thornton’s (1992) classification of five exercises (Bowler & Woehr, 2006; Lievens et al.,
2006). Nevertheless, like dimensions and OARs, exercises of the
same type often differ in a variety of ways. Indeed, our preliminary
analysis of exercise characteristics suggests such variability. Unfortunately, because of sparse reporting of exercise information,
we were unable to examine moderation by these exercise characteristics. We encourage future research to scrutinize the potential
moderating role of exercise characteristics on the psychometric
properties of AC ratings.
A second limitation is that the present study did not directly
account for dimensions. Unfortunately, insufficient information is
typically presented in a given study to account for both dimensions and exercises. However, recent research has examined
both simultaneously and found that dimension and exercise
factors account for unique variance in performance (Hoffman,
Melchers, et al., 2011). As this type of research matures, we
encourage future reviews to directly compare the role of dimensions to exercises in ACs.

Finally, some of the relationships were based on a small number
of studies, particularly for the correlations between the CA and OP
and individual differences. However, all of the relationships with
criterion variables were based on at least nine independent samples. In addition, the IB, RP, and LGD, three of the more popular
AC exercises (Spychalski et al., 1997), had at least three independent samples contributing to each relationship (mean k ⫽ 10).
Furthermore, the number of studies used in this meta-analysis is on
par with that in other reviews of the AC literature (e.g., Meriac et
al., 2008).

Conclusions
This study presents a large-scale analysis of the criterion-related
validity, nomological network, and content of managerial simulation exercises. The findings indicate that, although exercise scores
might be used as a supplement to more traditional dimensionbased scores, replacing dimensions with exercises would be a
mistake. Future research is encouraged to more closely consider
the scoring and interpretation of AC exercises, their characteristics, and the extent to which they adequately capture prosocial and
prorelational behaviors.
