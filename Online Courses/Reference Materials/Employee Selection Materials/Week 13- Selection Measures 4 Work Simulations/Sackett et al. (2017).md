Assessment Centers Versus Cognitive Ability Tests: Challenging the
Conventional Wisdom on Criterion-Related Validity
Paul R. Sackett, Oren R. Shewach, and Heidi N. Keiser

Separate meta-analyses of the cognitive ability and assessment center (AC) literatures report higher
criterion-related validity for cognitive ability tests in predicting job performance. We instead focus on 17
samples in which both AC and ability scores are obtained for the same examinees and used to predict the
same criterion. Thus, we control for differences in job type and in criteria that may have affected prior
conclusions. In contrast to Schmidt and Hunter’s (1998) meta-analysis, reporting mean validity of .51 for
ability and .37 for ACs, we found using random-effects models mean validity of .22 for ability and .44
for ACs using comparable corrections for range restriction and measurement error in the criterion. We
posit that 2 factors contribute to the differences in findings: (a) ACs being used on populations already
restricted on cognitive ability and (b) the use of less cognitively loaded criteria in AC validation research.
Keywords: assessment center, cognitive ability, validation, job performance

In a nutshell, the argument we will develop is as follows, and
pertains to why a head-to-head comparison of AC and ability will
yield different results than comparing separate meta-analytic estimates of each: (a) Ability tests are commonly used to predict
narrower, more cognitively loaded criteria than ACs; (b) ACs are
broader than ability tests in the constructs they assess and are
typically used to predict similarly broad criteria; (c) there is an
emerging body of research showing lower ability test validity
against broader criteria than against narrow, cognitively loaded
criteria; and (d) samples are differentially affected by range restriction for ACs than for ability tests, and validity must be
examined in reference to the same test-taking population for both.
Below, we develop each of these four points.

A long-standing puzzle regarding assessment centers (ACs) is
the meta-analytic conclusion that general mental ability produces
higher mean criterion related validity than ACs (mean corrected
r ⫽ .51 vs. .37; Schmidt & Hunter, 1998). Based on these findings,
it is quite reasonable to question why a very expensive AC would
be used, when a much less expensive and notably less timeconsuming ability test fares considerably better.
In this article, we revisit the validity of these two types of
predictors, based on methodological concerns that lead us to question the current findings. Crucially, we note that the evidence for
the criterion-related validity of ability tests and ACs comes from
meta-analyses of largely nonoverlapping studies. Comparing metaanalytic results requires strong assumptions, namely that the samples, settings, and criterion measures used are either perfectly
comparable for the two predictors being compared, or that differences in samples, settings, and criterion measures used for the two
predictors have no systematic effect on validity. What we will
show is that these strong assumptions are not met—samples,
settings, and criteria differ for the two predictors. In order to
control for differences in samples, settings, and criteria, we adopt
a head-to-head comparison approach: We identify studies in which
both ability tests and ACs are used with the same sample and
validated against the same criteria. Below, we develop a conceptual argument as to why we expect a very different set of findings
in these head-to-head comparisons.

Ability Tests Are Commonly Validated Against
Narrow, Cognitively Loaded Criteria
For ability test validation, the dimensions chosen for inclusion
are generally narrower and focus on more cognitively oriented task
proficiency factors than in studies of AC validity. Schmidt and
Hunter’s value of .51 as the validity of cognitive ability tests is
drawn from Hunter’s (1983) analysis of 425 validity studies on the
use of the General Aptitude Test Battery (GATB) to predict job
performance criteria. Hunter (1983) offered no detail as to the
criterion measures used, but we have the original technical reports
for 26 of the GATB validity studies in our files. Most studies make
use of supervisory ratings on what was termed the Descriptive
Rating Scale, which included nine items: quality of work, quantity
of work, pace of work, job knowledge, adeptness in performing the
job, ability to perform a variety of job tasks, level of complexity of
satisfactorily performed job duties, problem solving, and overall
accomplishment. Some studies used only a subset of these nine
items. All items in the Descriptive Rating Scale focus on individual task performance. In our judgment, these dimensions can be

ACs Assess Broader Constructs Than Cognitive
Ability Tests, and Are Typically Used to Predict
Similarly Broad Criteria
In contrast to this narrow focus on cognitively loaded aspects of
task performance, the AC literature typically focuses on a much
broader range of criteria. As will be detailed later, the present
article focuses on 17 AC validation studies. ACs assess broad
dimensions, with AC dimensions in this set of validation studies
ranging from four to 25. Common AC dimensions in our database
are influence, consideration, drive, organization, and communication. Similarly, the vast majority of these validation studies involve
criteria of supervisory ratings on an extensive set of dimensions,
with the number of dimensions ranging from seven to 22. Common
criterion dimensions include leadership, communication, initiative,
judgment, conflict management, teamwork, job knowledge, selfdiscipline, motivating others, and organization. This is clearly a
broader performance space than the dimensions used in the GATB
validity studies described above. While the criterion dimensions
rated in the GATB studies focus on the task proficiency aspects of
Campbell’s (2012) taxonomy of performance components, these
AC criterion dimensions incorporate a number of Campbell’s
performance components, including communication, facilitating
team and peer performance, supervision/leadership, and management/administration. In short, the current literature compares how
well cognitive ability tests predict task proficiency with how well
ACs predict a broad composite of performance components.

Ability Tests Show Lower Validity Against
Broader Criteria
Gonzalez-Mulé, Mount, and Oh (2014) used Rotundo and Sackett’s (2002) formulation of overall performance as a composite of
task performance, citizenship, and counterproductive work behavior (reversed scored), and integrated separate meta-analyses of the
relationship between ability and these three criteria. They report
mean cognitive ability validity of ␳ ⫽ .69 for task performance
versus ␳ ⫽ .42 for a composite of the three criteria (the higher
value for task performance than in the Schmidt-Hunter analysis is
the result of use of an alternate approach to range restriction
correction). In other words, criterion-related validity was 39%
lower against a broad criterion composite than against task performance alone. Thus, the comparison of mean ability validity
with mean AC validity is suspect, as the criteria are not comparable in breadth, and ability tests are now known to produce lower
validity in predicting broader criteria.

Validity Must Be Examined in Reference to the Same
Test-Taking Population for Both Predictors
A concern with comparing ability and AC validity is the assumption of comparable samples. Both the ability and AC literatures make use of range restriction corrections. But if ability
validity and AC validity are to be meaningfully compared, both
must be corrected to a common applicant population. However,
this is not the case in research to date. A key issue here is that the

pool of candidates for ACs will commonly show smaller variance
in cognitive ability than the national workforce level variance used
as the correction factor in the Schmidt and Hunter (1998) findings.
One reason for smaller ability variance is that the candidate pool
for any individual job will generally show a smaller ability range
than the workforce as a whole due to self-selection (Sackett &
Ostgaard, 1994). Another reason for smaller ability variance is that
ACs are often used with internal candidates for promotion who
have been screened on cognitive ability at the earlier point of hire.
When addressing the question of the relative predictive power of
ACs versus ability tests in this context, correction for prior selection on the basis of cognitive ability would be inappropriate, as this
ability-restricted pool now constitutes the full applicant pool available for screening with the AC. Meaningful correction needs to be
applied to this pool of applicants who are to be potentially
screened with an AC: an argument that an ability test would
predict better than an AC must be in reference to a common
applicant pool.

The Head-to-Head Comparison as an Alternative
Strategy for Comparing Predictors
In light of these difficulties in comparing ability and AC validity, in this study, we limit the comparison to studies in which both
measures were used with the same samples, from the same jobs
and with the same criterion measures. In such settings, results are
more directly comparable. The issues outlined above (i.e., broader
criteria for ACs and a narrower range of ability test scores in AC
applicant pools than in the national population) led to the central
hypothesis of this study:
Hypothesis 1: If ACs and ability tests are used with the same
samples to predict the broad criteria that ACs are intended to
predict, ability tests will show lower criterion-related validity
than overall ability meta-analyses indicate, and the validity
advantage of ability tests over ACs will disappear.

Issues With Construct-Method Comparisons
We note that the practical issue of whether to use an ability test
versus an AC involves a comparison of a construct (ability) and a
method (AC). Arthur and Villado (2008) noted the difficulty of
making such a comparison using meta-analytic data, as a method
can be used to assess a variety of constructs. ACs, for example, can
differ in the dimensions they are designed to assess. Thus, a simple
comparison of mean validity for a method versus a construct is not
informative as to the value of any specific AC. For research
purposes, better explication of the constructs underlying any given
application of the AC method is needed. In practice, however,
decisions need to be made about predictor use, and questions, such
as whether better criterion-related validity against a given criterion
in a given setting is more likely using an AC or an ability test, will
be asked.
We note that meta-analytic findings can be used in different
ways. One use is as the evidentiary basis for claiming validity for
a specific AC application. Here we agree that the mean value for
a predictor method is not useful absent insight into the underlying
constructs. But a second use is in helping practitioners identify
predictors for consideration for use. If for a given predictor method

We take several approaches to addressing the potential for
variability in AC validity as a function of differences in how this
measurement method is instantiated. First, for all studies that
compare ability and AC, we report as much information as is
available about AC dimensions included and examine whether
criterion-related validity varies with these dimensions. We used
Arthur, Day, McNelly, and Edens’ (2003) classification of AC
dimensions into seven overarching dimensions to categorize AC
dimensions. Their dimensions are Consideration, Communication,
Drive, Influence, Organizing/Planning, Problem Solving, and
Stress Tolerance. Second, we also categorize the criteria against
which the ACs are validated in terms of these same seven dimensions and examine whether criterion-related validity varies with
the dimensionality of the criteria used. Third, in addition to reporting mean validity for ability tests and AC ratings, we report
credibility intervals to give insight into the range of values that
may occur as a result of variations in implementing the predictor
method.

Study Overview
We examined the relative predictive power of AC overall
ratings and cognitive ability tests in a head-to-head comparison
in the population of individuals being administered an AC,
asking the question of whether conventional wisdom that ability
tests are the superior predictor would still hold in such headto-head comparisons. We searched the literature for studies using
both an AC and an ability measure, and for which validity coefficients with job performance were reported for both. We then
conducted analyses on competing explanatory mechanisms for the
differential validity observed between ACs and cognitive ability
tests and proceed to discuss evidence for each competing explanation of the observed difference in validity.

Method
Selection of Studies
Two different strategies were employed to locate validity studies with the potential for inclusion in the current investigation.
First, we examined three existing meta-analyses on ACs (Gaugler,
Rosenthal, Thornton, & Bentson, 1987; Hardison & Sackett, 2007;
Hermelin, Lievens, & Robertson, 2007) and attempted to locate
each cited reference. We located 70 of the 95 cited studies.
Irretrievable studies were dated conference presentations, unpublished manuscripts, or unpublished technical reports. We contacted
study authors by e-mail in an attempt to obtain all unpublished
literature cited in these meta-analyses. Second, we conducted a
literature search for primary studies utilizing ACs published after
the year 2000, to obtain any studies published since the prior

meta-analyses. Online databases of PsycInfo and Social Sciences
Citation Index were searched using keywords such as assessment
center, predictive validity, cognitive ability, mental ability, and
intelligence. Our search was restricted to manuscripts in English.
Additionally, two studies meeting the inclusion criteria were obtained through e-mail communication with knowledgeable AC
researchers.

Inclusion Criteria
To make a head-to-head comparison of cognitive ability validity
and AC validity, studies were included if they met the following
inclusion criteria. First, studies needed to contain the prototypic
features of an AC, namely the use of multiple assessors to assess
multiple candidates on multiple attributes as the candidates complete multiple assessment exercises (Kuncel & Sackett, 2014).
Second, studies had to administer tests measuring the construct of
cognitive ability in addition to the AC. Third, studies had to
provide a subsequent job performance criterion, not in the form of
an exam score or a measure of job advancement. Fourth, studies
had to provide a cognitive ability–job performance correlation, as
well as an AC performance–job performance correlation. Finally,
to make results directly comparable for each study, AC and ability
measures had to be assessed with the same sample and using the
same outcome measure. In total, the final dataset was composed of
17 independent samples drawn from 15 studies, with a total N for
cognitive ability validity of 3,833 and a total N for AC validity of
3,865. Ns for the two are not identical, as one study (Tziner, Meir,
Dahan, & Birati, 1994) did not have ability scores for 32 individuals.

Coding Procedure
Coding for studies meeting inclusion criteria was conducted
separately by the second and third authors, who were experienced
graduate student researchers. The second and third authors reviewed all potential studies for inclusion. An overview of coding
procedures is described below.
We documented whether each study was a concurrent or predictive validation study, as this affects the degree of range restriction. Six studies were concurrent and 11 were predictive. Concurrent studies do not require correction for range restriction, as
everyone who goes through the AC has a performance measure. In
a few of the predictive studies, only candidates with high AC
scores were selected and subsequently had performance measures
available. For such studies, correlations were corrected for direct
or indirect range restriction either by the original study authors or
the current authors based on data reported in the primary studies.
In Table 3, it is noted whether range restriction corrections were
conducted by the original authors or the authors of the present
study. For three studies the information needed to correct ability
validity was not available, and for one study the information
needed to correct AC validity was not available. For these studies
we used the median correction factor across the other studies as the
basis for correction. When a range of sample sizes was reported in
a table, the smallest and most conservative number was used.
Studies reported different cognitive ability measures with various labels. For eight of these studies, multiple cognitive ability
tests were administered. In these instances, we selected the mea-

For AC measures, an overall assessment rating was made in 15
of the 17 samples. In two studies (Goldstein, Yusko, & Nicolopolous, 2001; Lievens, Harris, Van Keer, & Bisqueret, 2003), the
current authors mechanically combined dimension ratings to form

Studies reported various criterion measures, and decisions were
made as to which criteria best represented overall job performance.
In most cases, supervisory performance ratings were deemed the
most reflective of job performance. If a study reported both a
criterion for research purposes and for operational purposes, the
criterion for research purposes was used because operational criteria may be more likely to reflect underlying rater biases. Job
advancement criteria were excluded because of potential contamination due to the use of the AC ratings in the promotion decision
process, and criterion measures utilizing an examination (e.g., a
final exam at the end of a training process) were excluded because
exam performance assesses a conceptually different construct than
job performance.
Regarding moderators, for each study we recorded the percentage of AC dimensions and criterion dimensions falling into
each of the seven categories within Arthur et al.’s (2003)
classification of AC dimensions (Consideration, Communication, Drive, Influence, Organizing/Planning, Problem Solving,
and Stress Tolerance). As noted in the introduction, as the AC
is a method, this coding permits insight into the constructs
underlying each AC. The moderators of job type (entry level vs.
nonentry level) and sample type (civilian, police, or military)
were also coded to document characteristics of the subset of AC
validities studies for which a head-to-head comparison with
ability tests is possible.
To assess coding reliability between the second and third authors, percentage agreement was calculated. Coding agreement
across all study variables exceeded 90%, with a range of agreement for each variable from 88% to 100%. Any discrepancies were
discussed and resolved between the authors and consensus was
reached for all variables included in the dataset.

Analyses
We used random-effects meta-analysis to examine AC and
ability validity (Hunter & Schmidt, 2004). When the ability test
was part of the AC and available to assessors, semipartial
correlations were calculated for AC ratings and performance,
partialing out effects that cognitive ability may have on AC
validity. We examined relative magnitude of criterion-related
validity at the individual study level, conducting hypothesis
tests on each pair of ability and AC validities to determine in

what proportion of cases ACs had significantly higher validities
than ability tests.
The focus of analyses then shifted to examining competing
explanatory mechanisms for the differential validity between ability tests and ACs. To explore whether context played a role,
moderator analyses were conducted on sample type and job type.
We then evaluated whether dimensionality of the criterion explained differential validity between ability tests and ACs, which
was examined by conducting analyses of the relationship between
AC dimensionality and AC validity. The underlying logic was that
if ACs predict broader, less cognitively loaded criteria better than
narrow, cognitively loaded criteria, then ACs with more broad
dimensions and lower cognitive load would display larger AC
validity than those with more narrow, cognitively loaded dimensions. This was addressed by evaluating the degree to which ACs
were cognitively loaded (by examining meta-analytic correlations
of AC dimensions with cognitive ability), and whether cognitive
load of the AC predicted AC validity. The final issue examined
was whether differences in range restriction could explain differences in ability test validity in the present set of studies versus
prior meta-analyses. To address this, we evaluated whether differences in AC versus ability selection ratios were plausible explanations for the difference in validity between ability and AC.

Potential Explanations for the Lower Ability Test
Validity in These Studies
We had posited two reasons to expect lower AC validity in these
head to head comparison studies. One is prior range restriction on
ability. As we have noted, the group that constitutes the population
to which the AC is administered may have been initially selected
on the basis of cognitive ability. We asked the hypothetical question,
What selection ratio on cognitive ability would have had to operate at
prior stages to produce the ability validity estimate obtained in our
sample of studies (␳ ⫽ .22), if the true state of affairs was that without
prior selection the validity of the ability test matched Schmidt and
Hunter’s (1998) value of ␳ ⫽ .51?

To address this we rearranged the direct range restriction formula
to solve for the restricted standard deviation given that restricted
and unrestricted validity is known, and used a formula from
Schmidt, Hunter, and Urry (1976) to estimate the selection ratio
that corresponds to a given ratio of restricted to unrestricted
standard deviation.1 We judged the resulting selection ratio of .05
(i.e., only individuals in the top 5% on ability were selected) too
extreme to be plausible for several reasons. First prior work on
identifying the degree of range restriction on ability across settings
estimates this degree of selectivity on cognitive ability as extremely rare (Alexander, Carson, Alliger, & Cronshaw, 1989;
Schmidt & Hunter, 1977). Second, military settings commonly use
an ability threshold for screening, rather than top-down selection
(Sackett & Mavor, 2003). While enlistees being screened for
higher level positions have restricted range relative to the national
population, the restriction would not come anywhere close to the

equivalent of a 5% selection ratio. Thus, we suggest that range
restriction due to prior selection on ability is a partial, but not
complete, contributor to the lower validity figure found in our
sample.
A second posited contributor to our finding of ability tests’
lower validity is the difference between the criterion measures
used in these studies and those prototypically used in validity
studies with ability tests. We have shown that the criteria used in
AC validation research are considerably broader than the task
performance criteria commonly used in ability test research. In the
introduction, we noted that Gonzalez-Mulé et al. (2014) reported
that ability test validity was 39% lower when a broad, multidimensional criterion was used, rather than a heavily cognitively
loaded task performance criterion. Applying that 39% value here
would reduce the ability validity of .51 to .31; restriction of range
equivalent to a prior selection ratio of approximately 70% would
be needed to reduce the ability value further to the value of .22
observed in our data. We view this as a plausible selection ratio to
further reduce the ability validity to that observed in our sample.
Another factor meriting discussion is the dominance of military
and police settings in the set of 17 samples for which head-to-head
comparisons between AC and cognitive ability validity were made.
We offer two arguments against the possibility that findings in
these settings are idiosyncratic. First, as noted above, AC validity
is comparable in these studies to the broader AC literature, which
argues against the position that there is something about military or
protective service settings that makes performance less predictable. Second, Ones, Dilchert, Viswesvaran, and Salgado (2010)
offered a systematic review of prior meta-analyses, and conclude
military versus civilian setting can be rejected as a moderator of
ability test validity. We conclude that settings for our sample of
studies are not an explanatory factor for differences between
current and prior findings.
Thus, we view the broader conceptualization of job performance
in the criteria used in AC validity studies as a major contributor to
the difference in ability-performance relationships found in our
sample of head-to-head ability-AC comparisons versus the relationships reported in prior meta-analyses. We view as a secondary
contributor to this difference the narrower range of ability in the
population of candidates for ACs. While this study is by no means
the last word on the subject, it does offer a very different perspective on the relative predictive power of ACs and ability tests in the
context in which ACs are commonly used. It suggests that ACs are
commonly superior predictors of the criteria against which researchers elect to validate ACs.
It is important to note that we are not challenging the .51 value for
the criterion-related validity of ability tests reported by Schmidt and
Hunter (1998), as long as one recognizes that the value applies to the
prediction of task performance with the national population of individuals applying for medium complexity jobs as the reference population. We show that a lower mean value of .22 is obtained using

Methods Versus Constructs
We return to the issue of methods versus constructs. As ACs are a
method, there may be variation across studies in how the AC method
is implemented, and this variation may affect criterion-related validity. We find it instructive to present the SD␳ values for the two. For
ability the SD␳ ⫽ .15; For ACs, the value from Gaugler et al. (1987)
is SD␳ ⫽ .13; the value from the later Hermelin et al. (2007) metaanalysis is .14. Thus, the variation in validity for ability tests is
substantial, and the variation for AC is slightly smaller. For our
sample of head-to-head studies, SD␳ values are somewhat smaller: .10
for ability and .09 for ACs. Thus, while ACs are a method, they do not
produce more variability than ability tests. We believe that for both
predictors it is important to emphasize SD␳ values rather than interpreting mean ␳ as “true validity.”

Practical Implications
In terms of practical implications, we show that in 17 head-to-head
comparisons, the criterion-related validity of the AC exceeds that of
the ability test in 15 of the comparisons. Given the method versus
construct issue, we must stop short of a blanket statement of the
superiority of ACs over ability tests, as one can easily design an AC
that differs in a variety of ways from the prototypic AC. At a
minimum, we rebut the received wisdom that one can generally
expect higher validity using an ability test than an AC. Rather than
approaching the AC as a less valid predictor whose use would need to
be justified in terms of the relative value of other factors, such as
greater candidate acceptance of ACs (Macan, Avedon, Paese, &
Smith, 1994), the potential developmental use of AC performance

feedback (Thornton & Rupp, 2005), and smaller racial/ethnic group
mean differences (Dean, Roth, & Bobko, 2008), the present findings
lead to a conclusion that, for predicting the broad criteria of interest to
AC users, ACs commonly match or exceed the validity achieved
using an ability test. While a local validity study would be required to
understand the functioning of any specific AC, a decision to embark
on exploring an AC in a given setting can be undertaken with some
optimism as to the predictive value of ACs relative to ability tests.
One caveat is that the performance criterion in all studies was a rater
judgment; should AC assessor judgments share common biases with
rater judgments, AC validity would be inflated. Studies with
nonrating-based criteria would be useful in this regard.

A Call for Caution in Comparing Predictors Based on
Meta-Analytic Findings
We suggest that this study usefully calls attention to the implicit
assumptions underlying any comparisons based on meta-analytic
findings. As we noted earlier, such comparisons require either that
samples, settings, and criterion measures be comparable for the predictors being compared, or that there is strong evidence that variation
in samples, settings, and criterion measures do not affect validity
estimates. We suggest that the head-to-head comparisons strategy
employed in this study merits broader consideration in comparisons
between other predictors of job performance. Clearly, this strategy is
limited to pairs of predictors that are jointly examined, and thus will
only be applicable in some circumstances. A useful starting point may
be other comparisons involving cognitive ability tests as one of the
predictors, as this is the standard against which the predictive power
of other predictors is commonly compared.
