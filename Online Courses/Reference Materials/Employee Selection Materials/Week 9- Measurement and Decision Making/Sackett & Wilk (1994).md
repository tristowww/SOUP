Within-Group Norming and Other Forms of Score
Adjustment in Preemployment Testing

Various forms of score adjustment have been suggested
and used when mean differences by gender, race, or ethnicity are found using preemployment tests. This article
examines the rationalesfor score adjustment and describes
and compares different forms of score adjustment, including within-group norming, bonus points, separate
cutoffs, and banding. It reviews the legal environment for
personnel selection and the circumstances leading to the
passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1991. It examines score
adjustment in the use ofcognitive ability tests, personality
inventories, interest inventories, scored biographical data,
and physical ability tests and outlines the implications
for testing practice of various interpretations of the Civil
Rights Act of 1991.

n the early 1980s, the United States Employment Service (USES) began making increased use of the General Aptitude Test Battery (GATB) in referring job
seekers to potential employers. Because Black and Hispanic GATB mean scores are lower than White mean
scores, USES converted GATB scores to percentile scores
within racial-ethnic groups and referred candidates to
prospective employers on the basis of these within-group
percentile scores. Thus the same raw score would be converted to a markedly different percentile score if the examinee was White, Black, or Hispanic. The result was
that candidates from various groups were referred to employers at comparable rates: An employer requesting
candidates scoring at or above the 80th percentile would
receive a list made up of the White, Black, and Hispanic
applicant pool members scoring above the 80th percentile
within their own group.
This practice of within-group norming was challenged by the U.S. Department of Justice in 1986 as unfair
to White candidates. A National Academy of Sciences
(NAS) committee appointed to study the issue produced
a book-length report in 1989 (Hartigan & Wigdor, 1989)
supporting referral based on normed scores. However, as
public attention was drawn to the issue, there was a strong
outcry against the practice, leading to a provision in the
Civil Rights Act of 1991 prohibiting within-group norming. The Act stated that it would be an unlawful practice
for an employer

groups. If the differences are due to biased measurement
of the characteristic in question, there is clear need for
some action to deal with the problem, perhaps involving
some form of score adjustment and perhaps involving
revision or replacement of the selection device. Subsequent sections of this article discuss methods for determining whether measures are biased and review research
findings regarding bias in commonly used selection devices. If the differences are not due to bias, but rather
reflect real group differences in the characteristic being
measured, and if the selection device is in fact related to
job performance, then the employer faces the dilemma
of wanting to achieve productivity gains through the use
of the selection device, but at the same time wanting to
reduce or eliminate adverse impact against members of
any group. It is this dilemma that drives many of the
discussions of score adjustment, as employers seek ways
of balancing these competing goals.

Rationales for Score Adjustment
Score adjustment takes a number of forms, including
converting scores to percentiles within race or gender
groups, adding a fixed number of points to the scores of
members of particular groups, and others. These are reviewed and compared in the next section of this article.
This section focuses specifically on the arguments and
controversies surrounding score adjustment.
In some settings score adjustment on the basis of
group membership is done without much apparent controversy. The legally mandated practice of adding points
to the scores of veterans in some public sector settings is
a well-known example. In other settings, particularly
when the issue involves score adjustment on the basis of
race or gender, score adjustment practices have been an
incendiary issue. Debate has tended to deal with two settings: physical ability tests, which produce substantial adverse impact against women, and cognitive ability tests,

which produce substantial impact against some racial
and ethnic minority groups, particularly Blacks and
Hispanics (these findings are documented in the section
on consequences).
Disputes about score adjustment can be argued on
legal, technical, and social grounds. Legally, before the
passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, various commentators differed strongly as to the permissibility of the
practice (e.g., Delahunty, 1988; Hartigan & Wigdor,
1989). On technical grounds, score adjustment may be
offered as a mechanism to eliminate biased measurement.
On social grounds, arguments tend to be cast in terms of
individual merit versus general societal good. Opponents
of score adjustment tend to argue for the sanctity of individual merit and the inappropriateness of minority
group membership as a factor in selection decisions (e.g.,
Gottfredson, 1988). Arguments about attaching social
stigma to members of the aided minority group are also
common.
We find it useful to separate the rationales offered
for score adjustment into three categories. The first is
purely philosophical: Increased minority representation
is argued to be of such value that minority preference is
warranted to achieve it. The second is that score adjustment is needed because of bias measurement in the test.
If a given test score does not carry the same meaning for
members of all groups, then score adjustment is needed.
The third is that score adjustment is needed when a particular way of using a test is at odds with an espoused
position as to what constitutes fair test use. We will explore
the rationales offered for score adjustment in some detail.
Position 1: Adjust Scores to Attain Business or
Societal Goals
One common theme in arguments for minority preference is that of righting a social wrong. The long history
of social inequities endured by members of racial and
ethnic groups in this country is viewed as a wrong so
grave as to warrant strong measures, including minority
preference in job allocation. A second common theme is
an appeal to pluralism: All groups deserve a fair and proportional share of the society's benefits, and jobs are
among those benefits. A third theme that has emerged
strongly in the last few years is that workforce diversity
contributes to a firm's success. The term diversity is used
with varying degrees of inclusiveness, ranging from a narrow focus on groups protected by civil rights law to concerns for issues such as sexual orientation.
Proponents of minority preference to increase diversity argue for a variety of benefits. First, examinations
of demographic trends suggest that increasing proportions
of new entrants to the workforce over the next several
decades will be women and minority group members.
Firms will find themselves competing for a limited applicant pool, and actions taken today to increase diversity
will make the firm more attractive to job applicants in
the future. Second, as their customer base becomes increasingly diverse, firms may find that their customers
view diversity as a factor in deciding where to take their

Position 2: Adjust Scores to Alleviate Test Bias
Test critics routinely assert that tests are biased against
members of various groups. Various causes of test bias
are offered. Among the most common are that tests use
language specific to majority groups, that tests use item
formats that give advantage to majority groups, and that
test-taking skills have no link to real-world job behavior.
(See Jensen, 1980, for a critical review of these claims.)
Whatever the causal mechanism, the arguments boil
down to assertions that the regression line relating test
scores to measures of the job-relevant criterion of interest
has a different slope for one group than for another (the
test predicts performance well for the majority group, but
less well for the minority group), has a different intercept
for one group than for another (minority group members
get lower scores than majority group members equal in
ability), or differs in both slope and intercept. As a result,
regression analysis has become accepted as the mechanism for investigating claims of test bias. According to
the Standards for Educational and Psychological Tests
(1985): "The accepted technical definition of predictive
bias implies that no bias exists if the predictive relationship of two groups being compared can be adequately
described by a common algorithm (e.g., regression line)"
(P- 12).
Two caveats are in order concerning this approach.
First, it is dependent on the quality of the measure of the
job-relevant criterion of interest: If that measure is systematically biased against the minority group in question,
then the method cannot be used to identify the presence
or absence of predictive bias. Second, the approach requires larger sample sizes than will be available for the

different slopes by group. As the slope of the regression
line indexes the predictive validity of the test for the group
under consideration, differing slopes indicate differential
validity: The test is, in fact, a more effective predictor of
performance in one group than in another. Such a finding
would be particularly disturbing should the slope approach zero for one group, as in Figure la. Predicted
performance for majority group members is positively
related to test scores; that is, the higher test scores correspond to higher predicted performance. The performance of minority group members, however, is predicted
to be the same across all possible test scores. Such an
occurrence would certainly preclude further use of the
test unless and until subsequent research demonstrates
that the finding was artifactual. Empirically, however,
there is little evidence for differential validity in the domains where it has been investigated. Extensive research
with cognitive ability tests suggests that differential validity
by race and gender is found no more frequently than
would be expected by chance (see the section on consequences for a review). However, the issue has not been
investigated extensively in other domains such as personality assessment.
Turning to the implications of intercept differences
by group, we see two possibilities: a larger intercept for
the comparison group, indicating that the use of the majority group regression line (or a common regression line)
would underpredict the performance of minority group
members, and a lower intercept for the minority group,
indicating that the use of the majority group regression
line would overpredict the performance of minority group
members. In the case of underprediction as in Figure lb,
the minority group is disadvantaged by amount zu and

line would apply to both majority and minority groups.
In the case of overprediction, the use of the majority group
regression line is to the advantage of the minority group
and thus is not likely to be challenged by the minority
group. Score adjustment would again be a technically
appropriate solution, but the adjustment would involve
a reduction in the scores of minority group members.
The social and political consequences of such a course of
action make such adjustments an unattractive course of
action for many organizations.

Position 3: Adjust Scores When Justified by a
Particular Perspective on Fairness
This category refers to rationales for score adjustment
that pair the observation that selection systems are imperfect predictors of job performance with the assertion
that the burden of these imperfections should not fall
disproportionately on minority groups.
A variety of criteria for assessing the impact of imperfect selection systems on minority group members
have been put forward under the rubric of "models of
selection system fairness." As one example, Thorndike
(1971) proposed an approach in which the success rate
(i.e., the proportion of job applicants who would exceed
a specified level ofjob performance if hired) is determined
for each group. For a selection system to be fair, the rate
of selection from each group must be proportional to
these success rates. Top-down selection will meet this
standard only when majority-minority differences on the
selection device used are equal in magnitude to majorityminority differences in job performance. A variety of
models of test fairness were put forward in the late 1960s
and early 1970s. An extensive review is beyond the scope
of this article; see Hunter and Schmidt (1976), Petersen
and Novick (1976), and Schmidt and Hunter (1974) for
critiques and comparisons of fairness models.
In the 1970s, approaches such as Thorndike's were
grouped with the regression model of test bias discussed
above under the rubric "models of test fairness." A key
insight of that era was the recognition that models such
as Thorndike's were qualitatively different from the
regression model. The regression model deals directly with
the meaning of individual test scores: It addresses the
question. Does a given score have the same meaning (i.e.,
predict the same level ofjob performance) across groups?
and it has come to be viewed as the means of assessing
predictive test bias. In contrast, it has been demonstrated
that models such as Thorndike's do not deal directly with
flaws in the test. Rather, violations of the Thorndike model
are inevitable when a test that predicts performance with
less than perfect accuracy and on which group differences
exist is used in a top-down fashion.

The NAS committee on the GATB based its endorsement of score adjustment on such a perspective on
selection system fairness. As this has proved to be controversial, we will discuss the committee's approach in
more detail. We will describe here how and why this approach differs from approaches commonly taken by psychologists.
As a starting point, note that Black-White differences in measured job performance are much smaller
than Black-White differences in scores on cognitive ability
tests. Observed differences in job performance are approximately 0.3-0.4 SD. Similar differences are found
for both subjective and objective performance measures
(Ford, Kraiger, & Schechtman, 1986). Observed differences in performance are smaller than true differences in
performance because of measurement error. However,
even if correcting for measurement error produces BlackWhite differences in the 0.5-0.6 SD range, this is still
much smaller than the 1 SD difference in ability test
scores. From this Black-White difference in job perfor934

mance, we can determine the number of White and Black
applicants who would be hired under different scenarios
(e.g., size of applicant pool and proportion of of Blacks
and Whites in the applicant pool) if one were able to
select on the basis of actual job performance. What
emerges clearly is that selecting on the basis of job performance will result in the hiring of more Blacks than
would be the case if hiring were on the basis of the cognitive ability test (because the Black-White difference in
on-the-job performance is smaller than the Black-White
difference in cognitive ability). If one gave all applicants
a job tryout and kept the highest performers (i.e., hiring
on the basis of what is the criterion in the typical validity
study), one would retain considerably more Blacks than
would be hired using the present test. This observation
is at the heart of the committee's adoption of an approach
that focuses on the probability of selection given a specified level of job performance, rather than the focus on
the probability of a specified level of job performance
given selection.
In contrast to the committee's approach is the position that because only test performance is known before
hire, it is appropriate to focus on success given selection,
rather than selection given success. Some have also argued
that because the phenomenon of smaller job performance
differences than test differences is not explicitly racial
(with a less than perfectly valid test, any lower scoring
group with a larger difference from the majority group
on the predictor than the criterion will produce this result), it is not appropriate to remedy the situation for a
racially denned group (cf. Hunter & Schmidt, 1976). In
other words, proponents of this position can accept that
all low scorers will be subject to disproportionate rates of
false rejection in order for employers to enjoy the benefits
of testing, but not that some low scorers (i.e., Blacks)
should be spared the effects of imperfect prediction, as
would be the case if score adjustment is used.

groups protected by civil rights laws, the goal is the reduction or elimination of differences between groups.

Within-Group Norming
Within-group norming involves converting individual
scores to either standard scores or percentile scores within
one's group. Because this approach is sensitive to differences in both means and standard deviations across
groups, it is more consistently successful in reducing adverse impact than is the bonus point approach. As with
the bonus point approach, the score adjustment can be
based on reference population data or on a local sample.
When the norming is based on data from which selection
decisions are to be made (sample referenced), the approach will generally eliminate adverse impact3; when
the norming is based on broad norms (population referenced), the possibility of adverse impact in a given sample remains.
Like the bonus point approach, within-group norming may be undertaken as a pragmatic approach to eliminating adverse impact. In some domains, however, the
rationale is that a given score has a markedly different
psychological meaning in one group than in another and
thus that the appropriate score for meaningful interpretation is one's standing within the group. The masculinity-femininity scale of the California Psychological Inventory (CPI) illustrates this perspective: Men and women
respond in markedly different ways, and therefore the test
developers' perspective was that the only meaningful way
to interpret a score was in terms of one's standing within
one's gender group.
Separate Cutoffs
The use of separate cutoffs for different groups is in practice identical to the bonus point approach: Setting a cutoff
5 points lower for one group produces the same outcome
as adding 5 points to the scores of members of that group.
One at least symbolic difference is that the separate cutoff
approach makes very clear what is being done: A lower
standard is being used for one group than for another. If
test scores are computed by the human resource department and made available to the hiring manager, it is not
uncommon to find that, when score adjustment is used
and adjusted scores are reported, the hiring manager is
not aware that adjustment has taken place. With separate
cutoffs, there is no ambiguity about the meaning of test
scores.

Banding
Background.
Banding refers to a set of approaches
to test use in which individuals within a specific score
range, or band, are regarded as having equivalent scores.
The concept, if not the name, has a long history. One
example is the use of stanine scores, in which raw scores
are converted to single digit scores ranging from 1 to 9.
Banding will be treated here in some detail, as interest in
it has grown because of its withstanding recent court
challenges. Early conceptions were motivated by a desire
to avoid overinterpretations of small test score differences
in light of the less than perfect reliability of psychological
tests. A simple illustration of banding would be the situation in which all individuals with raw scores between
91 and 100 on a 100-item test would be assigned to Band
1, all between 81 and 90 to Band 2, all between 71 and
80 to Band 3, and so forth. All individuals in Band 1
would be selected before turning to candidates in Band
2, but within Band 1, selection would not be in order of
raw test scores. All within the band are seen as equivalent
on the construct of interest. Order of selection of candidates within a band would be either random or on the
basis of some additional selection criterion.
Bandwidth could be determined arbitrarily, as in
the above example. Alternatively, it could be based on
the measurement properties of the test. It is common to
define bandwidth in terms of the standard error of the
difference between scores. For example, any score less

One alternative to the approach described above is to add a minority preference

Banding simply broadens the notion of what are considered as ties. Because all individuals within the band are

only members of the minority group are screened in if
the number of openings to be filled is equal to or less
than the number of minority group members in the top
band, unless a specification is added that the proportion
of openings filled by minority group members is restricted
to the proportion of minority group members in the applicant pool.

A new form of banding that has
been proposed recently is the sliding band (Cascio, Outtz,

Once all individuals scoring 100 have been selected, the
highest raw score remaining is 99. The sliding band concept would reformulate the band at this point: The original band included scores from 91 to 100; the new band
includes scores from 90 to 99. The key to this notion is
that scores not significantly lower than the highest remaining score should not be treated as different.
One motivating factor for the sliding band approach
is increased minority selection. If strict minority preference is used within a band, selection would be as follows:
(a) Select minority group members in the top band; (b)
select majority group members of the top band randomly
or on the basis of some additional applicant characteristic;
(c) as soon as all individuals with the top score in the
band (i.e., 100) are selected, slide the band from 91-100
to 90-99; (d) select the newly eligible minority group
members with a score of 90; (e) return to selecting majority group members of the top band until all individuals
scoring 99 have been selected, at which point the band
slides again to include individuals scoring 89, and so forth.
The crucial issue for sliding bands is the selection
of the majority group members with the top score in the
band. Until those individuals have been selected, the band
cannot slide and make additional minority group members eligible for selection. With random selection within
a band, it is possible that individuals with the top score
may be the last chosen. Thus Cascio et al. (1991) suggested
top-down selection within group within a band. This ensures that bands slide quickly. In our example it would
work as follows: (a) select minority group members top
down within the first band, (b) select majority group
members scoring 100, (c) slide the band from 91-100 to
90-99, (d) select minority group members scoring 90, (e)
select majority group members scoring 99, (f) slide the
band from 90-99 to 89-98, (g) select minority group
members scoring 89, (h) select majority group members
scoring 98, (i) slide the band from 89-98 to 88-97, and
(j) continue in this fashion until positions are filled.
What this example makes clear is that this approach
is exactly the same as a bonus point approach, with bandwidth as the number of points to be added to the scores
of minority group members. Alternately, it can be seen
as producing different cutoffs. If, for example, the firm
in the example above found that all openings were filled
when Step h was reached, the end result would be the
selection of all majority group members scoring 98 or
higher and all minority group members scoring 89 or
higher. Murphy (1993) pointed out that when bandwidth
is determined by the standard error of the difference between scores, it is in essence a function of the reliability
of the test. Thus depending on test reliability, a banding
approach with minority preference may or may not result
in the elimination of adverse impact.

Empirical Keying by Group
Forms of score adjustment discussed up to this point involve taking group membership into account after a raw
test score has been obtained. We now consider approaches
in which group membership information is needed in
order to derive a score for a candidate.
Empirical keying involves examining relationships
between individual items and a criterion of interest (e.g.,
job performance, turnover, theft) and selecting items for
inclusion in a selection instrument on the basis of their
relationship with the criterion. Consistent with the empiricism of this approach is the consideration of the possibility that individual items that are effective predictors
for some groups may be less so for other groups. From a
common item pool, different sets of items may make up
the empirical keys providing optimal prediction for different groups.
Empirical keying is commonly discussed in the same
breath as "dustbowl empiricism": At the extreme, empirical keying can be done with complete disregard for
any psychological understanding of what is being measured or of why item-criterion relationships are found.
The prototypical application of empirical keying is the
use of scored biographical data. A commonly cited example is the military's finding during World War II that
response to the single item "Did you ever build a model
airplane that flew?" predicted pilot success in flight training almost as well as did an extensive battery of tests
(Henry, 1965).
Empirical keying is common in the life history and
personality domains. The relationship between responses
to a large pool of items and a criterion of interest is examined, and items showing a relationship are retained
for cross-validation. A set of items surviving cross-validation are then used to derive a score for subsequent job
applicants.

questionnaire with a set of items probing accepting responsibility early in life. The item "Did you have a paper
route as a child?" is found to have a substantial item939

Item Elimination Based on Group Performance
It is useful to distinguish between two different scenarios
in which test items are eliminated on the basis of group
performance. The first involves activities taking place in
a research context during test development. A large pool
of items may be tried out, and items eliminated for a
wide variety of reasons, ranging from the item's loading
in a factor analysis to its item-total correlation to group
performance on the item. We view this as outside of the
purview of the Civil Rights Act of 1991: The prohibition
against using group membership in scoring tests cannot
reasonably be extended to items tried out in test development but not used.
The second approach involves item elimination after
a test is administered. The prototypical example is the
job knowledge test used in a public sector setting (e.g., a
promotional examination for police sergeants). Because
of test security concerns, the test can be given only once.
If a promotional exam is needed every two years, a new
examination is constructed each time, and security concerns prohibit large-scale item tryout, such as administering the test to all current police sergeants in a forresearch-only context.
One approach involves determining item difficulty
for each item for each group and eliminating items for
which item difficulties differ by some specified amount.
This approach reflects the assumption that differential
item difficulty reflects item bias. This approach is not
well regarded by measurement specialists, as it discounts
any possibility of true group differences on the construct
of interest. A variety of more sophisticated approaches
to examining group differences in item performance have
been developed under the general rubric of "differential
item functioning." Conceptually, rather than asking "Is
this item harder for members of Group X than it is for
Group Y?" these approaches ask "Is this item harder for
members of Group X with true score Z than for it is for
members of Group Y with true score Z?"
At one level these after-the-fact item removal methods can be construed as group-based score adjustment
because group differences are the basis for decisions about
item retention. On the other hand, once the offending

items are removed, the test is scored in the same manner
for members of all groups. Thus this may fall within the
purview of the Civil Rights Act of 1991 if it is interpreted
broadly as requiring that candidates giving the same responses should receive the same test score.
Summary
In this section we have noted the high degree of similarity,
in many situations, of the results obtained using a number
of approaches to test use that take group membership
into account after an initial raw score has been obtained.
These include adding a constant number of points, converting scores to within-group percentiles, top-down selection from separate lists, and the setting of different
cutoffs for various groups. We also noted that a recently
advocated approach to banding, involving the use of sliding bands and minority preference within bands, can be
shown to be equivalent to adding points to minority group
members' scores and to setting different cutoffs.
We contrasted these after-the-fact methods with approaches in which group membership is used as part of
the test development process, either as part of an itemby-item review to determine differential item functioning
or as part of the development of separate scoring keys for
different groups. These latter methods are undertaken not
to eliminate group differences in scores but to obtain more
accurate measurement or more accurate prediction.
Thus group membership can be taken into account
either in the test development process or after the fact
and can be motivated by reducing group differences or
by increasing accuracy of measurement or prediction.

Initial interpretations of the law focused on intentional

adverse impact: A test that rejected members of one group

of a test used to discriminate under the language of the

in Griggs v. Duke Power Co. (1971), in which the company
required a high school diploma and a score above the
national mean on the Wonderlic Personnel Test and the
Bennett Mechanical Comprehension Test. Each of these
requirements rejected a higher proportion of Black than
White applicants, and the company did not have evidence
of the validity of the selection system. The Court stated,
"The touchstone is business necessity. If an employment
practice which operates to exclude Negroes cannot be
shown to be related to job performance, the practice is
prohibited."
In 1978 federal agencies charged with civil rights
enforcement issued the Uniform Guidelines on Employee
Selection Procedures (1978), which described the agencies'
perspective on what constitutes adverse impact and what
constitutes showing job relatedness. The Guidelines offered as a rule of thumb what has come to be known as
the "four-fifths" rule: A passing rate for any group that
is less than four fifths of the group with the highest passing
rate will be regarded as evidence of adverse impact. The
Guidelines acknowledged criterion-related validity, content validity, and construct validity as acceptable methods
for demonstrating job relatedness and offered detailed
statements of what would constitute acceptable evidence
of job relatedness when each strategy was used.
Thus Title VII, as interpreted by the Supreme Court
in Griggs and by federal agencies in the Guidelines, has
become the basis for most testing litigation. The presence
of adverse impact triggers a need for the employer to produce compelling evidence of the job relatedness of the
test in question.
Worthy of special mention is the commonly held
notion that any test used by an employer is required to
be job related. This is the case for public sector jobs that
are governed by merit system rules; however, there is no
such requirement for private sector employers. Only when
adverse impact is shown to be present is there such an
obligation.
Affirmative Action
Affirmative action is a broad term that means very different things to different people. Although it generally
connotes making extra efforts to increase the employment
November 1994 • American Psychologist

of minority group members, there is disagreement as to
what constitutes acceptable mechanisms for doing this.
Affirmative action in the form of extra efforts to broaden
minority representation in the applicant pool is not at
all controversial; forms of affirmative action that involve
some form of minority preference are quite controversial.
Although Title VII states that employers will not be required to grant preferential treatment to the members of
any group, the Supreme Court has noted that Congress
did not state that employers will not be permitted to grant
preferential treatment (United Steelworkers v. Weber,
1979).
It is useful to identify three different circumstances
under which affirmative action rationales for minority
preference have been upheld. The first is court-ordered
affirmative action after a firm has been found guilty of a
Title VII violation. Section 706g of Title VII indicates
that when a firm is found to have intentionally engaged
in an unlawful employment practice, the court may grant
any form of equitable relief it deems appropriate.
The second is the implementation of a consent decree, which is a court-enforced agreement between an
employer and an enforcement agency. Given a pattern of
minority employment suggestive of past discrimination,
an employer may agree to a plan for future minority hiring rather than proceeding with litigation.
The third is the use of voluntary affirmative action.
At issue is whether an employer who does a self-analysis
and discovers a pattern of minority underutilization can
implement an affirmative action plan with a minority
preference component to remedy the situation. A pivotal
Supreme Court decision on this question was handed
down in 1979 in United Steelworkers v. Weber (1979), in
which the Court ruled that a voluntary plan reserving
openings in a training program for minority group members in light of a manifest imbalance in the firm's workforce was permissible. The Court noted three features of
the plan that it deemed critical: It did not create an absolute bar to majority employment; it did not displace
majority workers; and it was temporary, lasting only until
the racial imbalance was eliminated.
Thus minority preference is permissible under a variety of circumstances, involving either a finding of discrimination, a court-monitored plan to remedy an imbalance, or a voluntary plan to remedy an imbalance. In
this context it should be noted that Section 116 of the
Civil Rights Act of 1991 states that "nothing in the
amendments made in this title shall be construed to affect
court-ordered remedies, affirmative action, or conciliation
agreements, that are in accordance with the law." Thus
the ban on score adjustment does not apply to the settings
outlined above.
We find it interesting that minority preference is
permitted to remedy an existing imbalance but is not
permitted proactively to prevent the creation of an imbalance. Consider an employer with a perfectly balanced
workforce who wishes to make use of a selection device
that is shown to be job related but that has adverse impact
on a particular group. Absent an imbalance, minority

Case of the GATB
The recent history of the Department of Labor's GATB
illustrates the complicated interplay of scientific and policy issues that surround employment testing.4 The GATB
is a general test of cognitive functioning that includes a
number of psychomotor subtests to assess manual and
finger dexterity. It is administered by the USES, an agency
created in the 1930s by an act of Congress expressly to
help the disadvantaged job seeker. Until 1980 the GATB
was used primarily as an aid to vocational counseling. In
an atmosphere of declining American competitiveness
and in response to the business orientation of the Reagan
administration, the Department of Labor proposed that
the GATB be adopted throughout the USES system to
screen applicants for virtually all jobs.
Approximately 19 million people pass through the
1,800 local offices annually, and approximately 3.5 million of them are actually placed in jobs. If even one third
to one half of all registrants were given the GATB, this
would be by far the largest testing program in the country.
In order to get the greatest possible economic benefit
from testing, the program was set up so that people would
be referred to jobs in order of test score. Because the
probability is that the higher test scorer will also be the
better performer on the job, this top-down method of
choosing from among the applicants on file would give
the employer that subset of available job seekers with the
greatest expected performance.
However, USES recognized that referral in strict order of test score would also make the employer—and
USES—vulnerable to charges of discrimination under
Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The research
staff had evidence that the mean GATB score for Black
applicants falls about one standard deviation below the
mean score in the White group. In practical terms, this
meant that if 25% of the White group were referred, only
942

5% of the Black group would be referred to a typical job
handled by the USES.
To prevent this from happening, USES sought a
compromise between the governmental interest in promoting economic productivity and the equally compelling
interest, given the USES mission, in helping disadvantaged minorities into the economic mainstream. The solution used was to compute each registrant's GATB score
as a percentile score within his or her own racial or ethnic
group (Black, Hispanic, and other). In other words, the
USES used the within-group norming approach described
earlier in this article.
This strategy retains the top-down approach to referral, but in a qualified way. It effectively wipes out the
average score differences among the groups because Black,
Hispanic, and White candidates assigned a given percentile score—say, the 70th percentile—would be referred
at the same time, even though their raw scores on the
GATB would be substantially different. In the new system,
those referred from the available pool of applicants would
be the Black, Hispanic, and White applicants with the
highest predicted performance in their respective groups.
At the same time, minority applicants would be referred,
on average, in numbers proportionate with their presence
in the applicant pool, thus avoiding the exclusionary effect
that testing would otherwise cause.
In the fall of 1986, William Bradford Reynolds, then
Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights in the U.S.
Department of Justice, challenged the GATB referral system because of the within-group scoring strategy. In
Reynolds's view, computing test scores by racial and ethnic groups constituted an illegal and unconstitutional violation of an applicant's right to be free from racial discrimination. And because the effect of the score adjustments is to raise the scores of Blacks and Hispanics,
Reynolds found that it advances the interests of one group
at the expense of another and as such constitutes intentional racial discrimination, in this instance what is commonly called reverse discrimination.
Officials of the Departments of Labor and Justice
agreed to disagree until a study could be conducted by
the National Research Council (NRC), the working arm
of the NAS, and a report presenting the study committee's
findings and recommendations was published under the
title Fairness in Employment Testing: Validity Generalization, Minority Issues, and the General Aptitude Test
Battery (Hartigan & Wigdor, 1989).
The publication suggested that the two goals of simultaneously increasing work force efficiency and bringing Blacks into the economic mainstream are not mutually exclusive. The crucial issue is to find a way to use
the GATB so that it will not screen out virtually all Black
and Hispanic job seekers on the one hand and unduly
abridge the rights of majority-group job seekers on the
other.

Aftermath of the National Academy of Sciences
Report
The above discussion focused on the issue of the role of
testing in balancing the competing objectives of minority
employment and productivity enhancement. What became very clear in the year following the release of the
NAS committee's report was that such discussions were
too abstract. In the mind of the general public, score adjustment was "cheating." Media coverage of the USES
testing system increased, led by a series of articles in the
Richmond Times-Dispatch. Newspaper tag lines on the
topic included "equality through double standards,"
"cheating on the tests," "Big Brother's test scores," and
(in reference to the nickname of General Schwarzkopf,
whose Desert Storm heroics were in the forefront at the
time) "Normin' stormin.'"
The precursor to the Civil Rights Act of 1991, at
that time the Civil Rights Act of 1990, was under debate
at the same time and was labeled a "quota bill" by thenpresident George Bush. Although the act made no reference to quotas, it was argued that the provisions of the
bill were so difficult to comply with that employers would
be forced into quota hiring. The ban on score adjustment
that became Section 106 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991
was introduced in response to claims that the GATB referral system constituted a quota system and that supporting such a system constituted supporting quotas.
Members of Congress supported the language of Section
106 virtually without exception. While denying that the

Act was a quota bill, supporters of the Act did not want
to be tagged as supporters of quotas.
Thus the ban on score adjustment was a direct response to the USES GATB system. There is no evidence
that there was broader consideration of the implications
of the Act for any other setting than the use of cognitive
ability testing.

Consequences of Score Adjustment for
Different Types of Tests
In this section we review group differences and their relationship to job performance for a variety of types of
tests, including cognitive ability, personality, physical
ability, biodata, and vocational interest measures.
Cognitive Ability Tests
The phrase cognitive ability tests encompasses a variety
of conceptually distinct domains, the most prototypic of
which are verbal, numerical, and reasoning ability. A
characteristic that has long been noted is the substantial
positive correlation among measures of various cognitive
abilities. Resulting from this observation is the notion
that underlying the various cognitive abilities is a common
factor, identified around the turn of the century by Spearman and referred to as g, for general cognitive ability.
It has been hypothesized that g is the factor that
accounts for the relationship between cognitive ability
tests and performance in various jobs. An extensive body
of literature shows that g is related to performance across
a wide range of jobs and that specific ability tests explain
little additional variance in job performance over that
accounted for by # (cf. Gottfredson, 1986; Hunter, 1986;
Jensen, 1986; Ree & Earles, 1991; Thorndike, 1986).
Black-White differences of approximately 1.0 standard deviation units and Hispanic-White differences of
approximately 0.6-0.8 standard deviation units have been
widely and consistently reported for measures of cognitive
ability (Hartigan & Wigdor, 1989; Jensen, 1980;Osborne
& McGurk, 1982). Efforts to find ways to reduce these
differences through the use of different item formats (e.g.,
formats not reliant on mastery of the English language)
have not been very successful. Note that the issue is not
merely whether a test can be devised that reduces group
differences, but whether group differences can be reduced
while retaining the same levels of reliability and criterionrelated validity. Gender differences are not generally found
on composite measures of g. Although differences may
be found in individual subtests, the pattern of results does
not consistently favor men or women (Jensen, 1980).
The implications of these differences for personnel
selection have long been recognized. The Black-White
difference on a cognitive ability test was a central feature
in Griggs v. Duke Power Co. (1971), the first testing case
to be considered by the Supreme Court after the passage
of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the case that gave
credibility to the concept of adverse impact as a basis for
challenging employment tests. As a means of reducing
adverse impact, a number of test publishers made use of

The crucial question is whether adverse impact reflects real differences in the characteristic under investigation or whether the differences reflect some form of
bias so that a given test score has different meanings if
obtained by a member of one group than if by a member
of another group. As noted in the foregoing section on
rationales for score adjustment, there is a well-accepted
mechanism for examining whether there is predictive bias
in the use of tests, namely, the comparison of regression
lines relating test scores to job performance. If regression
slopes and intercepts are the same for the groups under
consideration, there is no predictive bias. Scores have the
same meaning for individuals regardless of group membership. If the regression lines differ, a finding that use of
the reference group regression line underpredicts the job
performance of members of the minority group would
indicate predictive bias against the minority group. Although a finding of overprediction would also indicate
bias, the bias would operate to the advantage of the minority group.
There is an extensive literature examining the question of predictive bias in the use of cognitive ability tests,
which has been well reviewed by Bartlett et al. (1978);
Jensen (1980); and Schmidt et al. (1980). This literature
does not show evidence of underprediction of Black or
Hispanic performance. Although regression slopes do not
differ more frequently than would be expected by chance,
intercepts frequently differ. The differences, however, indicate overprediction, rather than underprediction, of
minority group performance.
In summary, research on cognitive ability tests for
personnel selection has documented consistent predictive
validity for a wide range of jobs, a lack of predictive bias
against Blacks and Hispanics, and large and consistent
adverse impact by race.
Personality Inventories
Personality inventories are self-report measures of what
might be called traits, temperaments, or dispositions.
Some inventories focus on characterizing individuals
within the normal adult range of functioning, whereas
others focus on the identification of psychopathology.
Many personality instruments produce measures of a
substantial number of personality characteristics—wellknown measures such as the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI; see Dalhstrom, Welsh, &
Dahlstrom, 1972; see Dahlstrom, Welsh, & Dahlstrom,
1972) and the California Personality Inventory (CPI; see
Gough, 1987)—although some measures of single characteristics exist (such as the many commercially available
measures of honesty and integrity).
944

It has long been recognized that gender differences
exist for a variety of personality dimensions. It is common
to see personality measures scored using gender-specific
norms. Scoring relative to one's gender is an inherent
part of the scoring system for a number of well-known
instruments, including the MMPI and CPI. It is interesting to note that this practice does not seem to have
been viewed as at all controversial. Whenever we found
a measure that routinely used gender-specific norms in
its scoring, we searched test manuals, handbooks, and the
like for a discussion of the rationale for the practice. We
typically found none. Our sense is that this reflects the
fact that most personality inventories were developed for
purposes of description, rather than for purposes of prediction of performance in an employment setting. For
descriptive purposes, it is equally accurate to report an
individual's standing relative to the general adult population, to women in general, or to college-educated women
in their 50s. It is only when one's purpose is prediction
that one encounters the question of whether the score
that should be used as the basis for prediction for each
individual should be a general score, a gender-specific
score, or one's standing in some other reference group.
Thus we sought two types of information. The first
dealt with the magnitude of gender differences on various
commonly used personality measures. The second dealt
with evidence of predictive bias in personality measures,
namely, evidence that unadjusted scores predict a different
level ofjob performance if the score is obtained by a man
than if the same score is obtained by a woman.
As the number of personality measures available is
enormous, we restricted our search to instruments commonly used in employment settings. We relied on a survey
of psychologists who conducted psychological assessments
for employment decisions (Ryan & Sackett, 1987), which
listed the personality measures most commonly used in
that context: the Sixteen Personality Factor Questionnaire
(16PF; see Manual for the 16PF, 1972), the GuilfordZimmerman Temperament Survey (GZTS), the CPI, the
MMPI, the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI), and the
Edwards Personal Preference Schedule (EPPS; see Edwards, 1959).

An examination of Table 2 clearly shows evidence
of what some view as a continuing problem in the personality domain, namely, the lack of a common vocabulary for describing personality. We encourage caution
in attempting to compare d values for similarly named
scales across tests for two reasons. First, labels may have
different meaning across inventories. Second, the normative samples used for each instrument may be quite
different: A very different pattern could conceivably result
if all of these instruments were administered to the same

Consider the characteristic of dominance, addressed
by most of the instruments under consideration here. Table 2 reports d values of 0.13 for CPI Dominance, 0.80
for 16PF Dominance, 0.45 for GZTS Ascendance, 0.86
for EPPS Dominance, and 0.55 for Jackson PRF Dominance. Thus the male mean exceeds the female mean
on all of these scales, and with the exception of the CPI,
the difference is fairly sizable. What are the consequences
of these differences in a selection context?
To answer this, it is useful to consider two common
ways in which personality measures are used in employment settings. First, a personality measure can be used
in a mechanical way: A desired score range is specified
on a scale, and individuals whose scores do not fall within
that range are flagged. This may result in the automatic
rejection of a candidate for employment or in routing the
candidate through a more elaborate screening process.
For example, a firm may set a policy that applicants for
high-level sales jobs scoring below the mean on a Dominance scale are referred to a psychologist for an in-depth
psychological assessment, with the psychologist making
a recommendation as to whether the applicant should be
considered further. Applicants scoring above the mean
automatically move on to the next stage in the employment process. In this case, the use of a Dominance scale
will most likely have adverse impact for female job candidates if dominance scores are used without some form
of adjustment, such as the conversion of scores to withingender percentiles.
In contrast to this mechanical method of test use for
selection decisions is the judgmental integration by a psychologist of scores across all of the scales making up a
personality inventory into a recommendation about a
candidate's likely pattern of subsequent job behavior.
When this clinical judgment approach is used, a given
score on a given scale is interpreted in the context of the
candidate's scores on other scales and in the context of
other information about the candidate available to the
946

psychologist. The psychologist may or may not be able
to articulate a model of what information is attended to
and how it is combined to produce an evaluation of the
candidate.
What is particularly noteworthy in the clinical integration of personality inventory data is that one piece
of knowledge that the psychologist brings to the task is
his or her knowledge of normative data for various groups.
For example, knowledge of how top-level managers perform on a particular inventory may lead the psychologist
to interpret a candidate's profile in reference to this norm
profile, rather than in reference to general adult norms.
Similarly, knowing that gender differences exist on some
scales may affect the psychologist's interpretation in a
candidate's scores. This has interesting implications for
possible interpretations of the Civil Rights Act of 1991's
ban on score adjustment. If the law is interpreted as prohibiting reporting gender-normed personality inventory
scores, psychologists who believe that gender norming is
appropriate will simply do the score adjustment mentally
as part of the process of integrating information and
forming an evaluation of the candidate. Thus interpreting
the Act as covering personality measures is likely to affect
only situations in which mechanical decisions based on
scale scores are made and may simply result in employers
moving away from mechanical scoring rules.
The discussion to this point has focused on whether
gender differences are found and how they may affect test
usage. We turn now to the issue of the appropriateness
of gender norming when using personality measures for
employment decisions.
At issue is the question of the meaning of gender
differences on measures such as dominance. Does a d of
0.5 indicate that on average women are less dominant
(with appropriate care taken to clearly understand how
dominance is defined and operationalized in the inventory
in question)? Or do items that tap dominance in male
samples do so less effectively in female samples? Or do
women interpret and respond to the items tapping dominance in a different way than men do, so that men and
women equal in dominance obtain systematically different scores on the scale? As discussed in the context of
ability tests, there is a well-accepted mechanism for examining whether there is predictive bias in the use of
tests, namely, the comparison of regression lines relating
test scores to job performance. If regression slopes and
intercepts are the same for the groups under consideration,
there is no predictive bias: Scores have the same meaning
for individuals regardless of group membership.
In contrast to the cognitive ability domain where
predictive bias has been examined in a large number of
studies, we were unable to find any examinations of predictive bias in the use of personality measures for employment decisions. In addition to our own search of the
literature, we contacted the authors of two recent, largescale meta-analytic reviews of the validity of personality
measures for personnel selection. Barrick and Mount

score adjustment.

Physical Ability Testing
A subset of jobs places considerable physical demands on
workers, involving muscular strength, cardiovascular endurance, flexibility, and the like. Preemployment testing
for physically demanding jobs typically takes one of two
different approaches. The first focuses on task sampling
and involves developing as direct a sample or simulation
of actual job tasks as possible. Tests of this sort are typically "homemade," rather than commercially purchased,
as the tests are specific to the job and work setting in
question. Asking a candidate for a firefighter position to
drag a dummy down several flights of stairs to simulate
the rescue of a fire victim typifies this approach. Tests
following this model are typically defended on content
validity grounds, and disputes about the tests revolve
around the adequacy of the job analysis on which the test
is based and the fidelity with which the test mimics the
actual job setting.
The second approach focuses on physical ability
constructs such as muscular endurance and aerobic power.
The tests are not specific task samples but rather are standardized measures of specific ability constructs. Their
use in a personnel selection setting is based on a job analysis that links physical ability constructs to job tasks. The
underlying logic is that of prediction, rather than task
sampling: Individuals with higher scores on physical ability constructs identified through job analysis are hypothesized to perform better on the job in question. This hypothesis is tested using the criterion-related validity
model, relying either on a local validity study or on the
transportability of validity evidence from other settings.
A central concern in the area of personnel selection
for physically demanding jobs focuses on adverse impact
against women. The data we review here on male-female
differences in physical ability focus on ability constructs,
rather than task samples. The task sampling approaches
produce measures idiosyncratic to each setting, and results of such applications are rarely published. The lack
of access to this research coupled with the idiosyncratic
nature of these job- and setting-specific tests thwarts attempts to meaningfully summarize male-female differences on these measures. Thus our review will focus on
measures of physical ability constructs.
We present here two types of data. The first involves
research that documents male-female differences in representative samples of the population; the second looks
at differences among applicants for, or incumbents in,
specific jobs. We hoped that this would allow us to address
the issue as to whether self-selection affects the magnitude
of male-female differences. In other words, even if marked
male-female differences exist on some physical ability
construct in the general population, it is possible that the
pattern of differences would be quite different among
those individuals who choose to apply for particular
physically demanding jobs.

ability constructs in broad national populations. The first

Biodata
Virtually all employers make use of biographical, or life
history, data as part of the selection process. For a great
many employers this is done informally through a subjective evaluation of resumes or application blanks. Other
employers make use of formal, scorable methods of evaluating life history data. At the heart of these approaches
is an empirical linkage between patterns of life history
data and job performance. Scoring systems are determined by comparing the responses of groups of high and
low performers. A variety of these methods exist, differing
on a number of dimensions. One dimension is the format
in which biographical information is sought. This ranges
from applying formal scoring rules to a traditional application blank to administering a lengthy set of multiplechoice questions. Another dimension is the content of the
information that is sought. One school of thought is that
biodata should be restricted to historical and verifiable
information, such as education and work experience;
others cast a much wider net and include questions about
values, future plans and aspirations, unverifiable questions
about reactions to life events (e.g., "What subject did you
like least in school?"), and questions asking for responses
to hypothetical events. A third dimension is the process
by which responses are scored. One approach can be labeled item-level empiricism, in which each scorable item
from the application blank or biodata form is examined
separately to determine whether responses to that item
differentiate high and low performers. The end result is
a score for each candidate that reflects the number of
items answered consistent with the responses of high performers. The score has no specific psychological meaning.
It merely reflects the degree to which an applicant gave
responses consistent with those of high performers.
An alternative approach seeks a meaningful factor
structure underlying biodata responses. For example, sets
of items may be intercorrelated and may load on factors
that can be interpreted and given names such as "academic achievement," "parental control," and "early financial independence." Scores on these factors are then
correlated with job performance. Thus unlike item-level
empiricism, factorially based approaches do produce
scores with psychological meaning.

Interest Inventories
We offer only a few brief comments on the use of interest
inventories. Although these measures are intended for
use in vocational guidance, there is at least some intuitive
appeal to the notion that checking to see if an applicant's
interest pattern matches that of individuals who are employed in the particular job in question may be a useful
part of the hiring process.

Of particular interest in the context of this article is
the consistent finding of large gender differences in responses to interest inventory items. The three most widely
used inventories respond to this finding in different ways.
The Strong-Campbell Interest Inventory has developed
separate male and female scales for various occupations
and reports individuals' scores on both the male and female scales. The Kuder Occupational Interest Survey uses
common scales for men and women but reports scores
compared with both male and female norms. The SelfDirected Search uses common scales for both men and
women and does not take gender into account in either
scoring or score reporting. Gender norms are available
in the test manual. (See Walsh & Osipow, 1986, and Watkins & Campbell, 1990, for overviews of interest measurement.)
There is little evidence that interest inventories are
extensively used in the context of personnel selection and
very limited research on the validity of interest inventories
as selection devices. That research has not been encouraging (see Guion, 1965; Schneider & Schmitt, 1986).
Dawis (1991) reported that there have been no investigations of predictive bias in using interest inventories to
predict job performance, and thus there are no empirical
grounds for preferring any of the three approaches to
gender differences outlined above. We advocate caution
in considering interest inventories as part of selection systems, absent local research addressing validity and predictive bias for the specific job in question.

Conclusion
We begin by restating the language of Section 106 of the
Civil Rights Act of 1991, namely, that it shall be an unlawful practice for an employer
in connection with the selection or referral of applicants or candidates for employment or promotion to adjust the scores of,
use different cutoffs for, or otherwise alter the results of employment related tests on the basis of race, color, religion, sex,
or national origin.
Our intent in this final section is to review possible interpretations of this statutory language and offer commentary based on the issues and findings reviewed in this
article. We first offer a summary of our key themes.
First, we have documented that substantial group
differences are found on a variety of measures commonly
used as bases for personnel decisions. Devices ranging
from cognitive ability tests to personality measures to
physical ability tests show group differences by race or
by gender.
Second, we have described a well-accepted mechanism for determining whether group differences are real
or are the result of bias, namely, the use of a model of
predictive bias to compare the regression lines relating
test scores with measures of job performance across
groups. The critical insight is that if group differences are
the result of bias, then a given test score would be linked
to differing levels of performance for different groups. For
example, if a 10-point mean difference on a test was, in

fact, due to bias against a particular minority group, then
minority group members should exhibit a higher level of
job performance than would majority group members
with the same test score.
Third, in domains where this regression model of
predictive bias has been applied, such as cognitive and
physical ability testing, the data do not support the position that group differences are the result of bias. This
argues against continued insistence that group differences
reflect nothing but test flaws. Note that this is also not a
blanket acceptance of all group differences as real. For
some types of selection devices there is little or no research
that applies the regression model, and there is no doubt
that within any area of testing, bad tests can be created
through ignorance or through malice. The key, again, is
that an accepted mechanism exists for examining the
question of bias.
Fourth, we attempted to differentiate between the
concepts of (a) predictive bias as a technical characteristic
of the use of a particular test in a particular setting and
(b) selection system fairness as a value judgment as to
the appropriateness of a method of test use or a pattern
of test outcomes. For example, the NAS committee on
the GATB endorsed score adjustment on the basis of the
position that a selection system is unfair if it produces a
larger group difference (and thus a lower rate of minority
hiring) than the group difference in job performance. If
an employer were to hire solely on the basis of cognitive
ability, he or she would be choosing to focus on an applicant characteristic on which Black-White differences
are exceptionally large. The problem, from the perspective
of the NAS committee's approach, is not that the ability
test is biased but that it is unfair to rely solely on a characteristic on which Blacks fare particularly badly, in
comparison with other job-related characteristics. That
the ability test is job-related and that ability tests can be
part of the selection system is not under dispute. The
issue is overreliance on a selection device with a larger
group difference than is found in job performance.
There is agreement within the psychological testing
community regarding the assessment of predictive bias;
there is no agreement as to the issue of fairness. The position on fairness articulated above requires a number of
value judgments, such as whether increased minority
employment per se is a goal to seek.
Fifth, the fact that many employers truly value both
diversity and productivity needs to be faced head on. If
a valid selection device has adverse impact, an employer
using that selection device cannot simultaneously maximize both productivity and minority hiring. To assert
that the two are not in conflict is to deny meaningful
group differences or at least to misunderstand and underestimate the impact of group differences. Approaches
such as the within-group norming method used with the
GATB are attempts at compromise. If an employer values
both diversity and productivity, then top-down selection
within group achieves the diversity goal at the least possible harm to the productivity goal that would be maximized by strict top-down selection.

We believe that the Civil Rights Act of 1991 cannot
prevent employers who value diversity from devising selection systems that will produce this result. The Act may
force employers to use less efficient selection systems.
Employers may eliminate valid predictors if they have
adverse impact. They may "bury" predictors with adverse
impact by using them in such a way that they have a
negligible effect on selection decisions (such as setting a
cutoff so low that virtually all applicants pass). They may
use less reliable selection systems, as group differences
are reduced by increasing the error variance in a test.
They may have decision makers subjectively combine test
scores and interview data, adding flexibility to the interpretation of test scores. In short, the Act may force employers with a diversity goal to turn to selection systems
that make a lower contribution to productivity.
Sixth, we reviewed a variety of ways of using tests
that take group membership into account. We showed
that adding points to minority group members' scores,
within-group norming, top-down selection from separate
lists, and setting differential cutoffs are functionally
equivalent in many circumstances. We paid particular
attention to banding and noted that a variety of approaches to banding exist. One crucial observation is that
only when minority preference within a band is included
as a feature of the banding approach does banding have
the potential to markedly reduce group differences in selection rates. A second crucial observation is that the
sliding-band/minority-preference approach recently articulated in the psychological literature can be shown to
be equivalent in outcome to adding a constant number
of points to minority group members' scores. Thus banding can be seen as a way of achieving the results of score
adjustment without the formal altering of scores.
We view the sliding-band/minority-preference approach as another example of an approach to selection
system fairness. It represents an espoused value-based
position as to how test scores should be used to make
personnel decisions. Although it makes use of psychometric tools (i.e., the use of the standard error of the
difference between scores to define bandwidth), the position that a lower scoring minority group member should
be preferred over a higher scoring majority group member— unless the scores of the two candidates are significantly different at a 95% level of confidence—is a value
judgment. Note that if the two individuals being compared are at the very top and very bottom, respectively,
of the band in question, then the probability is just under
95% that the high-scoring candidate's true score is higher
than that of the lower scoring candidate. Probabilities do
favor the higher scoring candidate.
The approaches to test scoring we have considered
in the above paragraphs involve taking group membership
into account after a test score is obtained. We also reviewed approaches that take group membership into account in the test development process. These include the
elimination of test items on the basis of their performance
in item tryout and the development of separate scoring
keys for different groups. We argue that taking group

of Section 106 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991 in light of
the above issues. We will pose and then address a series

1. Should the Act prohibit any approach to test use
that takes group membership into account in any way?
This interpretation would require that any individuals
responding in the same way on a test should receive the
same test scores regardless of group membership and
should have the same employment decision made about
them regardless of group membership. The findings reported in this article indicate that such a position can
interfere with psychologists' goals of developing valid selection devices that lack predictive bias against protected
groups. As noted above, taking group membership into
account would be necessary should a given test be shown
to exhibit some forms of predictive bias, namely, a finding
that performance is underpredicted for a particular group.
Also as noted above, taking group membership into account would be necessary should research demonstrate
that the development of separate scoring keys for different
groups is needed to produce comparable levels of validity
for the groups under consideration. This leads us to a
recommended interpretation of the Act:
Section 106 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991 should be interpreted
as prohibiting after-the-fact score adjustments undertaken solely
to reduce or eliminate adverse impact. Taking group membership into account is permitted if doing so can be shown to increase accuracy of measurement or accuracy of prediction
without increasing the adverse impact of the test.
We believe that such an interpretation is possible under
the statutory language of the Act. The Act prohibits score
adjustment "on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or
national origin." We interpret this as prohibiting adjustments done solely for purposes of reducing adverse impact
against protected groups. Adjustments made to alleviate
predictive bias or the development of different scoring
keys by group are approaches that use group membership
information in order to increase the accuracy of prediction of performance, not simply to alleviate adverse
impact.
2. Should banding be a permissible approach under
the Act? We first note that only banding approaches that
take group membership into account come under scrutiny
because of the Act. A banding approach that uses random
selection within a band, for example, does not take group
membership into account. It also does not typically produce the gains in minority group selection that are obtained with approaches to banding that do incorporate
minority preference. Banding approaches that give mi952

nority preference within a band often prove equivalent
to approaches that are expressly prohibited by the Act,
such as the use of separate cutoffs. Given this equivalence,
arguments for banding with minority preference must
rest on arguments that banding procedures neither explicitly adjust scores nor explicitly use different cutoffs
and thus that they do not violate the Act.
Banding is premised on the observation that a test
score difference between two people does not guarantee
that the higher scoring individual truly possesses more of
the characteristic being measured, given the less than perfect reliability of tests. There is considerable merit in the
notion that small differences between candidates should
not be overinterpreted. At that level there is some appeal
to banding. However, the notion that score differences
should be ignored unless the scores differ significantly
from one another is not a principle of test use that is
generally held. At the level of the individual, there is the
possibility that two individuals with cognitive ability test
scores differing by, say, one standard deviation unit do
not really differ in cognitive ability. However, in the aggregate there is no doubt that the average level of cognitive
ability among individuals scoring one standard deviation
apart is markedly different. Thus banding with minority
preference cannot be viewed as mandated by psychometric theory. It represents a value judgment as to what
constitutes fair test use, namely, that score differences
between majority and minority group members should
be ignored unless significantly different from one another,
while treating nonsignificant differences within a group
as meaningful. Decisions as to whether it should be permitted can, then, be driven not by the requirements of
psychometric theory but rather by issues outside the scope
of psychological research. Descriptively, the approach
does balance the competing goals of diversity and productivity. Arguments that banding with minority preference is not prohibited by the Act may be very attractive
to those who value such a balance.
3. Should Section 106 apply only to cognitive ability
tests or to all tests? The issues are identical regardless of
the type of test involved. There is no technical reason to
apply the Act to one type of test and not to another. However, to the extent that the Act was passed without full
information about its consequences, one can argue for as
narrow an interpretation as possible.
Consider the following scenario: (a) A firm valuing
both diversity and productivity wishes to fill a job with a
substantial physical strength component; (b) a strength
test has been developed and validated, showing a comparable linear relationship between strength and performance for both men and women; and (c) the strength test
shows a male-female d value of 1.5. Thus the test has
adverse impact, and the adverse impact is "real" in that
lower strength translates to lower performance. The firm
cannot achieve its diversity goal without (a) dropping the
test and ignoring the physical demands of the job; (b)
setting a cutoff low enough that enough women pass to
meet the diversity goal, thus losing the productivity gains
available through top-down selection of male applicants;

Note that all three of these approaches are similar,
if not identical, in terms of the number of positions eventually filled by men and by women. All are comparable
in achieving diversity. But the approaches that do not
involve score adjustment result in lower levels of productivity. This finding will occur whenever the selection
device under consideration is both a valid predictor of
performance and has adverse impact against a protected
group. Thus the Act will not thwart a firm seeking to
meet a diversity goal; it will simply result in the firm
accepting a larger productivity loss to attain this goal than
would be the case if score adjustment were permitted.
Thus a broad interpretation of the Act may prohibit
psychologists from using tests in a manner that balances
these competing goals. If the goals are seen as legitimate,
and if one accepts that tests can both be valid and have
adverse impact, then one concludes that Section 106 of
the Civil Rights Act of 1991 is not an effective mechanism
for regulating personnel selection and that the Act should
be interpreted as narrowly as possible.

Additional Observations
An issue that remains particularly troubling to us is the
appropriate course of action to be taken when a firm, for
logistic or convenience reasons, uses a selection system
that either focuses entirely or gives undue emphasis to
job-related selection devices with large adverse impact
against particular groups, while ignoring or underemphasizing job-related selection devices with less adverse
impact.
The Act prohibits score adjustment in either referral
or selection. We are particularly concerned about referral
processes in which applicants are tested at a preliminary
stage and only a subset are passed on for subsequent stages
in the selection process. If a selection device with large
adverse impact is used as the first hurdle, with relatively
few candidates proceeding to the next stage, then large
proportions of low-scoring groups are denied the opportunity to demonstrate in subsequent stages that they have
other attributes that, if known, would lead the employer
to prefer them over other candidates.
This problem is averted by putting all candidates
through all stages of the selection procedure. However,
consider the case of the GATB in which the USES offered
to screen candidates for employers using the GATB, and
top scorers were then referred to the employer for further
screening. The employer was not free to choose whether
to place the GATB first or last in the selection process.
This is a common occurrence in large-scale selection systems. With a very large number of applicants, relatively
inexpensive paper-and-pencil tests are administered initially, and the applicant pool is reduced to a size for which
more extensive screening is feasible. Thus for the convenience of the employer or for logistic reasons, a selection
system with larger adverse impact is used than would be
November 1994 • American Psychologist

the case if all selection devices were administered to all
candidates.
This is the observation that led the NAS committee
to recommend score adjustment. The issue is not that
the GATB is inadequate as a measure of cognitive ability;
it is one of giving cognitive ability a controlling role in
determining who would eventually be selected. We believe
that to date this distinction has not been clearly made
and thus that discussion about the appropriate course of
action under these circumstances has been hindered by
confusion of the technical merits of tests and the role of
tests in selection systems. We hope that fruitful discussion
will follow.
