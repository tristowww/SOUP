## Scale of Measurement {#slide-3}

How a variable is measured  

Nominal  -- categorize candidates by their eligibility status (eligible or not)

Ordinal  -- rank candidates in order of preference of suitability for the role

Interval  -- items where differences between scores are consistent and meaningful (cognitive ability, personality)

Ratio  -- used to measure quantities like number of years of relevant experience, where zero years means a lack of experience

## Multiple Predictor Methods:  Combining Predictor Scores {#slide-11}

::: {.smartart .list1 layout="list1"}

## Sackett & Wilk (1994) {#slide-32}

Within-group norming and other forms of score adjustment in preemployment testing

- Examines the practice of adjusting test scores based on applicant demographics (within-group norming) to address potential bias in tests
- Goals:  Account for potential cultural or socioeconomic factors that might disadvantage certain groups on standardized tests.
- Methods

<!-- -->

- Within-group norming-  comparing test scores of an applicant to the average scores of individuals from their specific demographic group
- Regression adjustment-  statistically adjusting test scores based on the relationship between tests scores and demographic variables 

32

## Sackett & Wilk (1994) {#slide-33}

-  Concerns

<!-- -->

- Score adjustments can mask underlying test bias
- Potential for unintended consequences

-  Alternative solutions

<!-- -->

- Focus on developing tests with minimal bias
- Use of multiple selection methods

## Kuncel  et al., (2013) {#slide-34}

Mechanical versus clinical data combination in selection and admission decisions: A meta-analysis

- Compares the effectiveness of combining  mechanical  (statistical) and  clinical  (judgment-based) methods for making selection decisions
- Results: Mechanical combination of data (e.g., test scores, GPAs) generally leads  to more accurate predictions  of job performance compared to solely relying on clinical judgement
- 
- Clinical judgement can still be valuable:

<!-- -->

- Identifying red flags in an applicant's background
- Evaluating factors not captured by standardized tests (e.g., soft skills, cultural fit)

## Article discussion questions {#slide-35}

1. Reliance on Subjectivity vs. Data-Driven Selection:

- Highhouse  (2008) argues against the overreliance on intuition and subjectivity in selection. Do you agree?
- What are the potential consequences of solely relying on subjective judgments during the hiring process?
- How can organizations effectively combine data-driven methods with a degree of human judgment in selection decisions?

2. Score Banding and its Implications:

- Campion et al. (2001) discuss the trade-offs associated with score banding.
- What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of using score banding in employee selection?
- How can organizations ensure that score banding practices are statistically sound and do not lead to a loss of valuable information about individual candidates?

3. Addressing Test Bias: Score Adjustments vs. Alternative Solutions:

- Sackett & Wilk (1994) explore the limitations of score adjustments for addressing test bias.
- What are the ethical concerns surrounding the practice of score adjustments based on demographics?
- Considering the limitations of score adjustments, what alternative strategies can be implemented to ensure fair and unbiased selection practices?

4. Integrating Research with Practice:

- Kuncel  et al. (2013) highlight the superiority of mechanical data combination compared to solely relying on clinical judgment.
- Based on the research presented, how can organizations translate these findings into practical applications for improving the effectiveness and fairness of their selection processes?
- What challenges might organizations face in implementing data-driven selection methods, and how can these challenges be overcome?

5. Ethical Considerations and Legal Implications:

- All articles emphasize the importance of fair and unbiased selection practices.
- How can organizations ensure that their selection methods comply with legal regulations and ethical guidelines established by the EEOC?
- What potential legal ramifications could organizations face due to biased selection practices?

Additional Discussion Points:

- Explore the role of technology in modern selection processes, including the use of online assessments and artificial intelligence tools.
- Discuss the importance of candidate experience throughout the selection process, even when utilizing data-driven methods.
- Consider the potential impact of unconscious bias on selection decisions, and strategies for mitigating its influence.
