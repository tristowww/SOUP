## Mechanical vs Clinical Data Combination in Selection and Admission Decisions: A Meta-Analysis {#slide-2}

Kuncel  et al. (2013)

2

## Mechanical vs Clinical Data {#slide-3}

Mechanical Approaches : apply an algorithm or formula to each participant's score

Clinical (Holistic) Approaches : include both individual judgments of data and group consensus meetings

Brunswik  Lens Model

Assumes people perceive information in their environment and combine one or more pieces of information into a judgment or prediction

3

## Meta-Analysis Results {#slide-4}

Larger correlations for mechanical methods over clinical methods

Substantially larger for job performance 

In predicting job performance, the difference between the validity of mechanical and clinical data combination methods translates into a population-level improvement in prediction of more than 50%

Predictive validity of clinical combination can result in a 25% reduction in correct hiring decisions

4

## Mechanical vs Clinical Arguments {#slide-5}

People argue that clinical (holistic) methods have the advantage since frequent changes in jobs or circumstances can lead to a situation where the equation is no longer appropriate...

When actually:

Performance dimensions of jobs have remained relatively stable

Linear models are robust to changes unless a sudden change from positive to negative weight

The use of an expert's subjective weights, integrated into a modified equation still outperforms holistic methods

## Implicit Beliefs {#slide-8}

Two implicit beliefs about employee selection:

People believe that it's possible to achieve near-perfect precision in the prediction of employee success

People believe there is intuitive expertise in the prediction of human behavior

"One must be willing to accept error to make less error"

Feelings of fit are attractive because it allows people to believe the future is inherently probabilistic

"Experts":

Rely on few pieces of information

They lack insight into how they arrive at predictions

They exhibit poor  interjudge  agreement

They become more confident in their accuracy when irrelevant information is presented

Most people believe that aspects of an applicant's character are far too complex to be assessed by scores, ratings, and formulas

## Within-Group Norming and Other Forms of Score Adjustment in Preemployment Testing {#slide-10}

Sackett & Wilk (1994)

## Score Adjustments {#slide-11}

The effects of group differences are greater as organizations become more selective

Rationale for Score Adjustments:

To right a social wrong

To alleviate bias

Fairness

Making sure that imperfections in predictors of job performance don't disproportionately affect minority groups

11

## Types of Score Adjustments {#slide-12}

Bonus Points : adding a constant number of points to the scores of all individuals who are members of a particular group

Within-Group Norming : converting individual scores to either standard scores or percentile scores within one's group

Separate Cutoffs : setting separate cutoffs for different groups

Top-Down Selection From Separate Lists : ranking candidates separately within groups and then selecting top-down from within each group based on preset rules

Banding:  individuals within a specific score range (or band) are regarded as having equivalent scores

12

## Group Differences {#slide-13}

There are substantial group differences on a variety of measures that are used for personnel decisions

Cognitive ability tests

Personality measures

Physical ability

To determine if group differences are real or the results of bias use a model of predictive bias to compare the regression lines relating test scores with measures of job performance across groups

## Case Study {#slide-18}

Bowl of Soup Inc., is a family-run canned soup manufacturer that has seen rapid growth over the past couple of years but has high turnover. The owner Billy has recently stepped down to allow his daughter, Bobby, to take over the company. Currently, Bowl of Soup does not have a standardized selection system. Bobby is interested in implementing structured interviews to help remedy this. Since discussing the change with the hiring managers, she has heard a lot of pushback regarding her undermining their expertise. The hiring managers feel they know what to look for in a candidate and adding structure is going to make the interviews even more awkward. Bobby wants to know how to effectively communicate the need for the change and what possible pushback she will encounter.
