## Automated Video Interview Personality Assessments: Reliability, Validity, and Generalizability Investigations {#slide-2}

By: Louis Hickman, Nigel Bosch, Vincent Ng, Rachel Saef, Louis Tay, Sang Eun Woo (2022) 

## Background  {#slide-3}

- It is becoming common for organizations to automated video interviews (AVIs) 
- AVIs use machine learning to evaluate interviewees for early-stage applicant screenings since AVIs can reduce the time it takes to hire employees and AVIs can save organizations money
- AVI's use computers to assess the verbal, paraverbal (includes speech rate and voice quality) and nonverbal (includes smiles and visual attention) behaviors of interviewees
- These verbal, paraverbal, and nonverbal behaviors are then used as predictors in machine learning models to assess the personality traits of interviewees
- The researchers trained machine learning models to predict the interviewee self- and interviewer-reported traits of participants
- This study\'s main purpose is to critically assess the psychometric properties of automated video interview personality assessments (AVI-PAs).

## Findings {#slide-4}

- Most of the convergent correlations between self- and interviewer-reported traits were found to be positive and significant. Self-other agreement was the highest for extraversion and the lowest for conscientiousness.
- Models trained on interviewer-reports demonstrated improved convergence and discriminant validity compared to self-report models.
- The usage of interviewer-reports was found to be reliable for extraversion, conscientiousness, and openness across samples. However, the reliability for self-report models was poor.
- The AVI-PA scores were found to correlate with academic outcomes such as standardized test scores and GPA. Interviewer-report models were found to explain more variance than compared with self-reports.

## Real World Implications  {#slide-5}

- These results indicate that the psychometric properties of the AVI-PAs may remain fairly consistent when the models trained on one set of questions are used to assess interviewees that were asked a different set of questions 
- There is concern that the algorithmic bias of AVIs may be biased against African Americans due to the use of facial recognition software which measures nonverbal behavior. Such software is typically less accurate for people with darker skin tones since less light is reflected into the camera, which makes it more difficult to observe the contours of the face 
- There are concerns about whether AVIs comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act since certain disabilities affect speech, movement, and facial expressions. Applicants protected by the ADA may be adversely affected or discriminated against.
- If practitioners modify the algorithms for their organization, validity evidence must be assessed for the resultant, new test. If these procedures are challenged in court, an organization could be held responsible for failing to assess the validity of the AVIs they use.

## The future of artificial intelligence at work: A review on effects of decision automation and augmentation on workers targeted by algorithms and third-party observers:  {#slide-6}

By: Langer & Landers (2021)

## Background  {#slide-7}

- In the past few decades researchers and practitioners have explored how decision-making automation and augmentation can increase the decision quality and efficiency at organizations
- Automation: 
- Refers to entirely automating decision processes by entirely eliminating human control
- Augmentation: 
- Refers to the addition of automated systems to help support the decisions made by humans.

## Background (contd.) {#slide-8}

- First party:
- Refers to individuals that use or interact with the output of such systems to make decisions which impact other individuals
- First parties have at least some direct control over whether and to what extent an artificial system will affect the decisions they personally make. 
- Second parties:
- Refers to individuals that cannot choose whether they want to be affected by systems, their outputs, or decisions based on those outputs unless they choose to exit the decision-making context entirely, such as by quitting their job. 
- Second parties include employees that are assigned work tasks from automated systems, employees whose shifts are determined automatically, and job applicants whose application information is assessed by automated systems.
- Third parties:
- Refers to individuals that observe an automated or augmented decision without being directly impacted by that decision. Although, they are not directly affected by an automated or augmented decision they may anticipate that they could become a second party in the future. 

## Findings {#slide-9}

- Most individuals believe that automation systems take into account more factors overall but fail to consider qualitative information, unique conditions, and decontextualize and quantify decision processes. 
- Most of the reviewed studies concluded that individuals perceive automated decisions as less fair than human decisions for performance evaluations, personnel selection, and scheduling scenarios.
- Individuals favor human control in high-stake decisions and seem to typically perceive augmented decisions more favorably than automated decisions. 
- Research from Longoni et al. (2019) demonstrated that the more unique a person feels the less they want an automated system to evaluate them.

## Real World Implications  {#slide-10}

- The issues of accountability and responsibility are commonly mentioned when automated systems are used for high-stakes decisions. 
- When people rely on automated systems situations may arise where it is unclear who is accountable for errors 
- This article demonstrated that that most of our current understanding of the effects of these automated and augmented decisions on second and third parties comes from independent contractors that report their experiences with algorithmic management systems. 
- As a result, the generalizability of this literature to other work contexts with more complex decision-making systems  remains unclear. 

## What\'s on job seekers\' social media sites? A content analysis and effects of structural effects on recruiter judgments and predictive validity: {#slide-11}

By: Liwen Zhang, Chad H. Van Iddekinge, John D. Arnold, Philip L. Roth, and Filip Lievens (2020)

## Background  {#slide-12}

- Previous surveys have found that 70% or more of recruiters and hiring managers search applicants' social media (SM) accounts and some even use this information to screen applicants, with Facebook being the site reviewed most frequently.  
- In addition, 36% of recruiters instruct their applicants to grant them access to their Facebook information, and the majority of applicants comply. 
- There is concern that use of SM information that may be irrelevant to the job and could decrease the accuracy of selection decisions or result in adverse impact. 

## The Three Research Studies in this Article {#slide-13}

- Study 1: Content Analysis of Job Seeker SM Information
- Study 2: SM Information and Recruiter Evaluations
- Study 3: Structuring the Assessment of SM Information

## Study 1: Content Analysis of Job Seeker SM Information Background   {#slide-14}

- All participants had to be actively searching for a job and had to have a Facebook account. 
- Assessed what types of information is available on job seekers' SM sites

## Study 1: Results {#slide-15}

- Political party affiliation was found on 5.3% of the Facebook accounts 
- However, an additional 21.1% of the Facebook accounts included information about the political views of participants (but not their specific political party affiliation). 
- The Facebook accounts also included information organizations typically assess, including education level (56.8%) and work experience (41.0%). 
- 51.1% of the sites Facebook accounts had profanity
- Around one quarter (25.6%) of Facebook accounts showed or suggested participants consuming alcohol

## Study 2: SM Information and Recruiter Evaluations Background {#slide-17}

- The participants included undergraduate and graduate students that were near graduation and were searching for jobs or preparing to do so
- To complete this study participants all had a Facebook account and provided the researchers access to their account.
- The researchers assessed if job seekers' SM information is related to recruiter evaluations

## Study 2: Results {#slide-18}

- Recruiter evaluation ratings were found to be higher for females than for males
- Participants whose SM accounts included information about their religious beliefs received lower ratings than those whose SM accounts did not include religious information
- Education, work-related training or skills, and written communication skills were positively associated with recruiter evaluations
- Profanity, alcohol use, drug use, sexual behavior, violence, illegal activities, and negative work attitudes/behavior were significantly and negatively related to recruiter ratings

## Study 3: Structuring the Assessment of SM Information Background {#slide-19}

- The researchers randomly selected 81 of the 140 job seekers from Study 2 to participate in this study
- Applicants' SM accounts often contains minimal job-relevant information. 
- The types as well as the amount of SM information can vary greatly across applicants
- Therefore, the researchers assessed if structured SM assessments show stronger evidence of criterion-related validity than compared with unstructured assessments?

## Study 3: Results {#slide-20}

- The structured assessments were found not to relate to in-role performance and actually demonstrated a marginally significant, negative relationship with extra-role performance 
- Turnover intentions were not significantly predicted by the unstructured or the structured assessments. 
- Ultimately, structuring SM assessments was found not improve criterion-related validity.

## Real World Implications {#slide-21}

- The findings of this study support concerns that reviewing applicants' SM accounts opens the possibility for personal information to effect staffing decisions
- Generally the Facebook accounts of job seekers do not include much information that is relevant to many types of jobs
- Overall, organizations that use SM accounts such as Facebook for hiring purposes, or that are considering doing so, should reconsider this practice until more substantial emerges.
- This research provides empirical support for the common advice that individuals should be careful what they post since information posted on job seekers SM accounts can affect recruiter evaluations
