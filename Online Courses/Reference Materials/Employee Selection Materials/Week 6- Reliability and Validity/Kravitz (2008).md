THE DIVERSITY–VALIDITY DILEMMA: BEYOND
SELECTION—THE ROLE OF AFFIRMATIVE ACTION
DAVID A. KRAVITZ
George Mason University

Several of the most valid predictors used to make employment decisions
create a diversity–validity dilemma (Pyburn, Ployhart, & Kravitz, this
issue, 2008). This diversity–validity dilemma can be resolved by (a)
reducing adverse impact through a variety of technical steps (Ployhart
& Holtz, this issue, 2008) or (b) using affirmative action to increase
representation of the disadvantaged groups. This paper focuses on the
second approach. The paper begins with a very brief review of the legal
bases of affirmative action and a summary of the research on affirmative action attitudes. This is followed with reviews of research on the
ongoing existence of workplace discrimination, the economic impact of
affirmative action on target groups and organizations, and stigmatization
of target group members by others and by target group members themselves. Most problems with affirmative action apply only or primarily
to preference-based forms, so nonpreferential approaches to affirmative
action are recommended to increase the attraction, selection, inclusion,
and retention of underrepresented group members.

Organizations face a diversity–validity dilemma when they base employment decisions on valid predictors that produce large subgroup differences (Pyburn, Ployhart, & Kravitz, 2008). One way to resolve this
dilemma is to reduce subgroup differences in the selection system (Ployhart & Holtz, 2008). A second approach is to increase representation of
disadvantaged groups through affirmative action, defined as “any measure,
beyond simple termination of a discriminatory practice, adopted to correct
or compensate for past or present discrimination or to prevent discrimination from recurring in the future” (United States Commission on Civil
Rights, 1977).

controversy, this paper begins with a brief summary of research on affirmative action attitudes. It then reviews research on five questions: (a) Does

The Basis of Affirmative Action

Detailed descriptions of the law can be found elsewhere (Gutman,
2000; Pyburn et al., 2008), so only a few key points are given here. The legal bases for affirmative action in the United States are varied. Of most importance, Executive Order 11246 (EO 11246), issued by President Johnson
in 1965, requires federal contractors to take affirmative action to ensure
that all individuals have an equal opportunity for employment, without
regard to race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. Other federal regulations require affirmative action for the employment of individuals with
disabilities (Section 502 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973) and for certain
veterans (Section 4212 of the Vietnam Era Veterans’ Readjustment Assistance Act). Affirmative action plans also may result from court orders or
from consent decrees resolving lawsuits filed by the Equal Employment
Opportunity Commission (EEOC) or private plaintiffs. Finally, affirmative
action is required by certain state and local regulations, and some employers establish affirmative action plans (AAPs) on a purely voluntary basis.
Which actions are required, permitted, or forbidden depends on the
legal basis of the AAP. To comply with the regulations that implement EO
11246, federal contractors must attempt to detect and eliminate all practices that make it more difficult for members of targeted groups (women
and racioethnic minorities) to succeed. Therefore, the organization must
perform adverse impact analyses on selection procedures and either eliminate or validate all procedures that have adverse impact against these
groups. Guidance regarding appropriate selection procedures is offered
by the Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures (Equal
Employment Opportunity Commission, 1978). The firm must periodically complete a utilization analysis and make the results available to the

Attitudes Toward Affirmative Action

Public attitudes affect public policy through the election of political
leaders. Attitudes of organizational leaders determine organizational policies and attitudes of managers influence implementation. Attitudes of employees affect the impact of affirmative action on interpersonal relations.
In short, affirmative action attitudes can have profound effects at the national, organizational, and interpersonal levels. An extensive literature on
affirmative action attitudes assesses the impact of predictors of these attitudes, which can be assigned to two categories: attributes of the AAP and
attributes of the perceiver (Harrison, Kravitz, Mayer, Leslie, & Lev-Arey,
2006).
Attributes of the Affirmative Action Plan

AAPs can be assigned to four categories that vary in strength. The
weakest category is the simple elimination of discrimination. Somewhat
stronger are opportunity enhancement policies, such as targeted recruitment or training, designed to help the target group without directly affecting employment decisions. Still stronger is the selection of target
group members when their credentials are equivalent to those of nontarget
applicants—a tiebreak procedure. The strongest category includes quotas
and strong preferences, in which target group members may be selected
over more qualified individuals who are not in the target group. Strong
preferential treatment is usually illegal and even a tiebreak procedure must
be justified by prior discrimination or substantial underrepresentation of
the target group (Pyburn et al., 2008). AAP strength appears to have a
monotonic negative effect on Whites’ attitudes, but Blacks and Hispanics
do not distinguish as strongly among the three weaker forms of affirmative action and may even prefer recruitment to the simple elimination of
discrimination (Kravitz & Klineberg, 2002). Research also suggests that

Attributes of the Perceiver

Attitudes toward affirmative action are related to several attributes of
the perceiver—the individual whose attitude is being assessed. There is
an effect of racioethnicity, with Blacks being most supportive, Whites being least supportive, and Hispanics and Asians typically (but not always)
expressing intermediate levels of support (Bell, Harrison, & McLaughlin,
1997; Harrison et al., 2006). Nevertheless, the effect of racioethnicity on
attitudes is absent for the weakest AAPs and then increases with AAP
strength (Kravitz & Klineberg, 2002). A sex difference also exists, with
women usually more supportive than men, and this effect appears to increase with AAP strength (Harrison et al., 2006).
Of equal or greater importance than the demographic differences are
some opinion variables. Both racism and sexism predict opposition to affirmative action, though the effect of racism is moderated by AAP strength
(Harrison et al., 2006; Kravitz & Klineberg, 2004). Liberals and Democrats
express more support than do conservatives and Republicans (Harrison
et al., 2006). Support for affirmative action is positively associated with
the belief that workplace discrimination still exists (Harrison et al., 2006).
Opposition to affirmative action is also predicted by social dominance
orientation—a belief in the appropriateness of inequality among groups
and the desire to maintain this hierarchy (Federico & Sidanius, 2002).
The effects of demographic and opinion predictors are mediated, at
least in part, by beliefs about implications of affirmative action for the
perceiver’s self-interest (Kravitz, 1995). Finally, the strongest predictor
of attitudes toward affirmative action is the perceiver’s belief about its
fairness (Harrison et al., 2006). Fairness perceptions mediate the effects
of some other predictors, most notably AAP strength. Consistent with the
strong effect of fairness, opposition to affirmative action is often justified by statements regarding its unfairness. Research by Bobocel and her
colleagues suggests that the fairness argument is sometimes genuine but
sometimes serves as a rationalization for prejudice (Bobocel, Davey, Son
Hing, & Zanna, 2001).

action attitudes. First, attitudes are closely related to beliefs about how
the AAP is implemented. Second, attitudes are related to several perceiver attributes. These considerations imply that people will differ in

Some Controversial Issues
Question 1: Does Discrimination Still Occur?

If discrimination is no longer a problem, there is no need for affirmative action to address it. Unfortunately, discrimination is still a problem
(Fix & Struyk, 1993). For example, Bertrand and Mullainathan (2004)
sent thousands of fictitious resumes in response to help-wanted ads and
found that applicants with White names (e.g., Emily and Greg) received
50% more callbacks for interviews than did applicants with Black names
(e.g., Lakisha and Jamal). This result controlled for resume quality, which
had a significant positive effect on the callback rate for Whites but a small
mostly nonsignificant effect for Black applicants. Turning from race to sex
discrimination, Bayard, Hellerstein, Neumark, and Troske (2003) used a
nationwide matched employer–employee sample to explore the sex gap
in wages. They found that about one-third of the gap could be explained
by segregation of women into lower-paying occupations, industries, establishments, and occupations within establishments. Furthermore, a substantial gap remained after controlling for those differences and a variety
of human capital variables. Consistent with these results, the EEOC receives approximately 80,000 discrimination claims annually, with about
two-thirds involving racioethnic or sex discrimination (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 2005). In short, workplace discrimination based on demographic categories is a serious ongoing problem in the
United States.
Unfortunately, various aspects of normal psychological functioning
make discrimination very difficult to eradicate. For example, unintentional
discrimination can result from stereotyping (Krieger, 1995), in-group bias
(Tajfel, 1970), and implicit biases (Banaji, Bazerman, & Chugh, 2003).
People will not support actions designed to eliminate discrimination if they
believe it does not exist, as is true of many White Americans (Saad, 2003a,
2003b). This belief may be due to motivation (e.g., desire for a just world;
Begue & Bastounis, 2003) or cognitive limitations (e.g., inability to detect
discrimination; Twiss, Tabb, & Crosby, 1989). Even if an employee is
aware of having experienced discrimination, he or she may not complain
due to fear of retaliation, a fear that appears to be justified, given the
thousands of retaliation claims lodged annually with the EEOC (Equal
Employment Opportunity Commission, 2005). Even in the absence of
fear, people may not complain because they believe their evidence is too

For all the above reasons, it is unlikely discrimination will be eliminated in the foreseeable future. Therefore, organizations need ways to identify and counteract discrimination. Affirmative action laws are designed
to do precisely that by requiring antidiscrimination policies, utilization
analyses, and specification of additional steps when underutilization is
detected.
Question 2: What Is the Economic Impact of Affirmative Action
on Target Groups?

Even if one believes discrimination exists, one might oppose affirmative action if it does not help disadvantaged groups. The effectiveness of
affirmative action is addressed by modeling and empirical research. Mathematical models show that it could take decades for the mere elimination
of discrimination to result in equity in employment (Feinberg, 1984) or
earnings (Daymont, 1980). Even in the absence of discrimination, attainment of equity vis-à-vis qualifications would be affected by the initial
level of disparity, the frequency with which positions become available,
and the application and attrition rates of all demographic groups. Largescale multifirm research indicates that affirmative action has had a positive
effect on the employment status of White women, Blacks, and Hispanics
(e.g., Holzer & Neumark, 2000a; Kalev, Dobbin, & Kelly, 2006; Smith,
2001). Nevertheless, the results of this and other research indicate that
the effects of affirmative action are complex, varying across target group,
location, time, and position. As is true of any organizational policy, the
impact of any AAP doubtlessly depends on support from top leadership
and on details of the plan (Button & Rienzo, 2003).
Question 3: What is the Economic Impact of Affirmative Action
on Organizations?

The public debate surrounding affirmative action often centers on the
presumed consequences of affirmative action programs for organizations.
Critics claim that AAPs adversely affect performance by (a) forcing organizations to expend resources on implementation (Brimelow & Spencer,
1993), (b) forcing organizations to hire underqualified individuals (Zelnick, 1996), and (c) decreasing the motivation of employees who conclude that success is based on preferences for women and racioethnic minorities rather than on performance (Hatch, 1980). Supporters argue that
AAPs improve organizational performance by (a) providing organizations
with the resources represented by a diverse workforce (Cox, 1993) and

is no net effect on performance, however, perhaps, Holzer and Neumark
suggest, because these organizations also have superior human resource
management practices that enable them to make more effective use of their
employees.

Question 4: Does Affirmative Action Lead to Stigmatization of Target Group
Members by Others?

One criticism of affirmative action is that it causes other people to
question the competence of selected individuals who belong to the targeted groups. This stigmatization follows from the discounting principle
of attribution theory (Kelley, 1972). In the absence of additional information, selection of a person for a position is taken as evidence that the person
is qualified. But if the person belongs to a group targeted by an AAP, a
perceiver may not be sure whether the selection decision was due to the
individual’s qualifications or to the organization’s need to reach its affirmative action goal. Note that this reasoning applies only if the perceiver
believes that the organization’s AAP involves preferences.
Laboratory research suggests that “affirmative action hires” are rated
less competent than other hires when researchers tell their participants
that affirmative action involves preferences (Heilman, Battle, Keller, &
Lee, 1998). This effect is also found when affirmative action is undefined
(Garcia, Erskine, Hawn, & Casmay, 1981), consistent with the common
assumption among Whites that affirmative action involves preferences
(Golden, Hinkle, & Crosby, 2001). Nevertheless, stigmatization can be
greatly decreased or eliminated by providing incontrovertible information of the employee’s competence or successful performance on the job
(Heilman, Block, & Stathatos, 1997). Stigmatization also does not occur when it is clear that the AAP does not involve preferences (Evans,
2003).

Question 5: Does Affirmative Action Lead to Self-Stigmatization by Target
Group Members?

The discounting logic explained above suggests that individuals targeted by affirmative action may question the basis of their selection and
their own competence (Major, Feinstein, & Crocker, 1994). Such selfdoubt could also result from the self-threatening implication of receiving
help (Turner & Pratkanis, 1994). The discomfort of the situation may also
decrease their motivation to accept the position and to excel. In the case
of self-evaluations, the situation is further complicated by other psychological factors such as the tendency to think well of ones self (Dunning,
Heath, & Suls, 2005), a tendency that varies across demographic groups
(Mezulis, Abramson, Hyde, & Hankin, 2004).
Empirical results are mixed. Self-doubt and other negative reactions
have been observed among White female undergraduates who are told that
their selection was based solely on their sex and who are not given evidence
of their qualifications or performance (e.g., Heilman et al., 1998). Their
initial level of self-confidence regarding their competence at the assigned
job seems to be of critical importance (Heilman, Rivero, & Brett, 1991).
Nevertheless, self-stigmatization is absent or greatly reduced among preferentially selected White males (Heilman, Simon, & Repper, 1987) and
racial minorities (Taylor, 1994), and when subjects are given unambiguous evidence of their qualifications or performance (Heilman et al., 1991,
1998).
Practical Implications and Recommendations

The most consistent finding in research on affirmative action is that
preferences cause problems. Indeed, the mere assumption that affirmative action involves preferences, even if incorrect, causes problems. First,
strong preferences are usually illegal, and even treating racioethnicity or
sex as a plus factor is permissible only under very restricted circumstances
(see Pyburn et al., 2008). Second, the public opposes preferential AAPs
more strongly than weaker approaches, and some perceiver variables have
larger effects on reactions to preferential than to nonpreferential AAPs.
Therefore, the use of preferences is likely to provoke social conflict. Third,
only preferential approaches have the potential to decrease the human capital available to firms and thereby decrease organizational performance.
Fourth, only preferential forms of affirmative action lead to stigmatization
of affirmative action targets by others and by themselves.
The preceding points imply that firms should almost never use preferences; the limited conditions under which preferences are legal are detailed
in Pyburn et al. (2008). Instead, firms should take actions to eliminate

The Basics

The firm should obviously begin by satisfying the core requirements
of affirmative action law and regulations. It should forbid discrimination,
create a complaint procedure for use by employees who believe they have
experienced discrimination, and assign oversight responsibility to an appropriate manager. These actions might be useful even for organizations
that are not required to have an AAP, because they may contribute to the
development of a positive culture.
Gather Data

Because casual observation is unlikely to detect discrimination, as
demonstrated by research cited above, relevant data must be compiled.
The utilization analyses required by EO 11246 compare the organization’s
workforce with the relevant labor market and will reveal disparities. The
results can be combined with other data to calculate an index value that
summarizes the changes needed to correct disparities within each job group
(Stutz & Massengale, 1997). In addition to workforce distributions and employment decisions (e.g., selection and promotion), compensation should
be analyzed. The OFCCP provides guidance at http://www.dol.gov/esa/
regs/compliance/ofccp/compdata.htm. These analyses could be used as evidence in litigation, so careful consideration must be given as to how they
are conducted and reported. The firm’s legal counsel should be involved.
The numerical analysis may be supplemented with a diversity audit
designed to reveal policies or practices that create actual or perceived
problems for members of disadvantaged groups (Grensing-Pophal, 2001).
The audit can obtain information in a variety of ways, including reviews of

If the analyses or audit reveal problems, the organization should take
steps to eliminate the problems. It should begin by establishing goals pertaining to measurable outcomes (Jayne & Dipboye, 2004). For underutilization, the goals should be based on staffing needs and on the availability

held accountable for making progress toward their assigned goals (Digh,
1998; Lockwood, 2006). Such accountability requires that appropriate outcomes be measured and feedback provided (Jayne & Dipboye, 2004). A
survey of HR professionals found that a majority of firms collects metrics
on diversity-related practices, but only about 12% provide incentive pay
that is tied to the achievement of diversity goals (Esen, 2005). Both actions
are much more common among large firms than small ones.
Take Action

Finally, the organization should take actions to eliminate the problems.
What actions should be taken? The possibilities are legion and may involve
structural, cultural, and behavioral changes (Holvino, Ferdman, & MerrillSands, 2004). Few strong recommendations can be offered because the
effectiveness of any given action is affected by many factors, including
the firm’s context, business strategy, culture and climate, and workforce.

selection–attrition (ASA) model posits that an organization’s culture affects who is attracted to the organization, who is selected to join the organization, and who chooses to leave the organization (Schneider, Goldstein,
& Smith, 1995). Considering the ASA model in light of diversity concerns could lead to an attraction–selection–inclusion–retention (ASIR)
approach. Specifically, organizations should use a variety of policies and
practices to establish a culture that will attract members of underrepresented groups, increase their rates of selection, and enhance their sense
of inclusion and, hence, retention. As the ASIR model suggests, the best
long-term approach to eliminating underutilization is to create an organizational culture in which everyone feels valued and welcome (Thomas &
Ely, 1996). The ASIR model suggests a link between affirmative action and
diversity management because an effective diversity management program
includes actions designed to ensure inclusion and will help organizations
attain their affirmative action goals (Holvino et al., 2004). Indeed, Kalev
et al. (2006) include existence of an AAP as one of seven diversity practices in their empirical study of factors that affect representation of White
and Black men and women in managerial ranks. A plethora of programs
could make the workplace more inviting for members of underutilized
group and could increase retention. Creation of an independent diversity
department and staff would signal a commitment to inclusion (Kalev et
al., 2006), particularly if the head of the department was included in the
top management team.
Attraction. Members of target groups cannot be hired unless they
apply for positions. Therefore, the organization should use targeted recruitment to make relevant individuals aware of positions and motivate
them to apply (Kalev et al., 2006). It is important to realize that potential employees will evaluate the organization when deciding where to
apply for positions. If the organization is not known as an employer of

be needed to obtain talented diverse employees. Some general advice and

(McPherson, Smith-Lovin, & Cook, 2001). The use of appropriate recruiting strategies is one of the most common diversity practices (Esen, 2005).

Selection. The diversity–validity dilemma exists because, even among

All the steps discussed to this point will go for naught if applicants decline job offers (Murphy, 1986). On the basis of past research, McKay and
Avery (2006) propose a model to predict applicants’ job acceptance decisions, which they posit are most immediately affected by their perceptions
of the diversity climate. Employees’ diversity climate perceptions are also
essential for determining a sense of inclusion and retention, to which we
now turn.
Inclusion and retention. Attracting and hiring members of underrepresented groups are just the first steps—it is also necessary to help them
succeed, motivate them to stay, and retain them. Which interventions will
best increase retention depends on the stage of the organization’s development, from exclusionary to multicultural (Holvino et al., 2004). To
maximize support and avoid backlash, the organization should emphasize
actions that are open to all employees (Frase-Blunt, 2003). For example,
taking steps to decrease incivility of all types, as suggested in Table 1, will
help all employees but will be particularly helpful to those who are most
frequently subjected to harassment (i.e., female, racioethnic minority, and
gay employees). A potential danger of diversity initiatives is that they are
likely to raise the expectations of target group members, who may react
negatively if the organization fails to meet those expectations (Society for
Human Resource Management, 2001).
Training of managers and employees is one of the most common diversity strategies (Kalev et al., 2006). Although it is associated with perceived effectiveness of diversity practices (Esen, 2005), research suggests
that diversity training can actually backfire. Under some conditions, it is
associated with a decrease in minority representation (Kalev et al., 2006).
Training is also said to be one of the most expensive diversity practices
(Society for Human Resource Management, 2001). Therefore, great care
should be given when deciding what types of training to provide—training
in employment law will probably be needed, but training designed to
change attitudes may lead to resistance. The mixed effects also imply that
organizations should carefully assess the effects of their diversity training
programs.

Both mentoring and affinity groups (also called affiliation groups, employee resource groups, internal caucus groups, and so on) can increase
managers’ ability to supervise diverse employees and can support career
development for underrepresented group members. Mentoring provides
the protégée with guidance, support, and connections to informal networks. It is associated with a variety of hard and soft benefits (Allen, Eby,
Poteet, & Lentz, 2004; Kalev et al., 2006). Members of affinity groups
can obtain similar benefits from their more experienced colleagues. In
addition, mentoring and affinity groups can provide guidance to mentors
and top management about the perspective of the underrepresented group.
The organization may also choose to provide benefits that are available
to all employees but are of special value to members of underrepresented
groups. For example, family friendly policies tend to be of special use to
women, whose careers are often hindered by their disproportionate share
of the caregiver load (Hewlett & Luce, 2005; Kalev et al., 2006).
Finally, the organization could signal its commitment to diversity by
taking actions outside its boundaries. Community outreach activities could
include supporting external groups or celebrations of special interest to
members of underrepresented group.
Assess and Revise

The success of these endeavors should be assessed on a regular basis and changes made as necessary. Affirmative action employers must
compute the utilization analyses on an annual basis; other employers may
want to follow suit simply to maintain an understanding of their current
status, assuming, of course, that they intend to address any problems that
are revealed. As mentioned above, it is possible to compute an index that
summarizes the diversity status of a unit and the changes needed to attain
unit goals. In addition, employee surveys can identify areas of concern, as
can effective use of exit surveys.

In conclusion, affirmative action can address the diversity–validity
dilemma by facilitating the attraction, selection, inclusion, and retention
of underrepresented group members. The state of affirmative action today
is well summarized by the title of Faye Crosby’s (2004) book: “Affirmative
Action Is Dead; Long Live Affirmative Action.” Preferential forms of affirmative action are rarely legal, and virtually all the problems associated
with affirmative action are associated with preferences. In this sense, affirmative action is (legally) dead. Nevertheless, demographic-based discrimination still exists, and organizations must take actions to eliminate such
discrimination and overcome the effects of past discrimination. Nonpreferential forms of affirmative action, from utilization analyses to targeted
recruitment to diversity management programs, are designed to eliminate
and compensate for such discrimination. In this sense, affirmative action
is alive and likely to live long.
