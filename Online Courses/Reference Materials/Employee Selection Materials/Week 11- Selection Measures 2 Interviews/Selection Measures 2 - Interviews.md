## Employment Interview on Trial: Linking Interview with Litigation Outcomes Williamson et al. (1997) {#slide-20}

## Author Goals and Method  {#slide-21}

Goal 1: examine the  conceptual link  between interview structure and litigation outcomes in discrimination cases

Goal 2: evaluate the  empirical relationship  between interview structure and the trial court judges decisions in reported employment discrimination 

- Focused on analyzing written case reports involving discrimination allegations in employment interview 

<!-- -->

- Meeting specific criteria 

## Takeaways {#slide-22}

Conceptual link:

- Enhancing legal defensibility: Standardization, ensuring job-relatedness, reducing subjectivity and potential bias, and enhancing perceptions of procedural justice

Empirical link:

- 3 composites: interview objectivity, standardization, job-relatedness, and multiple interviewers
- Similarity of importance of structure in all claims 
- Measures of structure intercorrelated

<!-- -->

- 

"It is gratifying that the factors judges consider when explaining court decisions correspond to recommendations long made by IO psychologists for improving the interview. It is reassuring to see that those aspects of interviewing systems found through research to contribute to enhanced reliability and validity are also viewed by the courts as enabling the interview to protect against unlawful employment discrimination" (Williamson et al. 1997).
