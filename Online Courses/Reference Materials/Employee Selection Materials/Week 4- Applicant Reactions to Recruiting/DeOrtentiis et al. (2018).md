Build or Buy? The Individual and Unit-Level Performance of Internally
Versus Externally Selected Managers Over Time

At some point, hiring managers in all organizations face the decision of whether to fill open positions
with internal candidates (e.g., through promotions) or to hire external candidates (e.g., from competitors
or new entrants into the labor market). Despite this ubiquitous choice, surprisingly little research has
compared the effectiveness of internal and external selection or has identified situations in which 1
approach may be better than the other. The authors use theory on human capital resources to predict
differences between internal and external hires on manager- and unit-level outcomes. Analysis of data
from a quick-service retail organization (N ⫽ 3,697) suggested that internally hired managers demonstrated higher levels of individual job performance and commanded lower starting salaries than externally
hired managers. At the unit-level, operations led by internal hires demonstrated higher performance on
organization-specific criteria (i.e., service performance), whereas no internal– external differences were
found on more general criteria (i.e., financial performance). They also found some evidence that
differences in unit service performance decreased over time (but did not diminish completely) as external
hires improved at a slightly faster rate than internal hires. Overall, these findings underscore the
complexity of the recurring “build or buy” decision. The results also suggest that internal hires generally
outperform external hires, both individually and collectively, and they do so for less money.

The present study was conducted to offer new insights regarding
the very practical, yet underresearched, topic of internal versus
external staffing decisions. Using longitudinal data collected from
a quick-service retail organization, we examine internal and external hires in unit manager roles. This study contributes to the
limited existing literature on internal and external selection in
several ways. First, we integrate theory and research on human
capital (e.g., Nyberg et al., 2014; Ployhart et al., 2014), internal
labor markets (e.g., Bidwell, 2011), and organizational staffing
(e.g., Schneider, Goldstein, & Smith, 1995) to explain possible
differences between internal and external candidates hired into
managerial positions. For example, we propose that internally
selected managers may outperform externally selected managers
because internal hires possess higher levels of organizationspecific skills and greater knowledge of the organization’s values
and priorities.
Second, previous research has focused on internal versus external selection of line employees working in the financial sector
(Bidwell, 2011; Chan, 2006). The present study provides some
initial data and insights regarding the internal– external decision
with respect to the selection of unit managers. Our focus on
managers is important because managers are among the most
strategically valuable employees to an organization, and their
choices and behavior can have significant consequences for unit
and organizational effectiveness. For example, managers often are
responsible for implementing human resource practices (e.g., selection, training and development, performance management) that
can affect the organization’s human capital resources (Sikora,
Ferris, & Van Iddekinge, 2015). Further, managers shape a unit’s
climate, which is critical for effective performance in service
organizations (Schneider, Ehrhart, Mayer, Saltz, & Niles-Jolly,
2005). In addition, managers also are accountable for unit-level
outcomes, such as customer satisfaction and profitability. As such,

Theory and Hypotheses
A holistic theory that explains similarities and differences between internal and external selection, across levels and over time,
is lacking. Consequently, we blend theory and concepts on human
capital, labor markets, and organizational staffing to contribute to
the development of such theory and hypotheses. To begin, human
capital theory (Becker, 1964) has been used to understand employee differences in wages, mobility, and more recently, performance (Bidwell, 2011). According to this theory, human capital
primarily consists of firm-specific and general skills. Firm-specific
skills are relevant only to the particular organization where they
are developed and cannot be easily transferred if individuals
change organizations. In contrast, general skills represent proficiencies that are relevant to various jobs and organizations and
thus are transferable from one organization to another (Coff &
Kryscynski, 2011). As organizations will not benefit as readily
from firm-specific skills gained at other organizations, only general skills are transferrable in labor markets (Barney, 1991).
Ployhart et al. (2014) suggested that human capital theory does
not provide a strong basis for understanding human capital as a
resource because it emphasizes human capital from the perspective
of individuals while ignoring the perspective of units and organizations. Further, the authors argued that variations in the content
and level of human capital make it difficult to use human capital
theory to understand the effects human capital can have for an
organization. To address these concerns, Ployhart et al. proposed a
focus on human capital resources (HCR), which reflect “capacities
based on individual KSAOs [knowledge, skills, abilities, and other
characteristics] that are accessible for unit-relevant purposes” (p.
381). They emphasized that HCR may exist individually, as well
as collectively at the unit or organizational level.
Extending this prior work, we separate HCR into two main
types. Organization-specific human capital resources include
knowledge (e.g., knowledge of one’s specific job; Tesluk & Jacobs, 1998) and skills (e.g., skills related to organization-specific
routines and processes) that are tied to an organization.
Organization-specific HCR are related to social capital, such as
relationships with coworkers and knowledge of subordinates’
strengths and weaknesses. Finally, we suggest that these resources
include an understanding of the organization’s values and priorities. As with firm-specific skills and knowledge, organizationspecific HCR acquired in one organization have little value to

The distinction between organization-specific versus general
human capital resources has been used to explain performance
differences among employees (e.g., Becker, 1964; Blair, 1999;
Crook, Todd, Combs, Woehr, & Ketchen, 2011). This distinction
also may help explain and predict differences between internal and
external hires (Althauser, 1989; Bidwell, 2011; Lepak & Snell,
1999). Because of their time in the organization, internal hires
possess some level of organization-specific HCR (Lepak & Snell,
2002). In contrast, a common characteristic of all external hires is
that they possess no previous experience within the hiring organization. As a result, they initially lack organization-specific HCR.
Thus, the decision to select internal versus external candidates will
influence the amount and distribution of organization-specific
HCR within an organization. In the sections that follow, we
develop hypotheses that explain how differences between internal
and external hires may manifest in valued outcomes at individual
and unit levels.

Internal Versus External Hires and Manager-Level
Outcomes
Manager job performance. One of the most important aspects of firm-specific skills (and by extension, organizationspecific HCR) is that they allow employees to perform their jobs
more efficiently (Chan, 1996; Ployhart & Moliterno, 2011). Internal hires, who are thought to possess higher levels of these resources, should perform better than external hires for three reasons. First, internal hires possess knowledge and skills uniquely
related to their job and organization. Further, specific knowledge
and skills are the most proximal predictors of job performance and
mediate the effects of general human capital (e.g., cognitive ability) on performance (Campbell, McCloy, Oppler, & Sager, 1993;
Hunter, 1983; Ployhart & Moliterno, 2011). Second, internal hires
likely possess greater social capital from established relationships
with other organizational members that external hires would not
yet possess. These network resources may enable internal hires to
initially outperform external hires, particularly in highly interdependent work environments, such as those found in quick-service
organizations. Third, because of their superior organizationspecific knowledge and skills and social capital, internal hires
should be able to lead their units effectively than external hires,
such as by establishing a clear direction and shared norms (Purdue,
2001; Taggar & Ellis, 2007).
However, organization-specific HCR encompass more than just
knowledge, skills, and social capital; managers who possess a
higher level of such resources also may know more about the
criteria their organizations value the most. We refer to the unique
standards for determining the quality of performance as
organization-specific criteria. Such criteria reflect priorities of
organizations regarding job-related outputs, as well as the standards used to evaluate those outputs. For instance, although two
organizations may emphasize the same outcome (e.g., customer
service), each organization may seek somewhat different behaviors
with respect to that outcome. Further, success and failure may be

defined differently based on the unique standards of each organization.
The existence of organization-specific criteria may contribute to
performance differences between internal and external hires. For
example, evaluations that rely primarily on subjective indicators
(e.g., performance evaluations, customer ratings) may reflect the
unique values of a specific organization more than objective indicators (e.g., sales, profits). Because of this, internally hired managers, who possess higher organization-specific HCR, will better
understand what their organizations (and more specifically, their
supervisors who serve as representatives of the organization) value
in the performance appraisal process (e.g., Hoffman & Woehr,
2006). Further, theory and research on the ability to identify
criteria (Jansen et al., 2013; Oostrom, Melchers, Ingold, & Kleinmann, 2016) suggest that individuals who correctly perceive performance criteria when participating in an evaluative situation
(e.g., a selection interview) use that knowledge to guide their
behavior and, in turn, tend to outperform those who are less
perceptive about the performance criteria. In a similar way, knowledge of organization-specific criteria may allow internal hires to
better focus their efforts on criteria that have the largest impact on
desired outcomes. In contrast, externally hired managers, who
initially possess little or no knowledge of organization-specific
criteria, may be at a disadvantage when it comes this form of
criteria.
In sum, fundamental differences exist between hires that may
influence the performance appraisal process. Internal hires possess
an advantage over external hires because internal hires possess
greater firm-specific knowledge and skills, greater social capital,
and a better understanding of the firm’s norms and culture. These
forms of organization-specific HCR should translate into a better
understanding of the outcomes the organization desires and how to
affect those outcomes. Thus, we expect that internal hires will be
more capable of performing in a manner that is valued by their
organization.
Hypothesis 1: Internally selected managers will receive higher
job performance ratings than externally selected managers.
Salary. The organization-specific HCR found in internal hires
(and not in external hires) also has implications for salary. The
acquisition and retention of human capital occurs through a labor
market (Barney, 1986; Campbell, Coff, & Kryscynski, 2012). In
these markets, organization-specific HCR have no value outside of
the organizations where they exist, and thus organizations do not
compete with one another to retain these resources. As a result, it
often costs more to acquire new employees than it does to promote
existing employees (Campbell et al., 2012). This is particularly
true when labor markets are tight (i.e., when there is a limited
supply of external candidates) and organizations face strong competition from other organizations for external applicants.
Two prior studies found that internal hires tend to receive lower
starting salaries than external hires (Bidwell, 2011; Chan, 2006),
and there are several reasons why we expect to observe a similar
trend in the selection of unit managers. First, because organizationspecific HCR have no value to other organizations, managers who
possess a high level of this resource (e.g., internal hires) may
choose to remain with their organization to maximize its worth
(Coff & Kryscynski, 2011). Thus, organizations can pay less for

internal hires because their organization-specific resources are less

external candidates, internal candidates tend to possess more information to assess the “fit” between themselves and the job and
organization to which they are applying. Applicants’ fit perceptions tend to be based on the match between (a) what they value

KSAOs and the demands of the job (Kristof-Brown, Zimmerman,
& Johnson, 2005). Because applicants do not tend to seek organizations where a poor fit exists (Schneider et al., 1995), internal
hires may be willing to provide a “discount” to their current
employer if they perceive a strong fit between themselves and the
organization. This allows organizations to offer internal candidates
lower starting salaries than those offered to external candidates.
Finally, all job seekers face mobility costs regarding job search,
relocating, and so forth. For example, employees who are highly
embedded in an organization (e.g., those with high levels of
organization-specific HCR) often incur notable financial and social costs by moving to a different organization (Lee, Burch, &
Mitchell, 2014). When seeking employment in another organization, internal employees must consider not only if the benefits of
working for a new employer are significantly greater than remaining with their current organization, but also if those benefits justify
the potential mobility costs (Wright, McMahan, & McWilliams,
1994). Furthermore, as most of the internal candidates’ value is
likely due to their organization-specific HCR, they may not be able
to offset mobility costs by commanding higher salaries (Campbell
et al., 2012). As a result, internal hires may remain with their
current organization to avoid the risks and mobility costs of
changing organizations (Lee et al., 2014).
In summary, internal candidates (1) possess organizationspecific HCR that hold little value to other organizations, (2) may
be willing to forgo higher pay for better person-job/organization
fit, and (3) seek to avoid mobility costs. For these reasons, we
expect the starting salaries of internal hires will be lower than the
starting salaries of external hires.
Hypothesis 2: Internally selected managers will have lower
starting salaries than externally selected managers.
Subsequent promotions. We also examine whether internal
or external hires advance more quickly within the organization
after their initial promotion or selection into a managerial role. As
discussed, internal hires are thought to perform better due to higher
levels of organization-specific HCR. However, promotions may be
granted for reasons other than high performance (Drory & VigodaGadot, 2010; Gandz & Murray, 1980). For example, promotions
may be used to recognize or motivate employees. Because
organization-specific resources hold little value outside the organization in which they were acquired, internal hires possess less
leverage for gaining rewards (Becker, 1964).
As with starting salaries, internal and external hires’ tolerance
for mobility costs may affect their likelihood of getting promoted.
External hires either (a) possess a willingness to sacrifice HCR tied
to their current organization by accepting a position with another
organization (e.g., job-to-job transfers) or (b) possess no
organization-specific HCR to lose (e.g., new job entrants). Thus,
organizations may grant external hires promotions in title, salary,
or both as a way to retain those employees. By contrast, organi-

to receive a subsequent promotion than externally selected

Internal Versus External Hires and Unit-Level
Outcomes
Although HCR exist within individual employees, we suggest
that managers’ human capital also has unit-level consequences.
Indeed, managers occupy a central role whereby their human
capital and behavior shape the unit’s structure, organization, and
climate (Hausknecht & Holwerda, 2013). Managers also influence
the operational nature of their unit, including its staffing, retention,
and performance management (Sikora et al., 2015). Viewed from
a different perspective, the loss of a manager from a unit (i.e.,
manager turnover) tends to have stronger and more negative unitlevel consequences than the loss of the manager’s direct reports
(Hale, Ployhart, & Shepherd, 2016; Hausknecht & Holwerda,
2013). Managers are particularly important in retail environments
because they set the service climate and standards of service
delivery (Hong, Liao, Hu, & Jiang, 2013; Liao & Chuang, 2007;
Schneider et al., 2005). Thus, the consequences of service manager
HCR exist at both the individual and unit levels.
We suggest that the effects of HCR on unit outcomes may
depend on whether the criteria are organization-specific or more
general. As discussed, internal hires tend to possess organizationspecific HCR, including an understanding of the criteria their
organization values and how to perform relative to those criteria.
In the retail service industry, organizations often distinguish themselves based on their customer-oriented philosophies, climates,
and values (Dubé & Maute, 1998; Sin & Tse, 2000). That is, how
one organization defines quality service may be different than
another organization’s definition. In turn, the service climate a
manager develops becomes specific to their unit, its employees,
and its customers. As a result, organization-specific criteria based
upon these values and definitions also may vary between organizations. In contrast, external hires do not possess any organizationspecific resources when they join an organization. External hires
thus lack both knowledge of organization-specific criteria and how
to impact those criteria. Therefore, internal hires may be better
positioned to influence the types of service-related outcomes their
organization values.
However, both internal and external hires should possess knowledge of general criteria. For example, financial outcomes such as
sales and profits are common across organizations and may be
influenced by similar behaviors regardless of the organization.
Thus, internal hires’ greater organization-specific HCR may not
provide them an advantage over external hires when the criteria are
more general and familiar to both types of hires.
In sum, internal hires possess organization-specific HCR, and
they know the criteria their organization values and how those
criteria are measured and evaluated. These factors should enable

Persistence of Differences Between Internal and
External Hires
Time within an organization is needed for workers to acquire
organization-specific HCR, such as by becoming familiar with
organizational routines and processes (Becker, 1964; Tang, 2012;
Tsui, Pearce, Porter, & Tripoli, 1997), establishing relationships
with coworkers, and being exposed to different roles and functions
(Gibbons & Waldman, 2004; Ortega, 2001). Thus, when internal
hires assume their new role, they likely possess a significant
amount of organization-specific resources. In contrast, external
hires do not possess any organization-specific resources when they
join an organization.
However, organization-specific HCR are acquired over time as
employees gain opportunities to learn. For instance, knowledge
and skills related to a particular organization (or positions and
tasks unique to that organization) are learned over time through
formal and information mechanisms (Choi & Jacobs, 2011; Noe,
Tews, & Marand, 2013). Also, it takes time for managers to learn
about their subordinates’ capabilities and identify which employees can fill certain needs or provide advantages for the unit (Burt,
1997). In addition, as managers remain within an organization,
they can begin to identify their superior employees and better use
those employees to improve the unit (Adler & Kwon, 2002).
Finally, socialization over time allows managers to learn, share, or
create norms and values within their unit. This allows external
hires to foster greater trust between themselves and their employees, who, in turn, may be more likely to accept and commit to
decisions managers make (Putman, 1993).
The learning curve to acquire organization-specific HCR should
be similar for both internal and external hires. Internal hires are
farther along this curve, and thus may experience a decrease in the
rate at which they can gain additional organization-specific resources. In contrast, external hires are not as far on the curve and
thus have more opportunities to gain additional resources. Therefore, with time, external hires should possess similar levels of
organization-specific HCR as internal hires. For these reasons, we
expect that time in the position or organization will have a greater
impact on external hires than on internal hires.
Hypothesis 5: Initial unit service performance differences
between internally and externally selected managers will decrease over time, such that the performance of external managers will increase at a faster rate than the performance of
internal managers.

Method
Organization and Sample
We obtained data from a quick-service retail organization based
in the United States (the Florida State University Human Subjects
Committee approved our use of these data for this article, HSC No.
2017.22838). The organization operates several different lines of
business, and we had data on unit managers within three of the
organization’s businesses. The mobility barriers into and out of
manager positions within this organization are sufficiently permeable to allow a constant flow of internal and external candidates,
which provided an excellent opportunity to study both types of
hires simultaneously. Data were available for 3,697 managers:
2,112 internal hires who were promoted from a lower-level position within the organization (e.g., crew member) and 1,585 external hires. The sample comprised 50.6% males and 49.4% females,
and the average age was 32.01 years (SD ⫽ 8.99). Regarding
race/ethnicity, 51.5% of managers were White, 22.1% were Hispanic, and 17.5% were Black. The remaining managers were
Asian, Native American, or of multiethnic backgrounds.

Measures
Manager variables. We created a dummy-coded variable
called internal– external status to reflect whether managers were
internal (0) or external hires (1). Internal hires represent managers
who were promoted from a nonmanagement position within the
organization. In contrast, external hires represent managers who
were completely new to the organization and came from another
organization, were previously unemployed, or were entering the
full-time workforce for the first time (e.g., recent high school
graduates). Manager job performance was measured using annual
evaluations that managers’ supervisors (e.g., regional coordinators) provided for administrative purposes. Supervisors used a
Likert-type scale with anchors from 1 (lowest performance) to 5
(highest performance) to evaluate managers on several factors,
including judgment, communication skills, problem solving skills,
and effective use of unit employees. Performance ratings were
available for 2,134 managers.
Starting salary represented new managers’ annual salary. Salary
data were available for all 3,697 managers. To maintain pay
practice confidentiality, we performed a linear transformation on
the salaries of each manager.1 Thus, starting salary does not
represent the actual dollar value of a manager’s salary, but rather
relative pay compared to other managers. A dummy-coded variable called subsequent promotion was used to note whether managers received a promotion (e.g., to regional manager) subsequent
to their promotion (internal hires) or selection (external hires) into
their first manager position (0 ⫽ not promoted, 1 ⫽ promoted).
Promotion data were available for all 3,697 managers.
Unit variables. Unit data were collected on a monthly basis
over a 3-year period, so there were 36 measures of each variable.

financial performance when testing Hypotheses 4 and 5 by including units’ performance scores from the prior month.3

Analyses
We tested Hypotheses 1–3, which focus on manager-level outcomes, using ordinary least squares (OLS) and logistic regression.
We tested Hypotheses 4 and 5, which focus on unit-level outcomes
over time, using random coefficient modeling with restricted maximum likelihood estimation (Bliese, Chan, & Ployhart, 2007;
Raudenbush & Bryk, 2002). We examined one model with unit
service performance as the dependent variable and one model with
unit financial performance as the dependent variable. Level 1
represented time (1 to 36 months) and Level 2 comprised variables
related to the manager or unit, as well as the control variables.
We followed the approach presented by Bliese and Ployhart
(2002) to identify the most appropriate way to model each unitlevel analysis. One factor we considered was how to model time at
Level 1. We examined both linear and quadratic growth functions.
For unit service performance, a model with both linear and quadratic growth terms as random effects displayed the best fit.
Specifically, the model with only the linear term as a random effect
had a ⫺2 restricted log likelihood statistic of 546,012 compared to
a value of 545,847 for the model with both the linear and quadratic
term as random effects (difference in log likelihood ⫽ 165, p ⬍
.01). As such, we initially allowed both growth terms to vary
across managers. However, the random effects for this model
would not compute as a result of too little variance in the quadratic

921

term. Thus, in the final model for unit service performance, we
allowed only the intercept and linear growth terms to vary but kept
the quadratic growth term fixed (i.e., the quadratic term did not
vary among managers). A similar result was found for unit financial performance, such that a model with both linear and quadratic
growth terms as random effects displayed the best fit. Specifically,
the model with only the linear term as a random effect had a ⫺2
restricted log likelihood statistic of 93,398 compared to a value of
93,379 for the model with both the linear and quadratic term as
random effects (difference in log likelihood ⫽ 19, p ⬍ .01). Thus,
we allowed both growth terms to vary across managers.
In sum, the final model for unit service performance included an
intercept, a linear growth term as both fixed and random effects,
and a quadratic growth term as a fixed effect. The final model for
unit financial performance included an intercept, a linear growth
term, and a quadratic growth term as random effects. In addition,
we considered the most appropriate error structure for the models
and found that an autoregressive structure fit best in both cases.

Discussion
The present study addressed an important and recurring decision
that virtually all organizations face: whether to promote from
within or to hire from outside. Drawing upon and integrating
theory on human capital, labor markets, and organizational staffing, we developed and tested hypotheses regarding how and when
performance differences may exist between internally and externally hired managers and their units.
The results revealed several new and important insights. First,
previous research provided initial evidence to suggest that internally selected employees perform better than externally selected
employees, yet make less money (Bidwell, 2011; Chan, 2006). Our
study reveals that although internal hires demonstrate slightly
higher individual job performance, external hires have higher
salaries. Furthermore, the present results suggest that internally
selected managers tend to receive fewer promotions than externally selected managers. This finding is consistent with the idea
that organizations do not have to use promotions to retain internal
hires because these managers may be less willing to enter the labor
market and lose the value of their nontransferrable organizationspecific HCR. In contrast, organizations may need to use promotions to retain external hires, who already have shown a willingness to forgo organization-specific HCR acquired from other

organizations, as well as absorb possible mobility costs that can
result from changing organizations.
Second, the present study provides some of the first data regarding the impact of internal versus external selection on outcomes at the unit level. Specifically, we find that units managed by
internally selected managers demonstrate higher customer service
performance. A key insight of our study is that internal hires not
only perform their individual jobs better, but they also appear to
manage their units more effectively. However, the types of
organization-specific HCR that matter at the unit level may be
different than those relevant to the individual level. For example,
managers can use their knowledge of subordinates’ capabilities to
raise the effectiveness of their units by matching the requirements
of certain tasks to the capabilities of their unit members. Managers’ understanding of the employee relationships within their unit,
such as which employees work or do not work well together,
represents another type of organization-specific HCR that can
influence unit outcomes. By creating cohesive and efficient work
groups, managers with these forms of organization-specific HCR
(i.e., internal hires) can maximize the effectiveness of their employees in a way that managers lacking this HCR (i.e., external
hires) cannot. Thus, organization-specific HCR are valuable to
both managers and their units.
Third, Bidwell (2011) found that individual-level performance
differences between internal and external hires tend to decrease
over time. The present study suggests that this pattern generalizes
to certain unit-level outcomes. Specifically, the service performance of units managed by external hires improves faster over
time than the service performance of internal hires’ units. This
finding is consistent with the idea that external hires experience a
faster learning curve regarding organization-specific HCR than
internal hires. Thus, time in the organization enables external hires
to perform more like internal hires. However, in our data set, units
managed by internal hires still outperform external hires’ units
after 3 years. To determine when external hires would be expected
to achieve the same level of performance as internal hires, we
assumed the same level of difference between external and internal
hires and estimated future performance beyond the 3-year timeframe. Results suggest it would take about 6 years for external
hires to achieve the same level of service performance as internal
hires. These findings shed new light on the return on investment

Managerial Implications
The present study provides decision-makers results they can use
to compare the pros and cons of filling positions by promoting
from within or hiring from outside the organization. The results
suggest that organizations should fill manager position with internal candidates whenever possible. Simply put, internally hired
managers are likely to perform better, stay longer, and cost less
than externally hired managers. Internal hires may be particularly
superior to external hires when (a) the most valued criteria are
organization-specific (rather than general) and (b) when new hires
need to influence those criteria soon after they assume the job and
cannot wait to develop the requisite organization-specific HCR.
Furthermore, internal selection may create positive spillover effects by motivating other employees who see that the organization
rewards hard work with promotions (Bidwell & Keller, 2014).
However, situations exist in which it may be neutral or preferable to select external candidates over internal candidates. For
example, if the performance criteria are more general and common
across organizations, the choice of internal versus external may not
be as important. In which case, factors such as the lack of viable
or diverse internal candidates may lead organizations to focus on
external candidates. In addition, there may be times when organizations seek to expand their workforce and need to pull from
external labor markets. Finally, we noted how filling a position
with an internal candidate may create a new opening that then may
have to be filled. This can result in shifting organization-specific
HCR from one position or unit to another, possibly creating a
deficit of specific resources where one did not previously exist.

Limitations and Directions for Future Research
Future research should attempt to address limitations of our
study, as well as build upon our findings. First, although the
internal– external differences we observed generally were consistent with theory and arguments related to HCR, we did not directly
measure the resources internal and external hires possessed. Future
research should explore ways to measure these resources to more
closely examine the role they play in differences between these
two types of hires. Future research also could explore the specific
HCR differences that may exist between internal and external
hires. For instance, we suggested that internal hires may have
greater network resources compared to external hires. Another
possible resource difference includes knowledge of organizational
systems and processes that enables internal hires to accomplish
their work more efficiently. Organizational characteristics also
may affect the nature and importance of organization-specific
HCR. For example, internal hires in a large organization may
possess knowledge of the organization as a whole, but possess
little or no knowledge pertinent to their new unit.
Future research also could explore whether internal and external
managers adopt different leadership styles or behaviors. For example, internally promoted managers may use their knowledge of
coworkers they now supervise to direct and motivate them. In
addition, internal managers may better understand the attributes
necessary to succeed in the organization, and thus may be better
able to identify and develop talent within their unit. On the other
hand, external managers may focus more on change-oriented leadership behaviors. For example, external managers may be better
able to identify areas where improvement is needed. Their lack of
existing relationships with other organizational members also may
enable external hires to act and not be as concerned about how
their actions might affect their relations with others.
Second, previous research on internal versus external staffing
has focused on nonmanagement employees working in the finan-

925

cial sector. The present findings suggest that internal– external
differences generalize to management-level employees in the
quick-service industry. Nonetheless, our findings are based on data
from a single organization, and thus the inferences we made might
not generalize to other organizations, industries, and so forth. For
example, because so much of the “human element” is controlled
and routinized in quick-service organizations (Reich, McCleary,
Tepanon, & Weaver, 2006), the effects we observed may be
smaller than what might be observed in more knowledge-intensive
jobs and organizations (e.g., consulting). In addition, internal–
external differences may be more pronounced or impactful in
organizations in which having a unique identity is valued (e.g.,
companies seeking to pursue a “niche” or differentiation strategy)
and less impactful in organizations that pursue a broader strategy
(e.g., cost leadership strategy; Porter, 1985; Snow & Snell, 1993).
Third, we did not have data on external hires’ prior organization
or job. Future research should examine whether the degree of
organization/job similarity influences internal– external hire differences. For example, theory and research on prior experience and
transfer of learning (e.g., Dokko, Wilk, & Rothbard, 2009; Salomon & Perkins, 1989) would predict that external hires who move
from an organization with one set of norms, values, and expectations to an organization that differs substantially on such factors
would be at a greater disadvantage (relative to internal hires) than
external hires who move to an organization that is similar to their
previous organization. There even may be instances when internal
hires experience a greater change than external hires, such as when
internal hires move to a job or unit that is quite different from their
previous job/unit.
Fourth, although we were able to examine whether and how
internal– external differences with regard to unit performance
change over time, we did not have data to examine such differences with regard to individual manager performance ratings. This
prevented us from seeing whether internal– external differences in
performance ratings reflected a similar or different trend as what
we observed for the unit-level outcomes. In addition, we examined
whether and how internal versus external hires differed with respect to pay, performance, and promotions. Future research could
try to address how internal versus external selection may affect
other aspects of the organization and its employees. For example,
research suggests that promoting from within can influence outcomes such as job satisfaction and organizational commitment
(Mathieu & Zajac, 1990; Porter, Steers, Mowday, & Boulian,
1974). In addition, it often is assumed that relying on internal
selection will limit organizational innovation. However, we are not
aware of any studies that have directly tested this premise. Future
studies also could explore whether the proportion of internal and
external hires within an organization matters. For example, do
organizations with a higher percentage of one type of employee
perform better than other organizations? Is there an optimal mix of
internal and external hires?
A new insight from the present study was that internal– external
differences may depend on whether the criteria are general or
organization-specific. Future research should replicate and extend
this finding. For example, researchers might attempt to quantify
the extent to which particular criteria are unique or similar across
organizations. It also would be informative to try to measure
internal and external hires’ knowledge and perceptions of the
performance criteria within their organization to see if these fac-

The present results suggest that it may take quite some time for

feedback internal applicants received regarding their initial selection test scores.

Conclusion
The present study examined a staffing issue that has received
surprisingly little scholarly attention despite its considerable importance to practitioners and organizations. The overall results
suggest that organizations should strongly consider filling managerial positions by promoting from within. Indeed, internal hires
generally outperform external hires and cost less in terms of salary
and other incentives (e.g., promotions). Yet, there are situations in
which selecting internal candidates or external candidates may be
less consequential, such as when the most valued performance
criteria are general rather than organization-specific. We hope
these results provide useful information to organizations faced
with internal– external staffing decisions. We also hope this study
will provide a springboard for future research on this important but
largely neglected topic.
