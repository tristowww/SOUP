## Applicant Attraction to Organizations and Job Choice:  A Meta-Analytic Review of the Correlates of Recruiting Outcomes {#slide-4}

Chapman et al., 2005

## Chapman et al., 2005: Background {#slide-5}

A meta-analytic review of the wealth of extant literature regarding applicant attraction, job choice and reactions to selection processes. 

Researchers began by creating an operational definition of "recruiting" based off extant definitions to delineate their review. 

Predictor and Outcome variables were identified for the recruitment process; some examples for the former include perceived fit and recruiter characteristics, for the latter, job choice and acceptance intentions.

Three potential moderators were identified: race, gender and applicant type (actual or simulated). 

## Chapman et al., 2005: Methods {#slide-6}

298 recruiting-related studies were reviewed; 71 contained data relevant to the variables identified in this review. Sources for these studies included psych/management/education databases, recruiting reviews, recent conferences and directly contacting SMEs.

Data extracted from studies and coded to ensure label-content agreement for variables and scales.

Data was subjected to a number of different analyses, meant to determine things like homogeneity of effect sizes between studies, detecting the significance of moderator variables and so on. 

## Chapman et al., 2005: Outcomes {#slide-7}

Perceived fit being one of the strongest predictor variables in terms of Applicant Attraction Outcomes (unsurprisingly).

The behaviors and knowledge of recruiters is more valuable for positive applicant attraction outcomes than their demographics; certain factors such as cognitive ability, personality, attractiveness ought to be explored in this regard.

Gender differences: Perceptions of fairness more attractive to men, women care more about job characteristics than men.

Weighted least squares regressions of the predictor-outcome relationships found little to no evidence supporting moderator effects for gender (besides the aforementioned two) or race (in any). 

Real applicants were significantly more invested in variables like job and organizational characteristics as well as justice perceptions compared to simulated laboratory applicants.

## Build or Buy? The Individual and Unit-Level Performance of Internally Versus Externally Selected Managers Over Time {#slide-8}

DeOrtentiis  et al., 2018

## DeOrtentiis  et al., 2018: Background {#slide-9}

Despite the ubiquity of organizations having to decide whether it is more advantageous to hire internally vs. externally, very little research has been done regarding what specific advantages either method offers.

Researchers directed the focus of their study towards managers due to their strategic value to organizations and their consequentiality for organizational and unit effectiveness.

DeOrtentiis  et al. proposed that internal hires would outperform external hires regarding criteria that are more organization-specific, conceptualized as  organization-specific human capital resources (OSHCR)  versus  general human capital resources (GHCR)  which are representative of KSAOs.

Certain outcomes of interest for researchers were manager-level job performance, salary, subsequent promotions and unit-level performance differences between the two types.

## DeOrtentiis  et al., 2018: Methods {#slide-10}

Longitudinal data was obtained from a quick-service retail organization based in the US; the sample consisted of 3,697 managers (2,112 internal hires vs. 1,585 external hires). This title's mobility barriers were described as highly permeable and candidates of both types flowed in and out of the position with great regularity.

Manager job performance was measured via their annual performance evaluation, their salary was their salary data.

Unit service performance was measured via customer ratings and unit financial performance was measured via sales per labour hour.

Manager-level data was subject to ordinary least squares and logistic regression, whereas unit-level data was subject to random coefficient modelling.

## DeOrtentiis  et al., 2018: Outcomes {#slide-11}

Internally selected managers have higher individual job performance despite being paid less than externally selected managers. Internally selected managers also tend to be promoted less.

Internally selected managers manage their units more effectively; their units displayed higher service performance than "externally" managed units.

Internally selected manager's higher level of OSHCR allows them to manage their units more effectively and perform more effectively; time allows external managers to catch up but  DeOrtentiis  et al. speculate that it would take about 6 years for this gap to close based on their data.

## Understanding Attitudes Toward Affirmative Action Programs in Employment: Summary and Meta-Analysis of 35 Years of Research {#slide-13}

Harrison et al., 2006

## Harrison et al., 2006: Background {#slide-14}

Affirmative action programs (AAP) are a rather divisive subject in the US and a sizeable body of literature regarding the matter has emerged in the past 35 years; Harrison et al. sought to summarize and meta-analyze it.

AAP Prescriptiveness is described in this study as the extent of consideration given to applicants based off of their demographic characteristics alone, which can range from pre-selection support to being highly consequential.

Harrison et al. propose many hypotheses regarding perceiver characteristics and their influence on one's attitude towards AAPs.

Examples include: AAP attitudes being positively affected by the extent of personal employment discrimination experienced by a perceiver and AAP attitudes being negatively affected by racism and AAP attitudes being more negative among individuals who identify with the Republican party and so on.

## Harrison et al., 2006: Methods {#slide-15}

Literature was collected from a variety of sources, including psychological databases, contacted SMEs, professional newsgroups and discussion lists; ultimately, 110 studies were reviewed.

Data was extracted from the various studies and research and coded to ensure scale reliability and construct validity.

Effect sizes were cumulated from all relevant, reviewed studies; data was subject to other analyses in order to ensure independence of samples/effect sizes and assess likelihood of moderator effects.

## Harrison et al., 2006: Outcomes {#slide-16}

Personal experiences with discrimination interestingly, seems to have no impact on one's attitudes towards AAPs, positive or negative.

Highly prescriptive AAPs are significantly associated with personal self-interest but not collective self-interest.

Rather than increasing along the spectrum of prescriptiveness, beliefs about target group discrimination remained positive throughout its entirety (even at the lower end of prescriptiveness).
