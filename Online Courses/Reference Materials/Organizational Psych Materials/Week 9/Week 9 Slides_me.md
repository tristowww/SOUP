## Stressors & Strains {#slide-4}

![9
 ](ppt/media/image3.jpeg "Picture 2")

- Stressors (environmental demands)  -- external pressures or demands (ex: role ambiguity, organizational change, workload)
- Acute stress effects  -- when demands cause an immediate reaction. This can be emotional (ex: frustration, anxiety), physical (ex: elevated heart rate), or cognitive (ex: difficulty concentrating)
- Strains (illness)  -- if acute stress is not managed it can lead to more significant problems (strains). These can be physical (ex: heart disease) or mental health issues (ex: depression or anxiety)
- Intervening Variables  -- factors that can buffer or worsen the stress process. These can be individual psychological attributes, coping mechanisms, resilience, social support, personality traits.

## Models of Work Stress {#slide-16}

- Demands-Control Model  ( Karasek , 1979)

<!-- -->

- Most work stress comes from when employees face heavy demands but are given little control over their work
- High demands and low control lead to higher stress levels, whereas high demands and high control can lead to lower stress.

16

## Models of Work Stress {#slide-17}

- Conservation of Resources Theory  ( Hobfoll , 2001)

<!-- -->

- Stress is driven by the threat of resource loss, actual loss, or insufficient resource gain. People strive to acquire, protect, or conserve resources.
- Resources are valued:  They can be personal, social, or material (e.g., money, status).
- Resource loss  is more salient than resource gain, and stress occurs when resources are depleted.
- Coping  involves strategies to maintain or recover resources.

17

## Models of Work Stress {#slide-18}

- Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) Model  (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007)

<!-- -->

- Expands on Demands-Control by saying both job demands and job resources should be considered.
- High job demands (e.g., workload, time pressure) lead to stress unless employees have adequate resources (e.g., autonomy, support).
- Resources can be external or internal

## Grant et al., 2007 {#slide-25}

- Managerial practices create tradeoffs btw different dimensions of employee well-being (happiness, health, and relationships)
- Managerial interventions can lead to improvements in one dimensions of well-being  (e.g., job satisfaction or mental health) while potentially causing declines in other areas (e.g., physical health or work-life balance).

<!-- -->

- High-performance work and work-life balance programs can have positive and negative effects on well-being
- Managers should be aware of these tradeoffs when making decisions 

<!-- -->

- Supportive work environment for employee well-being

25

## Grant et al., 2007 {#slide-26}

- Well-being has multiple dimensions

<!-- -->

- Job satisfaction may increase with high work autonomy or challenging tasks, but these benefits can lead to  burnout  or  physical strain  if not managed properly.
- Work redesign  and incentive  compensation systems  often target psychological well-being by enhancing engagement and satisfaction but may also encourage overwork or increased stress, impacting physical well-being.
- Practices that focus heavily on  team-building  can foster  social well-being  but may reduce individual autonomy, which is critical for psychological well-being.
