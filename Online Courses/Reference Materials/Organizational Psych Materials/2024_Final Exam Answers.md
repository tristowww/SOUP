1.  Work attitudes, such as job satisfaction and organizational justice, are central to understanding employee behavior and organizational outcomes. Reflect on how these attitudes are shaped and discuss their potential impact on key outcomes like absenteeism, turnover, and job performance. Based on your understanding, do you think improving job satisfaction or perceptions of organizational justice has a greater influence on overall organizational success?

> **Work attitudes and organizational outcomes:**

- **Definition and Shaping of Work Attitudes:**

  - Work attitudes are evaluations of job-related elements (e.g., job satisfaction) or broader organizational factors (e.g., organizational justice).

  - **Factors shaping attitudes:**

    - **Individual factors:** Personality traits, values, and expectations.

    - **Job characteristics:** Autonomy, variety, and workload (Hackman & Oldham, 1980).

    - **Organizational factors:** Fair policies, supportive management, and cultural alignment.

- **Impact on Outcomes:**

  - **Job Satisfaction:**

    - Correlates with job performance through models (direct, indirect, reciprocal) (Judge et al., 2001).

    - Predicts lower absenteeism and turnover, improving organizational efficiency.

  - **Organizational Justice:**

    - Linked to trust, organizational commitment, and reduced workplace conflicts (Cropanzano et al., 2007).

    - Fairness perceptions directly impact employee engagement and team cohesion.

- **Which Has a Greater Impact:**

  - **Improving job satisfaction:**

    - Directly enhances performance metrics like productivity and retention.

    - Individual-focused interventions are often easier to implement.

  - **Improving organizational justice:**

    - Sustains long-term team trust and collaboration.

    - Addresses broader structural issues, impacting collective performance.

2.  Organizational culture and climate are often used interchangeably, yet they represent distinct concepts with unique implications for employee behavior and organizational outcomes. Reflect on the differences between organizational culture and climate and their respective roles in shaping organizational effectiveness. Using one theoretical model of culture (e.g., Schein's Three Levels of Culture, Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions) and one model of climate (e.g., Schneider's Organizational Climate Theory, Litwin & Stringer's Model), explain how these frameworks can be applied to diagnose and improve organizational performance.

> **Organizational culture vs. climate:**

- **Differences Between Culture and Climate:**

  - **Culture:** Deeply rooted, shared assumptions, and norms that guide organizational behavior (qualitative focus). Example: Schein's iceberg model of visible and invisible cultural elements.

  - **Climate:** Employees' collective perceptions of organizational practices and procedures (quantitative focus). Example: Schneider's safety or service climates.

- **Theoretical Models:**

  - **Culture: Schein's Three Levels of Culture**

    - **Basic assumptions:** Unconscious beliefs about \"how things are done.\"

    - **Espoused values:** Formal statements (e.g., mission statements).

    - **Artifacts:** Visible structures, language, rituals.

  - **Climate: Schneider's Organizational Climate Theory**

    - Focuses on shared perceptions (e.g., safety climate improves employee adherence to safety practices, reducing accidents).

- **Application for Improvement:**

  - **Culture:** Align leadership behavior with stated values, use artifacts like rituals to reinforce norms.

  - **Climate:** Address gaps in employee perceptions through open communication, training, and reinforcement of key practices.

3.  An employee, Alex, works in a high-pressure customer service role where they frequently face demanding clients and tight deadlines. Recently, Alex has started showing signs of emotional exhaustion and disengagement, including occasional outbursts at colleagues and a lack of willingness to assist with tasks outside their formal duties.

Using Affective Events Theory, analyze how workplace events might have triggered Alex\'s emotional reactions. Discuss how these emotions could contribute to both Counterproductive Work Behaviors (CWBs) and a decline in Organizational Citizenship Behaviors (OCBs). Propose a strategy for Alex's manager to improve their emotional well-being and reduce the occurrence of CWBs, drawing from stress management frameworks like recovery strategies by Sonnentag & Fritz.

**Alex and Affective Events Theory:**

- **Affective Events Theory:**

  - Emotional reactions stem from workplace events (e.g., client demands causing frustration).

  - These emotions influence attitudes (e.g., job satisfaction) and behaviors (e.g., OCBs and CWBs).

- **OCBs and CWBs:**

  - **OCBs:** Voluntary behaviors (e.g., assisting colleagues) decline as negative emotions increase.

  - **CWBs:** Stress and frustration may trigger counterproductive acts like withdrawal or conflict.

- **Managerial Strategies:**

  - **Stress Management:**

    - Recovery activities (Sonnentag & Fritz, 2007) like relaxation, physical detachment, and mastery experiences.

    - Encourage positive emotional reflection (Bono et al., 2013).

  - **Well-being Interventions:**

    - Create clearer expectations and improve workload distribution.

    - Foster a supportive team environment to rebuild positive emotions and engagement.

4.  Sarah is a project manager in a fast-paced tech company. Recently, she has been experiencing high levels of stress due to increasing workload (job demands), tight deadlines, and conflicting responsibilities. Sarah reports feeling burnt out and has started making mistakes in her work. Despite these challenges, she enjoys the autonomy she has in deciding how to structure her tasks.

Using one model of work stress (e.g., Demands-Control Model, Job Demands-Resources Model, or Conservation of Resources Theory), analyze Sarah's situation. Discuss how her job demands and resources contribute to her stress and burnout. Propose both individual coping strategies and organizational interventions that could alleviate her stress and promote her overall well-being.

**Sarah and work stress models:**

- **Model of Stress:**

  - **Demands-Control Model:**

    - High job demands with low control heighten stress. Sarah's autonomy partially buffers this.

  - **Job Demands-Resources Model:**

    - Workload (demand) is stressful, but autonomy (resource) provides a moderating effect.

- **Strategies for Coping:**

  - **Individual Coping:**

    - Problem-focused approaches (e.g., prioritizing tasks, setting boundaries).

    - Use of recovery strategies like mindfulness or physical activity.

  - **Organizational Interventions:**

    - Introduce flexible deadlines and distribute responsibilities to reduce overload.

    - Encourage supportive management to reinforce Sarah's autonomy positively.

5.  Teams operate at multiple levels of analysis, from individual behaviors to group dynamics and organizational context. Discuss how individual-level factors (e.g., motivation, role clarity), team-level factors (e.g., communication, conflict management, shared mental models), and organizational-level factors (e.g., leadership, culture) influence team performance.

Using relevant theories and models (e.g., Tuckman's Stage Model, McGrath's Model of Team Effectiveness), explain how these levels interact and contribute to either positive or negative team outcomes.

**Teams and levels of analysis:**

- **Individual-Level Factors:**

  - Motivation: Clear role definitions and rewards improve engagement.

  - Role clarity: Reduces ambiguity, preventing conflicts or delays.

  - Personality: Agreeableness and conscientiousness often enhance team harmony.

- **Team-Level Factors:**

  - Communication: Regular and open discussions improve coordination.

  - Shared mental models: Team alignment in goals and strategies boosts performance.

  - Conflict management: Task conflict can stimulate innovation, but relationship conflict should be minimized.

- **Organizational-Level Factors:**

  - Leadership: Leaders who balance directive and participative styles enhance team cohesion.

  - Culture: Inclusive cultures foster trust, reducing in-group/out-group tensions.

- **Theoretical Integration:**

  - **Tuckman's Model:**

    - Teams in \"Storming\" need conflict management and clear role definitions.

  - **McGrath's Model:**

    - Effective teams integrate inputs (skills, resources), processes (coordination, strategy), and outputs (goals achieved).

- **Conclusion:**

  - Holistic approaches to team improvement require attention to individual behaviors, team dynamics, and organizational support structures.

  - Example: Improving communication (team-level) coupled with leadership training (organizational-level) enhances outcomes.
