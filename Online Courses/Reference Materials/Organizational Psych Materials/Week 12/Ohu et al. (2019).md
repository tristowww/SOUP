Work–family conflict affects employee performance and well-being. However, despite the underlying
focus of work–family research on family health and well-being, we have limited knowledge about the
impact of role-based stressors, such as work–family conflict, on child health. In this study, we propose
and test the stressor-self-regulatory resources-crossover framework. In the spirit of extension of existing
work–family research to other cultural settings, we report on two multisource studies conducted in
Nigeria to explain whether, how, why, and when parental work–family conflict relates to child health. In
Study 1, we collected multisource data from parent– child pairs in low-income families to test whether
parental self-regulatory resources explain why work- family conflict relates to child health, resulting in
findings that support the stressor-self-regulatory resources-crossover framework. In order to identify
possible targets for future organizational-based interventions, we collected Study 2 data from parents and
their children (who were enrolled at private schools) to test whether job autonomy and job demands
altered the relationship between parental self-regulatory resources and child health. Moderator analyses
of the multisource data reveal that self-regulatory resources matter for child health only when job
demands are high or when job autonomy is low, pointing to potential intervention and policy levers.
Keywords: work–family conflict, crossover, job stress, child health

Study 1
Work–Family Conflict, Outcomes of Work–Family
Conflict, and the Crossover Gap
According to Greenhaus and Beutell (1985), work–family conflict pertains to employees experiencing work and family role
demands that conflict with one another. Conceptually, the interactions between the work and family domains can be negative,
resulting in interference or conflict, or positive, resulting in facilitation. Concepts such as work-to-family conflict and family towork conflict further specify the origin of demands and the outcome sphere affected by the competing role demands. In this
paper, we focus on work-to-family interference, which, for our
current purposes, we define and treat as a stressor that is based on
work role demands interfering with family role demands. Due to
the likelihood that time-based and strain-based role conflict both
work to influence self-regulatory resources, we concurrently discuss time-based and strain-based work-to-family conflict and
henceforth refer to this conflict, including both time-based and
strain-based conflict, as WFC.
When work–family interference affects the employees’ life satisfaction and health, these nonwork domain negative reactions that
result from WFC are labeled negative spillover (Bakker et al., 2009).
In contrast, when we examine how the experience of work stressors
and resultant strain are transmitted to others, we refer to the process as
crossover (Westman, 2015). The first is an intra-person transfer, the
second interperson. Crossover processes are often studied as they
affect couples—a partner’s job stressors and strain, such as burnout,
transfer to the employees’ spouse and affect behavior of the spouses
toward each other (e.g., through social undermining behaviors; Westman, Etzion, & Danon, 2001). The theoretical explanations for how
and why crossover occurs often rely on transfer of affect and emotional contagion processes such as mimicry (Bakker et al., 2009).
However, the strain from job stressors, such as WFC, also crosses
over to family members because it affects patterns of interactions, and
results in social undermining, which in turn leads to negative affect
and family member emotional exhaustion (Bakker, Demerouti, &
Dollard, 2008).
Despite much theoretical and empirical progress in crossover
research since Jackson and Maslach’s (1982) seminal demonstration of how burnout relates to displays of anger at home, our
understanding of how WFC relates to child outcomes and particularly child health remains limited, and results where they exist are
often conflicting: The majority of studies report negative child
health and well-being outcomes when parents experience work–
family interference, while a smaller number of studies report positive
child health outcomes (Cho & Ciancetta, 2016). Parenting behaviors (Sallinen, Kinnunen, & Rönkä, 2004), and lack of parental
involvement in recreational activities (Lau, 2010) and family
meals (Eisenberg, Olson, Neumark-Sztainer, Story, & Bearinger,
2004), are among factors that translate parental work–family interface issues into child health outcomes. Still, in order to design

The Stressor-Self-Regulatory Resources-Crossover
Theory
We propose the stressor-self-regulatory resources-crossover model
(Figure 1) to contribute to the understanding of the psychological
processes that explain the relationship between WFC and child health
outcomes. Self-regulation has been defined as effortful cognitive
processes that require control and flexible, goal-directed adjustments
of thought, behavior, and affect (Bridgett, Burt, Edwards, & DeaterDeckard, 2015). Adequate self-regulation plays a key role in the
execution of tasks that require sustained attention and effort (Bandura,
1991; Bridgett et al., 2015; Mischel, Cantor, & Feldman, 1996).
Parental WFC, a job-related stressor, can lead to a depletion of
self-regulatory resources, which can in return cross over and affect
child health.
Christian and Ellis (2011) describe self-regulation as an executive function of the person and as energy that controls thoughts and
behavior. Self-regulatory resources manifest themselves in higher
levels of state self-control, particularly when related to the regulation of behavior (Christian & Ellis, 2011). In other words, selfregulatory resources play a key role in motivation, and particularly in
behaviors that require sustained effort (Baumeister, Vohs, & Tice,
2007). Conceptually and definition-wise, there is significant overlap
between the conceptualizations of self-regulatory resources at the
intersection of self-control and perceptions of loss of self-control due
to limitations in expendable energy or depletion of energy. The
definition of self-regulatory resources that we apply here overlaps
with that of Christian and Ellis (2011) and Mawritz, Greenbaum,
Butts, and Graham (2017), going back to Schmeichel and Baumeister’s definition: Self-regulatory resources constitute “the internal resources available to inhibit, override, or alter responses that may arise
as a result of physiological processes, habit, learning, or the press of
the situation” (Schmeichel & Baumeister, 2004, p. 86). Mawritz et al.
(2017) define self-regulation as “a process of using self-regulatory
resources to control undesirable impulses and override subsequent
behavioral responses” (p. 1485). Note that we view fatigue as distinct
from self-regulatory resources—fatigue can be both physical and

psychological and contains a broader spectrum of psychological and
behavioral manifestations than self-regulatory resources (which are
more pertinent to the extent to which individuals can control themselves to engage in behaviors that require energy depletion).
Self-regulatory resources can vary across individuals as a trait
and within individuals across time as a state (Bridgett et al., 2015;
Finkel & Campbell, 2001; Gailliot, Schmeichel, & Baumeister,
2006; Muraven & Baumeister, 2000). As a trait, individuals differ
in their levels of self-regulatory resources, which can be used to
self-regulate all aspects in life. As a state, self-regulatory resources
are limited and consumable resources, which may be depleted by
tasks requiring attention and effort (Bridgett et al., 2015; Christian
& Ellis, 2011; Gailliot et al., 2006). In our studies, we examine
state self-regulatory resources, which can be influenced by work–
family conflict. Given their substantial trait component, the selfregulatory resources as a state may maintain a certain level until
the impulse (e.g., work–family conflict) disappears or changes.
Thus, consistent with recent conceptualizations of self-regulation
(e.g., Mawritz et al., 2017), we construe self-regulatory resources
as a state variable, with impact of antecedents of self-regulation
impairment accumulating over time through stressor exposure.
The parenting of small children and teenagers requires substantial self-regulatory resources. For parent involvement in schoolwork, in discussions about educational and personal goals, in
participating in joint activities, or for exercise, expenditure of
parental self-regulatory resources is required. The availabilities of
self-regulatory resources are affected by WFC, which demands
devotion of self-regulatory resources to the work domain when
competing family demands arise. When self-regulatory resources
are limited, the execution of tasks that require self-regulatory
resources is affected negatively. A lack of self-regulatory resources is
also linked to increased counterproductive work behaviors (Christian
& Ellis, 2011) and decreased behaviors that require self-control and
effort, such as maintaining an exercise regimen (Nägel, Sonnentag, &
Kühnel, 2015).

through a host of well-documented mechanisms. In their recent
meta-analysis, Bridgett et al. (2015) support an intergenerational
transmission of self-regulation. Specifically, Bridgett et al., show
that parents’ child-rearing practices are affected by their own self-

Other parenting behaviors directly related to child health include
setting rules of behavior, monitoring activities, helping children build
and sustain healthy life habits (such as participation in athletics), and
making demands according to the developmental needs of the children. These active parenting behaviors are in direct competition with
parental behaviors that require fewer self-regulatory resources, such
as letting children watch TV or use electronic devices. These parenting behaviors that positively affect child health can, among other
means, be achieved through an expenditure of parental self-regulation
and involvement. Basically, we argue that when parents complete
work and transfer to the family domain, they still possess enough
regulatory resources in order to face the demands of executing positive and active parenting behaviors that positively affect child health.
They would thus be more involved in their children’s lives and more
likely to provide access to stimulating and enriching activities than
parents who lack self-regulatory resources after completing work.
We thus propose that parental self-regulatory resources explain,
at least partially, why WFC relates to child health outcomes.

Study 1 Method
Participants
Participants consisted of students and parents from three public
and government-owned schools in Lagos, Nigeria. In Nigeria’s
urban areas, government schools are predominantly attended by
children of low-income families as their tuition and fees are
usually lower than those at private schools. Public schools in
Nigeria are often affected by nonpayment of teacher salaries and
constrained access to resources. In selecting public schools for the
current study, we partnered with the Lagos State government, who
advised us on three schools that would provide for a representative
cross-section of public schools in the greater Lagos urban area.
The majority of parents (71.3%) only had a primary or secondary
school education, and 37.8% of the families had more than 3
children under the age of 18 living in their household. Average
incomes of individual parents who participated were $58 yearly in
the lowest 25th percentile and $294 yearly in the 75th percentile.
Sample job titles for the responding parents included “trader,”
“hair dresser,” and “cleaner/maid”.
The context of our study, the city state of Lagos, is culturally
complex. Urbanization, modernity, and globalization may have
caused changes in kinship behavior, but the extended family structure remains strong in Nigeria, and as Mikell (1997) points out,
people retain their ideologies as well as their social connections
despite these migrations (cited in Aryee, 2005, p. 267). On aver-

age, a Nigerian mother gives birth to 5 children and a typical
household comprises 4.9 individuals in urban and 5.9 individuals
in rural areas, with more than 32% of households consisting of
more than 6 members. Child mortality rates are among the highest
in the world, with nearly 10% death rates among newborns. Rural
dwellers with extended family relations in urban areas like Lagos
often relocate to the city in search of a better life for themselves or
their children. Nigeria does not have an extensive social security
support system for older individuals. In spite of this, nursing
homes are almost nonexistent because of the widely held African
belief that older people, particularly parents, deserve lifelong veneration and support. It is thus common for aging parents to move
in to live with their married adult children, particularly daughters.
This “parenting their parents” or “intergenerational parenting” (Aryee,
2005, p. 267) imposes an extra burden on employed parents. For an
in-depth review of Nigeria and its historical context and family
structures, see Falola and Heaton (2008).

Methods and Procedures
Parent data were collected via structured interviews. Student
data were collected using paper surveys completed during class
time. As adult literacy rates in Nigeria are below 60% (UNESCO,
2012), we initially conducted pilot testing of survey tools with
parents who matched the demographics of the target population.
Parents were, for the most part, unable to complete survey tools on
their own due to literacy levels. Thus, we created a structured
interview guide based on the survey questions, which was administered by trained research assistants. For this guide, research
assistants read each item aloud and asked parents to respond to the
questions with a 1–5 numerical rating corresponding to their
agreement with each statement (1 ⫽ strongly disagree, 5 ⫽
strongly agree).
Altogether, we obtained data from 289 parent– child pairs. The
majority of child participants were male students (63.2%), and the
majority of parent surveys were completed by the mothers (66.8%).
The children were on average 15.22 years old (SD ⫽ 2.22). Response
rates could not be calculated as parents were invited to participate in
the study at a school town hall meeting, and interested parents came
in to complete the structured interviews on a number of days that were
posted by school officials. Parents were instructed to answer the
questions about themselves, with only one parent responding per
family. Parents who completed the structured interviews received an
umbrella in exchange for participation. Due to missing values, the
final sample size used for analyses was 144.
We ran missing value analyses and found out that the large
number of missing values was largely due to parents only responding to a small subset of the requested demographic variables. For
instance, missing value analysis revealed that out of 289 pairs, 56
parents did not report the number of children under age 18 at home
and 39 parents did not report their own gender. Because the data
were collected on campus where research assistants read the survey to the parents, a large number of them were reluctant to report
personal information to strangers (i.e., research assistants). We
also conducted independent sample t tests to compare the data that
were used in the analysis and those that were not. Test results
showed that there were no significant differences between the two
sets of data.

Measures—Parent Survey
Work–family conflict. Parental WFC was measured with 4
items developed by Wayne, Musisca, and Fleeson (2004). Sample
items include “Your job reduces the effort you can give to activities at home” and “Your job makes you feel too tired to do the
things that need attention at home.” Parents were asked to indicate
the extent that they agreed or disagreed with each statement by
selecting from 1, Strongly Disagree, to 5, Strongly Agree. Items
were scored such that high scores indicate high levels of conflict.
The Cronbach’s alpha is .76.
Self-regulatory resources. Parental self-regulatory resources
were consistent with recent research on self-regulation, operationalized as parental self-control. Self-control is recognized as a
behavioral marker for self-regulatory resources: Low self-control
is an indicator of self-regulatory depletion (Christian & Ellis,
2011). Therefore, self-regulatory resources were assessed using 5
items intended to assess self-control (Christian & Ellis, 2011).
Items include “If I were given a difficult task right now, I would
give up easily,” “My mind feels unfocused right now,” “I would
want to quit any difficult task I was given,” “I feel like my
willpower is gone,” and “Right now I would find it difficult to plan
ahead.” Parents were asked to indicate the extent that they agree or
disagree with the statement by selecting from 1, Strongly Disagree, to 5, Strongly Agree. We reverse coded all the items so that
high scores indicate high levels of self-regulatory resources. The
Cronbach’s alpha is .77.

Measures—Child Survey
Child health. For the purposes of this study, we operationalize child health as the child’s own subjective perception of their
general health. Child overall health was assessed with three items:
“In general, how is your overall health?” “Compared to others your
age, how is your overall health?” and “Compared to one year ago,
your overall health is. . . .” These items were adapted from general
health items from DeSalvo, Fisher et al. (2006). Researchers
commonly utilize child health self-report items similar in nature to
the items adapted for the current study, and the items tend to show
adequate reproducibility, concurrent and discriminant validity, and
high correlations with physical functioning and emotional health
measures (DeSalvo, Bloser, Reynolds, He, & Muntner, 2006;
DeSalvo, Fan, McDonell, & Fihn, 2005; DeSalvo, Bloser et al.,
2006). Children were asked to indicate their condition by selecting
from 1 to 5 (1 ⫽ Poor, 2 ⫽ Fair, 3 ⫽ Good, 4 ⫽ Very Good, 5 ⫽
Excellent). Cronbach’s alpha for the scale was .60.
Controls. We considered several potentially relevant control
variables including parent gender, child gender, and the number of
children under age 18. Previous research suggests a relationship
between child gender and child health (Gabhainn et al., 2008). For
example, the Health Behavior in School-aged Children collaborative
cross-national study found that girls are more likely than boys to
report lower overall health. The number of children influences demands for health care for children (Child Trends, 2015). As the
number of children increase, especially the children under age 18, the
demands for health care increase. In addition, research has shown the
different roles of fathers and mothers in influencing children’s health
(Lamb & Tamis-Lemonda, 1996; Teitler, 2001). We thus integrated
these control variables in our models to parse out the variance between these controls and our predictor variables.

Study 1 Discussion
Overall, Study 1 lends support to our central hypothesis. Parental WFC relates indirectly to child health through self-regulatory
resources (i.e., self-control): Parental WFC relates to decreased
self-regulatory resources, and these lower self-regulatory resources,
in turn, relate to lower child health. Theoretically, our results support
the stressor-self-regulatory resources-crossover model (Figure 1) in
that self-regulatory resources appear to partially explain how parental
WFC relates to child health outcomes.
Study 1 demonstrates that parental WFC does not only function
as a role-based stressor for employees but extends prior research
on crossover by providing preliminary support for the stressorself-regulatory resources-crossover framework using WFC as a
specific role stressor and focusing on child health as an outcome.
These findings point to limited self-regulatory resources as one
explanatory mechanism and possible point for intervention.
Moving forward, it will be necessary to identify modifiable
factors or moderators that can impact the strength of this
relationship between parental self-regulatory resources and
child health. Once these have been established, there may be
room for policymakers, human resource professionals, and employees to alleviate the negative relationship between stressors, such as
WFC, and child health by targeting these factors that affect the
strength of this relationship. Furthermore, results supporting the relationship between parental work stressors, such as WFC, and child
health need to be replicated across different family SES levels and
international regions.

Study 2 Method
Data and Sample
We established a partnership with a private school system in
Lagos, Nigeria that consists of an all-boys and an all-girls secondary school (equivalent to U.S. middle and high school combined).
Participants consisted of students and parents from these two
private schools. Pilot testing of survey tools with parents who
matched the demographics of this target population found that
parents’ literacy levels were high enough to easily complete survey
tools on their own. Thus, we collected parent survey data using
online surveys, and collected student data using a web-based
survey at the boys’ school and paper surveys at the girls’ school
(where pilot testing revealed that Internet connections were insufficient to collect web-based data). Unlike the sample in Study 1,
the participants in this sample have higher income and education,
representing middle- to upper-class families in Lagos, Nigeria.

Altogether, we obtained 189 parent– child survey pairs. The
majority of participants were male students (79.4%), and the
majority of parent surveys were completed by fathers (60.8%).
The students were on average 14.67 years of age (SD ⫽ 2.03).
Parents (not including the income from the spouse) made on
average $39,666 per year ($13,137 per year constituted the 25th
percentile, and $62,784 per year the 75th percentile), with sample
job titles of “Executive Director,” “Manager,” and “Senior Planning Officer.” All parents reported having university or postgraduate degrees; 54.6% of the families reported having more than 3
children under age 18 living in their household.
Missing value analysis revealed that even though parents reported their gender, a large part of the rest of the survey was often
not filled out, with 82 parents missing responses on self-regulatory
resources, 96 parents missing responses for WFC, 97 parents
missing responses for autonomy, and 98 parents missing responses
for job demands. Due to missing values, the final sample size used
for analyses was based on samples of 76 parent– child pairs.
The main reason for missing values for Study 2 pertains to parent
attrition toward the end of the survey tool, which may, in part, be
because many people in our sample did not have prior experience
participating in research or completing surveys. Independent sample t
tests showed that only one variable was significantly different (the
parents whose data was usable reported a higher level of selfregulatory resources than the parents whose data was not usable due
to missing values).

Measures
We used the same scales as we used in Study 1 to assess
parent-rated WFC (␣ ⫽ .83), parent-rated self-regulatory resources
(␣ ⫽ .84), and child-rated self-perceptions of their general health
(␣ ⫽ .80).
Job autonomy. Parent-rated job autonomy was assessed with
3 items from Ng, Ang, and Chan (2008). Parents indicated the
frequency of the occurrence with their current employer of each
statement by choosing from 1 to 5 (1 ⫽ never, 2 ⫽ rarely, 3 ⫽
sometimes, 4 ⫽ often, 5 ⫽ very often). A sample item is “Determining how work exceptions are to be handled.” High scores
indicated high levels of job autonomy. (␣ ⫽ .92).
Job demands. Parent-rated job demands were assessed with 7
items from Karasek (1979). Parents indicated the frequency of the
occurrence with their current employer of each statement by
choosing from 1 to 5 (1 ⫽ never, 2 ⫽ rarely, 3 ⫽ sometimes, 4 ⫽
often, 5 ⫽ very often). A sample item is “To what extent does your
job require your working fast?” High scores indicated high levels
of job demands. (␣ ⫽ .85).
Controls. We used the same set of control variables as we
used in Study 1. They are child gender, parent gender, and the
number of children under age 18 at home.

General Discussion
Our study extends research on the crossover of work stressors to
family outcomes by proposing and testing the stressor-self-regulatory
resources-crossover framework in Study 1. Further, in Study 2, we
demonstrate that parental self-regulatory resources matter for child
health particularly in situations when job autonomy is low or when
job demands are high. Theoretically, our study contributes by extending the theoretical mechanisms proposed and tested that explain why
and how WFC can cross over to the family domain. Consistent with
research on intergenerational transmission of self-regulation (Bridgett
et al., 2015), parental self-regulatory resources partially mediated the
relationship between parental WFC and child health. Even though this
finding points to interesting levers for potential future interventions,
further examination of additional theoretically relevant pathways may
shed light on other useful mechanisms. For instance, Grzywacz and
Smith (2016) argue that specific parenting behaviors, such as weaving
mechanisms, explain why parental work stressors relate to child
health. It is plausible that parenting behaviors that rely on selfregulatory resources further explain why improved self-regulatory

Findings of Study 2 explain that parental self-regulatory resources
are particularly critical for employed parents who experience high

levels of job demands or low job autonomy. The moderating role of
job autonomy points to the importance of employee discretion and
psychological control as factors that can impact the self-regulatory
resources– child health pathway. In addition, we noticed in testing the
moderation effects, when we put both job demands and job autonomy
into the equation, job demands emerged as a stronger moderator in
influencing the self-regulatory resources– child health relationship.
This result is in line with the statement from Hobfoll (2001). Hobfoll
proposed that “resource loss is disproportionally more salient than
resource gain” (p. 343). Job demands indicate potential or actual loss
of resources; job autonomy, on the contrary, has been regarded as one
of the most important resources (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007). When
comparing the moderation effects of job demands and job autonomy,
it is plausible that job demands emerged as the stronger moderator.

Directions
Even though this paper constitutes one of the beginning steps of a
larger effort to understand how parental work stressors relate to family
and child well-being, we cautiously encourage organizations and

researchers to conduct intervention research targeting work–family
conflict and employee self-regulatory resources. Our findings, in
conjunction with research conducted in education and health settings,
suggest the workplace as a potentially promising target for intervention for human resource practitioners and policymakers interested in
addressing child health and the associated negative ramifications of
child ill health for parental absenteeism (Boekaerts & Corno, 2005;
Stadler, Oettingen, & Gollwitzer, 2009).
The current paper has implications for policy and management on
the African continent, a geographical region frequently ignored by
mainstream management scholars. For instance, policy interventions
geared toward reducing parental work–family conflict and enhancing
parental job autonomy may ultimately relate to improved child health.
Of course, further empirical work needs to be aligned with policy
interventions to determine the effectiveness of interventions geared
toward addressing these issues. We note Aryee’s (2005) assertion that
“there are no compelling reasons to expect that employed parents in
urban sub-Saharan Africa will experience work-family and familywork conflict differently from the consequences reported in the extant
western literature.” (p. 276). Although we employ WFC here as an
antecedent, considering that our main effect findings align with what
extant literature says about the WFC– child health relationship, we
would encourage studies to try to replicate our indirect and moderation effects in other contexts.
Findings of this paper also point to similar work–family conflict
child health relationships among families at opposite ends of the
economic spectrum: Basic relationships and hence potential interventions may be equally meaningful for low- and high-income families.
If this relationship holds true for the diverse socioeconomic contexts
of the two studies in this work, findings of this study lend additional
support to the universality of work–family interface experiences.
A number of limitations point to the need for further extension.
First, even though we propose a generic stressor-self-regulatory
resources-crossover framework, we focused on general WFC as a
unique parental role stressor that can relate to child health. We
recommend future research test the stressor-self-regulatory resourcescrossover framework we proposed and tested in Study 1 by independently examining time, strain, and behavior WFC as well as
focusing on stressors that are also likely candidates for a relationship with child health. For instance, based on research on social
contagion and evidence supporting social stressors at work, incivility, bullying, and social undermining may cross over and relate
to child well-being. We recommend that the mediating role of
self-regulatory resources be reevaluated in the context of these
social stressors. Consistent with research that proposes a need for
resources to match demands (De Jonge & Dormann, 2006), a
closer look at social contagion-related mediators derived from
frustration-aggression models may prove fruitful as we extend our
understanding of work stressors and strains with high crossover
potential.
Second, even though both our studies use multisource data from
parents and their children, inferences regarding the causal direction of
relationships need to be further strengthened in future studies. In our
studies, we demonstrated a relationship between WFC and child
health. Examining these constructs within a diary or longitudinal
design would allow for directionality inferences and for the reverse
directionality (child health predicting WFC) to be examined and ruled
out. Particularly, diary study methods have the potential to shed light
on the directionality of relationships that we propose in the stressor-

599

self-regulatory resources-crossover framework in Study 1. If events
where work interferes with family on one day predict lower displays
of self-regulatory resources among working parents, and less active
involvement with children, then stronger inferences regarding the role
of work–family conflict as an antecedent of child health could be
drawn.
Third, we examine the influence of parental self-regulatory and
job-related influences on child health; however, although we look at
both child and parent perspectives in the current study, we focused
solely on the work experiences of the responding parent. Our results
provide initial evidence that parental work experiences are related to
child outcomes. Future research examining the interactive influence
of both parents’ work experiences and self-regulatory resources is an
interesting next step for this area of research. For example, the
influence of one parent’s work stressors and associated depleted
self-regulatory resources may be diminished if the other spouse has
high self-regulatory resources and is actively involved in the parenting
process.
Fourth, even though the majority of the work–family interface
literature treats job autonomy as a precursor of work–family conflict,
we conceptualize job autonomy as a work-related resource that can
buffer how work–family conflict and limited self-regulatory
resources relate to outcomes. Our treatment of job autonomy as a
factor that can reduce the strength of the proposed relationships is
consistent with some recent job stress research (Liu et al., 2015;
Mauno et al., 2006); the majority of job stress research points to
job autonomy as a precursor of work–family conflict rather than as
a buffer or moderator. We argue that the moderator perspective
makes sense given Knecht, Wiese, and Freund’s (2016) work
showing that work–family conflict is surprisingly temporally stable, leaving limited room for predictors influencing relationships
over time. Still, our conceptualization is not consistent with how
job autonomy is most commonly conceptualized in the literature.
The current study highlighted relationships between parental work–
family conflict and relevant child outcomes. Even though we cautiously advise employers and human resource professionals to take
our study as one supporting autonomy-oriented interventions, we did
not explicitly test an intervention in our study. Hence, we recommend
that future research on the work–family interface focus on designing
short, low-cost interventions targeting employee autonomy.
We suspected parental self-regulatory resources might influence
their parenting behaviors (e.g., setting rules of behavior, monitoring
activities, and helping children build and sustain healthy life habits).
Subsequently, we anticipated that these parenting behaviors would in
return be linked to child health. However, given the preliminary
nature of our study as one that explores links between parental work
factors and child health, we did not assess any parenting variables and
thus could not draw any specific conclusions from our studies. We
recommend future research to examine other explanatory parental
variables in these relationships. Looking at the role of additional
parental factors—such as parental involvement, time spent with children, family rules, and spousal self-regulatory resources—in child
health will help build on the current findings to help better inform
policy and evidence-based interventions.
Our studies are unique in that we examine the work–family
interface in a densely populated region in sub-Saharan West Africa, where only very limited research on the work–family interface has been conducted (Aryee, 2005). Through a partnership
with government entities and the public and private school sys-
