>
> This seminal review establishes the three-dimensional model of burnout (emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment) that serves as the theoretical foundation for most burnout research. The authors emphasize that burnout results from chronic workplace stressors and poor person-environment fit. For student populations, this framework suggests that academic environments with high demands and low resources create conditions conducive to burnout development.
>
> **2. Shin, H., Puig, A., Lee, J., Lee, J. H., & Lee, S. M. (2011). Cultural validation of the Maslach Burnout Inventory for Korean students. *Asia Pacific Education Review, 12*(4), 633-639.**
>
> This study validates the Maslach Burnout Inventory-Student Survey in a Korean context and examines cultural factors affecting student burnout. The research demonstrates that burnout manifests similarly across cultures but with varying intensity based on academic cultural norms. The study supports the use of established burnout measures in student populations while highlighting the need to consider cultural context in burnout research.
>
> **3. Salanova, M., Schaufeli, W., Martínez, I., & Bresó, E. (2010). How obstacles and facilitators predict academic performance: The mediating role of study engagement and burnout. *Anxiety, Stress, & Coping, 23*(1), 53-70.**
>
> This longitudinal study examines the relationship between academic obstacles/facilitators, engagement, burnout, and performance in university students. The research demonstrates that social support acts as a facilitator that promotes engagement and reduces burnout, while excessive workload serves as an obstacle. The study provides empirical support for the buffering effect of social resources on student burnout.
>
> **4. Mokgele, K. R., & Rothmann, S. (2014). A structural model of student well-being. *South African Journal of Psychology, 44*(4), 514-527.**
>
> This study develops and tests a comprehensive model of student well-being that includes burnout, engagement, and social support variables. The research demonstrates that social support significantly predicts lower burnout levels and higher engagement among university students. The structural equation modeling approach provides methodological guidance for examining complex relationships between social support and burnout outcomes.
>
> **5. Jacobs, S. R., & Dodd, D. (2003). Student burnout as a function of personality, social support, and workload. *Journal of College Student Development, 44*(3), 291-303.**
>
> This cross-sectional study examines how personality factors, social support, and academic workload interact to predict student burnout. The research finds that social support moderates the relationship between workload and burnout, with higher support buffering against burnout effects. The study provides evidence for the protective role of social support in high-stress academic environments.

- **Theory Building Implications:**

- The widespread adoption of AI tools in research could fundamentally alter theory development in organizational psychology. On the positive side, AI could democratize access to comprehensive literature reviews and help researchers identify novel theoretical connections across disciplines. This aligns with Ashforth\'s emphasis on breadth in theory building and could facilitate the \"knowledge weaving\" process described by Simsek et al. However, there are concerning implications related to the issues raised by Ferris et al. about theory proliferation. If AI makes it easier to generate numerous theoretical propositions, we might exacerbate the problem of having too many untested theories. The risk exists that AI-assisted research could become more about recombining existing ideas rather than developing genuinely novel theoretical insights.

- **Ethical Considerations:**

- Researchers using AI tools have several ethical obligations. First, complete transparency about AI assistance is essential - the research community needs to understand how AI influenced question development, literature review, and theory construction. Second, researchers must maintain responsibility for verifying all AI-generated content, as AI tools can produce plausible-sounding but inaccurate information. Third, we must consider whether AI assistance affects the originality and authenticity of intellectual contributions. While AI can enhance human capabilities, the core theoretical insights and creative connections should remain fundamentally human contributions. As AI capabilities expand, the field will need to develop clear guidelines about appropriate use and disclosure requirements to maintain scientific integrity while harnessing these powerful tools for advancing knowledge.
