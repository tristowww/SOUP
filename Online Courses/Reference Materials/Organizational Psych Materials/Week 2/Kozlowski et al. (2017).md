To launch this Special Issue of the Journal of Applied Psychology celebrating the 1st century of the journal
we conducted a review encompassing the background of the founding of the journal; a quantitative assessment
of its evolution across the century; and an examination of trends examining article type, article length,
authorship patterns, supplemental materials, and research support. The journal was founded in March of 1917
with hopeful optimism about the potential of psychology being applied to practical problems could enhance
human happiness, well-being, and effectiveness. Our quantitative content assessment using both keyword
frequencies and latent semantic analyses of raw content, in both bottom-up (corpus driven) and top-down
modes (analyst driven), converged to document an evolution ranging from a broad and exploratory applied
psychology to a more focused industrial psychology to an industrial and organizational psychology to an
organizational psychology. With respect to other trends, during the first 4 decades 20 to 30% of journal items
were book reviews, which then abruptly ceased in the mid-1950s. Articles have grown increasingly longer
over time. Author teams are increasingly larger, and sole authored articles are vanishingly small in frequency.
The use of supplemental materials and articles reporting research support have surged dramatically in the most
recent period. Across the various foci we examined, our review portrays the evolution of the journal as
reflecting the development of a mature, focused, and cumulative scientific discipline addressing psychological
science applied to work and organizations.

The Founding of the Journal
Background
Psychology emerged from philosophy as a scientific discipline
in the last quarter of the 19th century. Marking that emergence in
the United States of America (U.S.A.), the American Psychological Association (APA) was established in 1892 (Evans, Sexton, &
Cadwallader, 1992). As reviewed by Koppes and Vinchur (2012)
and Landy (1997), in the quarter century following the formation
of APA, a core group of psychologists emerged who developed an
interest in enhancing both the scientific rigor and practical utility
of psychology. They wished to enhance the relevance and popularity of the young discipline by focusing its theory and methods
on important societal issues and problems with the goals of improving effectiveness and well-being. Emerging from this group
were the founders of the Journal of Applied Psychology, namely
G. Stanley Hall, John Wallace Baird, and Ludwig Reinhold (L. R.)
Geissler.

Founders
At the time the Journal of Applied Psychology was founded, all
three founders taught at Clark University. Clark, located in
Worcester, Massachusetts, was founded in 1887. Clark was one of
the earliest research universities founded in the U.S.A. in the
modern era, boasting the second oldest graduate school, and was at
its inception an all-graduate institution. We provide brief biographical information for each of the founders, based on Wikipedia as
well as additional sources cited below.
G. Stanley Hall was born in 1846 in Ashfield, MA. In 1878, Hall
earned the first psychology doctorate awarded in the U.S.A. from
Harvard University in Cambridge, MA. Hall was strongly influenced by William James, with whom he studied while at Harvard,
and Wilhelm Wundt, with whom he studied briefly at Wundt’s
Leipzig laboratory in 1879. James is generally recognized as the
father of American psychology and Wundt is generally recognized
as the father of psychology (Evans et al., 1992), so Hall’s pedigree
is exemplary. He was at Johns Hopkins from 1882 to 1888, where
in 1883 he established the first formal American psychological
laboratory. Hall founded the American Journal of Psychology in
1887, was a cofounder and the first President of APA (1892), and
was also named the first president of Clark University (1889). He

remained in this post until 1920. While at Clark, Hall was intellectually active in establishing the field of educational psychology
and related laboratories. Darley (1968) noted that Hall (as indicated by Hall’s autobiography), although interested in the applications of psychology, was far more occupied with the American
Journal of Psychology and the then emerging field of educational
psychology, than his contributions to establishing the Journal of
Applied Psychology.
John Wallace Baird was born in 1869 in Motherwell, Ontario,
Canada. He was educated at the University of Toronto as an
undergraduate and for some graduate work. There he was introduced to psychology by August Kirschmann, who had assisted
Wundt in Leipzig before moving to Toronto. Like Hall, Baird
spent several months at the University of Leipzig, where he was
briefly a student of Wundt’s, before studying at the University of
Wisconsin. He later transferred to Cornell University where he
earned his PhD under Edward Bradford Titchener (a student of
Wundt) in 1902. From 1906 to 1909, he worked as an Assistant
Professor at the University of Illinois, where his research interests
began to broaden from the “pure” experimental psychology that
was advocated by Titchener into areas of applied psychology. In
1909, Baird moved to direct the psychological laboratory at Clark
University, where he later also became cooperating editor of the
Journal of Educational Psychology, and executive editor of the
American Journal of Psychology. In 1917, he cofounded the Journal of Applied Psychology and, in 1918, he was elected President
of APA. He died in 1919 just as he was designated to succeed G.
Stanley Hall as President of Clark University.
Ludwig Reinhold (L. R.) Geissler was born in 1879, in Leipzig,
Germany. He studied at the University of Leiptzig and then moved
to the University of Texas to complete his undergraduate degree.
Geissler completed his PhD at Cornell University in 1909, under
the advising of Titchener. He was an instructor at Cornell from
1909 to 1911, before becoming a “Research Psychologist” for the
National Electric Lamp Association in Cleveland, OH (1911–
1912), which was a branch of General Electric. He then shifted his
career, moving to the University of Georgia as an Associate
Professor in 1912, where he continued to conduct applied psychological research. His research involved the merchants of Athens,
Georgia— home of the university—with a focus on the effectiveness of advertising. Geissler moved to Clark University in 1916,
completing the Clark link connecting the three founders. Later, in
1920, he moved to Randolph-Macon Woman’s College where he
remained until his death in 1932.
Thus, common links threading among the three founders included (a) strong influences from Wundt and from Wundt’s former
students—including conducting applied psychological research,
(b) joint time spent at Clark University under Hall’s leadership,
and (c) leadership positions in APA and key APA journals. Table

Journal Evolution and Trends
Founding Vision
The founding of the Journal of Applied Psychology was very
much predicated on the practical applications of psychology; the
translation of psychological science to business, education, medicine, justice, advertising, and the broad range of human pursuits. In
their Foreword for the inaugural issue (1917), the founders observed:
The past few years have witnessed an unprecedented interest in the
extension of the application of psychology to various fields of human
activity . . . the voluminous and growing literature of educational
psychology testifies to a widespread belief that psychology is a
valuable asset to the educator . . . one finds increasing evidence, in
various quarters, of an equally widespread but more recent conviction
that a knowledge of psychology is no less serviceable in the practice
of medicine, in the administration of justice, and in various other
pursuits. . . . But perhaps the most strikingly original endeavor to
utilize the methods and results of psychological investigation has been
in the realm of business (p. 5).

It was a broad, positive vision to do good for all of society by
translating the methods and findings of psychological science to
provide practical applications that enhanced human effectiveness
and well-being.
The first issue’s Forward (1917) stated further:
. . . the problem which is here concerned is one which must appeal to
the interest of every psychologist who besides being a “pure scientist”
also cherishes the hope that in addition to throwing light upon the
theoretical problems of his science, his findings may also contribute
their quota to the sum-total of human happiness; and it must appeal to
every human being who is interested in increasing human efficiency
and human happiness by the more direct method of decreasing the
number of cases where a square peg is condemned to a life of fruitless
endeavor to fit itself comfortably into a round hole. The problem,
therefore, is one which touches the psychologist not only as a scientist
but in his relations to his fellowmen and to the practical concerns of
life; and it is equally of interest to the various groups of nonpsychological workers,– employers of skilled labor, employees, sociologists, criminologists, moralists, legislators, administrators of justice and many others (p. 6).

Early Research Foci
Given the founding vision, what sort of topics did the new
journal address? A nonrandom sampling of titles from the first few
issues provides a good sense that the vision was reflected in
journal content. For example, Issue 1 contained: Practical relations between psychology and the war; A trial of mental and
pedagogical tests in a civil service examination for policemen and
firemen; The legibility of a telephone directory; Mentality testing
of college students; A comparative study of white and negro
children; and A fourth method of checking results in vocational
selection. Subsequent issues included: What can the psychology of
interest, motives and character contribute to vocational guidance?; Psychological tests for stenographers and typewriters; Estimates of the military value of certain character qualities; Mental
tests of unemployed men; Theories of stuttering; Fundamental
theorems in judging men; Sex differences shown by 2,544 schoolchildren on a group scale of intelligence, with special reference to
variability; Twenty-three serial tests of intelligence and their intercorrelations; A standardized group examination of intelligence
independent of language. That should convey the applied flavor of
the time. There was plethora of work on tests and group differences as revealed by testing, but also an open sense of inquiry to
a range of problems and an abiding faith in the value of an applied
psychology.

Changing Scientific Foci
All of the historical accounts that examine the development of
industrial and organizational psychology (IOP) are sensitive to the
ways in which research and practice topics in the field have been
influenced by historically significant events or sensibilities of the
era at that time (Katzell & Austin, 1992; Koppes & Vinchur, 2012;
Landy, 1992, 1997). The formation of the journal occurred at the
height of the U.S.A.s involvement in the Great War, later renamed
World War I because it was merely the first. The opening paper by
G. Stanley Hall, one of the founders, was entitled, Practical
Relations between Psychology and the War (1917). As described
previously, many of the early investigations were energized by the
new technology of mental testing, the search for individual and
group differences, and practical applications for psychological
tests. The push for employment equality in subsequent eras made
salient racial and sex differences in the 1970s. The field broadened
in the 1980s and 1990s with an increasing interest in work relationships and organizational factors. More recently, scholars have
highlighted the shift in work structure from individuals to teams,
the rise in research on teamwork, and increasing attention on the
organizational context for work attitudes and behavior. In addition,
IOP research published in this journal and elsewhere has contributed to and been influenced by development in the broader psychology discipline, such as evolving emphases on behaviorism in
the 1940s and 1950s, cognition beginning in the 1960s, and so
forth (Katzell & Austin, 1992; Koppes & Vinchur, 2012). Thus,
the nature of IOP inquiry and science evolves over time, owing to
evolving nature of work, organizations, and society, as well as
developments in the broader discipline of psychology (Kozlowski,
2012). Our goal is to provide insights around that evolution over
the last century and, consistent with the essential nature of the
journal, to do so quantitatively.

Bibliometric Analysis Approach
To facilitate a quantitative bibliometric analysis, the complete
set of PsycINFO records for the Journal of Applied Psychology
were harvested by APA and provided to us. The PsycINFO XML
database contained 9498 records for the journal; essentially, all
published material from March, 2017 through March, 2016 including a few articles that were “in press.” Thus, the full dataset
comprised just over 99 years of content from March, 1917, the first
issue of the journal, through March, 2016 —the point at which the
records were harvested.
A key goal of our bibliometric analysis was to use objective
methods (as objective as practical) to characterize the corpus of the
scientific content of the journal. Thus, rather than developing a
subjective coding scheme, we conducted a quantitative examination that focused on (a) keywords, which were coded by APA when
the articles were classified into the PsycINFO database, and (b) the
actual raw content created by authors and expressed by the words
used in the titles and abstracts of the corpus. We used two general
analytical approaches to quantitatively characterize journal content: bottom-up and top-down.
The bottom-up approach imposed no a priori constraints on the
analyses. For keyword analyses, all keyword entries were counted
and ranked by frequency of occurrence. We report the top 25 most
frequently occurring keywords per decade, from 1917 through
2016. For the raw content analyses, as we explicate in more detail
subsequently, we used unsupervised Latent Semantic Analyses
(LSA) to automatically extract primary thematic categories represented in the corpus of titles and abstracts.

reviews (see Table 2) as analytic foci. For keyword analyses,
primary clusters of keywords that captured the essence of the
review topics were each searched; we report the resulting topic

Although it is desirable to capture the essence of the journal

parsing the database into temporal segments provides a more

be used for such a segmentation. We could have tried to identify
historical epochs, although their exact period is not always clear
and the periods are not of equal length. We could have tried to
segment by journal editor, but those periods were not always
consistent across the century. Ultimately, we parsed the database
by each of the 10 decades since the founding of the journal in
1917. This provided roughly equal time periods, it made segmentation precise, and it roughly corresponded with meaningful historical epochs across the century.

Analyses and Results
Publication type
It may be a surprise to today’s readers of the journal, but a
substantial component of the journal corpus for the first four
decades was book reviews. As the young science of applied
psychology began to develop, much of that formative development
was represented in books, and the content of those books was
captured in reviews that appeared in the journal. Book reviews
comprised a substantial proportion of journal content up until their
abrupt end in 1956, which coincides with the editorship of John
Darley. To characterize the extent to which book reviews contributed to journal content, we developed a text search algorithm to
classify book reviews and other content besides articles (i.e.,
editorials, monographs, corrections, and obituaries). For the sake
of transparency, we accounted for corrections and errata. Because
they were very small in number, we combined editorials, summaries, obituaries, and extraneous entries into an “other” category for
this analysis. Monographs were not signified in the database, so
they could not be identified in this analysis. The results are shown
in Figure 1 as a bar graph. Book reviews were relatively constant
in number across the first four decades of the journal, with a
frequency hovering in the mid-200s. As the number of published
articles increased over time, the proportion of book reviews decreased somewhat. Nonetheless, they were a significant proportion
of journal content until their demise. In the first decade of the
journal (1917–1926), book reviews comprised 40% of journal
content (240 of 605 entries). From that high point, the proportion
of book reviews declined steadily across the next three decades
such that they comprised 29% (253 of 861 entries) of the second
decade (1927–1936), 23% (203 of 883 entries) of the third decade
(1937–1946), and 21% (262 of 1246 entries) of the fourth decade
(1947–1956). Then, they disappeared abruptly.
One can also observe in Figure 1 that the number of articles
published in the journal increased steadily until the fourth decade
(1947–1956) and has since then been remarkably consistent hovering around 900 to 1,000 articles per decade. One can also
observe that proportionally there are relatively few corrections and
other content. Corrections first appear in the seventh decade

Article Length
Schmitt (2017) remarked on the increasing length of articles in
his comparison of 1994 and 2013. Figure 2 reports the mean length
of articles for each decade. The data indicate that the mean length
of articles for the first three decades (1917–1946) hovered between
6 and 7 pages with a SD about equal to the mean, ranging between
5 and 6 pages. At the fourth decade (1947–1956), the mean length
dramatically dropped to 3.32 pages with a SD of 2.74. There was
also a substantially larger number of articles published in that
decade, so perhaps length was adjusted to fit publication page
limits. However, this is speculative on our part. From that point on
(beginning in the fifth decade of the journal), mean article length
has increased steadily in each decade. A one-way analysis of

variance indicated that these differences in length are statistically
significant (F(9, 9454) ⫽ 428.40, p ⬍ .001). In his reflection
article, Schmitt (2017) coded the length of article sections for 1994
and 2013, noting that the introduction/theory and discussion sections are longer in 2013 compared to 1994. Consistent with
Schmitt’s observation, one can presume that extant scholarly requirements to advance theory, build on prior research findings, and
influence the direction of future research may be influencing
overall article length likely through longer introduction and discussion sections. This is consistent with an interpretation that the
field is accumulating knowledge and building on that foundation
as it advances. This indicates a maturity relative to mid-20th
century characterizations of research in the field as “dust bowl”
empiricism (cf. Ed Fleishman’s Editorial, 1971).

Other Trends
Finally, we also examined indicators of supplemental materials
(i.e., materials published online) and sponsorship (i.e., research
support from foundations, federal grants, etc.). Supplemental materials, which primarily appear in online versions of published

articles are largely confined to the post-Internet world. The data
indicate one instance of supplemental materials in print for the
second decade, otherwise there are no such materials until the most
recent decade. For, 2007–2016, there were 56 of 1,001 instances of
supplemental materials (5.5%). These materials are largely, although not exclusively, because of a policy instituted during the
editorial term of Kozlowski. In an effort to promote more transparency with respect to meta-analyses, a policy was instituted to
publish coding schemes and other essential information to allow
meta-analyses to be replicated, critically examined, and extended.
This trend of increasing use of online supplemental materials will
likely continue for other research (beyond meta-analysis) published in this journal (e.g., manipulation materials, computational
modeling, and other software code) in an effort to enhance accuracy, transparency, and rigor.
For sponsorship, there were no articles published with research
sponsors in the first eight decades of the journal (or they were not
recorded in the database). However, the ninth decade (1997–2006)
had 22 of 951 articles (2.3%) and the tenth decade (2007–2016)
had 294 of 1001 articles (29%) with sponsorship. That is a 10-fold
increase in sponsored research compared with the prior decade,
and signals a significant discontinuity with the past. Looking
across the trends of longer articles that are more cumulative, author

Keyword Analyses
We first conducted a bottom-up analysis of keywords. To conduct this analysis, we parsed the database into decade segments,
extracted the keywords to text, and created text files for each
decade. The files were each submitted to a text analyzer (http://
www.online-utility.org/text/analyzer.jsp). The text analyzer outputs the most frequently occurring words and word combinations
(i.e., single, double, triple, etc.). We then extracted the most
frequently occurring words or sensible word combinations. Note
that a small degree of judgment was required to determine whether
to report a single term or a compound (e.g., climate or organizational climate). In most instances, the frequency was identical and
we reported the most meaningful term (i.e., organizational climate). We made every effort to minimize judgment that would
influence the results.
The findings are reported in Table 3. As shown in the table, the
first four decades suggest a fair amount of content consistency.
Much of the focus was on intelligence and other types of tests (e.g.,
personality), group differences on tested factors (e.g., age, individual differences, sex, and race), and test characteristics (e.g.,
psychometrics, test construction, test scores, and validity). We
suspect that these foci conform to most readers’ stereotype of the
early history of the journal. There are also clear indications of
content breadth beyond testing, however. For example, the keywords suggest attention to a range of educational (e.g., academic
achievement; childhood development; educational psychology;
college, high school, and school students; learning; and reading
speed), vocational (e.g., adjustment, occupational guidance, occupational interests, and strong vocational interests blank), and personnel topics (e.g., business organizations, human resource management, industrial psychology, personnel selection, and sales and
management personnel).
The next four decades suggest the beginning of a transition from
an applied psychology centered on testing in educational and
business settings to an industrial (1957–1976) and then organizational psychology (1977–1996). The focus on testing remains, but
judging by the topics, the content focus is much broader than the
prior four decades. For example, attitudes which had been in the
rankings previously, now leap to the top of the list. New topics
such as communication, consumer behavior, creativity, decision
making, group performance, human-machine, interviews, job analysis, job applicant, job characteristics, job performance, job satisfaction, judgment, leadership, legal, meta-analysis, motivation,
organizational commitment, organizational behavior, personnel
evaluation, rating scales, social perception, stress, training, turnover, and work conditions make their appearance for the first time
in the top 25 keywords. One can discern the full transition to
industrial and organizational psychology.
The two most recent decades (1997–2006 and 2007–2016)
suggest a continuing evolution toward organizational topics. There
are far fewer fundamental test- and psychometric-relevant keywords in the top 25 ranking, and many more organizational topics.
New topics include conflict, employee interaction, family, justice,
organizational climate, social behavior, supervisor-employee in-

teraction, and teams; the last topic jumping from zero to 54 and

suggests an extension of the level of analysis from the individual,
which dominated the first eight decades, to encompass work
groups, teams, and the broader organizational context, as well as
the context beyond the organization—to family.

Latent Semantic Analyses
LSA is a computational tool for extracting the primary conceptual themes embedded in a large corpus of text that represents a
meaningful domain. It is a form of automated content analysis
(Landauer, Foltz, & Laham, 1998; Smith & Humphreys, 2006). In
brief, a corpus is examined to identify a set of key lexical terms
that are frequent and co-occur. The assumption is that words close
in meaning tend to occur in the same semantic context. These
words serve as concept seeds that are used to bootstrap a thesaurus
building process. The thesaurus builder iteratively learns a set of
concepts or classifiers from the corpus. Then the text comprising
the corpus is classified to produce a concept co-occurrence matrix.
The matrix is used to create a two-dimensional concept map with
a clustering algorithm. Highly connected sematic concepts are
clustered into higher-order themes based on the dominant concept
node in the cluster (Smith & Humphreys, 2006). Connections
between and among concepts are represented by links that connect
concepts within and between thematic clusters. We used Leximancer software (Version 4) to conduct the LSA.
There are a few important points to note for interpretation of the
concept maps and thematic summaries displayed in the figures.
First, the theme clusters are heat coded (i.e., the most relevant/
important themes are red-orange and range to less relevant/important themes that are green-blue). In addition, each concept map is
annotated with the thematic summary, which highlights the relative importance of the themes. These are also heat coded to aid
interpretation. Second, the size of concept nodes represents their
importance to the theme (i.e., each theme is labeled by its largest
concept node). Thus, one can use the theme summary to gauge the
relative importance of the themes across the concept map. Third,
the concept nodes are linked in a semantic network, the lines of
which illustrate connections between and among concepts within
themes and between themes. Collectively these features— concept
theme clusters, their relative importance, and the network of linkages among concepts and themes—allow one to interpret the
content and connections of the corpus of interest.

We now begin to see an evolution that increasingly shows
evidence of an emergent organizational focus in the content of the
journal. In the next decade (1987–1996), tests do not even appear

as a concept node. Study focused on job-relevant behavior (e.g.,
job, related, behavior, work, support, and relationship) and performance (e.g., ratings, effects, training, and task) are the most
important clusters, respectively. For the 1997–2006 decade, relationship (e.g., organizational, support, and role) and job (e.g.,
work, employees, satisfaction, and commitment) are the two most
important clusters, respectively. Test is back as a theme cluster, but
its link structure is sparse. Training is not even a node. Finally, for
the decade of 2007–2016, organizational is the most important
theme (e.g., behavior, employees, work, and social) followed by
performance (e.g., task, group, effects, team, and leadership). Test
is a node in a job cluster, with test linked to a validity cluster and
an evidence node. Training is back as a thematic cluster, albeit the
smallest one, linked to effects, team, and leadership concept nodes
in the performance cluster. In summary, the decade-based LSA
assessment is highly consistent with the overall bottom-up and
top-down LSAs and also with the bottom-up and top-down keyword analyses. What one can discern as one looks across the 10
decades of the journal’s publication record is the early evolution
from a broader applied psychology to a more focused industrial
psychology very much centered on testing and selection in the first
four decades; the development of a somewhat broader but classic,
empirically based industrial and organizational psychology in the
three decades of 1957 to 1986; and the evolution of an increasingly
organizational psychology beginning with the 1987–1996 decade,
crystalizing across the next two decades from 1997 to 2016 (see
Murphy, 2017). One can speculate endlessly as to why this is
occurring, but occurring it is. We suggest that changes in the
broader psychological science, along with the evolution of organizations (e.g., industrial to service, domestic to global, and industrial production to knowledge work), increasing workforce complexities confronting organizations (e.g., discrimination, changing
workforce demographics), and the evolving nature of work (e.g.,
global, distributed, and team-based) are, as they have been, driving
the focus of IOP research published in the journal.

Special Issue Process
Genesis
Given that special issues in the Journal of Applied Psychology
are quite rare, we think that it is important to describe the genesis
of the project for the sake of transparency. The impetus was a
suggestion by Salas, Associate Editor, to Kozlowski, Editor, sometime in 2012. Salas had helped to organize a special issue to
recognize the 50th anniversary of the founding of Human Factors,
and suggested that we ought to organize an effort to celebrate the
centennial of the Journal of Applied Psychology. Kozlowski
thought it was an excellent suggestion, but that it would be a
complicated project because it would span the terms of two editors.
It would need to be planned during his editorship, but executed
during the term of the next editor, who had yet to be selected at that
time. Meanwhile, general planning took place while Kozlowski
and Salas waited for APA to select the successor editor, Gilad
Chen, in 2013.
Kozlowski approached Chen with the idea, and he was quickly
on board, even though he was busy with the process of assembling
his team of associate editors. The first hurdle to surmount for a
special issue in an APA journal is getting permission from APAs

and prospectus for the special issue, and received a positive approval vote in September 2013. A key aspect of our plan is that all

Topics
We selected topics for the special issue that represented enduring themes across the life of the journal such as individual differences, research methods, selection, and training, as well as those

Author Teams
One of the novel aspects of this special issue is that we wanted
to invite author teams, not solo authors, which is not standard
practice. We proceeded by assembling teams of desirable authors
for each of the topics. We focused on identifying accomplished
experts who have contributed to the substantive topic in significant
and different ways to get a diverse pool of authors. We identified
a “lead” author for each of the topics as a point of contact, and
discussed the idea of composing the author team. Virtually everyone was enthusiastic about the idea, and we were able to assemble
a phenomenal group of scholars to write the review topics. That
said, there are also many more potential authors than we could
accommodate; our apologies. We believe that the quality of the
reviews published in this special issue validates our approach.

Summary and Conclusion
The Journal of Applied Psychology was launched with a hopeful
and expansive optimism about the positive impact of an applied
psychology on enhancing human well-being and happiness across
a wide range of societal settings. The founders were very keen on
not just advancing psychological theory for its own sake, but for
doing so in ways that would yield meaningful practical benefits to
society, organizations, and the common person. In many ways,
their dream has been realized and then some. In the articles and
editor reflections that follow, the contributions of applied psychology to 19 important topics relevant to building the workforce,
managing the workforce, managing differences within and between organizations, and exiting work are reviewed, summarized,
and celebrated. The contributions have been broad and deep; there
is much to celebrate for this centennial.
Yet, it is also the case that the journal has evolved substantially
since its founding. It is more narrowly focused on organizations,
rather than on the breadth of applied psychology across a wide
range of settings.3 It is less open to exploring new ideas for the
sake of curiosity and more focused on knowledge accumulation
and theoretical advancement. It is less balanced across science and
practice, and more explicitly science-centric. These are all markers
of disciplinary maturity and focus, increasing scientific impact,
and methodological sophistication. The field has grown up and is
a solid citizen.
However, as one ages, grows mature, and reflects on the past,
one can celebrate current success while also being nostalgic about
the youthful exuberance, wide-eyed wonder, and open curiosity of
one’s youth. Although many of the early research questions that
were posed and studied were simply naïve (Darley, 1968), there
are benefits to be had in reflecting on the core values that motivated the founding of the journal. After the reviews and editor
reflections that follow this article, we return to conclude this
Special Issue with a closing article to encapsulate and celebrate the
many contributions across the 19 topic areas. With an eye to the
future, we advance several recommendations to renew, reinvigorate, and regenerate the founding vision of the Journal of Applied
Psychology and to propel it for the next 100 years.
