This is the third editorial commentary in a three-part series that addresses introductions, implications, and interestingness. The ﬁrst, focusing on the introduction design, is about what the
beginning of a paper should look like. The second, focusing on practice implications, is about
what the end of a paper should look like. The third discusses how to develop and ﬁt attentiongrabbing ideas into the academic conversation in the literature. We hope this editorial series provides inspiration and ideas about publishing papers that people want to engage with.

apt than in harnessing the collective insights of the extant literature to inspire new research
questions. Such questions seek to build insights and understanding by enriching knowledge
developed in prior studies. In turn, those insights shape the very knowledge base that inspires
them, supporting future collective contributions. At least, in principle.
The reality is that developing compelling questions is fraught with uncertainty, often
entailing a nonlinear process of sense-making and sense-giving. In our experience, a signiﬁcant part of that uncertainty resides in discerning what is known, knowable, and unknown on
a focal topic. Owing to the ﬁeld’s porous boundaries and various disciplinary crossovers, we
all are confronted with the attentional demands of a research-rich world. The pressure to
publish novel contributions in hyperspecialized lanes of inquiry has further increased the
level of “ambient noise in the room,” making it harder to identify compelling questions
from the entirety of both concordant and discordant conversations.1 Given the demand for
novelty, researchers may also feel pressured to push the conversation in new, unexplored
directions without a rich assessment of where the conversation is at. Perhaps not surprisingly,
studies overlaying superﬁcial, incomplete, and incompatible questions upon the literature
constitute an appreciable portion of manuscripts rejected by the ﬁeld’s journals. Quite possibly, a core reason these so-called gap-driven studies tend to be perceived as uninteresting is
that they perform this superimposition ineffectively, without a careful review of what is
known, unknown, and important to know.
This editorial introduces a methodological process to support scholars in deriving compelling questions from research streams. At their core, research streams are developed by communities of scholars seeking to advance knowledge claims concerning a topic. By knowledge
claims, we broadly refer to an assertion that something is true. These claims may pertain to a
phenomenon of interest or the state of our theoretical knowledge. We contend that compelling
questions are those positioned to advance the developmental status of knowledge claims
aligned to the interests of a community of scholars. We use knowledge weaving as a metaphor
for achieving that alignment: combining the warp of distinct knowledge claims (i.e., what
content is signiﬁcant) with the weft of their developmental status (i.e., how to advance that
content). Without both ingredients, the researcher risks shoehorning a question into the
focal literature without substance.
Thus, compelling questions as knowledge weaving requires that researchers elevate themselves from a granular focus on preconceived notions and consider the literature’s extant
knowledge claims from a wide-angle lens. The central thrust of the associated process is a
focused and systematic consideration of where knowledge claims have been, currently are,
and where they should go next. The research question is thus constructed from a rich
fabric informed by the fundamental tenets of the literature while being sensitive to the foundational need for advancing the current understanding concerning a knowledge claim.

Stage I: Identifying Knowledge Claims
When researchers select a focal body of research, identifying and analyzing knowledge
claims can help simplify the overwhelming complexity of examining and assessing that
body of the accumulated knowledge over time. The choice of knowledge claims is critical
because it directs everything the researcher does in our process. Depending on the researcher’s interest and goals, one or several knowledge claims might be identiﬁed from the outset.
On the one hand, investigating several knowledge claims can be overwhelming, timeconsuming, and resource-intensive. On the other hand, exploring a more limited set of
claims provides fewer opportunities to derive good questions.
The science and art of conducting the review in either approach is an entirely separate
matter. To us, relevant theoretical frameworks need to play a decisive role in settling those
trade-offs. This is because a great deal of management research is focused on creating and
developing new understanding and application through theoretical knowledge. Such knowledge claims could deal with various issues, such as theoretical boundaries, units of analysis,
constructs, and causal mechanisms (Post, Sarala, Gatrell, & Prescott, 2020).
When an organized framework of extant knowledge claims is lacking, we suggest consideration of at least four forms of knowledge claims—namely, researchers’ taken-for-granted

beliefs, ideas, and principles (key assumptions); what researchers espouse to know theoretically and/or empirically (stylized facts); what researchers consider as common empirical, theoretical, or methodological limitations (enduring critiques); and what researchers suggest as
possible weaving threads between the focal literature and other bodies of knowledge to identify pertinent claims yet to be advanced (substantive omissions).
Consider the case of research on the upper echelons view of the ﬁrm. At the kernel of this
theory are the twin key assumptions that executives are boundedly rational and that their
demographic attributes provide reasonable proxies for cognitive processes that shape executive attention, perception, and interpretation (Hambrick & Mason, 1984). Two stylized facts
of this stream are that the inﬂuence of executive demographic attributes on strategic choice
and outcomes is more robust to the extent that the top management team is behaviorally integrated and to the extent top managers have high levels of decision-making discretion
(Hambrick, 1994; Simsek, Veiga, Lubatkin, & Dino, 2005). An enduring critique has been
a lack of attention to the underlying cognitive processes and mechanisms by which executive
characteristics inﬂuence ﬁrm behavior and outcomes (Lawrence, 1997). Finally, a substantive
omission (until recently) concerns the role of executive values and ideology in shaping strategic choices and organizational outcomes (Chin, Hambrick, & Trevino, 2013).
To develop an inventory of speciﬁc knowledge claims relevant to each of these four
knowledge claim forms, researchers might consider an umbrella review—an approach that
“aggregate[s] ﬁndings from several [prior] reviews that address speciﬁc questions” (Grant
& Booth, 2009: 103). Having been used widely in medicine and health sciences, umbrella
reviews summarize vital insights and evidence in academic communities with a view
toward future research directions (Whittemore, Chao, Jang, Minges, & Park, 2014). So
long as the literature review is sufﬁciently large, umbrella reviews provide an apt method
to summarize the settled, widely agreed-upon aspects of the literature and the ongoing critiques and shortcomings that require research attention. Umbrella reviews also offer the
opportunity to highlight omissions, biases, and oversights in the body of existing work.
Previous reviews, syntheses, and meta-analyses should be identiﬁed to perform an
umbrella review. Consolidating and cross-referencing insights from these works, researchers
catalog speciﬁc knowledge claims that pertain to the stylized facts, key assumptions, extant
critiques, and signiﬁcant omissions. Naturally, this requires that authors engage deeply with
the focal literature, read seminal works, and appreciate the lineage of contributions over time.
Such an approach naturally exploits the cumulative nature of literature reviews while also
being sensitive to the unique facets and aspects of distinct knowledge claims assessed. The
challenges might emerge when focal literature lacks relevant reviews or researchers are
faced with topical knowledge crossovers.3 In such cases, researchers will need to reduce
research complexity to a manageable level relative to the select knowledge claims such as
broad versus narrow stylized facts and omissions.

Stage II: Assessing Developmental Status of Knowledge Claims
Once researchers develop an inventory of signiﬁcant and speciﬁc knowledge claims
(within and across claim types), they can examine their developmental status. These knowledge claims play a fundamental role in the subsequent diagnosis. The guiding question in this
stage is, What is the developmental status of a given knowledge claim?

Following the umbrella review, one option is to return to the underlying body of literature
and perform a contemporaneous, independent systematic review to evaluate the condition of
each knowledge claim. This systematic review could draw upon the articles referenced by
prior reviews and works after the publication of each review or in related ﬁelds that shed
light on the essential claims. This independent systematic review provides the necessary
information to determine the developmental progress of each claim identiﬁed.4
To summarize the status of each knowledge claim, we suggest consideration of three possible states—stable, fragile, and unstable. Stable knowledge claims are those where a large
body of evidence lends direct and indirect support such that it is increasingly unlikely that
the claim will be overturned by contradictory data short of a paradigm shift driven by new
tools, techniques, or bodies of evidence. Fragile knowledge claims are those which have
proven resilient to a limited degree of probing, but other signiﬁcant and plausible boundary
conditions remain unexplored. We label such claims as fragile because they are more likely to
be revised or overturned as the knowledge claim is tested under new circumstances. Unstable
knowledge claims are held only tenuously due to inconsistent ﬁndings and can be modiﬁed in
the light of emerging data and evidence.5
Key Assumptions. Several assumptions often underpin a research area. While some of
these assumptions are often questioned in scholarly dialogue and debates, they provide a
fertile basis for new research questions. We refer to assumptions that have been veriﬁed
across empirical settings as conﬁrmed (Stable). Questioning the situations under which the
assumption does not apply and why this is so offers the basis for interesting research questions. And challenging and replacing assumptions have long been considered a source of
compelling questions (Davis, 1971), but this is often easier said than done. Qualiﬁed assumptions hold under speciﬁc settings and circumstances, but not others, and thus naturally lend
themselves to questions concerning their boundary conditions (Fragile). When assumptions
are not born out in empirical reality and are unfounded (Unstable), the crucial question is how
replacing the assumption changes the theoretical explanation and questions that can be raised.
Stylized Facts. A stylized fact is an empirical observation that, while true as a broad tendency, is not necessarily true in every speciﬁc instance (Kaldor, 1961). When substantial progress has been made in establishing and verifying stylized facts to the point that an empirical
observation holds across settings and contexts, the claim is classiﬁed as robust (Stable). The
question that arises from robust stylized facts is how far and wide these facts generalize and
apply—thus, opportunities abound for reﬁnement and elaboration and deepening understanding of the causal mechanisms underpinning these effects. Questions might also seek to understand how they link with other stylized facts to explain a phenomenon. However, in many
cases, stylized facts are slow to emerge in that their generality has not been fully established
(Fragile). The opportunity and source of research questions are more fully establishing the
boundary conditions, both theoretically and empirically, to contribute to a more robust
ﬁnding or the revision of existing conclusions. The emphasis of questioning might also
involve examining the construct in conjunction with other constructs. Finally, some facts
might be classiﬁed as incipient—they have been observed in a limited number of studies
and settings (Unstable). Here the focus of the research question could be moving research
towards establishing a new stylized fact or overturning those incipient claims.

While many scholars rightfully focus on establishing the presence of a relationship, it is
important to also pay attention to mounting evidence regarding the absence of a relationship.
While the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence in the context of a single study,
repeated demonstrations of the absence of a relationship across many settings increase the
likelihood that no relationship in fact exists. That said, such a lack of evidence is not
always easy to assess due to the well-known ﬁle-drawer problem (for studies directly examining the effect) or the evidence that is not foregrounded in other studies (e.g., studies that
capture correlations between two constructs as control variables, rather than substantive relationships of interest). A close and deep reading of the literature may help extract these otherwise “hidden” insights from the body of existing work.
Enduring Critiques. Researchers seeking inspiration for questions may also focus their
attention on the status of systematic literature critiques. Knowing the complete history of
these critiques could be a real advantage for the researcher when positioning a study
around them. While resolved critiques provide a less compelling basis for new questions
(Stable), a creative examination of the outstanding, unaddressed critiques, or what we call
blind spots (Unstable) can often result in a signiﬁcant contribution. Questions that seek to
create consensus around partially addressed critiques can also substantially contribute to
the literature (Fragile).
Substantive Omissions. Research questions can also arise from signiﬁcant knowledge
gaps in a body of research. An “unknown unknown” would, for example, be the case
where the researcher discovers a new knowledge creation opportunity subjectively. By contrast, a known unknown would entail research inspired by a knowledge gap embedded in
prior research. This is an area where borrowing ideas and theories from different disciplines
could, for example, prove valuable. Clearly, known knowns are areas where researchers recognized an omission and have already conducted research to ﬁll that gap (in other words, the
omission has already been identiﬁed and addressed in the period between when it was raised
and when a researcher seeks to identify a new question to tackle).

Stage III: Prospecting for Alignment
Once the evolutionary state of each claim has been reasonably determined (such as stable,
fragile, and unstable), the most important step the researcher can take is to generate an initial
list of potential questions aligned with the select state.6 Even though each knowledge claim
can lead to research questions with an effective alignment, certain combinations and alignments appear to be particularly promising. For example, building on stable stylized facts
allows researchers to “stand on the shoulders of giants” and add further nuance to understanding these well-known relationships or point out important boundary conditions. However, a
new question may also challenge a stylized fact because, for example, an underlying construct
is misspeciﬁed, or the analytical approach has been wrong. Similarly, researchers can breathe
new life into literature by repairing overturned and unstable assumptions. Or by challenging
an assumption, the researcher might be able to produce an interesting question. Likewise, rectifying stable, unanswered critiques addresses numerous open calls to solve known and signiﬁcant challenges with the literature. Finally, by appropriately selecting fertile “canyons” of

understanding rather than probing for small, barren “gaps” in our knowledge, researchers
have a better chance of discovering uncharted territories for a new line of research.
In all these examples, the crucial issue is that the research questions generated in this
process are effectively aligned with a given knowledge development state. In many ways,
a research question will be less compelling if the researcher fails to accurately assess the
status of a knowledge claim or misalign the question relative to that assessment.
Together, the prospecting stage can thus be a crucial yet challenging aspect of the process
for three reasons. First, multiple potential questions can arise from a speciﬁc knowledge claim
type—particularly if the claim is complex and conditional. Second, achieving alignment
between the developmental status of a knowledge claim and the related question is a creative
act. And third, the researcher may still have (too) many potential research questions at the end
of the prospecting effort. Not all of these questions will likely have the same potential despite
their alignment. Hence, a winnowing process is necessary.

Stage IV. Evaluating Intrinsic Goodness
The fourth stage is where the researcher starts focusing on different research question possibilities to select one or two with the most signiﬁcant contribution potential. This is also the
stage where researchers’ speciﬁc objectives and preferences can matter greatly. Researchers
come to research questions with differing backgrounds, skills, resources, and disparate objectives and wants. We all make choices based on information, perception, or intuition, and the
choice of a research question(s) can reﬂect one or more of these elements. And at times, the
researcher will need to choose among competing priorities and choose the research questions
that can satisfy the needs of a stakeholder, such as a funding agency. But even when such
conditions are present, a further assessment of research questions in terms of intrinsic goodness for knowledge contribution can be useful. By intrinsic goodness, we mean the researcher’s deliberate efforts to choose research questions that have the most signiﬁcant effect on the
current developmental status of a knowledge claim. Because conducting research to answer
questions is a time- and resource-intensive endeavor, researchers must navigate inescapable
trade-offs, three of which we believe are of greatest signiﬁcance at this stage.
A ﬁrst trade-off concerns what Katz and Kahn (1966) describe as the maintenance role of
scholarship, the “keepers of the ﬂame” in reﬁning and advancing knowledge contributions
versus the adaptive role of scholarship as the “vanguard in generating new knowledge”
(Corley & Gioia, 2011). Thus, one aspect of intrinsic goodness entails a question’s potential
for advancing core theoretical knowledge claims in an exploitative vs. exploratory fashion.
Even as we refer the reader to Whetten (1989) for a comprehensive understanding of such
possibilities along the lines of What, When, How, Whom, etc., not all knowledge claims
can address all the dimensions, nor do the dimensions have the same exploitative and exploratory potential to advance theoretical knowledge.
A second trade-off relates to the extent to which research questions weave backward- and
forward-looking knowledge claims. The latter requires what Corley and Gioia (2011: 13)
refer to as prescience, or “the process of discerning or anticipating what we need to know
and, equally important, of inﬂuencing the intellectual framing and dialogue about what we
need to know.” One approach is to begin with the relevant knowledge claims and then shortlist those most pertinent to addressing the grand societal challenges of the future. The focus is

Stage V. Prototyping for an Audience
A research question is ultimately a strategic choice akin to a value proposition development for a product. It exists in a “market” context, taking on signiﬁcance and meaning
from an audience’s perspective. An audience encompasses the real or perceived readers,
users, or stakeholders who might be interested in reading and using the questions and learning
more about the answer to them.
The “question-audience ﬁt” is partially achieved through the earlier stages, but there is
room for ﬁne-tuning by being able to “read the room” and determine what types of knowledge
contributions are either (a) most timely given the contemporary zeitgeist or (b) most well
received by the target audience based on style and taste preferences. To that end, it might
be instructive to consider the marketing notion of “product-market ﬁt,” drawing on the wellknown segmentation-targeting-positioning logic. This logic suggests that the researcher ﬁrst
identify the potential audience group(s) of their knowledge claims, select which audience
group(s) they should target, and then frame research questions accordingly.
Choosing a primary audience for a research question in the management ﬁeld can sometimes be challenging because research questions may have relevance to multiple potential
audiences. There are various factors that the researcher can use to classify audiences, such
as the phenomena they study, the academic societies they belong and attend to, the journals
where they tend to publish, the theories they built upon, and empirical contexts. And it is
always tempting to target a broader and larger audience group for greater reach and
impact. Yet trying to be compelling to all audiences can be a source of confusion and risks
by not contributing enough to any audience. Thus, the researcher always needs to consider
and identify the primary audience without ignoring other potential audiences. Although we
have no easy solution for dealing with this multiple audience challenge, it may be helpful
to think about three issues when grouping different audiences: who are they; what are their
reading and knowledge preferences and needs; and what can address those preferences
needs and wants. In this sense, one of the better ways of ﬁnalizing the select questions
might be to consider what answers each question might reveal for the targeted audience(s).

Critical Contingencies
The summary in Table 1 indicates that researchers might need to modify the process to suit
their needs and contingencies. First, the process will naturally vary depending on the
researcher’s knowledge of the focal stream and knowledge claim. The core idea is to ascertain
the current developmental status of a knowledge claim as a launching pad for generating
research questions. When the researcher already possesses such knowledge, not all the outlined stages may be relevant, and the need for a detailed and systematic engagement may
not be necessary.
The second contingency is the body of available knowledge to be examined. We encourage researchers to draw upon a variety of evidence bases, including books, book chapters,
policy papers, conference proceedings, and works-in-progress in evaluating the development
status of a knowledge claim. Doing so can help to overcome the ﬁle-drawer problem, the tendency for authors to publish (and editors to accept) positive results (Rosenthal, 1979). A large
enough sample permits better diagnosis and more opportunities for prospecting new research
questions. Moreover, the combined and coordinated use of different article types could help
the researcher better track and validate knowledge claim types and their developmental status.
As the volume, velocity, and variety of a focal literature increases, researchers need to spend
more time identifying and prioritizing which knowledge claims are signiﬁcant to the select
body of research. Starting the research question journey on a large body of research
without the proper knowledge claims could lead to erroneous or misconceived conclusions
about the focal body of research.
Third, by stressing the importance of understanding signiﬁcant knowledge claims comprehensively and thoroughly, the process can be helpful to support deductive, inductive, and
abductive research approaches. However, the execution details need to be harmonized with
the requirements of different research approaches. For example, reframing the original
research question in qualitative research is an ongoing process that can increase novelty
when competently executed. So, it is important to view research as a process during which
researchers are open to reframing, refocusing, or adjusting the research question as they
assimilate the research and build more accurate mental models of the structure and topography of the existing literature. In this process, knowledge claims can serve as a conceptual
anchor to guide and summarize the progress. The precision of individual knowledge
claims demands clarity and does not require complicated analyses.
Fourth, having a question with good intrinsic value is insufﬁcient. The type of audience(s)
targeted can impact the ultimate value of the outlined process. The reality is that not many
research questions in our ﬁeld can be equally relevant to multiple audiences and contexts.
An explicit focus on an actual or perceived audience’s needs and making sure audience considerations are not ignored can be critical. Doing so allows the researchers to attune the
content of questions for a speciﬁc audience and bound a question(s) effectively. Put differently, ﬁnalizing the research question also needs to occur in the context of framing it for a

target audience. Following Gitlin (1980: 6), we broadly use the notion of framing to mean
how the researcher selects, emphasizes, and presents speciﬁc aspects of a knowledge claim
to make it more salient for an audience. Such framing consideration requires additional
effort from the researcher, but insofar as it helps to generate a question in a relationship to
the empowerment and enlightenment of a speciﬁc audience, it is ultimately worth the effort.
Fifth, the process might look different if the researcher already has a good idea of what
knowledge claims the target audience may ﬁnd relevant. This might, for example, be the
case for a paper under an advanced review stage at a journal. Suppose the reviewing panel
has already agreed upon the validity and signiﬁcance of the study’s knowledge advance. In
that case, the research team might be better off focusing on the matters of rigor and execution.
Conversely, suppose the review panel still raises questions about the study’s motivation and
positioning. In that case, the outlined process can be helpful—whether for the resubmission or
submission to another journal.
Finally, it seems to us a favorable review decision in the ﬁeld’s high-quality journals is
unlikely without consideration of the ideas outlined in the last three stages. Conversations
about whether to escalate the commitment to a paper with uncertain outcomes are never
easy but necessary, and these three stages can prove helpful in that determination. A
related question is whether the researcher should pursue questions that do not address the
knowledge claims of stylized facts, key assumptions, enduring critiques, or substantive omissions. Our experience is that such questions tend to fail during the review process. By
analogy, they may resemble the entrepreneur with a product or technology that does not
address a pressing customer problem. But instead of abandoning questions of interest that
do not neatly ﬁt into these categories, the review process may lead to reframing the initial
question in a way that increases its relevance to the developmental status of such knowledge
claims. So, just as the entrepreneur may pivot to reconstitute the initial value proposition to
better address customer problems, scholars can reﬁne the initial questions to their audience’s
knowledge development needs and wants. Because the journal review process often proves to
be a “death-valley” to prototype a value proposition, we encourage the researcher to consider
these three stages before the journal submission. Despite the efforts invested to date, researchers must be prepared to accept the presence of sunk costs and move on from questions ultimately ill-suited for the current state of scholarly conversation (either because their
contribution comes too early, too late, or too hastily in the discussion).

Concluding Observations
This editorial suggested that carefully reading and interpreting the developmental status of
signiﬁcant knowledge claims in a research stream is a critical step in deriving and communicating compelling questions. To that end, we offered an organized conception and process for
weaving knowledge claims. Our goal was to help researchers optimize their effort and time,
not reinvent a process each time.
In our formulation, compelling questions build on a foundational purpose where the selection and systematic assessment of knowledge claims occur concerning the empowerment and
enlightenment of a speciﬁc audience (research streams). As such, we believe that knowledge
weaving possibilities can be facilitated by a common process—determining the developmental state of relevant knowledge progress and challenges to position a question(s) with the

Simsek et al. / Compelling Questions in Research

potential to advance that state and, in so doing, enlighten an audience. Creativity in this
process arises from what knowledge claim(s) the researcher picks to assess and how they
execute that assessment to position a question(s) to advance that claim. Thus, compelling
questions as weaving knowledge claims is not so much an act of discovery as an act of creativity. To paraphrase the quote attributed to Albert Szent-Gyorgyi, it involves seeing what
everybody else has seen and thinking what nobody else has thought.
The more we use previous research strategically, the better our scholarship and ﬁeld will
be. We risk underutilizing our collective knowledge if we do not productively leverage the
existing corpus of research to develop new questions. To that end, we can hardly think of anything more worth developing than the ability to ask questions about the questions we generate
from the ﬁeld’s research streams. We hope this editorial will provide a vocabulary and structure to our thinking about the questions we ask and help spark discussion to expand our effort
to that end. At stake is the healthy growth of our scholarship and collective intelligence.
