### Section B Example (Multiple Goals):

**Excellent answer (12-13 pts):**

\"Neal et al.\'s (2017) dynamic self-regulation framework explains that people manage multiple goals simultaneously with limited cognitive resources, requiring constant prioritization and creating switching costs. At HealthFirst, the system fails because: (1) **Goal conflict**: Physicians face visit volume targets vs. satisfaction scores vs. documentation---these inherently compete (more visits = less time per patient = lower satisfaction). Neal et al. would predict this creates resource depletion as physicians constantly try to balance incompatible demands. (2) **No prioritization guidance**: When goals conflict, the system provides no decision rules. Which matters more---seeing the 28th patient or spending extra time to ensure satisfaction? (3) **Resource exhaustion**: Monitoring multiple metrics simultaneously depletes cognitive resources, reducing performance on all goals. (4) **Gaming as rational response**: Scheduling only healthy patients reduces goal conflict by manipulating the environment---a predictable adaptation to impossible demands. The framework predicts exactly what\'s happening: when multiple goals compete without prioritization support, people experience overwhelm, performance suffers, and they find ways to reduce conflict (gaming, reduced helping).\"

### Section C Example (Redesign):

**Excellent answer (12-13 pts):**

\"**Differentiated Approaches:**

**Physicians**: Replace fixed targets with flexible ranges (24-30 patients/day) allowing context adjustment. Use goal bundles: \'Maintain ≥4.5 satisfaction while seeing 26-28 patients/day, prioritizing satisfaction for complex cases.\' This acknowledges trade-offs and provides decision rules. Add learning goals for complex cases.

**Nurses**: Establish safety-first hierarchy---medication accuracy non-negotiable, procedure volume flexible based on unit acuity. Use team-based goals to reduce competition. \'Unit maintains \<0.1% errors and completes 200+ procedures daily\' encourages helping.

**Tactics for Managing Competing Goals:**

\(1\) **Explicit Prioritization Hierarchy**: \'When goals conflict: Safety/quality → Patient experience → Efficiency.\' Provides decision rules Neal et al. (2017) identify as critical for multiple-goal management.

\(2\) **Sequential Goal Focus**: Designate protected time blocks---morning focus on volume, afternoon on documentation. Reduces concurrent goal monitoring and switching costs. Rotate monthly emphasis across different goals to prevent chronic neglect of any goal.\"

### Section A Example (AET & Justice):

**Excellent answer (13-14 pts):**

\"**Affective Events Theory** (Weiss & Cropanzano, 1996) explains that workplace events trigger emotional reactions that accumulate to shape attitudes and behaviors. Jordan experienced three significant negative events:

\(1\) **New micromanaging supervisor**: Daily event creating repeated frustration and loss of autonomy---negative affect accumulates over time. (2) **Missed promotion**: Discrete high-impact event causing disappointment, anger, feeling undervalued---crystallizes into attitude that career prospects are limited. (3) **Reorganization without input**: Creates anxiety and lack of control. These events accumulate to produce Jordan\'s cynicism and withdrawal.

**Justice Violations** (Colquitt, 2001):

**Distributive**: Promotion outcome unfair---Jordan had positive performance, tenure, and reasonable expectations. External hire received position Jordan deserved based on contributions.

**Procedural**: Multiple violations---no transparent process, Jordan had no voice in decision, no opportunity to compete fairly, reorganization made without input. Violates Colquitt\'s (2001) procedural criteria of consistency, voice, and bias suppression.

**Interactional**: Severe violations---promotion came \'without explanation\' (informational justice failure), no communication about reorganization (informational), micromanaging shows lack of respect and trust (interpersonal justice). All components of interactional justice violated.\"

### Section B Example (Comparative Impact):

**Excellent answer (12-13 pts):**

\"Research (Colquitt et al., 2001) shows that **organizational justice has stronger effects than job satisfaction** on withdrawal behaviors and citizenship performance, though both matter. The meta-analysis found:

**For withdrawal behaviors** (turnover, absenteeism): Procedural and interactional justice are stronger predictors than satisfaction. Single justice violations can trigger turnover even with high satisfaction because justice violations break the psychological contract and damage trust. Jordan\'s **job search activity** likely stems more from promotion injustice than daily dissatisfaction---the violation signals limited future prospects. **Monday absences** suggest avoidance of the micromanaging supervisor (interactional justice violation).

**For performance**: Satisfaction has weak relationships with task performance (explaining why Jordan\'s performance reviews remain positive). **Why justice matters more for Jordan**: The discrete violations (promotion, reorganization) are justice events that damage trust organizationally. Justice violations create attributions of unfairness that are more behaviorally potent than general dissatisfaction. Jordan can point to specific unjust treatment, making withdrawal feel justified.\"
