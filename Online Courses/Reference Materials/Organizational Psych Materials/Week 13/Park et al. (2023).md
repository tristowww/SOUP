Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this study is to review the overall trends in diversity, equity and inclusion (DEI)
research in the management field.
Design/methodology/approach – We performed a scoping review to comprehensively understand how DEI
research has been conducted and to inform future research and practices in the management field. We reviewed
725 articles published from 1976 to 2022 in SSCI-indexed management journals. We examined publication
profiles (number of articles and research focus), publication tendency (journals and years), chronological
publication trends, work environments (traditional vs remote/virtual work settings) and dimensions of DEI,
including age, gender, race and culture.
Findings – We identified six common DEI research themes: (1) DEI management and practice, (2) perspectives
on DEI, (3) team/group diversity, (4) DEI conceptualization, (5) leadership for DEI and (6) DEI climate. Finally,
discussion, implications and recommendations for future research are presented.
Originality/value – Our research provides a comprehensive outline of the DEI research and suggests future
directions to contribute to and advance knowledge on DEI in the management field.
Keywords Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, DEI, Scoping review
Paper type Literature review

Diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) have become increasingly recognized as critical issues in
society. This social phenomenon is also reflected in the workplace with organizations
increasingly promoting DEI initiatives. Awareness of the value of DEI began to grow during the
Civil Rights Movement in the 1960s, which led up to the Civil Rights Act of 1964 that prohibits
discrimination in employment based on race, color, sex, religion, and national origin (Hall,
2005). In 1965, President Lyndon B. Johnson signed Executive Order (EO) 11246 mandating
that federal contractors take affirmative action to prevent employment discrimination on the
basis of race, color, religion, and national origin and to ensure equal employment opportunities.
These early social movements and the subsequent social change were the initial momentum for
organizations to commit to diversity and fairness (Crosby et al., 2006).
For about six decades, various efforts have been exerted to promote diversity and fairness in
work, education, and other social contexts. However, DEI is still an important concern in many

Later, the focus expanded to age (Age Discrimination Act of 1975), disabilities (Americans

the concept and focuses of DEI continued to evolve. Although organizations continue to find
ways to promote DEI, it is an ongoing challenge in management (Hays-Thomas, 2004).
The world has developed rapidly and become more complex. As a result, people from
diverse backgrounds need to work together in organizations reflecting employees’ different
gender, race, color, religion, and nationality as well as different professional expertise,
education, perspectives, and experience. Globalization, driven by the advancement of
technology, has further broadened the dimension of DEI in organizations in terms of
geography, national origin, race, and ethnicity as well as workers’ cultural backgrounds,
languages, religions, perspectives, and communication styles (Fagbe et al., 2019; Kim and
Bhawuk, 2008). The recent pandemic also forced people to work in new ways, which has
added new dimensions of diversity to organizations in terms of work arrangements including
work schedules, locations, and employment relationships (Spurk and Straub, 2020). This
changing environment has forced organizations to face the challenges of embracing diversity
and ensuring inclusion and fairness. To continue promoting DEI in organizations, we need to
understand what has been done so we can have a fresh perspective on the history of DEI and
chart a path forward. Thus, this study aims to understand the overall picture of the state of
research on DEI, identify research gaps, and suggest a future research agenda to expand
knowledge of DEI in the management field. The research questions guiding this study are: (1)
What do previous studies reveal about DEI research trends in terms of the years and journals?
(2) What DEI dimensions have previous studies examined? (3) What are the common themes
in the DEI literature? and (4) What areas need to be investigated further to fill the research gaps
in DEI?
To answer these questions, we conducted a comprehensive review of a large number of
studies over the past 45 years to gain a broad understanding of DEI research in the management
field. As research on DEI has increased and accumulated, a number of studies have provided
inclusive and overall reviews including the topics, dimensions, and outcomes of DEI
(e.g. Roberson, 2019; Trinada et al., 2021; Janssens and Zanoni, 2021). For example,
Roberson (2019) reviewed various conceptualizations, theories, outcomes, and practices of
workplace diversity in multiple disciplines including industrial and organizational
psychology, human resource management, and organizational behavior. Janssens and
Zanoni (2021) also provided a comprehensive review of DEI research in the organization
and management disciplines. They also identified the limitations of previous research that did
not adequately address intriguing social changes. These studies used systematic review
methods, which can provide in-depth analysis regarding various topics and dimensions of DEI
research using a well-organized knowledge development process. However, these studies only
examined a limited number of exemplary studies, so they were unable to show the entire and
inclusive patterns and trends of DEI research.
To fill this research void, we conducted a scoping review to analyze a large number of
papers: 725 articles over 45 years. We identified the DEI research trends by summarizing the
publication years, journals, main DEI topics, and work environments in which DEI topics have
been examined. We also focused on identifying dimensions of DEI and the common themes
that have frequently been discussed in the DEI research. Finally, we identified patterns of new
emerging DEI topics and dimensions to help researchers expand their perspectives and
identify newer topics in the field.

on DEI. Diversity, equity, and inclusion are closely interrelated concepts. Thus, by analyzing
these three concepts together rather than only focusing on each concept separately, this
research provides a broader understanding of DEI research in the management field. In

Literature review
DEI studies as critical issues in organizational management have exponentially developed in
the last three decades since scholars (e.g. Cox and Blake, 1991) suggested a greater focus on
diversity management in the organization (Holmes et al., 2021; Janssens and Steyaert, 2019;
Roberson et al., 2017). Early studies focused on representational diversity (Roberson et al.,
2017). Scholars contended that increasing the number of diverse workers would enhance
organizational effectiveness by bringing innovative and creative ideas to the organization.
However, researchers quickly recognized the complex characteristics underlying diverse
groups, such as unconscious bias against differences (see Leslie et al., 2020; Roberson et al.,
2017) and historically institutionalized inequality between groups (DiTomaso, 2021).
Discrimination and stereotyping stemming from prejudice can affect the cognitive,
emotional, and behavioral aspects of individuals within organizations (Randolph et al.,
2023). They realized that these barriers can hinder groups from achieving desired outcomes if
organizations only focus on numerical diversity. There is currently wide consensus that
diversity is a double-edged sword for organizational effectiveness. It can be a source of
conflict and obstacles to group cohesion and social integration, but it can also be a source of
synergy and learning for creativity (Stahl and Maznevski, 2021). Accordingly, recent studies
have gone beyond representational diversity. In this section, we introduce the research streams
by synthesizing the findings from a broad range of scholarly studies on DEI in organizations.
Conceptualization and theory development
Diversity has been conceptualized in various ways in the literature, and the scope of diversity
has become broader and deeper (Triana et al., 2021). Demographic diversity, defined as “readily
observable individual differences such as sex, race, and age” (Triana et al., 2021, p. 2138), has
been the most common scope. However, scholars have also explored the effect of a broader
range of diversity, such as job-related diversity, including education, work experience, and
functional background, as well as deep-level diversity, such as class, religion, personality,
values, attitudes, and beliefs (e.g. Ahmad et al., 2021; Bhardwaj et al., 2021; Bell et al., 2011;
Triana et al., 2021). Harrison and Klein (2007) compiled various conceptualizations and
proposed a typology that distinguishes three dispersion forms of work units: separation, variety,
and disparity. These forms include observational, job-related, and deep-level diversity.
According to this comprehensive conceptualization, diversity is characterized as disagreement
on continuous attributes such as values and attitudes, uniqueness, and inequality of desired
resources among unit members (Harrison and Klein, 2007). Many researchers have based their
studies on this typology and conceptualization (see Roberson et al., 2017).
In addition to individual characteristics to conceptualize diversity, scholars have also
examined group composition and its effect on group-level outcomes. Demographic faultlines
refer to “hypothetical dividing lines that split a group or a team into two or more subgroups
based on one or more individual attributes” (Thatcher and Patel, 2012, p. 970). Since faultlines
were first introduced, scholars have conceptually and empirically investigated how faultlines
contribute to various diversity-related outcomes (Thatcher and Patel, 2012). As a related
concept, prejudice, defined as “a negative attitude toward another person or group formed in
advance of any experience with that person or group” (see Lavan and Ropez, 2023, p. 897), has
been the subject of extensive scholarly investigation, with researchers exploring its
antecedents and consequences (Randolph et al., 2023). The present body of research
expands the scope of diversity studies by offering sophisticated insights into the detrimental

effects of discrimination, bias, and prejudice, which serve as antitheses to creating a diverse
and inclusive organizational environment (see Lavan and Ropez, 2023).
Scholars have used social identity (Tajfel, 1978) and self-categorization theories (Turner,
1982) to explain why demographic diversity matters in organizations. These theories explain
why individuals classify themselves and others into specific social identity groups and how
their identity influences interpersonal interactions and intergroup relations. Drawing upon
self-categorization theory, identity-blind, identity-conscious, and multicultural ideologies
have been proposed as frameworks for ameliorating intergroup relations (Leslie and Flynn,
2024). Other theories have been used, tested, and developed to explain the nomological
network between multi-level antecedents and diversity and organizational effectiveness
(Holmes et al., 2021; Roberson et al., 2017; Roberson, 2019). For example, scholars
supporting the value-in-diversity theory (e.g. Cox and Blake, 1991; Williams and O’Reilly,
1998) have viewed diversity in the work unit as a source of creative and unique ideas for
innovation and problem-solving and postulated that a work unit with demographic diversity
could lead to better performance.
Scholars have also developed and elaborated on various theoretical frameworks and
research models with DEI-focused moderators and mediators to examine the link between
diversity and organizational effectiveness. Social exchange theory (Blau, 1964) explains the
reciprocal exchange of employees’ favorable work attitudes towards an organization’s efforts
to build and maintain a pro-diversity atmosphere. In addition, Van Knippenberg et al. (2004)
developed the categorization-elaboration model (CEM) to explain how intergroup biases and
misunderstandings caused by diversity restrict group information exchange and integration
processing, which are preconditions for experiencing the synergetic benefits of diversity. CEM
has influenced studies in terms of the underlying mechanisms of why and how organizations
can encourage information exchange and mitigate the disruptive effects of categorization
(Roberson et al., 2017; Roberson, 2019). Also, Van Knippenberg and colleagues (2007)
defined diversity beliefs as “beliefs about the value of diversity to workgroup functioning, as a
moderator of responses to diversity” (p. 209). They argued that diversity benefits group
performance when group members believe so. This conceptualization has become a basis of
diversity and inclusion climate conceptualization.
Diversity and inclusion climate as an underlying mechanism
As an underlying mechanism in the nomological network of diversity, scholars have paid
increasing attention to diversity as an organizational climate in which members need sufficient
agreement on the value of diversity (Chan, 1998). Several studies have also explored how a
shared perception of diversity is formed (e.g. Cox, 1994), how inclusive climate is different
from diversity climate (Nishii, 2013), and how diversity and inclusive climates increase
organizational effectiveness such as organizational commitment (Li et al., 2019) and
performance (McKay et al., 2011). Cox (1994) also proposed multiple-level antecedents to
determine the organizational diversity climate in the interactional model of cultural diversity
theory. The theory proposes that several factors at multiple levels interact to shape the extent of
an organization’s diversity climate. Diversity climate is defined as “the degree to which a firm
advocates fair human resource policies and socially integrates underrepresented employees”
(Mckay et al., 2008, p. 352). Nishii (2013) differentiated inclusion climate from diversity
climate, arguing that diversity climate may stimulate resentment or backlash from privileged
groups; therefore, it does not encourage a synergetic effect from diverse employees. Nishii
(2013) focused on the genuine effect of inclusion, and constructed dimensions of inclusive
climate with three dimensions: fairly implemented employment practices, integration of
differences, and inclusion in decision-making.
These climate studies, especially inclusive climate studies, position inclusive climate as
a critical underlying mechanism—a moderator or mediator—between representational
diversity and organizational outcomes (Nishii, 2013; Li et al., 2019). Holmes et al.’s (2021)

Diversity management practices as an underlying mechanism
Bernstein et al. (2020) reviewed multiple diversity studies and concluded that representational
diversity does not promote an organization’s inclusive climate. They introduced the theory of
generative interactions and emphasized the importance of robust organizational practices to
generate genuine and deep interaction, which lessens the effect of default dynamics and leads
to inclusion and equity. Bernstein and colleagues (2020) indicated that diversity management
practices are one of the most critical organizational-level interventions to change employees’
perceptions of stereotyping and form an inclusive climate. As a result, these interventions
influence organizational outcomes (see K€
ollen, 2021; Guillaume et al., 2017).
These organizational interventions, known as diversity management practices, were
initiated when affirmative action and equal opportunity programs were introduced. They
aimed to promote employment and career development for minority groups (e.g. black and
female employees) as a result of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (K€
ollen, 2021). Most
organizations now implement diversity management practices, which include recruiting
diverse employees, diversity-related employer branding, diversity training, diversity-oriented
human resource (HR) practices, work-life initiatives, mentoring and sponsorship, and
employee resource groups (Avery and Mckay, 2010; K€
ollen, 2021; Nishii et al., 2018). Most
research has empirically explored whether these diversity management practices have
increased representational diversity and diversity/inclusive climate in organizations and
contributed to organizational effectiveness (Nishii et al., 2018).
Nishii et al.’s (2018) review and other comprehensive reviews on organizational outcomes
and the effectiveness of diversity management programs have shown mixed and inconsistent
results (Bernstein et al., 2020; K€ollen, 2021). Leslie et al.’s (2014) meta-analysis indicated that
affirmative action programs limit the target’s performance outcomes as programs may
strengthen negative stereotypes and lower the self-competence of program recipients. In
response, researchers introduced the process model, which emphasizes that the outcomes of
diversity practices are determined by the underlying mechanisms, such as the implementation
process, stakeholders’ intentions, perceptions, and interpretations of the programs, as well as
the contextual characteristics (Leslie et al., 2014).
Given that leaders are critical agents to effectively implement diversity practices, scholars
have also studied leadership types and behaviors, indicating that leaders have a strong
influence on various decisions and are an important source of social information (Chanland
and Murphy, 2018; Greer et al., 2012; Nishii and Mayer, 2009). For example, Silver and
colleagues (2023) underscored the pivotal role of leadership in shaping diversity climates,
criticizing the destructive nature of leader biases and advocating for constructive leadership
allyship to ameliorate social inequalities within organizations. In addition, research
investigating the way to decrease prejudice and discrimination using diversity management
program have been explored. Waples and Botsford Morgan (2023) proposed a multi-level
model that posits that macro-level societal events related to prejudice and discrimination can
exert a trickle-down effect on meso and micro levels, thereby influencing the development of
specific diversity management programs. Also, Cox (2023) introduced new diversity training
program – habit-breaking training that can lead to meaningful and sustainable improvement on
DEI issues in an organization.

In sum, DEI research has grown substantially and expanded to include various subtopics,
particularly over the last 30 years. Researchers agree that having only representational
diversity does not guarantee desirable outcomes. Reviewing the diversity literature provides
insights on the complicated underlying mechanisms at the individual level, shared perception
level (culture and climate), and organizational level (program implementation). These factors
interact to generate positive or negative organizational outcomes. Building on this
understanding, we review a wide range of DEI studies published in management journals to
better understand the chronological research trends, emergent diversity dimensions, and
dominant topics in specific periods using the scoping review method.
Methods
To comprehensively review the literature on DEI published in management journals, we
conducted a scoping review. A scoping review is a useful approach to examine the scope,
volume, focus, and overall state of research on a given topic and to determine if conducting a
systematic review on this topic is worthwhile (Arksey and O’Malley, 2005; Munn et al., 2018).
Our study aims to identify the scope, patterns, and trends of DEI research and to identify the
research gaps with implications for future research and practice.
Literature search and inclusion criteria
We searched articles on Web of Science, one of the most comprehensive databases in social
science (Birkle et al., 2020). Because our review focuses on DEI in work settings but not in
social, community, and educational settings, our search terms included the following two
parts: (1) diversity, equity, and/or inclusion in (2) an organizational context. We searched
articles using Boolean operators and the keywords as follows: (DEI or “Diversity, equity,
* inclusion” or Divers* or Inclusi* or Equit*) AND (“At work” or Workplace* or Corporate*
or Firm* or Compan* or Government* or Organization* or Sector* or Enterprise* or
Employee* or Worker* or Work* or Culture* or Climate*). We did not limit the time period of
articles because we wanted to see the entire publication history in the management field.
We searched articles that included the keywords in the title, abstract, and author keywords,
yielding a total of 480,282 articles. We removed articles that were in non-management fields,
non-journal articles (e.g. books, book chapters, proceedings, reviews, notes, meeting
abstracts), non-Social Sciences Citation Index (SSCI) journal articles, and not written in
English. As a result, 6,306 articles remained.
We reviewed the abstracts of the 6,306 articles and excluded irrelevant articles based on the
following exclusion criteria: (a) articles in non-peer-reviewed journals or non-management
journals; (b) articles including the keywords, “diversity,” “equity,” and/or “inclusion,” with
different meanings (e.g. diverse as a simple predicate, equity as a financial term, brand equity,
crowdfunding equity); (c) articles that examined diversity, equity, and/or inclusion in nonwork settings (e.g. K-12 or higher education, social, or community settings); (d) articles that
examined concepts of diversity, equity, and/or inclusion that were not relevant to the scope of
this study (e.g. business diversification, portfolio diversification, product diversification,
partner diversity, stakeholder diversity, economic diversity, ecosystem diversity, biodiversity);
and (e) articles that included the terms diversity, equity, and/or inclusion, but they were not the
main topic (e.g. as part of implications of research on other topics). As a result, 725 articles
were identified. The process of article selection is illustrated in Figure 1.

focused mainly on diversity, 10 articles discussed diversity along with equity, 18 articles
discussed diversity with inclusion, and two articles discussed all DEI topics together.
In addition, 30 articles focused on inclusion: 10 articles focused only on inclusion, 18 articles
focused on inclusion with the concept of diversity, and two articles focused on inclusion along
with diversity and equity. In addition, 23 articles focused on equity: 11 articles focused mainly
on equity, whereas 10 articles explored equity with diversity and two articles explored DEI
together.

management journals, which is 39.3% of all DEI articles published from 1976 to 2022.
Although the number of all publications on diversity, inclusion, and equity soared in phase 4,
78.6% of the articles focused on diversity. Of the 285 articles, 224 articles focused on diversity:
184 articles focused mainly on diversity, 26 articles discussed diversity along with inclusion,
seven articles discussed diversity with equity, and seven articles were from the perspective of
all DEI. In addition, 85 articles discussed inclusion: 51 articles only discussed inclusion, 26
articles discussed inclusion with diversity, seven articles discussed inclusion from the
perspective of DEI, and one article discussed inclusion with the concept of equity. Although
research on inclusion appeared later than research on diversity and equity, a focus on inclusion
increased greatly during phase 4. In contrast, there was relatively less research on equity than
research on diversity and inclusion. During this phase, there were 24 on equity: nine articles
focused only on equity, seven articles focused on equity along with diversity, seven articles
focused on equity from the perspective of DEI, and one article focused on equity with
inclusion.

personality diversity, contract diversity, family/marital diversity, geographical diversity,

sensitivity. In addition to the general/overview articles, eight articles examined equity/equality
from the perspective of gender, and three articles explored pay equity in relation to gender,
disabilities, and age.

Discussion
This study reviewed the overall trends in DEI research by examining the research streams,
publication profiles (number of articles and research focus), publication tendency (journals
and years), work environments as one aspect of DEI, and the dimensions of DEI. In early
studies, scholars conceptualized DEI and developed related theories to clarify the concepts and
scope and to understand different aspects of DEI (e.g. Harrison and Klein, 2007). More
recently, scholars have paid more attention to diversity and inclusive climate to highlight the
importance of a collective consensus on valuing and implementing DEI in organizations
(e.g. Mckay et al., 2011). Diversity management practices have also been discussed as an
organizational intervention to contribute to organizational outcomes and effectiveness (see
K€ollen, 2021; Nishii et al., 2018). Additionally, scholars have examined employees’
perceptions of DEI and individual factors that influence their perceptions to explore different
dynamics and interactions among the individual differences and organizational contexts
(see Guillaume et al., 2017; Leslie et al., 2020).
In terms of topics, the most frequent and dominate topics over more than 45 years have been
diversity only or diversity-related topics, rather than discussing inclusion, equity, or a
combination of these areas. More than 80% of the articles have been published in human
resource-related journals (42.5%) and management journals (41.1%). In terms of the
publication tendency, the research focus has changed over the years. A focus on equity
research has greatly diminished. Before 1994 (phase 1), there was no inclusion research, but
equity studies (related to pay, sensitivity, and perceptions) were dominant. From 1994 to 2010
(phase 2), the number of diversity studies substantially increased. In the 2010 and 2020s
(phases 3 and 4), diversity and inclusion research increased dramatically, but there has been no
increase in equity research.
Since scholars have frequently discussed different remote working settings and their
influences due to the COVID-19 pandemic, we also considered different work environments
(e.g. traditional, remote, and/or virtual work settings) as one aspect of DEI and examined the
related topics in the 725 reviewed articles. Only 1.4% (10 articles) discussed virtual work
environments or geographically remote work settings in relation to diversity and inclusion.

In light of the dimensions of DEI, the findings indicated that the research focused more on
one dimension of DEI for each study (e.g. gender, age, and race) than on integrating multiple
dimensions. Because each dimension of DEI may have different causes, influences, and
consequences in organizational contexts, it may be difficult for researchers to conduct more
integrative research on DEI dimensions. By categorizing the dimensions into multiple levels
(e.g. demographics, job-related, and deep levels), scholars may examine the dynamics of each
category first and then combine all categories of DEI dimensions to expand their findings and
perspectives on DEI. Of the 725 articles, over 300 articles were categorized in the overview/
general DEI research. This pattern seems to indicate that researchers have focused on overall
diversity management and that practical application is still lacking.
Implications for research and practice
From a scholarly perspective, our review indicated that the current literature seems to focus
mostly on the features and characteristics of DEI in the workplace. Scholars have agreed that the
complicated dynamics of DEI can lead to different outcomes in the workplace (Bernstein et al.,
2020; Li et al., 2019). Thus, our findings provide fundamental information that can lead to further
exploration of how the dimensions and components of DEI affect employee and organizational
outcomes and how DEI can be influenced by organizational contexts and climate.
Our review also shows the trajectory of DEI research expansion. The results from this
scoping review demonstrate that dimensions in DEI studies have expanded from surface-level
diversity to broader and deeper diversity since phase 3 (2011–2017). Non-observable diversity
includes job-related differences such as education, work experience, and functional
background, which diversify people’s ability to work. It also includes deep-level
differences such as attitudes, beliefs, and values that can be learned over time through
interactions with diverse groups (Horwitz and Horwitz, 2007). This classification was
constructed and developed in phase 2 (1994–2010) in team diversity studies (e.g. Milliken and
Martins, 1996; Pelled, 1996). However, studies exploring job-related and deep-level diversity
as main topics appeared in phase 3.
This topic expansion indicates that the focus of diversity research has moved from social
equity to business cases. Surface-level differences that are immediately observable have long
been studied as sources of discrimination and inequality. Thus, research and practices have
focused on psychological processes such as similarity attraction, stereotyping, and
unconscious bias or socially embedded inequality (see DiTomaso, 2021). This early
research focused extensively on legal protections and affirmative action as a strategy as well as
practices to increase demographic diversity to reach social equity following the introduction of
Title VII (Leslie et al., 2014). The focus of diversity research moved to business cases after the
Reagan administration launched the concept in the 1980s. Since then, many researchers have
explored non-observable diversity as a source of specific skills, innovation, and creativity
(Janssens and Zanoni, 2021) through conceptualization (value-in-diversity theory, Cox and
Blake, 1991; categorization-elaboration model, Van Knippenberg et al., 2004) and empirical
studies (e.g. religious diversity, Gebert et al., 2014; professional diversity, Mitchell et al.,
2015; affective diversity, Kouam�e et al., 2015).
Diversity dimensions became more diversified in phase 4 (2018–2022) including
neurodiversity (Roberson et al., 2021), employment diversity (van den Groenendaal et al.,
2023), and religious/faith diversity (Park et al., 2022). This trend indicates that human
characteristics can be a diversity dimension even though true differences may not exist. In
addition, research on interactions and dynamics between different dimensions blur the historically
strong faultlines in organizations, thus strengthening the trend (Leicht-Deobald et al., 2021).

This trend of an expansion in the DEI research is reflected in the number of articles
discussing the overall topic of equity, diversity, and inclusion. In the early phase (1976–1993),
the majority of published studies focused on equity as a research topic, but the number of
articles with this exclusive focus has decreased and research has moved from social equity to
business equity cases. Since phase 2 (1994–2010), the majority of DEI research has
investigated diversity as the main topic and explored the relationship between diversity and
organizational performance (e.g. Horwitz and Horwitz, 2007). This move to a diversityeffectiveness focus again directed researchers’ interest toward inclusion as a way to resolve the
mixed results on organizational outcomes (Nishii et al., 2018). Researchers have criticized
diversity management that only focuses on increasing demographic diversity, and they have
emphasized the importance of organizational culture and climate to maximize positive
organizational performance and outcomes among diverse groups (e.g. Holmes et al., 2021).
Given this research trajectory and the current anti-discrimination movements and social
events (e.g. Black Lives Matter, #MeToo movement, and COVID-19), a few scholars have
called for a resurgence of DEI research and practices that promote social change to address and
resolve the root causes including socially and historically embedded inequality in the
workplace (see Post et al., 2021). Our review could help researchers develop theoretical links
to DEI research. For instance, social inclusion theory can be adopted to discuss technology and
education (Baker and Sibona, 2022; Byrne, 1999), which are some of the DEI dimensions
identified in this study. The dimensions of uniqueness, belongingness, and fairness in diversity
and inclusive climate (Nishii, 2013; Shore et al., 2011) can be applied to address the multiple
characteristics of DEI (e.g. gender, age, race, and values) in work groups and organizational
contexts. These theoretical connections could help scholars develop research questions for
further research such as what theory can be adopted to discuss DEI in research and practice,
and what components could be added to inclusive/diversity climate dimensions (uniqueness,
belongingness, and fairness) in DEI research.
Our findings reveal the emerging topics and dimensions in the field. For example, since the
pandemic hit in 2020 and flexible work arrangements became the new normal, the number of
studies focusing on virtual, remote, and hybrid work settings has dramatically increased in the
management field (e.g. Klonek et al., 2022; Shockley et al., 2021; Straus et al., 2022). This
pattern of remote work is evident in DEI studies as researchers have started to discuss diversity
and inclusion in virtual and hybrid work settings (Eisenberg et al., 2021; Farh et al., 2021). In
addition, this literature review indicates that the scope of diversity has expanded from
demographic diversity to deep-level diversity such as employees’ attitudes, personalities, and
values (Triana et al., 2021). Our findings can help researchers expand their perspectives on DEI
research by examining new dimensions of diversity such as neurodiversity, contract diversity,
marital diversity, and other emerging topics in recent studies and general DEI research.
Based on the findings of this study, we suggest research questions for further investigation:
(1) What are the similarities and differences of DEI research themes and trends in management
and other fields? (2) What characteristics of work environments are needed to improve the
quality of DEI in the workplace? (3) What interventions will improve the level of diversity
management and lead to better DEI practices? (4) What individual characteristics need to be
considered to discuss deep-level diversity in the workplace? (5) What organizational and
managerial support should be provided to support employees’ new dimensions of diversity
(e.g. neurodiversity and marital diversity)? and (6) What key antecedents and outcomes of DEI
in the workplace have previous studies identified?
From a practice perspective, our findings help practitioners better understand the contexts
of DEI to improve work practices in their organizations. By reexamining their DEI-related
projects, policies, and work routines, practitioners can identify what should be discarded or
adopted to adapt to situations that need to change. By collaborating with employees,
practitioners could collect and develop multiple cases that include critical information. For
example, organizations should collaboratively identify important preconditions, antecedents,
barriers, and challenges to implement DEI initiatives and work toward better DEI outcomes.

Limitations and recommendations for future research
There are several limitations in this study. First, this study selected and reviewed 725 studies
that included the search terms in the titles, abstracts, and keywords. This approach allowed us
to review a large amount of research related to our interest, but it also hindered us from
reviewing articles that did not meet the search criteria, even if articles covered our topic of
interest. In addition, since our study conducted a scoping review of 725 articles, a detailed
analysis of the content or context (e.g. country or industry type) was not performed. Second,
we included only articles published in management-related journals indexed in the SSCI from
the Web of Science database. Thus, limiting the fields, journal indexes, and databases might
have affected our results, and some important studies on DEI might have been excluded from
our review. Third, chronological publication trends, DEI dimensions, and common themes in
the general/overview research examined in this study could be different based on each
researcher’s perspective and positioning. Lastly, although this study did not conduct a detailed
analysis of publication countries, we observed that many studies were conducted within the
contexts of specific countries (e.g. the United States), while few or no papers were from certain
regions (e.g. Southeast Asia or Africa). Since this study aims to review overall trends in DEI
studies, DEI insights from countries with many papers might be overrepresented, whereas
those from countries with few or no papers included in our review might be relatively
neglected.
In future studies, various research methods (e.g. meta-analysis, bibliometric analysis, and
content analysis) could be adopted to expand the current findings and compare them with our
results. In addition, analyzing papers published across various disciplines, including those
from other indexes and databases could provide more comprehensive insights into DEI.
Further studies could identify different antecedents and outcomes of DEI by further analyzing
empirical research. The specific context of each study could be analyzed in more detail to
understand different situations and settings related to DEI-oriented projects, policies, and
environments. By comparing the DEI findings in different fields and cultural contexts, future
studies could explore how disciplinary and cultural differences affect DEI practices.
Additionally, future research could explore emerging issues in recent studies by tracking the
origin and research streams, comparing them with previous research trends, and identifying a
more specific future DEI research agenda.
