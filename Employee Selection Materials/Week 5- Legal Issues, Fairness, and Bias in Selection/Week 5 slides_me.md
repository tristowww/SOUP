## Cause of Adverse Impact {#slide-5}

-   Predictor validity coefficients & intercorrelations : if tests are not valid predictors for all groups or predictors are too closely related (highly intercorrelated) they could cause adverse impact 
-   Subgroup differences on each predictors:  different demographic groups may perform differently on different predictors that can lead to adverse impact from inherent test biases
-   Selection ratio:  Ratio of # of job openings to # of applicants. A low selection ratio can increase adverse impact likelihood 
-  Number of minority and majority applicants:  Proportion btw groups can impact the magnitude of adverse impact
-  Nature of job performance:  How job performance is defined and measured can lead to adverse impact

## Four-Fifths Rule for Adverse Impact {#slide-7}

-  Adverse impact exists if the selection ratio for discriminated against groups is less than 4/5ths (80%) of the selection ratio for the group with the highest ratio

Example:

60 men and 40 women applied for a job at a local hospital

30 men (50%) were selected, while 10 women (25%) were selected

Adverse impact ratio:

25%/50% = 50%

Since 50% \< 80%, this selection system contains adverse impact on women

## Strategy III: Criterion Weighting {#slide-12}

-  If performance is multidimensional, weighting dimensions by organizational importance may reduce subgroup differences in predictors.

- "Technical" criterion dimensions are more ability-laden, so greater differences.
- As weight less technical dimensions of performance, adverse impact decreases because of smaller relations to cognitive ability
- Criterion weights can account for up to 34% of variance in validities

## Strategy IV: Change Test Format {#slide-13}

-  Subgroup differences result from verbal/reading component on paper and pencil tests. Change test format (minimize verbal requirements) and subgroup differences will be reduced.

- Example: Use a video-based test of interpersonal skills instead of a paper and pencil test.

<!-- -->

- Smaller adverse impact for more visual test stimuli

## Strategy V: Enhance Applicant Perceptions {#slide-14}

-  Subgroup differences result from differences in test-taking motivation, the perceived appropriateness/face validity of tests, or instruction set.

- Example: Using a more face valid test enhances test-taking motivation and reduces score differences.

<!-- -->

- Minority applicants tend to hold more negative test perceptions
- These perceptions may relate not only to test scores but also applicant withdrawal
- Social context may be de-motivating

## Strategy VI: Use Test Orientation Courses {#slide-15}

-  Subgroup differences result from unfamiliarity with the test or unequal test-taking skills. Providing test orientation or coaching sessions will equate subgroups on necessary test-taking skills.

Example: provide 1 hour information and practice session before taking test.

-  Implications:

<!-- -->

- Produces very little reduction of subgroup differences
- Both groups improve about the same
- Those who start training with highest skills benefit the most
- Good public relations value; tend to foster very favorable applicant perceptions

## Dahlke & Sackett (2021) {#slide-21}

On the assessment of predictive bias in selection systems with multiple predictors

- Key Findings:

<!-- -->

- Compared to individual tests, analyzing composite scores from  multiple predictors  provides a more accurate assessment of predictive bias (differential prediction by race/ethnicity or sex).
- Traditional methods focusing on individual tests often  miss bias hidden in the composite score.
- Meta-analysis shows  moderate differential prediction  by race/ethnicity and sex in multi-predictor selection systems
- Generalizability: Observed trends in differential prediction were consistent across samples (suggesting  real group differences  in test scores not biased tests)

## Bobko  & Roth (2013) {#slide-22}

Reviewing, categorizing, and analyzing the literature on Black-White mean differences for predictors of job performance

- Key Findings of Meta-analysis of Black-White mean differences on various predictors yielded:

<!-- -->

- Small to moderate  differences favoring whites on  cognitive ability tests
- No consistent  differences on  personality tests 
- Mixed findings  on  non-cognitive measures , with some favoring Whites and others favoring Blacks
- These differences are  small in magnitude  and should be interpreted with caution

- Contextual factors and specific job requirements  significantly influences the meaning of these findings

## Potosky  et al. (2005) {#slide-23}

Combining Cognitive Ability & Alternative Measures

- Key Findings:

<!-- -->

- Combining  cognitive ability tests  with  alternative measures  (work samples, personality tests) can improve prediction accuracy without increasing  adverse impact
- Careful selection of alternative measures  is important as some (biodata) may not add value or increase adverse impact
- Use a  methodological framework  for selecting and combining predictors based on job-relatedness and legal considerations

- The job type and applicant vs. incumbent 

<!-- -->

- Validation and fairness should be considered when implementing measures

## Arthur et al. (2010) {#slide-24}

The magnitude and extent of cheating and response distortion effects on  unproctored  internet-based tests of cognitive ability and personality

- Key Findings:

<!-- -->

- Moderate levels of  cheating (10-20%)  were detected on  unproctored  cognitive ability tests , with personality tests potentially less susceptible. 
- Cheating significantly  inflated scores , potentially leading to misidentification of top performers
- Response distortion  had less of a clear overall impact on score validity

- Proctored assessments or anti-cheating measures may help but could have diminishing returns 
- Findings may not generalize to all platforms
