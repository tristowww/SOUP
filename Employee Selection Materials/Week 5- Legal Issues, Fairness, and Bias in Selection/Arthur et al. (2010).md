The use of unproctored internet-based testing (UIT) for employee selection is quite widespread. Although this mode of testing has advantages over onsite testing, researchers and
practitioners continue to be concerned about potential malfeasance (e.g., cheating and
response distortion) under high-stakes conditions. Therefore, the primary objective of the
present study was to investigate the magnitude and extent of high- and low-stakes retest
effects on the scores of a UIT speeded cognitive ability test and two UIT personality
measures. These data permitted inferences about the magnitude and extent of malfeasant
responding. The study objectives were accomplished by implementing two within-subjects
design studies (Study 1 N ¼ 296; Study 2 N ¼ 318) in which test takers first completed the
tests as job applicants (high-stakes) or incumbents (low-stakes) then as research participants
(low-stakes). For the speeded cognitive ability measure, the pattern of test score differences
was more consonant with a psychometric practice effect than a malfeasance explanation.
This result is likely due to the speeded nature of the test. And for the UIT personality
measures, the pattern of higher high-stakes scores compared with lower low-stakes scores is
similar to those reported for proctored tests in the extant literature. Thus, our results
indicate that the use of a UIT administration does not uniquely threaten personality
measures in terms of elevated scores under high-stakes testing that are higher than those
observed for proctored tests in the extant literature.

1.1. UIT-based testing
The advantages of internet-based testing build on the
utility of computer-based testing (Baron & Austin, 2000;
McBride, 1998), and can be characterized as improving
the efficiency of delivery. Thus, like computer-based
testing, internet-based testing may ensure that every
test taker receives the same instructions, precise timing,
and accurate scoring. In addition, internet-based testing
also permits the easy incorporation of modifications and
updates into existing systems. However, one advantage of
internet-based testing over computer-based testing is its
ability to be paired with internet-based recruitment
systems allowing applicants to search and apply for jobs
from their own homes at their convenience. Thus, a
unique advantage of internet-based testing is that it may
be used as a ‘test anywhere–anytime’ method. The ‘test
anywhere–anytime’ approach has several obvious advantages which include (a) applicants’ ability to test at times
and locations of their choosing; (b) reductions in onsite
testing-related costs (e.g., floor space, staff, and production costs); (c) reductions in the time-to-hire; and (d) an
enormous potential to increase the size of applicant
pools and subsequently, improve the hiring organization’s
selection ratio (Tippins et al., 2006).
However, to capitalize on the noted advantages of
being able to test anywhere and anytime, internet-based
tests must be administered in an unproctored manner.
(The term ‘unproctored’ is used here to refer to the lack
or absence of human observation of test takers. However, we acknowledge that it may sometimes be possible
to deploy electronic and technological monitoring systems that are intended to serve as substitutes for human
observers or proctors.) Yet unproctored testing engenders disadvantages that potentially compromise the integrity of UIT test scores and hence, the resultant
concerns about this mode of testing. For instance, a
major threat resulting from the use of UIT is the potential
for high levels of construct-irrelevant variance (Messick,

1995) which may be either technologically or psychologically based. Technologically based sources of construct
irrelevant variance include the speed and performance
properties of the test taker’s computer as well as the
method of connection to the internet – all factors that
affect how fast web pages (i.e., test items) are loaded and
subsequently, the amount of time the test taker has to
complete the test (Potosky & Bobko, 2004). A related
source of construct-irrelevant variance is the speed and
capacity of the internet server on which the test resides.
The focus of the present paper is on psychologically
based sources of construct irrelevant variance – that is,
characteristics of UITs that may produce test scores that
do not accurately reflect the test taker’s standing on the
construct of interest. Hence, a major source of construct-irrelevant variance in this context is the opportunity for malfeasant behavior, which we define as
deliberately falsifying one’s responses on a test in an
attempt to distort one’s standing on the construct of
interest. Malfeasant behaviors may take one of two forms
– cheating or response distortion. In reference to cheating, which is associated with ability and knowledge tests,
test takers may use illicit aids such as calculators and
dictionaries, surrogate test takers (e.g., a smart friend),
or pre-knowledge of test items that have been obtained
(e.g., saved, printed) from the testing website. On the
other hand, response distortion, which is associated with
non-cognitive measures, refers to deliberately falsifying
one’s responses as in faking, impression management, and
other forms of socially desirable responding (Paulhus,
2002). Conceptually, the propensity to engage in malfeasant behaviors is a function of a variety of individual
differences and contextual variables. The present study
focuses on two contextual factors – the outcomes
associated with the test scores (high- vs low-stakes
testing) and the type of test administered (ability vs
personality).

1.2. Testing stakes
Test outcomes, that is high- vs low-stakes testing, may
motivate different levels of malfeasance. Specifically, highstakes testing where test scores are used for selection or
other employment-related decisions (e.g., promotions in
municipal merit-based personnel systems) would be
expected to engender higher levels of malfeasance compared with low-stakes testing where test scores are used
for research or developmental purposes. Thus, given the
valued outcomes associated with high-stakes testing and
the expectation that test takers will be motivated to
present the most favorable impression of themselves, the
implied and sometimes explicitly stated concern with the
use of UITs under these conditions is that test takers are
more likely to engage in malfeasant behaviors (Tippins,
2009). Consequently, if this premise is correct, then one
would also expect higher scores on a high-stakes UIT

1.3. Malfeasant responding on UIT ability tests
Concerns about malfeasant behavior – in the form of
cheating – on UIT ability and knowledge tests are warranted because in situations where test security is a
concern, proctoring is the primary means by which
cheating is curtailed. Although we were unable to locate
any published empirical studies on the prevalence of
cheating in employment testing (however, see Burke,
2008), the prevalence of cheating in educational settings
seems to be quite widespread. For example, reviews by
Cizek (1999) and Whitley (1998) indicate that approximately half of all college students reported cheating on an
exam at least once during their college education.
Ability (and knowledge) tests are susceptible to cheating for several reasons. First, test takers’ scores play an
important role in whether they will be hired or not.
Second, the transparency and valence of ability test items
are clear – that is, ability test items have a correct or best
answer. Unlike personality measures, the desired response is a matter of fact. Consequently, it is reasonable
to assume that cheating can and does occur in applicant
testing and that it is likely to be exacerbated with the use
of UIT because the absence of proctors creates a
permissive environment for cheating. Thus, those who
are motivated to do so may use surrogates and advisors,
and illicit reference materials, calculators, and dictionaries in an effort to increase their ability test scores. The
resultant expectation is that the presence of cheating will
result in elevated test scores.
Technological innovations for monitoring the behavior
of UIT test takers such as the use of webcams to verify
the identity of the test taker and biometric identification
systems such as fingerprint, iris, and retina scans may be
possible options to quell some of these concerns. Other
layers of security for UIT testing may include authentication via keystroke analytics, electronic monitoring and
control such as real-time data forensics, browser lockdown, keystroke monitoring, and operating system and
internet access controls (Foster, 2008; see also Burke,
2008). However, currently, these seem not to be com-

Using UIT in conjunction with onsite retesting is more
efficacious than onsite testing alone as it reduces the
number of applicants who must be tested onsite. However, potential ethical and practical concerns about onsite
retesting have been effectively commented on by Pearlman (2009). In addition, onsite retesting reduces the
efficiency and advantages of UIT, especially its cost
effectiveness. Along these lines, Tippins et al. (2006)
discuss four approaches to UIT for selection purposes
and delineate the tradeoffs associated with each approach. One approach is to use UIT to deliver feedback
to applicants regarding their likely performance on subsequent onsite testing. Two approaches require onsite
retesting – a full length retest (i.e., repeating the original
tests or taking alternate forms) or an abridged retest.
The fourth approach entails UIT testing without retesting
– an approach that is the most permissive for cheating.
The primary reason for onsite retesting of UITs is the
threat of malfeasance on the part of the test taker and
consequently, that the resulting scores do not reflect the
individual’s standing on the construct of interest. Thus,
the need for retesting individuals would be mitigated if
one used testing approaches that were less susceptible to
cheating. One such approach is to use speeded tests that
by virtue of their time constraints, have the potential to
curtail the expected malfeasant behaviors. Of course, this
is predicated on the assumption that a speeded administration is consonant with the job-relatedness of the
test. (Another caution against their use – in the United
States at least – would be in situations in which accommodations for additional time associated with the Americans with Disabilities Act must be made.) Assuming
there is no pre-knowledge of test content, possible
modes of cheating (e.g., using additional aids or helpers)
are time dependent. That is, if individuals do not have
pre-knowledge of the test content, and are not using
surrogate test takers, then the time constraints under
speeded conditions should make it more difficult to, and
subsequently deter them from engaging in many malfeasant behaviors.

scores due to prior exposure to the test. Alternatively,

1.4. Malfeasant responding on UIT personality
measures
As previously noted, the primary malfeasant behavior of
interest with personality and other non-cognitive measures is response distortion. Although there is continued
debate about the extent, magnitude, and effect of response distortion (e.g., see, Edens & Arthur, 2000;
Ellingson, Sackett, & Connelly, 2007; Hogan, Barrett, &
Hogan, 2007; Hough & Oswald, 2008; Viswesvaran &
Ones, 1999), the conceptual expectation is that malfeasant responding results in elevated scores on desirable
characteristics and depressed scores on negative ones
that are inaccurate representations of the test taker’s
standing on the personality dimensions of interest.
Although there is no clear consensus about their effectiveness and efficacy, several techniques have nevertheless been proposed for preventing or minimizing
malfeasant responding on personality and other noncognitive measures. These include the use of forcedchoice responses, empirical keying, warnings of verification, and response elaboration (e.g., see, Hough, 1998 for
a review). However, absent from this list of techniques is
the use of test proctors because the presence or absence
of test proctors has little or no bearing on controlling
response distortion. Consequently, in contrast to ability
tests, concerns regarding response distortion on personality and other non-cognitive tests are the same in both
proctored and unproctored testing conditions. In summary, if test takers are motivated to present the most
favorable impression of themselves, then one would
expect higher scores under high-stakes (applicant) than
low-stakes (incumbent/research participant) UIT testing
conditions. If this is the case, then Time 1 (high-stakes)
scores should be higher than Time 2 (low-stakes) scores.
So in the context of the present study, a larger HL
difference compared with the LL difference would be
suggestive of a malfeasant effect.
Consistent with the extant literature (e.g., Birkeland,
Manson, Kisamore, Brannick, & Smith, 2006), we expected a larger HL retest difference compared with the
LL retest difference for the UIT personality measures.
However, because proctoring is not a means of controlling for malfeasant responding on non-cognitive mea-

In summary, using data from two independent studies,
the study had the following objectives:
1. To investigate whether observed HL-stakes and LLstakes retest score differences on a speeded UIT

first (high-stakes) administration of the test.
2. To investigate the magnitude and extent of HL-stakes
and LL-stakes retest score differences on UIT personality measures and assess the proportion of the test

2. Study 1
To accomplish the objectives of Study 1, we used a
within-subjects design in which 296 test takers first
completed a UIT assessment that included a speeded
cognitive ability test and an untimed personality measure
as job applicants (high-stakes) or incumbents (lowstakes), and later completed the same tests a second
time under the same conditions but as research participants (low-stakes).

2.1.3. Procedure
For both test administrations, participants completed the
cognitive ability and personality tests under UIT conditions. That is, they logged on to the internet test site
whenever they wanted, from any computer, and from any
location of their choosing. As previously noted, test takers
who had previously completed the tests either as applicants (Time 1, high-stakes) or incumbents who were
requested by their company to complete the measure
to generate a profile (Time 1, low-stakes) were invited via
email to retake the tests as research participants (Time 2,
low-stakes). To be invited to participate in the study,
participants must have completed testing at least 1 year
before the second administration. Because the Time 2
assessment was for research purposes and all participants
were informed of such when they were recruited, the
Time 2 assessment was considered to be low-stakes. This
allowed us to create two groups of participants. Applicants at Time 1 were considered to have experienced
high-stakes testing and low-stakes retesting (i.e., HLstakes, n ¼ 252) and incumbents at Time 1 were posited
to have experienced low-stakes during both testing and
retesting (i.e., LL-stakes, n ¼ 44). Consequently, the LLstakes group was considered to have the least incentive to
engage in malfeasant behaviors on the Time 1 assessment.

3. Study 2
Study 2 was undertaken as a constructive replication and
extension of Study 1. Thus, its objectives were similar to
those of Study 1 with the following differences. First, data
were available for only a personality test. In addition, this
personality test was different from the one used in Study
1. Consequently, an objective of Study 2 was to investigate whether the personality measure results that were
obtained for Study 1 would be replicated with a different
personality measure. Second, data for a falsification
scale were available for the Study 2 sample. So, a second
objective of Study 2 was to empirically assess the
relationship between Time 1 (high-stakes) and Time 2
(low-stakes) score changes and the falsification scale
scores with the goal of investigating the extent to which

(Hogan et al., 2007) operationalized as scores on the
falsification scale.
As with Study 1, we used a within-subjects design in
which data were obtained from 318 participants who first
completed a UIT assessment that included the personality
test as job applicants (high-stakes), and then as research
participants (low-stakes). Thus, like Study 1, there was a
HL-stakes group but unlike Study 1, there was no LLstakes group.

3.1. Method
3.1.1. Participants
The study sample consisted of 318 individuals who had
previously completed the operational UIT assessment as
job applicants and had agreed to participate in the study
by completing the test a second time (also using a UIT
administration). The mean age of the participants at Time
1 was 35.38 (SD ¼ 10.85). One hundred and fifty-six
participants were women and 158 were men. Four
participants did not report their sex. There were 222
Caucasians, 17 Asians, 37 African Americans, 26
Hispanics, and one American Indian. Ten participants
reported their race as ‘other,’ and five did not report
their race. Participants were compensated with either
US$10 (n ¼ 225) or US$20 (n ¼ 93) for their Time 2
participation.
The average Time 1/Time 2 retest interval was 306.98
days (SD ¼ 35.84, median ¼ 305.00). The differences between Time 1 and Time 2 scores were not related to the

4.1. Limitations, implications, and directions for
future research
The low response rate and the self-selective nature of the
study samples are recognized as a potential methodological threats. For instance, one could reasonably speculate that compared with those who did not engage in
malfeasant behaviors in the first administration, individuals who did would be less likely to volunteer to
participate in the second administration. However, the
general absence of differences between participants and
non-participants (see Table 1) mitigates this threat.
Indeed the only pronounced difference was on cognitive

ability (and to a lesser extent openness) where the
participants had higher scores than the non-participants.
In spite of this, the postulated effects were obtained not
only for cognitive ability, but also for the FFM dimensions
as well. Furthermore, in the context of applied research
in this domain, our response rate is not atypical (e.g., see
also Nye et al., 2008). Nevertheless, we recognize the
legitimacy of these potential threats and our results must
be interpreted within this context.
Another limitation is that the absence of criterion data
did not allow us to assess the comparative criterionrelated validity of the tests under the different testing
conditions. The extant empirical literature does not
address the effects of malfeasance on the criterionrelated validity of ability tests, however, the effects of
response distortion on the criterion-related validity of
personality measures has received some attention in the
extant literature (see Hough, Eaton, Dunnette, Kamp, &
McCloy, 1990; Ones, Viswesvaran, & Reiss, 1996). On the
basis of this research, it would seem that although the
true effect of response distortion on the criterionrelated validity of personality tests remains unclear
(Birkeland et al., 2006; Hough, 1998; Hough et al., 1990;
Ones et al., 1996), the impact appears to be low (Schmitt
& Oswald, 2006). Nevertheless, the availability of criterion data would have permitted a further assessment of
the effects of unproctored high-stakes cognitive ability
and personality testing.
Finally, the data presented here do not directly assess
malfeasance or the lack thereof. For instance, given the
elusive nature of cheating, it is difficult to directly
measure this behavior (i.e., we did not actually see the
participants cheating nor do we have any direct evidence
that they did). In lieu of this, our focus was on changing
the psychological conditions (i.e., perceived stakes), instead of the administration conditions (i.e., proctored vs
unproctored testing). In addition, we used a test–retest
design that did not suffer from the common limitation of
using incumbent-only data; our design had both applicant
and incumbent data in a within-subjects design. It is
reasonable to expect less malfeasance in low- compared
with high-stakes testing conditions. Therefore, changes in
test scores across these conditions were used to make
inferences about the levels of cheating and response
distortion. Consequently, for the cognitive ability test
there was no evidence to indicate that wide-scale or
systematic malfeasance unduly affected the test scores or
their psychometric properties. The data presented here
display satisfactory repeated-measures psychometric
properties. Therefore, our results do not support the
notion of high-stakes wide-scale malfeasance on the
speeded ability test used here.

distortion on the personality measures were made on the

4.2. Conclusions
The efficacy of UIT employment testing is threatened by
the possible influence of malfeasance on the part of
applicants. However, we provide evidence that even
under conditions where it is very intuitive to expect
widespread malfeasance (i.e., high-stakes unproctored
ability testing), there was no discernable effect on test
scores when the testing condition was designed to
counter such behavior. UIT using a speeded test format
appears to have reduced the opportunity for and thus the
prevalence of malfeasant behavior. So where appropriate,
the use of speeded UIT ability tests may be an additional
option or alternative to onsite retesting of UITs. Similarly,
our results suggest that using a UIT administration does
not uniquely threaten personality measures in terms of
elevated levels of response distortion that are higher than
those observed for proctored tests. Specifically, the
pattern of high- vs low-stakes retest effects observed
for our UIT personality measures were similar to those
observed for proctored personality measures reported
in the extant literature.
