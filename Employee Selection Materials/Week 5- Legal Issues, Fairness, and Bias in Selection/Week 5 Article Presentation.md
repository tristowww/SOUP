## The Magnitude and Extent of Cheating and Response Distortion Effects on  Unproctored  Internet-Based Tests of Cognitive Ability and Personality {#slide-2}

Winfred Arthur, Jr., Ryan M. Glaze,

Anton J.  Villado  and Jason E. Taylor

## Summary {#slide-3}

Background: Researchers continue to be concerned about cheating (malfeasance) on un-proctored internet-based tests (UIT's) used for selection

The researchers examined the extent and magnitude of retest effects for high-stakes and low-stakes testing scenarios for a cognitive ability test and two personality measures (UIT's)

Participants who had already taken each test (as either applicants or incumbents) were tested again as research participants

- Applicant   Participant = HL (High stakes to low stakes)
- Incumbent  Participant = LL (Low stakes to low stakes)

## Summary {#slide-4}

Two studies

Study 1  contained both HL and LL participants and included a cognitive ability test and personality measure

Study 2  contained only HL participants and included a different personality measure, but no cognitive ability test

Results

For the cognitive test, the test score differences between Time 1 and 2 were indicative of a psychometric effect, not malfeasance

For the personality measures, the differences in scores between the HL and LL groups were indicative of some malfeasance, but were similar to results from proctored tests

## Reviewing, Categorizing, and Analyzing the Literature on Black-White Mean Differences for Predictors of Job Performance: Verifying Some Perceptions and Updating/Correcting Others  {#slide-5}

Philip  Bobko  and Philip L. Roth

## Summary {#slide-6}

Background: In the research literature, there is a consistent finding that there are score differences in selection measures between Black and White applicants that result in adverse impact 

Focuses on methodological problems in these findings (mainly the use of incumbent samples instead of applicant samples)

Offers corrected measures of the differences between the groups (using the statistic  d , the standardized subgroup difference

This was done by calculating  d  using applicant samples instead of incumbent samples (incumbent samples likely cause  d  to be biased downward)

## Summary {#slide-7}

Results: larger  d 's for Work Samples, Biodata, Assessment Centers, Personality Tests, and Structured Interviews. Lower  d 's for Cognitive Tests

Authors emphasize that using applicant samples instead of incumbents often reveals larger  d 's than previously thought

## On the Assessment of Predictive Bias in Selection Systems With Multiple Predictors  {#slide-8}

Jefferey A. Dahlke and Paul R. Sackett

## Summary {#slide-9}

Background: There has been a consistent finding that selection systems (in academic settings) often overpredict or underpredict scores of different groups (differential prediction)

The authors propose that using composite predictors in selection systems will improve this issue

Composite Predictors:  each predictor variable is formed into a single composite, then tested for bias

Follows SIOP guidelines

Different from testing each predictor one at a time or testing each predictor all at once

Authors also utilized a modified  d  statistic which allowed them to meta-analyze effects from studies that used different instrumentation to measure the same constructs (standardized)

## Summary {#slide-10}

3 Studies

Study 1 : Reanalyzed data from a study that supported the use of composite predictors to demonstrate how they can nullify flawed conclusions

ACT Scores and Personality

Study 2 : Analyzed an existing dataset using predictor composites to demonstrate validity

Study 3:   Examined the differential prediction of both individual predictors and composite predictors 

Results: Composite predictors change the magnitude of differential prediction from using individual predictors, often decreasing  d . They also improved the accuracy of conclusions about differential prediction. The authors also found evidence that indicated that the historical overprediction of Black and Hispanic test scores are generalizable across schools. 

## Forming Composites of Cognitive Ability and Alternative Measures to Predict Job Performance and Reduce Adverse Impact: Corrected Estimates and Realistic Expectations  {#slide-11}

Denise  Potosky , Philip  Bobko  and Philip L. Roth

## Summary {#slide-12}

Background: Though composite predictors are generally considered to reduce adverse impact for cognitive ability tests used for selection, much of the prior research lacks validity and standardized difference values ( d). 

The researchers examined the impact of a conscientious measure, a structured interview, and biodata on the validity and standardized ethnic group differences of a cognitive ability test.

Study 1 : Effect of adding conscientiousness measure

Study 2 : Effect of adding a structured interview

Study 3 : Effect of adding biodata

## Summary {#slide-13}

Results:

Adding a conscientiousness measure to form a predictor composite resulted in a 5% reduction in  d

Adding a structured interview to form a predictor composite resulted in a 10% reduction of  d

Adding biodata to form a predictor composite resulted in an 8% increase in  d

Overall, the researchers concluded that adding other predictors is not an automatic "win-win", and that adverse impact was not significantly reduced
