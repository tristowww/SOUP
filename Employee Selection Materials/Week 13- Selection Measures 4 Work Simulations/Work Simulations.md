## A Review of the Content, Criterion-Related, and Construct-Related Validity of Assessment Center Exercises  {#slide-2}

![List](ppt/media/image2.png "Graphic 6")

Hoffman et al. (2015)

## Background {#slide-3}

This study uses meta-analysis and a qualitative review of exercise descriptions to evaluate the content, criterion-related, construct and incremental validity of 5 commonly used types of assessment center (AC) exercises

## Assessment Centers  {#slide-4}

The observation of assessing behavior in moderate- to high fidelity behavioral simulations

Most ACs are targeted towards managers and are designed to simulate the manager role

The text addresses a gap in research regarding the validity of AC exercises despite a focus on the \"exercise effect"

E mphasizes the importance of determining whether AC exercises predict job performance better than established predictors like General Mental Ability (GMA) and the Five-Factor Model (FFM)

## Nomological Network of Exercises   {#slide-5}

Definition

"I s  a representation of the concepts (constructs) of interest in a study, their observable manifestations, and the interrelationships between these "

Study aims to clarify interpretation of Assessment Center (AC) exercise scores, focusing on the relationship between exercise dimensions and psychological constructs like General Mental Ability (GMA) and the Five-Factor Model (FFM).

- It suggests that AC exercises may activate traits related to GMA, interpersonal skills (\"getting along\"), and achievement orientation (\"getting ahead\").

## Taxonomy of AC Center Exercise Characteristic    {#slide-6}

Proposes taxonomy of five key AC exercise characteristics: complexity, structure, interpersonal interaction, interdependence, and fidelity.

Complexity 

M anifests in tasks requiring problem-solving, information integration, and technical proficiency, notably seen in exercises featuring extensive information, tables, and data integration from diverse sources

Structure 

Managerial work, known for its ambiguity, suggests that assessment center (AC) tasks are generally unstructured, allowing for varied responses

Interpersonal interaction

Assessment centers (ACs) simulate social contexts, involving interactions with others

Interdependence 

E ntails reciprocal roles and collective responsibility

Fidelity

D egree of similarity between the simulated situation and that of the focal context, realism 

## Five AC Exercise Types {#slide-7}

In-Basket Exercises 

Simulates "the paperwork that arrives in the mailbox or on the desk of the typical manager"

Assessee is presented with a packet of information that read, prioritize, and address 

Leaderless Group Discussion 

Participants are given problems to resolve in a set amount of time 

Work in an unstructured group 

Role-Play 

Have one-on-one conversation with a role player who often plays a customer or employee, and the assessee conducts a meeting to resolve the issue given to them 

Case Analysis and Oral Presentation (#4 and #5)

For both, assessee reads written material (like a case study) and must prepare a set of recommendations 

In a CA they present it in written form and in an OP, they present it orally

## Main Findings  {#slide-9}

The findings indicate that, although exercise scores might be used as a supplement to more traditional dimension-based scores, replacing dimensions with exercises completely would be a mistake

They found weaker-levels of observed criterion-related validity, but using exercises rather than dimensions resulted in almost a 50% reduction of criterion-related validity

GMA was related to each of the five types of AC exercises, especially in-basket exercises 

These results provide support for the differential nomological network of the exercise types 

Trends showed high levels of fidelity, complexity, and ambiguity across exercise types

## Optimizing the Validity of Situational Judgement Tests: The Importance of Scoring Methods {#slide-10}

Weng et al. (2018)

## Background  {#slide-11}

This study investigates the influence of scoring methods on the criterion-related validity of situational judgment tests (STJs)

They examined five different consensus scoring methods and several integrated scoring methods for scoring the same STJs

Raw, standardized, dichotomous, mode, proportion 

## Consensus Scoring Methods  {#slide-12}

::: {.smartart .vList5 layout="vList5"}
**Proportion Consensus **

- A rating is scored by the proportion of respondents who have the same rating 

**Dichotomous Consensus  **

- The method employs the raw item mean across respondents as a scoring key to determine whether a respondent\'s indication of an item\'s correctness aligns with the group mean, awarding a score accordingly

**Mode Consensus **

- The mode is judges as correct. If a respondents s score is consistent with the mode, they get a 1. If its not, they get a 0

**Raw Consensus**

- Scoring process that involves computing a respondent\'s score on each item by inversely squaring the deviation between their rating and the item\'s scoring key, which is then aggregated to produce the scale score

**Standardized Consensus**

- Respondents\' ratings for all items are converted to z-scores, and their scores on individual items and the overall scale are determined using a raw consensus approach based on these z-scores
:::

## Hypotheses  {#slide-13}

::: {.smartart .vList2 layout="vList2"}
**Hypothesis 2**

- Compared to the raw consensus method, using standardized, dichotomous, mode, and proportion methods for an STJ will yield higher criterion-related validity at both the item and scale level 

**Hypothesis 1**

- Compared to the raw consensus method, using standardized, dichotomous, mode, and proportion methods for STJs will result in weaker relations between extreme response tendencies and overall STJ scores

**Hypothesis 3**

- Scoring midrange items using the mode and proportion consensus scoring methods will yield higher item criterion related validity than the raw consensus method will
:::

## Results {#slide-14}

Hypothesis 1  was the only one supported

Alternative distance consensus scoring methods (standard, dichotomous, mode, and proportion) are less prone to the extreme scoring tendency than popular raw distance consensus method 

Results suggest that integrating suitable scoring methods according to item characteristics could reduce the weakness of any single methods 

The f i ndings o ff er meaningful theoretical and practical implications for understanding the e ff ectiveness of scoring methods of SJTs as key assessment instruments in the context of  career decision making and counseling 

## Assessment Centers Versus Cognitive Ability Tests: Challenging the Conventional Wisdom on Criterion-Related Validity {#slide-15}

Sackett et al. (2017)

## Background  {#slide-16}

- Revisiting validity of general mental ability tests and assessment centers (ACs)
- Aim is to uncover potential differences in validity between general mental ability tests and assessment centers

The study compared Assessment Center (AC) overall ratings and cognitive ability tests directly to evaluate their predictive power and explored potential reasons for any differences in validity.

- 

![Head with Gears](ppt/media/image5.png "Graphic 6")

## Information on Past Research  {#slide-17}

- Assessment centers evaluate a broader range of traits like influence, consideration, drive, organization, and communication compared to cognitive ability tests, which focus more on task proficiency
- When comparing the accuracy of ability tests and ACs, adjustments need to be made for differences in applicant populations, particularly the narrower variance in cognitive ability often found in AC candidate pools

## Hypotheses {#slide-18}

Hypothesis 1:  If ACs and ability tests are used with the same samples to predict the broad criteria that ACs are intended to predict, ability tests will show lower criterion-related validity than overall ability meta-analyses indicate, and the validity advantage of ability tests over ACs will disappear.

## Results  {#slide-19}

The  study\'s findings  sharply contrast  with prior meta-analytic conclusions 

Schmidt and Hunter (1998) previously reported mean criterion-related validity of .51 for ability tests and .37 for ACs

This  study reveals a corrected mean validity of .22 for ability tests and .44 for ACs

Differences in findings are primarily attributed to variations in how ability tests function in current research compared to prior studies

In 15 out of 17 direct comparisons, Assessment Centers (ACs) demonstrate higher criterion-related validity than ability tests, suggesting potential superiority

## Background {#slide-22}

Situational judgment tests (SJTs) are commonly used in employee selection, assessing relevant knowledge, skills, abilities, or other characteristics through scenarios

Despite their widespread use, there\'s a lack of focus in the literature on the specific constructs measured by SJTs, highlighting the need for a more theoretically driven construct-level framework in research

## Discussion {#slide-24}

Failure to distinguish between method (SJTs) and constructs limits understanding and application of SJTs in selection contexts

SJTs frequently assess leadership and interpersonal skills due to their effectiveness in sampling complex interpersonal and administrative behaviors

Construct-based analysis reveals that teamwork, leadership, and interpersonal skills show higher validities for job performance compared to conscientiousness and job knowledge

Content-based matching of predictor and criterion constructs strengthens criterion-related validity of SJTs, but some results may be unstable due to small sample sizes

Method characteristics, such as SJT format, moderate criterion-related validities, indicating the importance of considering both method and construct characteristics in SJT research

## Where to go from here? {#slide-25}

Researchers should provide detailed construct information for SJTs to enhance comparability and validity evidence

Construct validation procedures should be refined to establish convergent and discriminant validity, improving confidence in measured constructs

Utilizing SJT construct information can help identify methodological features impacting outcomes, optimizing measurement approaches

Understanding how predictor methods influence measured constructs can help identify boundary conditions for SJTs

Practitioners should select SJTs based on job analysis and consider construct-criterion match to maximize predictive power in selection contexts
