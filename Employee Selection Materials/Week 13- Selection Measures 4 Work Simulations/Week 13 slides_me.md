## Types of Questions with Definitions {#slide-26}

Situational Judgement Tests

SJT questions present realistic, hypothetical scenarios and ask candidates to choose or rate the best response from a set of options. These questions evaluate a candidate\'s judgment, problem-solving, and decision-making skills.

Behavioral (STAR- Situation, Task, Action, Result)

Behavioral questions are grounded in the belief that past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. These questions often use the STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) format to elicit comprehensive responses.

Historical

Behavioral questions are grounded in the belief that past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. These questions often use the STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) format to elicit comprehensive responses.

Future-Oriented Scenarios

These questions are designed to assess a candidate\'s problem-solving and decision-making skills by placing them in hypothetical situations they might face on the job.

Cultural

Cultural questions assess how a candidate\'s values, behaviors, and interests align with the company\'s culture. They help determine what unique qualities a candidate might bring to the team.

## Sackett et al.,(2017) {#slide-30}

Assessment centers versus cognitive ability tests: Challenging the conventional wisdom on criterion-related validity

-   Challenging Conventional Wisdom : The study challenges the prevailing belief that cognitive ability tests are superior to ACs in predicting job performance by showing that ACs have higher criterion-related validity in direct comparisons.
-  Head-to-Head Comparison Approach : By comparing ACs and cognitive ability tests head-to-head in the same samples and using the same performance criteria, the study controls for potential confounding factors that could influence validity estimates.
-  Methodological Considerations : The research highlights the importance of methodological considerations in validation research, such as controlling for range restriction and ensuring that the same criteria are used to assess the validity of different predictors.
-  Broad versus Narrow Criteria : The authors suggest that cognitive ability tests are often validated against narrower, more cognitively loaded criteria, while ACs are used to predict a broader range of job performance criteria, which may contribute to differences in observed validities.
-  Sample and Setting Differences : The study acknowledges that differences in samples (e.g., applicant pools already restricted on cognitive ability) and settings (e.g., use of less cognitively loaded criteria in AC validation research) might contribute to the observed differences in validity between cognitive ability tests and ACs.

## Hoffman et al., (2015) {#slide-31}

A  review of the content, criterion-related, and construct-related validity of assessment center exercises

Criterion-Related Validity : The study finds that all five types of AC exercises show significant relationships with criterion variables (ranging from r = .16 to .19), suggesting moderate criterion-related validity. This indicates that these exercises can be useful predictors of job performance.

Nomological Network Analysis : The exercises are modestly associated with General Mental Ability (GMA), Extraversion, and to a lesser extent, Openness to Experience. However, they are largely unrelated to Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, and Emotional Stability. This suggests that while AC exercises can capture certain aspects of intelligence and personality, they may not comprehensively assess all key personality traits relevant to job performance.

Construct Validity : The meta-analysis shows that while AC exercises have some validity in predicting job performance, there\'s room for improvement in understanding and defining the constructs they are supposed to measure. This highlights the need for clearer construct definitions in AC exercises to enhance their predictive validity.

Complexity and Interpersonal Interaction : The content analysis of exercise descriptions provided some evidence of complexity, ambiguity, interpersonal interaction, and fidelity in the exercises, but not necessarily interdependence. This suggests that AC exercises are designed to simulate complex job scenarios requiring interpersonal skills, though they may not always require or simulate teamwork or interdependent tasks.

Recommendations for Future Research : The authors suggest a more construct-focused approach to AC research, advocating for explicit definitions of the constructs measured by AC exercises. This could help clarify their psychometric properties and improve their criterion-related validity.

Video-Based Exercises : The study implies that incorporating video-based elements into AC exercises might enhance their criterion-related validity by better simulating job-related scenarios, although this aspect was not directly analyzed in the study.

## Christian et al., (2010) {#slide-32}

Situational judgement tests: Constructs assessed and a meta-analysis of their criterion-related validities

- The article highlights a significant gap in SJT research, where many studies fail to report the specific constructs being measured by the SJTs, leading to a lack of construct-level focus in the literature.
- The authors argue for a more construct-focused approach in SJT research, emphasizing the importance of understanding the specific constructs measured by an SJT for interpreting its psychometric properties, such as reliability, validity, and subgroup differences.
- Through their research, the authors develop a content-based typology of the constructs assessed by SJTs, finding that leadership and interpersonal skills are the most commonly assessed constructs.
- Their meta-analysis reveals that SJTs measuring teamwork and leadership skills are particularly valid for predicting overall job performance, suggesting these constructs are critical for effective job performance.
- The study suggests that the criterion-related validity of SJTs can be improved by aligning the constructs measured by the SJT with the relevant facets of job performance, thus making a strong case for the construct-based matching approach.
- The authors find evidence that video-based SJTs tend to have stronger criterion-related validity compared to traditional paper-and-pencil SJTs. This might be due to video-based SJTs\' ability to more realistically reproduce job-related scenarios and context, thus providing a more accurate assessment of job-relevant constructs.
- The research calls for more detailed reporting of the constructs assessed by SJTs in future studies, to enhance the clarity and applicability of SJT research in employee selection and assessment practices.

## Weng et al., (2018) {#slide-33}

Optimizing the validity of situational judgment tests: The importance of scoring methods

 Extreme Response Tendency : Raw consensus scoring, a commonly used approach, is associated with an extreme response tendency, leading to lower scale validity compared to other scoring methods. This tendency adversely impacts the scores by favoring those who avoid extreme responses on the Likert scale, which could introduce bias into the assessment.

 Scoring Method Impact : Different scoring methods have varying effects on the criterion-related validity of SJTs. The study finds that integrated scoring methods, which combine different scoring strategies based on item characteristics, yield higher mean item validities compared to using a single scoring method.

 Item Characteristics and Scoring : The mode consensus method is particularly effective for scoring midrange items (items where the mean response is near the midpoint of the Likert scale), improving their item validity compared to raw consensus scoring. This suggests that the choice of scoring method can significantly impact the interpretability and fairness of SJT scores.

 Implications  for Practice : The findings suggest that selecting an appropriate scoring method for SJTs is crucial for enhancing their validity and reducing potential biases. Specifically, scoring methods that mitigate the influence of extreme response tendencies and consider item characteristics can improve the accuracy and fairness of SJT assessments.

Realism in Simulations : What are the trade-offs between high-fidelity and low-fidelity simulations in terms of cost, candidate experience, and predictive validity?

Diversity and Fairness : In what ways can work simulations be designed to ensure fairness and reduce bias across candidates from diverse backgrounds?

Assessment Center Design : How has the historical use of Assessment Centers during World War II by the OSS influenced contemporary practices in employee selection?

Group Dynamics : How might group-based assessments in an Assessment Center impact individual performance, and what steps can be taken to mitigate any negative effects?

KSAOs : Discuss the challenges of ensuring that work simulations accurately assess the KSAOs necessary for the job. How can we determine which KSAOs are most critical to simulate?

10,000-Hour Rule : How might the concept of the 10,000-hour rule to expertise be applicable or inapplicable in the context of selection through work simulations?

Assessor Training : What kind of training should assessors undergo to effectively evaluate candidates in a work simulation?

Candidate Feedback : Should candidates be given feedback on their performance in work simulations, and if so, how can this feedback be structured to be constructive?

Innovation in Assessment : With the advancement of technology, such as virtual reality, how might work simulations evolve, and what new opportunities and challenges could this present?

Assessment Center Outcomes : How do the results of Assessment Center evaluations correlate with long-term job performance and career progression?

Preparation for Simulations : To what extent should candidates be allowed to prepare for work simulations, and how might this influence the validity of the assessment?

Cost-Benefit Analysis : Discuss the cost-benefit analysis of implementing work simulations in the selection process, particularly for small to medium-sized enterprises.

Ethical Considerations : What ethical considerations arise in the use of work simulations, especially when they involve sensitive or high-pressure scenarios?

Cultural Relevance : How can work simulations be adapted to maintain cultural relevance and sensitivity across global operations?

Integration with Other Selection Methods : How should work simulations be integrated with other selection methods to create a comprehensive assessment framework?
