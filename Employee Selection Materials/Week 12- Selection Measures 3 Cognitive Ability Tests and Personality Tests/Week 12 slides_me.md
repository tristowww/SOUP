## Criticisms of Personality Tests {#slide-28}

::: {.smartart .hList1 layout="hList1"}
**Negative applicant reactions​**

- Applicants, in general, believe personality tests are less valid predictors of job performance.​

**Trivial validities​**

- Correlations for any individual trait with job performance are typically low (around r = .23).​
- However, when all traits are used simultaneously, correlations are higher.​

**Faking​**

- However, tests still have some validity, and it may be that being able to "act" conscientiously may be related to real job performance.​
- Individuals answer in a dishonest way.​
:::

## Types of Questions with Definitions {#slide-34}

Situational Judgement Tests

SJT questions present realistic, hypothetical scenarios and ask candidates to choose or rate the best response from a set of options. These questions evaluate a candidate\'s judgment, problem-solving, and decision-making skills.

Behavioral (STAR- Situation, Task, Action, Result)

Behavioral questions are grounded in the belief that past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. These questions often use the STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) format to elicit comprehensive responses.

Historical

Behavioral questions are grounded in the belief that past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. These questions often use the STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) format to elicit comprehensive responses.

Future-Oriented Scenarios

These questions are designed to assess a candidate\'s problem-solving and decision-making skills by placing them in hypothetical situations they might face on the job.

Cultural

Cultural questions assess how a candidate\'s values, behaviors, and interests align with the company\'s culture. They help determine what unique qualities a candidate might bring to the team.

## Schmidt & Hunter (2004) {#slide-38}

General mental ability in the world of work: Occupational Attainment and job performance

-  Focus:  This influential study by Schmidt & Hunter (2004) establishes  General Mental Ability (GMA)  as a central player in employee selection. GMA is not a single ability, but rather a constellation of cognitive skills that underlie effective job performance. These skills include:
- Reasoning:  The ability to analyze information, identify patterns, and draw sound conclusions.
- Problem-solving:  The capacity to effectively define problems, generate potential solutions, and evaluate their feasibility.
- Learning Aptitude:  The ease and speed with which new knowledge and skills can be acquired.
- Information Processing Speed:  The ability to rapidly perceive, analyze, and respond to information.

38

## Schmidt & Hunter (2004) {#slide-39}

Key Takeaway:  The research by Schmidt & Hunter delivers a powerful message: GMA, measured through well-designed cognitive ability tests, consistently surpasses other factors like experience and specific personality traits in predicting how well someone will perform in a job across diverse occupations. Individuals with higher GMA scores are demonstrably more likely to succeed in various job roles, from understanding complex legal documents for a paralegal position to troubleshooting technical problems for a software engineer.

## Gonzales- Mulé   et al., (2014) {#slide-40}

A meta-analysis of the relationship between general mental ability and  nontask  performance

- This meta-analysis by Gonzales- Mulé  et al. (2014) expands the conversation beyond core job tasks. They investigate the relationship between GMA and  non-task performance .
- Non-Task Performance Defined:  This encompasses behaviors that contribute positively to the workplace environment but are not directly tied to core job duties. Examples include:

<!-- -->

- Organizational Citizenship:  Going the extra mile to help colleagues, volunteering for additional tasks, or taking initiative to improve processes.
- Safety Compliance:  Following safety protocols and regulations to prevent accidents and maintain a safe work environment.
- Adaptability:  The ability to adjust to changing demands, new technologies, or unexpected situations with flexibility and a positive attitude.

## Gonzales- Mulé   et al., (2014) {#slide-41}

Key Finding:  The research confirms that GMA is not just about completing tasks effectively. Employees with higher GMA are more likely to exhibit these desirable non-task behaviors as well. This suggests that GMA can be a valuable indicator of an employee\'s overall contribution to the organization\'s success, not just in terms of meeting performance targets, but also in fostering a positive and collaborative work environment.

## Barrick & Mount (1991) {#slide-42}

The big five personality dimensions and job performance: A meta-analysis

-  Shifting the Lens:  This article by Barrick & Mount (1991) explores the impact of the widely studied  Big Five personality traits  (OCEAN model) on job performance. These traits are:

<!-- -->

- Openness to experience:  The tendency to be curious, creative, and open to new ideas.
- Conscientiousness:  The degree of organization, discipline, and reliability.
- Extraversion:  The level of sociability, assertiveness, and outgoingness.
- Agreeableness:  The inclination to be cooperative, helpful, and trusting.
- Neuroticism:  The tendency to experience negative emotions like anxiety, anger, and moodiness.

42

## Barrick & Mount (1991) {#slide-43}

Findings:  The research reveals a positive, but weaker relationship between the Big Five traits and job performance compared to GMA. Specific traits, like Conscientiousness, show a somewhat stronger correlation with performance in certain job contexts (e.g., higher Conscientiousness might be more important for a project manager role). However, GMA remains a more consistent predictor across a broader range of occupations.

## Morgeson  et al., (2007) {#slide-45}

-  Points to Consider:  The authors highlight potential issues associated with personality testing, such as:
- Social Desirability Bias:  Candidates may try to present themselves in a more favorable light by answering in a way they believe the employer wants, leading to inaccurate results.

<!-- -->

- Limited Legal Defensibility:  Some personality tests lack strong evidence for their job-relatedness, which can raise legal concerns during the selection process. If a candidate is not selected and believes the personality test played a role, they could potentially challenge the selection process based on discrimination if the test is not demonstrably linked to the specific job requirements.
- Potential Bias:  Certain personality tests might inadvertently disadvantage candidates based on factors like cultural background or personality type. For example, a test that emphasizes extroversion might unfairly disadvantage someone from a culture that values introspective thinking.
- Alternative:  The article by  Morgeson  et al. (2007) suggests focusing on well-designed GMA assessments as a more reliable, legally defensible, and consistent predictor of job performance across various jobs. GMA tests can be tailored to assess the specific cognitive abilities required for successful performance in a particular role.

45

## Morgeson  et al., (2007) {#slide-46}

Overall Insights:

These articles paint a clear picture: GMA emerges as a strong and consistent predictor of both task-related and non-task job performance across various occupations. While personality traits like the Big Five can offer some insights, their impact is less pronounced and their use in selection requires careful consideration due to potential limitations and legal concerns.

The takeaway?  GMA remains a valuable tool for identifying candidates with the cognitive abilities necessary to succeed in various job roles. However, a comprehensive selection process should likely go beyond just cognitive ability testing and consider other factors relevant to the specific job requirements and company culture. This might include assessing a candidate\'s skills, experience, and fit with the team through interviews, work samples, or reference checks.

## Article discussion questions and Takeaways {#slide-47}

Discussion Questions: Unveiling the Best Predictors of Job Performance

The articles we reviewed explored the effectiveness of cognitive ability and personality tests in employee selection. Here are some questions to consider:

GMA and Job Performance:

- Strength in Numbers:  Schmidt & Hunter (2004) highlight the importance of GMA. Do you agree that GMA is the single strongest predictor of job performance across all occupations? Can you think of any exceptions where other factors might be more important?
- Beyond Tasks:  Gonzales- Mulé  et al. (2014) demonstrate a link between GMA and non-task performance. How can organizations leverage this knowledge to build a well-rounded workforce? What are some specific non-task behaviors that GMA might predict?
- The Ethical Conundrum:  GMA testing can be expensive and may disadvantage individuals from certain socioeconomic backgrounds. How can organizations ensure fair and ethical use of GMA assessments in the selection process?

Personality Traits and Selection:

- The Big Five and Beyond:  Barrick & Mount (1991) explore the Big Five personality traits. While they show some correlation with job performance, is it valuable to consider these traits alongside GMA? If so, how can personality assessments be used effectively in employee selection?
- Faking Good?   Morgeson  et al. (2007) raise concerns about social desirability bias in personality testing. What strategies can organizations implement to mitigate this issue and ensure the accuracy of personality test results?
- Beyond the Test Score:   Morgeson  et al. (2007) advocate for a focus on GMA. Do you think personality traits are entirely irrelevant to job performance? Can you think of any situations where specific personality characteristics might be crucial for success?

The Selection Process:

- The Right Fit:  These articles highlight the importance of GMA and personality traits. What other factors should organizations consider during the selection process to ensure they hire the right person for the job and the company culture?
- Beyond Prediction:  Is the goal of employee selection solely to predict future performance? What other qualities or characteristics might be desirable in an employee beyond their cognitive abilities and personality traits?
- The Future of Selection:  Technology is constantly evolving. How do you think advancements in technology might impact employee selection practices in the future? Will cognitive ability and personality testing remain relevant tools?
