## Slide 4

1. BIODATA RESEARCH: A SELECTIVE REVIEW OF RESEARCH

Goldsmith (1922)

-  Examined the ability of nine "personal history" items (e.g., marital status, education, belonging to clubs) to predict the first-year sales of insurance agents
- Found that using a person's biodata score could enhance hiring decisions
- One of the first published studies that demonstrated the predictive ability of biodata

Defining and Operationalizing Biodata

- D ifferent definitions of biodata include a focus on capturing information about an individual's past behaviors, events, or experiences
- Lack of agreement in one definition makes it difficult to differentiate biodata from other measures

<!-- -->

- Mael (1991) suggested that biodata items should focus on historical events that shape behavior and identity to differentiate from other measures, such as personality traits, and emphasize the importance of verifiable events and job-related experiences in biodata items

Methods of Gathering Biodata

- Biodata is gathered through self-reporting (e.g., reporting work experience on an online job application)

## Slide 5

Strategies for Developing Biodata Scales

- Empirical biodata scales have been shown to outperform scales developed via other strategies (behavioral consistency, rational, and factorial)

Reliability and Validity

- Reliability: Internal consistency and retest reliability measure coefficients ranging from .50 to .90
- Validity: Criterion-related validity shows biodata as a good predictor of job performance and turnover (predictive validity estimates around .30 to .35 for job performance and training program success)

Adverse Impact

- Concerns about adverse impact on protected groups due to items such as age or educational level and items reflecting cognitive ability

<!-- -->

- Compared to other selection methods, biodata has modest adverse impact (Hough et al., 2001; Barrick & Zimmerman, 2005)

Applicant Reactions

- Ryan and Huth (2008) suggest that applicants would likely have negative reactions to biodata items perceived as lacking job relevance, being easily faked, or being overly personal in nature

## Slide 6

Incremental Validity

- Incremental validity of biodata indicates that it adds predictive power beyond other commonly used measures to predict job performance

<!-- -->

- Mount et al. (2000) found that biodata predicted supervisory ratings of performance even after accounting for tenure, general mental ability, and personality traits

Accuracy

- Studies involving students generally found accurate reporting
- R esults are mixed in selection contexts due to the susceptibility of biodata to faking

Generalizability

- Biodata scales are transferable across organizations and countries, and their validity tends to remain stable over time (Brown, 1981; Rothstein et al., 1990; Carlson et al., 1999; Laurent, 1970; Dalessio et al., 1996; Brown, 1978; Carlson et al., 1999)

Goal : Develop a conceptual framework for understanding and measuring work experience, conducting a meta-analysis to explore its relationship with job performance

- T wo dimensions of measurement: mode (amount, time, type) and specificity (task, job, organizational)

BACKGROUND

## Slide 15

- Experience:  Events that occur in an individual's life that are perceived by the individual

THE MEASUREMENT OF WORK EXPERIENCE 

Levels Perspective : Requires clear definitions of constructs, domains of interest, and measurement specificity levels

-  Helps in conceptualizing individual, team, and organizational aspects and understanding cross-level effects

## Slide 16

HYPOTHESIZED MODERATORS 

- Amount : Focusing on actual tasks performed rather than time spent would have the highest correlation with performance
- Type and Time : Focusing on the type of experience or timed spent were expected to have lower correlations with performance

<!-- -->

- Task-level specificity would be more strongly related to job performance than higher levels of specificity (job or organizational)
- Hard criteria, which involve specific performance measures, were expected to correlate more strongly with work experience than soft criteria, which are more global

## Slide 17

RESULTS

Measurement Mode

-  Prevalent usage of time-based measures, with fewer studies using amount or type measures of work experience

<!-- -->

- 79.5% used time-based measures, such as tenure in a particular job, company, or occupation
- 11.4% measured work experience in terms of amount, counting the number of times a specific task is performed
- 9.1% categorized work experience into types, such as comparing instructors' performance levels with recent graduates in Navy training courses, assuming instructors have more qualitative and quantitative experience

Level of Specificity

- Most studies measured work experience at the job level, while fewer examined task or organizational specificity

<!-- -->

- 13.6% of studies focused on specific tasks
- 68.2% of studies measured work experience at the job level
- 18.2% of studies examined work experience at the organizational level

Variability suggests inconsistent interpretations of the term "work experience"

## Slide 19

A META-ANALYSIS OF THE CRITERION-RELATED VALIDITY

of prehire work experience

Van Iddekinge et al. (2019)

## Slide 20

BACKGROUND

- Organizations emphasize the importance of work experience in job recruitment, with a majority of job listings requiring or preferring experience
- There has been little focus on prehire experience (i.e., the amount, duration, or type of work experience individuals acquire before joining a new organization), leaving a gap in understanding the validity of prehire experience as a predictor of performance and turnover

Goal : Bridge this gap by synthesizing data from primary studies to investigate the relationship between prehire experience and performance or turnover

## Slide 21

Hypothesis 1 : Prehire experience will be a weak predictor of future performance

Hypothesis 2 : Prehire experience will be more strongly related to training performance than to job performance

Hypothesis 3 : Relations between prehire experience and performance will be stronger for measures assessing relevant experience than for measures assessing general experience

Hypothesis 4 : Relations between prehire experience and performance will be stronger for qualitative measures than for quantitative measures

Hypothesis 5 : Relations between prehire experience and performance will be stronger when experience is measured at more specific levels (task and job levels) and weaker when measured at more general levels (occupation, organization, industry, and general levels)

Hypothesis 6 : Relations between prehire experience and performance will be strongest at the beginning of a new job but will weaken over time as workers gain experience in the job

Research Question 1 : Does job complexity moderate the criterion-related validity of prehire experience? 

Research Question 2 : Is the criterion-related validity of prehire experience stronger for task performance than for overall job performance? 

Research Question 3 : Does prehire experience predict future turnover intentions or actual turnover?

RESEARCH HYPOTHESES & QUESTIONS

## Slide 22

RESULTS

Hypotheses

- P rehire experience is a weak predictor of future performance, (H1) and its relationship with performance may weaken over time (H6)

Research Questions

1. Job complexity and validity of prehire experience share a U-shaped relationship

- Low/high job complexity = Stronger
- Moderate job complexity = Weaker

2. Prehire experience's validity does not significantly differ between task and overall job performance

3. P rehire experience is not a strong predictor of turnover

IMPLICATIONS

Negative transfer of learning : Experience gained in prior jobs may not necessarily apply to subsequent roles with different requirements or organizational contexts

- Emphasis on prehire experience may be resulting in potentially qualified applicants being screened out
- Prehire experience may be more effective for roles where initial performance is critical
